Lesson 12: The States - Where the Real Action Is

The federal government gets all the attention. The real action has always been at the state level.

State legislatures decide:

  • How congressional districts are drawn (this is gerrymandering).
  • Voting laws - who can vote, when, where, with what ID.
  • Abortion law (since Dobbs).
  • Public education standards and funding.
  • Whether to apply for a Convention of States.
  • Whether to ratify federal constitutional amendments.
  • The state criminal code.

The right has dominated state-level politics for a generation. Republicans currently control 28 state legislatures. Democrats fully control 18. The rest are split.

How did this happen? Three reasons:

1. ALEC. The American Legislative Exchange Council. Founded 1973 by Paul Weyrich (also co-founded the Heritage Foundation). ALEC writes "model bills" - pre-drafted legislation - that conservative state legislators copy-paste into their own state. ALEC's model bills are how voter ID laws, "right-to-work" labor laws, "stand your ground" laws, and many anti-abortion laws spread across so many states simultaneously.

2. REDMAP. A 2010 project by the Republican State Leadership Committee. They identified state legislative seats that were cheap to flip in the 2010 census year (because the winners would draw the next decade of congressional maps). They spent 30 million dollars and flipped 700+ state seats. The resulting gerrymanders entrenched Republican advantage in the House of Representatives until 2022.

3. Low turnout. About 15 percent of voters turn out for state-level primary races. State legislative general elections often have under 20 percent turnout. A small number of motivated voters has outsized impact.

If you want to actually shift things, your state legislator is the most important person you can pay attention to. Almost nobody calls them. Five calls about one bill is a lot to a state legislator. Twenty calls is a tidal wave.
