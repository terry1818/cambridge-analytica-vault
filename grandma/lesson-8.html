Lesson 8: The Courts - Capturing the Judiciary

Once you understand the Federalist Society pipeline from Lesson 7, the recent history of the Supreme Court starts to make sense.

What this court has done in the last few years:

  • 2022 - Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health. Overturned Roe v. Wade. 50 years of abortion law erased. 18 states have since banned or severely restricted abortion.
  • 2022 - New York State Rifle and Pistol Association v. Bruen. Struck down most state gun restrictions, requiring laws to match "historical tradition."
  • 2023 - Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard. Ended race-conscious college admissions.
  • 2024 - Loper Bright v. Raimondo. Overturned the 40-year-old "Chevron" rule that let federal agencies like the EPA, FDA, and SEC write technical regulations. Now judges decide what regulations are valid.
  • 2024 - Trump v. United States. Ruled that presidents have wide immunity from criminal prosecution for "official acts." This single ruling ended most of the criminal cases against Trump.

Each of these rulings was the result of years - sometimes decades - of work. The lawsuits were filed by groups funded by the Leonard Leo network. The justices who ruled were vetted by the Federalist Society. The academic articles cited in the rulings were written by professors who hold endowed chairs funded by the same network.

It is one machine. Not a conspiracy - a coalition.

And this is just the Supreme Court. The federal appeals courts, where most cases actually end, are even more dominated by Federalist Society judges.

The lifetime appointment. Federal judges serve for life. Trump's first-term appointees average around 50 years old. They will still be deciding cases in 2070. His second-term appointees are even younger.

If a Democratic president wants to reverse the direction of the federal courts, it would take 30 to 40 years of consistent presidential wins. That is not happening. Which is why the courts are now the most powerful branch of government for the foreseeable future.
