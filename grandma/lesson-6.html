Lesson 6: The Tech Right - Silicon Valley's Hard Turn

For most of the 2000s and 2010s, Silicon Valley was politically liberal-leaning. Tech executives donated to Democrats. Companies hosted Pride events. That changed dramatically between 2020 and 2024.

The story starts with the PayPal Mafia - the group that built PayPal in the late 1990s. The original group included Peter Thiel, Elon Musk, David Sacks, Reid Hoffman, Max Levchin, and others. After PayPal was sold to eBay in 2002, they scattered and founded or funded many of the major tech companies that followed (LinkedIn, YouTube, Yelp, Palantir, SpaceX, Tesla, OpenAI).

Most stayed politically moderate. A subset - led by Thiel - went hard right.

Peter Thiel. Founded Palantir (surveillance software, big Pentagon contracts). Was Trump's most prominent Silicon Valley supporter in 2016. Has personally funded:

  • J.D. Vance's Senate campaign (15 million dollars) - made Vance the Vice President.
  • Curtis Yarvin, a writer whose work argues for replacing American democracy with a CEO-monarchy.
  • Blake Masters, who ran for Senate in Arizona.
  • The lawsuit that bankrupted the news website Gawker.

Elon Musk. Was politically moderate-libertarian until around 2020. Bought Twitter (now X) for 44 billion dollars in 2022, reinstated banned right-wing accounts, gradually made the platform a hub for right-wing politics. Donated hundreds of millions of dollars to Trump in 2024. Ran "DOGE" (Department of Government Efficiency) in early 2025, cutting federal agencies.

Marc Andreessen. Venture capitalist (Netscape founder, Andreessen Horowitz). In 2023 published the "Techno-Optimist Manifesto" - which explicitly listed liberal democratic concepts ("trust and safety," "tech ethics") as enemies. Has openly aligned with the Vance wing.

J.D. Vance. Yale Law School graduate. Worked for Thiel after law school. Wrote "Hillbilly Elegy." Ran for Senate in 2022 with Thiel's money. Now Vice President. Has publicly cited Curtis Yarvin and other "post-liberal" thinkers as influences.

This is not all of Silicon Valley. Most tech workers and executives remain moderate or liberal. But the wealthiest and loudest right-wing faction now has direct access to the highest levels of government for the first time in American history.
