Lesson 11: The Constitution - Convention of States

This is the one most people have never heard of, and it may be the most important.

The U.S. Constitution can be changed two ways:

  1. Congress proposes an amendment. Two-thirds of both chambers, then three-fourths of states ratify. This has happened 27 times in 240 years.
  2. States call a convention. If 34 state legislatures officially apply, a constitutional convention must be called. Once called, the convention can propose any amendments it wants. This has never happened in U.S. history.

Right now, 19 state legislatures have officially applied for a Convention of States. They need 34. They have been quietly adding 1 to 3 states per year for over a decade.

The group running this is called Convention of States Action. The public face is a man named Mark Meckler (a Tea Party co-founder). The money comes from the same donor network as everything else in this book - the Mercer Family Foundation, the Koch network, and various religious right donors.

The official goals sound reasonable: a balanced budget amendment, term limits for Congress, and amendments to "limit federal power." But here is the problem:

Once a convention is called, there are no rules. The Constitution does not say how delegates are chosen. It does not say how they vote. It does not say what they can vote on. Legal scholars across the political spectrum - liberal and conservative - warn that a convention could propose to rewrite anything: the Bill of Rights, voting rights, presidential powers, the structure of the Senate, anything at all. The only check is that three-fourths of states (38) would have to ratify whatever comes out.

The current scoreboard: 19 states have applied. About 8 more have it pending in their legislatures. A few more are close.

And this is decided at the state level. Your governor and state legislators decide. Not the President. Not Congress. Your state representative has a vote on this.
