Lesson 3: How to Tell What's True

Before we go further, here is the most important rule in this whole book.

Not all claims are equally true. The research vault behind this book sorts every claim into three categories. You should do the same when reading anything political:

Tier 1 - Documented. Court records, sworn testimony, congressional reports, signed government documents. You can read the actual papers yourself. Examples in this book:

  • The 1,200 convictions for January 6 (federal court records).
  • The 5 billion dollar FTC fine against Facebook for the Cambridge Analytica breach (official FTC press release).
  • Mueller's indictments of 25 Russian nationals (federal indictments).

Tier 2 - Strongly reported. Major newspapers using named sources, multiple confirmations, internal documents. Not perfect, but cross-checked. Examples:

  • The New York Times and Guardian reporting on Cambridge Analytica.
  • ProPublica reporting on the 1.6 billion dollar Leonard Leo donation.
  • Washington Post reporting on Project 2025 contributors.

Tier 3 - Allegations and unconfirmed claims. Things that look bad but are not proven. We may mention them, but we do not treat them as fact. Examples:

  • Many of the claims in the Steele Dossier.
  • Single-witness accounts of wrongdoing without supporting documents.
Everything in this book is Tier 1 or Tier 2. If you ever read something elsewhere that makes you go "really?" - ask which tier it is. If nobody can tell you, treat it as Tier 3 and keep reading skeptically.

This rule applies to everyone, including writers you agree with. Especially writers you agree with.
