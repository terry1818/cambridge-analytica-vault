Lesson 14: Cambridge Analytica - The Data Heist

This is what kicked off this whole research project for the author. It is worth knowing in detail.

In 2014, a British company called Cambridge Analytica hired a Cambridge University academic named Aleksandr Kogan. Kogan built a Facebook quiz app called "thisisyourdigitallife." About 270,000 people took the quiz.

Here is the trick: at the time, Facebook's design allowed apps to harvest data not just on the person taking the quiz, but also on every Facebook friend of that person. So from 270,000 quiz-takers, Kogan and Cambridge Analytica obtained data on 87 million Facebook users - without consent, in violation of Facebook's stated rules.

Cambridge Analytica then used this data to build "psychographic" profiles. Five personality dimensions (called OCEAN - Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism). They categorized voters by personality type and ran different political ads to different personality types.

Examples (from internal documents and whistleblower testimony):

  • Voters scoring high in Neuroticism got ads about crime and immigration threats.
  • Voters scoring high in Openness got ads about change and disruption.
  • Voters identified as persuadable but disengaged got ads designed to discourage voting at all ("voter suppression" ads).

The firm was funded primarily by Robert Mercer (Lesson 4). Steve Bannon was its Vice President. The Trump campaign hired them for the 2016 election. Brexit-related campaigns also used them.

Consequences:

  • The Guardian and New York Times broke the story in March 2018, based on whistleblower Christopher Wylie.
  • Facebook was fined 5 billion dollars by the FTC in 2019 - the largest privacy fine in U.S. history.
  • Cambridge Analytica filed for bankruptcy in May 2018.
  • Mark Zuckerberg testified before Congress.
Cambridge Analytica is dead. The techniques are not. The same kind of data-driven personality-based political advertising is now standard practice. The only thing that has changed is that the companies doing it are less famous.
