Lesson 19: The Big Idea Underneath - Why 'Post-Liberal' Matters

Time to come back to Idea Three from Lesson 2. This is the strangest part of the book and the hardest to believe at first. It is also the most important.

The thinkers and writers at the intellectual core of the modern right have openly rejected liberal democracy. By "liberal" they do not mean Democratic Party or progressive politics. They mean the entire post-WWII American constitutional order: rule of law, separation of powers, civil rights, equal voting, checks on executive power.

They are not hiding this. They write about it. They give speeches. They publish books with explicit titles.

Curtis Yarvin (also known as Mencius Moldbug). A software engineer turned political writer. Writes that America should be replaced with what he calls a "monarchy" or "CEO dictatorship" - a single leader with absolute authority, replacing the existing constitutional structure. Has been funded by Peter Thiel since 2013. J.D. Vance has cited him by name in interviews.

Patrick Deneen. Professor at Notre Dame. Wrote a 2018 book called Why Liberalism Failed. The argument is that liberal democracy is fundamentally bankrupt and should be replaced. His follow-up book Regime Change (2023) calls explicitly for a "post-liberal" successor. Praised by Vance.

The Claremont Institute. A conservative think tank. Published the famous "Flight 93 Election" essay in 2016 (the argument: conservatives must "charge the cockpit" of America, because losing means death). Claremont fellows include many people now working in the Trump administration.

National Conservatism (NatCon) conferences. An annual gathering started in 2019. Mainstream Republican senators - Vance, Hawley, Cruz - share the stage with theocrats and post-liberal academics. The speeches are public. Many call for an end to "the liberal order."

J.D. Vance. The current Vice President. Has publicly said the post-WWII constitutional order needs to be replaced. Has cited Yarvin, Deneen, and the Claremont essays approvingly. His speeches use specific post-liberal terminology that, if you have read these writers, you immediately recognize.

This is the most important thing in this whole book.

What is happening is not "Republicans being Republicans" or "Trump being Trump." There is a worked-out intellectual framework underneath it. It has names. It has books. It has institutions. It explicitly proposes to replace American constitutional democracy with something else.

You may agree with them or disagree with them. But you should know they exist, you should know what they are saying, and you should not be surprised when their plans become policy.
