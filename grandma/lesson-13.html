Lesson 13: Voting - Who Gets to Vote, and How

The simplest way to win elections without majority support is to make sure not everyone can vote. This is not a new strategy in American history.

The 2013 turning point: Shelby County v. Holder. The Supreme Court struck down Section 5 of the Voting Rights Act, which had required states with histories of voter discrimination to get federal approval before changing voting laws. After Shelby, those states no longer needed approval. The changes started immediately.

What happened in the 10 years after Shelby:

  • 1,688 polling places closed across 13 states, mostly in minority communities. (Source: Leadership Conference on Civil and Human Rights, 2019 report.)
  • Voter ID laws expanded to about 36 states. Studies show they reduce turnout among the elderly, poor, and minority voters by a few percentage points - often the margin of close elections.
  • Voter roll purges accelerated. Georgia removed roughly 1.4 million voters from the rolls between 2012 and 2019 (under then-Secretary of State Brian Kemp). Many were eligible voters.
  • Reduced early voting hours in multiple states.
  • Closed Sunday voting in some states (this targeted Black church "souls to the polls" turnout programs).

Gerrymandering. State legislatures draw congressional maps after each census. The 2010 REDMAP project (Lesson 12) produced maps that gave Republicans roughly a 7-seat advantage in the House for the entire 2012-2022 decade, even in years where Democrats won more total votes nationally.

The Electoral College. The President is not elected by popular vote. The Electoral College gives small states disproportionate weight. In 2000, 2016, and 2024, the candidate with fewer popular votes either won the presidency or came very close.

The Senate. Each state gets two Senators regardless of population. California (39 million people) gets the same two as Wyoming (580,000). The Senate is currently structured so that a majority of the Senate represents about 17 percent of the U.S. population.

None of this is illegal. Almost all of it is in the Constitution or in state law. That is the point of Idea Two from Lesson 2: build the machinery to win without needing the most votes.
