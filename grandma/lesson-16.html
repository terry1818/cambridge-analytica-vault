Lesson 16: January 6 - What's Documented

You probably saw the footage. Here is what is documented in court, on camera, and under oath - not opinion.

The day, hour by hour:

  • 12:00 PM. Trump speaks to a crowd near the White House. He says "if you do not fight like hell you are not going to have a country anymore."
  • 12:53 PM. The first barriers at the Capitol are breached.
  • 2:13 PM. The crowd breaks into the Capitol building itself.
  • 2:24 PM. Trump posts on Twitter that Vice President Pence "did not have the courage" - while Pence is being physically evacuated through the Capitol.
  • 4:17 PM. Trump posts a video asking the crowd to go home. He calls them "very special people."

The damage:

  • 5 people died that day or shortly after, including Capitol Police Officer Brian Sicknick.
  • 140 or more police officers were injured.
  • Approximately 2.7 million dollars in damage to the Capitol building.
  • Several officers later died by suicide in the months following.

The prosecutions:

  • Roughly 1,500 people were charged with crimes related to that day.
  • Roughly 1,200 were convicted.
  • The leaders of the Proud Boys (Enrique Tarrio) and Oath Keepers (Stewart Rhodes) were convicted of seditious conspiracy - the first such convictions in decades.

The House Select Committee. A committee held 9 public hearings in 2022 and published an 845-page final report. It is publicly available. The hearings featured testimony from Trump's own White House staff, including his daughter Ivanka, his Attorney General Bill Barr, and his White House Counsel Pat Cipollone.

In January 2025, on his first day back in office, Trump pardoned or commuted the sentences of nearly all the January 6 defendants, including the seditious conspiracy convictions.

A complete pardon of insurrection-related crimes is a precedent. It tells you what kind of behavior the administration is signaling it will protect in the future.
