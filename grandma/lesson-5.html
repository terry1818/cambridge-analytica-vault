Lesson 5: The Religious Right - 40 Years of Coordination

In 1979, a televangelist named Jerry Falwell founded an organization called the Moral Majority. The goal: turn conservative Christians into a unified voting bloc. It worked. They helped elect Ronald Reagan in 1980.

In 1981, conservative leaders founded the Council for National Policy. It is a semi-secret forum where religious right leaders, big donors, and political operatives meet three times a year to coordinate. The membership list is private. The agenda is private. The group has no public office.

For 40+ years, the Council for National Policy has been the central coordination point for the right's political action. Many people you have heard of have been members: Tony Perkins, Jerry Falwell Jr., Steve Bannon, Kellyanne Conway, Leonard Leo, the Mercers, the DeVoses.

Christian Nationalism is the explicit version. It is the idea that America was founded as a Christian nation, that the founders intended Christian law to be the law of the land, and that this should be restored. Not all conservative Christians are Christian Nationalists - but the active political wing increasingly is.

The Seven Mountain Mandate is a theological framework that has spread through evangelical churches since the early 2000s. It teaches that Christians have a religious duty to dominate seven spheres of society: government, business, family, education, media, arts, and religion itself. This is the theological case for Christian political takeover.

Names to know:

  • Russell Vought. Currently runs the Office of Management and Budget (the agency that controls federal spending). Wrote large parts of Project 2025. Openly identifies as a Christian Nationalist.
  • Mike Johnson. Current Speaker of the House. Member of an evangelical activist network for two decades.
  • Charlie Kirk. Runs Turning Point USA, the largest right-wing youth organization.
The religious right is not a fringe. It is the most organized, most disciplined, longest-running political movement in modern American history. It also represents a shrinking share of the American population - which is why it has invested so heavily in non-electoral mechanisms (Idea Two from Lesson 2).
