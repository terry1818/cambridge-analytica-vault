# Hypothesis: The Convention of States movement functions as a vehicle to entrench conservative policy outcomes beyond democratic revision

## Claim

The Convention of States (COS) project — and the Article V "convention to propose amendments" route it pursues — is not best understood as a neutral, populist exercise in constitutional self-government. It is, on this hypothesis, an instrument of **constitutional capture**: a deliberate effort by a donor-funded conservative coalition to write a slate of substantive policy preferences (fiscal limits, restrictions on federal regulatory power, federal term limits) into the Constitution itself, where they would sit above and beyond the reach of ordinary majoritarian politics. The falsifiable core: if true, the movement's design, funding, model outputs, and procedural choices should consistently favor entrenchment of fixed outcomes over neutral process reform.

## Why it matters

If this hypothesis holds, the COS movement is the structural endgame of the broader arc this vault traces. Where [[Redistricting]] and the [[Electoral College]] distort *who wins*, and the [[Election Denial Infrastructure]] distorts *whether results are accepted*, a successful Article V convention would distort *what is even open to democratic decision* — moving contested policy questions out of the reach of future majorities permanently. It would convert a transient political coalition's preferences into supermajority-locked constitutional text. That is the most durable form of [[Democratic backsliding]] available within nominally legal channels: not winning elections, but removing questions from the electoral arena.

## Supporting evidence

- **Documented donor funding.** The COS parent organization, Citizens for Self-Governance, received more than $12 million from Koch-linked groups and other major conservative donors between 2010 and 2018; reporting also documents at least $500,000 from the Mercer family and large contributions routed through Donors Trust and the Vanguard Charitable Endowment Program. The funding profile matches the [[patron wing]] / [[Koch Network]] constitutional-capture pattern this vault tracks. (Tier-2: SourceWatch, Center for Media and Democracy reporting.)
- **Substantive, not procedural, aims.** COS's stated agenda is a slate of amendments to "limit the power and jurisdiction of the federal government"; a 2016 simulated convention produced amendments to abolish the income tax and narrow the Commerce Clause — substantive policy entrenchment, not neutral process reform. (Tier-2.)
- **Counter-majoritarian procedure.** A "one state, one vote" convention and the 34-state threshold mean a coalition representing a minority of the national population could drive amendments — consistent with an entrenchment, rather than majority-empowerment, design.
- **Leadership lineage.** COS leadership (Mark Meckler, the late Tom Coburn, the late Jim DeMint) sits squarely in the organized conservative movement, not in a cross-ideological process-reform tradition.

## Disconfirming evidence

- **The movement has not succeeded.** Per this vault's standing correction, only ~20 states have actually passed the COS application — well short of the 34 required, and the count has stalled. An entrenchment vehicle that cannot reach its threshold is, so far, a *potential* capture vector, not an achieved one.
- **Genuine federalism conviction.** Some COS supporters articulate a sincere small-government, anti-centralization philosophy; the movement need not be cynically designed to be ideologically motivated (compare the contested-intent debate around [[James M Buchanan]]).
- **Process-reform overlap.** A minority of Article V advocates (e.g., some on the left) support a convention for process reasons; the route itself is not intrinsically a capture instrument, which weakens any claim that COS *necessarily* equals capture.

## What would falsify this

The hypothesis would be substantially weakened or falsified if: COS's actual amendment proposals shifted toward genuinely neutral process reforms (e.g., anti-gerrymandering, campaign-finance transparency) rather than fixed conservative policy outcomes; if its funding diversified away from the concentrated donor network; if its leadership embraced one-person-one-vote convention rules; or if internal documents showed the leadership genuinely indifferent to which substantive outcomes a convention produced. Conversely, evidence that organizers privately treat the convention as a means to lock in specific outcomes would strengthen it.

## Current confidence

`#confidence/medium` — The funding pattern, the substantive (not procedural) agenda, and the counter-majoritarian convention rules are documented and align with the constitutional-capture frame. But the inference from "this is what it would do if it succeeded" to "this is its function" is partly interpretive, the intent question is genuinely contested, and the movement's failure to reach 34 states means the capture remains hypothetical. Medium, not high.
