# Hypothesis: Publication of psychographic-prediction-from-Facebook-Likes research enabled Cambridge Analytica

## Claim

[[Cambridge Analytica]]'s entire commercial proposition rested on **academic research published primarily by the Cambridge Psychometrics Centre** ([[Michal Kosinski]], [[David Stillwell]], Thore Graepel) between 2012-2017 demonstrating that Big Five personality and other personal traits could be predicted from Facebook Likes with high accuracy. **Without that academic foundation**, CA could not have credibly sold psychographic targeting to clients.

The publication of this research — particularly [[Source Kosinski Stillwell Graepel 2013 PNAS]] — therefore functions as the **enabling event** for the CA scandal, even though the researchers themselves opposed the misuse and warned about it in publications.

## Why this matters

- Implicates **academic publication norms** in influence-operations enablement
- Suggests that publishing capability-demonstrating research without safeguards has political consequences
- Raises questions about how AI research today (LLMs, computer vision, persuasion modeling) should be published given the foreseeable misuse

## Supporting evidence

- Kosinski et al. 2013 PNAS paper explicitly warned that the methods could "prove dangerous for individuals"
- CA's MyPersonality-style methodology was directly adapted from the Centre's work
- [[Aleksandr Kogan]] (Cambridge insider) was hired to "clone" the Centre's data after CA was refused direct access
- CA's marketing leaned heavily on Kosinski et al.'s academic legitimacy

## Disconfirming evidence

- Similar methodology was likely independently discoverable; CA might have used alternative approaches
- The 2013 paper itself called for transparency and user control — researchers acted responsibly within their domain
- "Cause" vs. "enable" is a fine distinction; the research is necessary but not sufficient

## Current confidence

`#confidence/medium` — pattern is documented but counterfactual is hard to evaluate.

## What would falsify this

Evidence that CA's actual methodology was independent of the Cambridge Psychometrics Centre work — would require finding CA's actual training methodology and showing it didn't depend on the published research.

## Implications

If true:
- Academic publication norms in influence-relevant research need reform
- Anonymizing/restricting capability-demonstrating research has policy value
- The academic-to-commercial pipeline for influence techniques is a structural problem, not a CA-specific anomaly
