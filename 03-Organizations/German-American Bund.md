## Summary

The German American Bund was the most significant American Nazi mass organisation of the interwar period. Founded in March 1936 in Buffalo, New York, as a successor to the earlier Friends of New Germany (which the Nazi government had dissolved to avoid diplomatic complications), the Bund under Fritz Kuhn claimed a membership of 25,000 dues-paying members at its peak, including approximately 8,000 uniformed stormtroopers (Sturmabteilungen). Its February 1939 Madison Square Garden rally — attended by roughly 20,000 people — is the most visible symbol of 1930s American fascism. Rachel Maddow's *Prequel* examines the Bund as evidence that organised fascism was a real domestic mass movement, not a fringe curiosity.

## Identity

The Bund explicitly modelled itself on the Nazi Party: uniforms, salutes, youth camps on the Hitler Youth pattern, a Führerprinzip organisational structure, and antisemitic ideology claiming that Jews controlled American finance, media, and government. It propagandised for German-American ethnic solidarity and against US military involvement against Nazi Germany. Despite Kuhn's claims of coordination with Berlin, the Nazi government maintained ambiguous distance from the Bund to avoid antagonising the Roosevelt administration.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Fritz Julius Kuhn** (1896–1951): German-born; served in WWI; emigrated to US via Mexico; became citizen 1934. Assumed Bund leadership in Buffalo, 1936. Arrested 1939 for embezzlement of Bund funds; convicted and sentenced to 2.5–5 years at Sing Sing. Citizenship revoked 1943 as a foreign agent. Deported to West Germany after the war; died Munich 1951.
- **Wilhelm Kunze**: Succeeded Kuhn; attempted to maintain the organisation but could not prevent collapse after Pearl Harbor.

## Funding & money flows

- Membership dues from 25,000 members
- Revenue from summer camps, publications (*Deutscher Weckruf und Beobachter* newspaper), and events
- Kuhn was convicted of personally embezzling Bund funds — the criminal case that ended his leadership
- Nazi government financial support: alleged but not conclusively proven; Berlin consistently denied official connection

## Activities

- Published antisemitic newspapers and pamphlets
- Operated youth camps ("Camp Siegfried" on Long Island, NY; "Camp Hindenburg" in Grafton, WI; others) modelled on Hitler Youth
- Organised demonstrations, marches, and meetings
- Maintained uniformed paramilitary units (Sturmabteilungen)
- February 20, 1939: Madison Square Garden rally, 20,000 attendees; Kuhn mocked Roosevelt as "Frank D. Rosenfeld" and called the New Deal the "Jew Deal"

## Successor / related entities

- American Nazi Party (post-war successor tradition, though not organisationally continuous)
- Various 1930s–40s fascist formations: [[Silver Legion of America]], [[Christian Front]], friends of New Germany

## Controversies / investigations

- Dies Committee (House Committee on Un-American Activities, pre-HUAC) investigated the Bund from 1938
- FBI monitored membership and leadership
- Kuhn's 1939 conviction for embezzlement was engineered in part by New York DA Thomas Dewey to destroy the organisation's credibility
- Multiple Bund members appeared as defendants in the [[Great Sedition Trial 1944]]

## Open questions

- The degree of direct Nazi government control vs. autonomous initiative by German-American activists remains debated by historians.
- The relationship between Bund camp alumni networks and post-war far-right organisations has not been fully traced.
