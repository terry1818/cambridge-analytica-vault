# Babel Street

## Summary

Babel Street is a Reston, Virginia data-analytics company, founded in 2009, that sells two products central to the modern surveillance state: **Babel X**, an AI-driven social-media and open-source-intelligence monitoring platform, and **Locate X**, a tool that tracks the location history of smartphones using advertising identifiers. Locate X effectively gives U.S. government agencies — including ICE, CBP, and the Secret Service — warrantless location surveillance: it relies on commercially available "bidstream" advertising data rather than on a court order. Babel Street's products have been used to demonstrate the tracking of phones at sensitive locations including abortion clinics. The company is a prime exhibit in [[Byron Tau]]'s account of how the government turned [[commercial surveillance]] into a national-security asset by *buying* data it could not lawfully *collect*. It exemplifies the vault's thesis that population-scale tracking capability is now a commodity available off the shelf.

## Funding & money flows

Babel Street's revenue is heavily government-facing. Documented and reported customers include U.S. Customs and Border Protection, Immigration and Customs Enforcement, the U.S. Secret Service, the Department of Defense, and the Treasury Department (whose purchase of Babel Street app data was reported by *The Intercept* in 2021). Procurement records and FOIA disclosures (e.g., EPIC's FOIA on CBP's use of the Babel Street location service) confirm federal contracting. Precise aggregate contract values are not consistently documented in reviewed public sources.

## Activities

**Locate X — location tracking.** Locate X reconstructs a device's location history using its advertising ID. Mobile apps that request location permission "for advertising" feed location data into the real-time-bidding ad ecosystem; data brokers collect that bidstream data and Babel Street repackages it into a searchable tool. An analyst can draw a geofence around a location and see which devices were there and where they went. *404 Media* reported in 2024 that the tool could be used to track phones at abortion clinics — a vivid illustration of the sensitivity involved.

**Babel X — social-media / OSINT surveillance.** Babel X ingests and analyzes posts across many social-media platforms and languages, applying AI to monitor and flag content. *Vice* reported that DHS used Babel X to analyze the social media of U.S. citizens and refugees.

**Warrantless-surveillance model.** The defining feature of both products is that they let agencies obtain information — location histories, social-media profiles — by *purchase* rather than by warrant. The U.S. Secret Service, per *404 Media* reporting, took the position that users "agreed to be tracked" via app terms of service, so no warrant was needed. This is exactly the legal gap Senator Ron Wyden's "Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act" was drafted to close.

## Controversies / investigations

**Warrantless location tracking (Locate X).** The core controversy is Fourth Amendment circumvention: *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) held that obtaining historical cell-site location data normally requires a warrant, yet Locate X lets agencies buy comparable location histories from the ad ecosystem without one. Civil-liberties groups, the EFF, and lawmakers have argued this is an end-run around constitutional protections. `#claim/contested` (the legal question of whether purchased ad-location data is constitutionally distinct from cell-site data is unsettled).

**Abortion-clinic tracking.** *404 Media*'s 2024 demonstration that Locate X could identify devices at abortion clinics drew sharp attention given post-*Dobbs* criminalization risk.

**Government social-media monitoring (Babel X).** Reporting that DHS used Babel X on U.S. citizens and refugees raised First Amendment and chilling-effect concerns.

**Litigation.** Class-action and privacy litigation has targeted the Locate X data-tracking model and the broader ad-location-data trade.
