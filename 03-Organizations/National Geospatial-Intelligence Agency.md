## Summary

The National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (NGA) is the US intelligence community's lead agency for geospatial intelligence — the collection, analysis, and distribution of imagery, mapping data, and activity-based intelligence derived from physical location. Established as the National Imagery and Mapping Agency (NIMA) in 1996 and renamed NGA in 2003, it sits within the Department of Defense and serves both combat-support and national-intelligence functions. In the context of the commercial-data surveillance thread, NGA is documented as a buyer of commercially aggregated location and human-activity data — data that is collected from consumer devices without warrant via the data-broker ecosystem, and resold to government agencies. Byron Tau's *Means of Control* (2024) identifies NGA as part of the broader intelligence-community practice of purchasing what the warrant system would otherwise require an agency to obtain through judicial process. This makes NGA directly relevant to the [[Hypothesis - Federal database supersedes CA model]].

## Identity

NGA's stated mission is geospatial intelligence in support of national security, military operations, disaster response, and border monitoring. It manages relationships with over 400 commercial and government data partners. Its commercial acquisition programs — including the Luno contract vehicle (valued up to $500 million) — procure finished intelligence products derived from satellite imagery, sensor data, geolocation signals, and open-source feeds. The Luno B component specifically addresses "human domain monitoring and situational awareness," a category that encompasses commercially purchased mobility and behavioral data.

## Leadership & key personnel

NGA is led by a Director (a senior civilian or flag officer) and Deputy Director. Frank Whitworth was confirmed as Director in 2022. The agency has historically been led by figures from the intelligence community's imagery and mapping disciplines.

## Funding & money flows

NGA is funded through the National Intelligence Program (NIP) and the Military Intelligence Program (MIP); exact budget figures are classified. Its commercial data contracts are publicly disclosed in contract award announcements. The Luno A commercial data contract was awarded at approximately $290 million; overall commercial data appetite has been described internally as a "huge appetite."

## Activities

- Collection and analysis of satellite imagery and geospatial data for defense and national security.
- Purchase of commercially aggregated location data and activity-based intelligence from private data vendors — a practice that sidesteps Fourth Amendment warrant requirements by exploiting the [[Third-party doctrine]].
- Support to military operations, border security, counternarcotics, and counterterrorism targeting.
- Sponsorship of unclassified GEOINT research via Tearline.mil.

## Controversies / investigations

The core controversy around NGA and commercial data is documented in Tau's *Means of Control*: intelligence agencies, including NGA, have purchased commercially aggregated location and behavioral data from data brokers as a method of circumventing the warrant requirements of the Fourth Amendment. This exploits the [[Third-party doctrine]] — the legal principle that data voluntarily shared with a third party (such as a smartphone app) loses constitutional protection. Civil liberties groups including the EFF and ACLU have identified this practice as a constitutional end-run. The [[Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act]], passed by the House in 2024, was directly aimed at closing this loophole.
