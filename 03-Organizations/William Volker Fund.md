# William Volker Fund

## Summary

The William Volker Fund was a charitable foundation established in 1932 by William Volker, a Kansas City, Missouri, home-furnishings magnate, originally to aid the poor, reform local health care and education, and fight machine politics. After Volker's death in 1947, control passed to his nephew **Harold Luhnow**, who redirected the fund away from local charity toward an ambitious ideological project: increasing the acceptance of "Old Right" and Austrian-school economic thought in American academia and public life. For roughly fifteen years the Volker Fund was, in the words of historians, one of the very few sources of significant money available to the embryonic libertarian movement — and so it became one of its key architects.

The Volker Fund matters intensely to this vault as the **mid-century origin point of the patron-wing funding model**. Long before the [[Koch Network]], the Volker Fund pioneered the technique of using a private fortune to underwrite sympathetic academics, fund the institutions that train them, and seed the think tanks that translate their ideas into policy. It paid Ludwig von Mises's salary at NYU, funded F. A. Hayek's position at the University of Chicago, financed the 1947 founding meeting of the [[Mont Pelerin Society]], and seeded the [[Foundation for Economic Education]] and, indirectly, the [[Institute for Humane Studies]]. It is the seed-funder node in the long lineage the vault calls "constitutional capture."

## Identity

- **Legal name(s):** William Volker Charities Fund.
- **Founded:** 1932, by William Volker.
- **Dissolved / restructured:** Operations wound down by 1962; remaining assets distributed in the early 1960s, with personnel and mission continuity passing to successor bodies including the Center for American Studies and, in lineage, the [[Institute for Humane Studies]].
- **Registered jurisdiction:** Missouri (Kansas City).
- **Subsidiary:** National Book Foundation — the Volker Fund's book-distribution arm.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **William Volker** (1859–1947) — founder; Kansas City businessman; the original charitable benefactor.
- **Harold Luhnow** — Volker's nephew; took control of William Volker & Co. and the Fund's board in 1947; the figure who transformed the Fund into an ideological-funding operation.
- **F. A. "Baldy" Harper** — Volker Fund staff economist; later founded the [[Institute for Humane Studies]] (1961), carrying the Volker mission forward.
- **Herbert Cornuelle and Richard Cornuelle** — Fund staff who managed grant programs.

## Funding & money flows

The Volker fortune came from William Volker & Co., a wholesale home-furnishings business. Under Luhnow the Fund's money flowed outward into:

- **Academic salaries and fellowships:** Underwrote the fellowship that brought **Friedrich Hayek** to the University of Chicago (his Committee on Social Thought position, outside the economics department); paid **Ludwig von Mises**'s salary as a visiting professor at New York University.
- **Foundational books:** Funded the lecture series that **Milton and Rose Friedman** turned into *Capitalism and Freedom*, and approved the grant that enabled **Murray Rothbard** to write *Man, Economy, and State*.
- **The [[Mont Pelerin Society]]:** When Hayek organized the 1947 founding meeting at Mont Pèlerin, Switzerland, the Volker Fund paid for 17 American economists — including Friedman, Henry Hazlitt, Leonard Read, George Stigler, and Mises — to attend. Through at least 1953 the Fund paid membership fees and travel for nearly all non-US members.
- **Think tanks:** As early as 1946 Luhnow earmarked Volker money for **Leonard Read** and agreed to fund the establishment of the [[Foundation for Economic Education]] — the first major post-war libertarian think tank.
- **Book distribution:** Through the National Book Foundation, the Fund donated libertarian and conservative books to college libraries nationwide (authors included Böhm-Bawerk, Hayek, Mises, Roscoe Pound, Leo Strauss, and Eric Voegelin).

## Activities

The Volker Fund's central activity under Luhnow was **building intellectual infrastructure**. Rather than fund a single cause, it underwrote the *people* (sympathetic economists in elite universities), the *institutions* (think tanks and societies), and the *texts* (subsidized books and their distribution) needed for free-market and Austrian ideas to gain academic respectability. This was a deliberate, multi-decade strategy: ideas first, policy later. The same blueprint — anonymous or low-profile money, academic respectability-building, then think-tank translation into policy — was later scaled up dramatically by the [[Koch Network]] and the donors in the vault's patron wing.

## Successor / related entities

- **[[Institute for Humane Studies]]** — founded 1961 by ex-Volker economist F. A. Harper; the most direct mission-successor.
- **[[Foundation for Economic Education]]** — seeded with Volker money; first major post-war libertarian think tank.
- **[[Mont Pelerin Society]]** — the international free-market network the Fund financially launched.
- **[[Koch Network]]** — later, vastly larger patron-wing operation built on the same fund-the-academy blueprint.

## Controversies / investigations

- The Volker Fund pioneered **donor anonymity in ideological funding** — economists Murray Rothbard and others later referred to such backers as "Mr. Anonymous." Critics on both left and (paradoxically) within the libertarian movement noted that a movement that prized "spontaneous order" was in fact deliberately and centrally financed.
- The Fund experienced internal ideological turmoil in its final years (a dispute over religion and traditionalism among staff and advisers), which hastened its winding-down.

## Open questions

- The full grant record of the Volker Fund is incompletely public; much is reconstructed from later memoirs and from Mises Institute and academic histories.
- How directly does the Volker-to-IHS-to-Koch lineage run as an institutional continuity versus a looser ideological inheritance? The personnel link (Harper) is documented; the framing as a single continuous "project" is interpretive.
