# Faith Wins

## Summary

Faith Wins is a church-based voter-mobilization operation founded in 2017 by Chad Connelly, the former first-ever National Director of Faith Engagement for the Republican National Committee. Its purpose is to "inspire, inform and mobilize pastors and faith leaders" to engage politically and to register and turn out evangelical voters. Faith Wins operates as a 501(c)(3) and does not formally endorse candidates; instead it pursues a non-electoral-on-paper but politically directional strategy — voter registration, pastor training, and "biblical worldview" issue education — that, by design, mobilizes a constituency that votes overwhelmingly Republican. Faith Wins matters to this vault because it is a clean modern example of the **church-as-precinct strategy**: the systematic conversion of congregations and pastor networks into an electoral turnout infrastructure. The organization claims to have registered over 2 million new evangelical voters across recent election cycles and to have hosted more than 20,000 pastors at its events. Connelly has been described by critics as a "Christian nationalist matchmaker" connecting right-wing pastors with politicians.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Chad Connelly** — founder and president. Connelly is a former chairman of the South Carolina Republican Party and was the RNC's first-ever National Director of Faith Engagement, where he built the party's pastor-outreach apparatus before leaving to run that strategy as an independent operation. His RNC background means Faith Wins applies a partisan-professional turnout methodology to the church world while wearing a 501(c)(3), formally non-partisan, label.
- Faith Wins operates through a field model of "church liaisons" — designated members within congregations who relay political information and run registration drives.

## Activities

Faith Wins's core activity is **turning churches into voter-mobilization units**. Its stated five-part mission: (1) ensure Christian voices are "heard and not ignored" in public life; (2) give faith leaders resources to leverage their influence; (3) identify "church liaisons" to disseminate political information within congregations; (4) conduct voter-registration drives; and (5) connect pastors and congregations with "political thought leaders, candidates, and elected officials."

In practice this means:

- **Pastor events and training.** Faith Wins hosts virtual and in-person briefings for pastors and faith leaders — claiming more than 20,000 pastor-attendances — that combine "biblical worldview" framing with practical instruction on political engagement and registration.
- **Voter registration at scale.** The organization frames the central problem as the "low-propensity Christian voter" — religious conservatives who do not reliably register or turn out — and runs registration drives in churches to convert that latent constituency into actual votes. It claims more than 2 million new evangelical registrations across recent cycles.
- **Issue education.** It distributes voter guides and issue material aligned with a religious-right agenda.

Faith Wins's careful structure — a 501(c)(3) that does not endorse candidates and frames its work as registration and education — is itself analytically significant. It allows a former RNC operative to run a partisan-effect turnout machine through tax-exempt religious channels. Critics (e.g., Flux's reporting) describe Connelly as a Christian-nationalist organizer matching right-wing pastors to politicians. In the vault's framework, Faith Wins is the **grassroots distribution layer** of the theocratic political project: where litigation firms change the law and donor conduits move money, Faith Wins converts the pews into precinct-level political capacity. It builds on, and feeds, the broader infrastructure that includes denominational clergy networks (e.g., the [[Southern Baptist Convention]]) and durable advocacy groups like [[Concerned Women for America]].
