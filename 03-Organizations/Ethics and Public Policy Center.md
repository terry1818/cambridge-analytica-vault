## Summary

The Ethics and Public Policy Center (EPPC) is Washington D.C.'s longest-running institute dedicated to applying religious moral frameworks — primarily but not exclusively Catholic and broadly Judeo-Christian — to American domestic and foreign policy debates. Founded in 1976 at Georgetown University, EPPC became the central institutional home of the Catholic neoconservative intellectual network identified by Damon Linker in *The Theocons*: its presidency rotated among [[George Weigel]], Elliott Abrams, and (under later leadership) Ed Whelan, while fellows included [[Michael Novak]], [[Robert P. George]], and their intellectual successors.

Under current president Ryan T. Anderson, EPPC has expanded its engagement with gender-critical arguments and post-liberal social conservatism, extending the theocon tradition into new culture-war terrain. EPPC celebrated its 50th anniversary in 2026.

## Identity

EPPC describes its mission as applying "a full and true account of the human person" to contemporary politics, law, and culture. This formulation — drawn from Catholic philosophical anthropology — signals the institution's intellectual lineage even as it presents itself as broadly ecumenical. The phrase "full and true account of the human person" is EPPC's translation of natural-law anthropology into accessible policy language.

EPPC sits at the intersection of the religious-right policy infrastructure, the neoconservative foreign-policy network (through Abrams and others), and the [[Federalist Society]] legal conservative movement. It has published work on abortion, religious liberty, bioethics, foreign policy, and — increasingly — transgender policy and higher education reform.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Ernest Lefever** — founder, 1976; expressed concerns about secular exclusion of religious values from policy.
- **[[George Weigel]]** — president 1989–June 1996; transformed EPPC into the leading Catholic neoconservative institution; remains Distinguished Senior Fellow.
- **Elliott Abrams** — president 1996–2001; neoconservative foreign policy intellectual; later served in George W. Bush and Trump administrations.
- **Ed Whelan** — president roughly 2004–c.2023; focused on judicial nominations and constitutional law; close to [[Federalist Society]] networks; well known for the 2018 episode in which he incorrectly identified a person as Christine Blasey Ford's alleged assailant on social media.
- **Ryan T. Anderson** — current president; author of *When Harry Became Sally: Responding to the Transgender Moment* (2018); former [[Witherspoon Institute]] fellow and *Public Discourse* editor.
- **[[Michael Novak]]** — longtime fellow; George Frederick Jewett Chair in Religion, Philosophy, and Public Policy at AEI overlapped with EPPC work.

## Activities

- Publishes policy essays, books, and commentary by fellows and visiting scholars.
- Hosts events and briefings in Washington D.C. for policymakers.
- Runs the "Evangelicals and Catholics Together" intellectual project (under Weigel's leadership, continuing).
- The "Human Dignity Project" addresses bioethics and end-of-life questions from a natural-law perspective.
- Anderson's tenure has added substantial focus on sex/gender policy and higher education reform.
- Former fellows have testified before Congress and served in executive branch positions across Republican administrations.

## Successor / related entities

- [[Witherspoon Institute]] — overlapping fellow networks; Ryan Anderson moved from Witherspoon to EPPC presidency.
- [[First Things magazine]] — key publication outlet for EPPC fellows.
- [[American Principles Project]] — advocacy counterpart; shares personnel with EPPC orbit.

## Controversies / investigations

- Ed Whelan's 2018 tweet thread incorrectly identifying a person as Christine Blasey Ford's alleged assailant during the Kavanaugh confirmation hearings caused significant controversy; Whelan apologized.
- Critics characterize EPPC as a Catholic partisan institution that uses the language of "Judeo-Christian tradition" to provide religious legitimation for Republican policy positions.
- Anderson's *When Harry Became Sally* was removed by Amazon in February 2021, generating a religious-liberty debate that EPPC used to raise its profile.

## Open questions

- The full scope of EPPC's funding from Leonard Leo-adjacent networks (Marble Freedom Trust, 85 Fund, etc.) has not been confirmed in primary sources reviewed.
- EPPC's relationship to the post-liberal/integralist turn among younger Catholic conservatives — does it adopt, resist, or manage the transition? — is an active open question.
