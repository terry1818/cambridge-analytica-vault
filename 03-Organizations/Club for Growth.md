# Club for Growth

## Summary

The Club for Growth is an American anti-tax and free-market advocacy organization founded in 1999 by [[Stephen Moore]], Thomas L. Rhodes, and Richard Gilder. Politico has called it "the pre-eminent institution promoting Republican adherence to a free-market, free-trade, anti-regulation agenda." Unlike most political action committees, the Club is best known for an aggressive **primary-election strategy**: it funds and endorses challengers against sitting Republicans whom it judges insufficiently committed to tax cuts and small government — a practice that has made the Club a feared enforcer of fiscal orthodoxy within the GOP.

The Club for Growth is central to the vault's "patron-wing" and "constitutional-capture" threads as a **discipline mechanism**. Where the [[Koch Network]] and the [[William Volker Fund]] built intellectual infrastructure, and donor families like the [[Uihlein family]] supplied money, the Club operationalizes that money as electoral pressure: it makes deviation from anti-tax orthodoxy electorally dangerous for Republican officeholders. Its first president, Stephen Moore, used the organization to target "Republicans in Name Only" (RINOs) and criticized GOP senators — Arlen Specter, Olympia Snowe, George Voinovich — who resisted tax cuts. The Club has operated through several legally distinct vehicles: the Club for Growth PAC, the Club for Growth Action super PAC, and the Club for Growth Foundation.

## Identity

- **Legal name(s):** Club for Growth (with affiliated Club for Growth PAC, Club for Growth Action super PAC, and Club for Growth Foundation 501(c)(3)).
- **Founded:** 1999.
- **Headquarters:** Washington, D.C.
- **Registered jurisdiction:** US.
- **Structure:** A family of entities — a 527/PAC, a super PAC (Club for Growth Action), and a tax-exempt foundation — allowing both disclosed political spending and less-transparent "issue" activity.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **[[Stephen Moore]]** — co-founder; first president, 1999–2004; later a Heritage Foundation economist and Trump-era economic adviser. Removed as Club president by the board in December 2004.
- **Thomas L. Rhodes** — co-founder; National Review-connected.
- **Richard Gilder** — co-founder; Wall Street financier and philanthropist.
- **David McIntosh** — former Republican congressman; longtime Club president in the modern era.

## Funding & money flows

- The Club for Growth raises money from fiscally conservative donors and bundles it to endorsed candidates. In 2003–2004 it was the largest single fundraiser for Republican House and Senate candidates outside the Republican Party itself, raising nearly $22 million.
- In the post-*Citizens United* era, Club for Growth Action (the super PAC) has spent heavily in primaries and general elections; major individual donors have included hard-right patrons such as the [[Uihlein family]].
- The Club's foundation arm permits 501(c)(3) "educational" activity; the multi-entity structure gives donors flexibility over disclosure.

## Activities

The Club for Growth's defining activity is **primary intervention**. While it favors open-seat races, it has also helped unseat incumbent Republicans judged to be insufficiently conservative on taxes and spending. Its endorsement comes with fundraising support and, through Club for Growth Action, independent expenditures — making a Club endorsement (or, conversely, a Club-funded challenger) a significant factor in Republican primaries.

The Club issues scorecards rating members of Congress on fiscal votes, lobbies against tax increases, and promotes a free-market, anti-regulation, anti-tariff agenda. Its relationship with the broader MAGA movement has been complicated: the Club clashed with [[Donald Trump]] at times over tariffs and particular candidates, while still operating within the hard-right coalition. Functionally, the Club complements the [[No Tax Pledge]] of Grover Norquist's [[Americans for Tax Reform]]: where the Pledge extracts a written commitment, the Club supplies the electoral enforcement.

## Successor / related entities

- **[[Americans for Tax Reform]]** — Grover Norquist's organization; the [[No Tax Pledge]] complements the Club's electoral enforcement.
- **[[Koch Network]] / [[Americans for Prosperity]]** — parallel free-market advocacy infrastructure.
- **[[Uihlein family]]** — major funders of Club for Growth Action.
- **Heritage Foundation** — where co-founder Stephen Moore later worked; ideological ally.

## Controversies / investigations

- The Club's strategy of attacking sitting Republicans has made it a recurring target of intra-party criticism; establishment Republicans accuse it of costing the party winnable general elections by backing unelectable primary winners.
- The multi-entity structure (PAC, super PAC, foundation) has drawn campaign-finance scrutiny over coordination and disclosure.
- The Club's on-and-off conflict with Trump over tariffs and endorsements is a documented fault line within the modern right.

## Open questions

- How much of the Club's recent funding flows through less-transparent channels, and who are its largest current donors?
- What is the net electoral effect of the Club's primary strategy — does it move Republican policy rightward, or mainly raise the cost of primaries?
- How will the Club's free-trade, anti-tariff orthodoxy reconcile with a Republican Party increasingly shaped by Trump-style protectionism?
