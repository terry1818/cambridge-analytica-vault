# Lockheed Martin

## Summary

Lockheed Martin Corporation, formed in 1995 by the merger of Lockheed Corporation and Martin Marietta, is the largest U.S. defense contractor by revenue and one of the largest in the world. It builds the F-35 and F-22 fighter jets, the Aegis Combat System, a wide range of missiles and missile-defense systems, and military satellites. For this vault, Lockheed Martin matters less as a maker of physical weapons than as a major node in the U.S. **surveillance and information-processing** state: it has long performed surveillance and data-processing work for the CIA, the FBI, the IRS, the NSA, the Pentagon, the Census Bureau, and the Postal Service. The company is now a leading example of the convergence between the legacy military-industrial complex and the new commercial AI sector — integrating large AI models (including [[Google]]'s Gemini) into its weapons platforms and combat systems. Lockheed Martin thus sits at the intersection this vault tracks: the old defense establishment, the new [[tech right]] AI firms, and the surveillance apparatus, increasingly fused into one "intelligent network-of-networks."

## Leadership & key personnel

- **James Taiclet** — Chairman, President, and CEO; has driven the "21st Century Security" strategy emphasizing AI, 5G, and networked systems
- The company's board and executive ranks rotate heavily with retired senior military officers and former Pentagon officials — the classic "revolving door" of the military-industrial complex

## Funding & money flows

Lockheed Martin's revenue — well over $70 billion annually — derives overwhelmingly from government contracts, principally the U.S. Department of Defense, plus foreign military sales and intelligence-agency work. Representative recent contracts:

- A C-130J aircraft contract increased to **$25 billion** in December 2025
- A **$328.5 million** military-equipment sale to Taiwan, awarded December 31, 2025
- Major ongoing F-35 program funding, the largest weapons program in history
- Surveillance and information-processing contracts with the CIA, NSA, FBI, IRS, and other agencies

The broader context: as of the mid-2020s the Department of Defense accounts for roughly 95% of all federal AI contract value, with that spending rising from hundreds of millions to over $4 billion — a market Lockheed Martin is positioned to capture.

## Activities

**Weapons platforms.** F-35 and F-22 fighters, the Aegis Combat System, missiles and missile defense, military satellites, and the Sikorsky helicopter line.

**Surveillance and information processing.** Lockheed Martin has long provided surveillance and data-processing services to the U.S. intelligence community and federal agencies — a side of the company less visible than its aircraft but central to this vault's surveillance-state focus.

**AI integration.** Under the "21st Century Security" vision, Lockheed Martin is weaving AI and machine learning through its platforms. It has integrated AI into the Aegis Combat System to accelerate threat assessment — technology reportedly used to help U.S. Navy destroyers counter Houthi missile and drone attacks. In October 2025 the company announced it was integrating [[Google]]'s Gemini generative-AI models into its workflows, an emblematic fusion of legacy defense prime and commercial AI.

## Controversies / investigations

- **Revolving door:** Lockheed Martin's deep personnel exchange with the Pentagon and intelligence community is a long-standing concern about defense-contractor capture of procurement decisions. `#claim/established`
- **Cost and oversight of the F-35:** The F-35 program has faced sustained criticism over cost overruns and sustainment expenses.
- **AI in weapons:** The integration of commercial AI (Gemini) into combat systems raises accountability and escalation concerns — the same debate that surrounded [[Project Maven]], now arriving inside the defense prime itself.
- **Supply-chain dependencies:** The Pentagon in 2026 began scrutinizing defense contractors' AI supply-chain dependencies, with Lockheed Martin among those reviewed.
