## Summary

Spiritual Mobilization was the first large-scale, corporately funded organization to systematically recruit Protestant clergy as ideological agents for free-market conservatism. Founded in 1935 by [[James Fifield]] at his wealthy First Congregational Church of Los Angeles, it operated as the prototype for all subsequent religious-right infrastructure: private corporate capital flowing through a non-profit religious organization to shape the political views of clergy, who then transmitted those views to millions of congregants. Its explicit ideology — which Fifield and scholars label [[Christian libertarianism]] — held that laissez-faire capitalism was the only economic system compatible with Christian freedom, and that the New Deal represented a form of pagan statism.

## Identity

Spiritual Mobilization occupied an unusual niche: it was neither a church nor a conventional political organization, but a clergy-to-clergy persuasion network. Its primary targets were Congregational, Presbyterian, and Episcopal ministers — mainstream Protestant denominations whose elite congregations included corporate executives and business owners. SM framed opposition to the New Deal not in business terms but in theological terms, making it harder to dismiss as mere corporate self-interest.

Its magazine *Faith and Freedom* (launched late 1940s) published content from leading libertarian economists (Ludwig von Mises, Henry Hazlitt, Leonard Read) alongside religious arguments, creating a unified libertarian-theological worldview for clerical readers.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **James W. Fifield Jr.** — founder, president, and chief ideologue. His theological method involved minimizing New Testament passages on wealth while emphasizing individual spiritual responsibility as the Christian alternative to state welfare.
- Board drawn from corporate leadership: by the mid-1940s, SM's board was described as a "who's who of the conservative establishment."

## Funding & money flows

- Funded primarily by major corporations and their executives: oil companies, General Motors, and other industrial interests contributed to SM's budget.
- 1947 budget: approximately $270,000 (approximately $3 million in 2025 dollars).
- [[J. Howard Pew]] (Sun Oil president) was among the most significant individual patrons.
- Corporate donors reportedly pressured competitors, suppliers, and employees to also contribute — an early example of the social coercion tactics that characterize dark-money networks.
- Evidence tier: documented in Kruse (2015) based on archival records; specific donor lists not fully public.

## Activities

- Built a network of 17,000+ "minister-representatives" across the country by the late 1940s.
- Published *Faith and Freedom* magazine monthly, featuring libertarian economists and anti-New Deal theological arguments.
- Ran a weekly radio program broadcasting Christian-libertarian ideology to lay audiences.
- Distributed libertarian books and pamphlets through the clergy network.
- Organized the "Freedom Under God" campaign in 1951 — an early iteration of the push that would eventually result in "under God" being added to the Pledge of Allegiance (1954) and "In God We Trust" becoming the national motto (1956).
- Annual "Freedom Under God" observances were held on July 4th, framing American civil religion in explicitly anti-statist terms.

## Successor / related entities

Spiritual Mobilization did not have formal successors, but its institutional template was replicated by:
- [[National Association of Evangelicals]] (parallel evangelical umbrella)
- Christian Freedom Foundation (founded 1950 by [[J. Howard Pew]] and Howard Kershner)
- [[Christianity Today]] (1956, funded by Pew)
- Later, the [[Heritage Foundation]], [[Council for National Policy]], and the broader New Right infrastructure operated on the same corporate-funding-of-religious-ideological-distribution model.

## Controversies / investigations

- SM's corporate funding model was criticized at the time by mainline Protestant leaders who argued it represented business interests using religion as a political shield.
- The organization's conflation of Christianity with specific economic policy positions was contested within Protestant denominations.

## Open questions

- Whether SM maintained formal coordination with the [[Mont Pelerin Society]] (founded 1947) or the [[Foundation for Economic Education]] beyond shared personnel has not been fully established.
- The complete list of corporate donors has not been publicly disclosed; Kruse drew on partial records.
