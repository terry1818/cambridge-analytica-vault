# CIA

## Summary

The Central Intelligence Agency is the United States' principal civilian foreign-intelligence service, responsible for human-intelligence collection, all-source analysis, and covert action abroad. It was created by the [[National Security Act of 1947]], the same statute that built the modern [[national security state]]. The CIA's founding charter deliberately excluded domestic law-enforcement and internal-security powers — those were left to the [[FBI]] — because President Truman feared an American secret police. For this vault the CIA is significant for three reasons: it is the institutional embodiment of the Cold War security state whose logic the vault tracks; it has a documented history of violating its domestic-surveillance ban (Operation CHAOS); and in the 2020s it, like its sister agencies, has been identified as a purchaser of commercially available bulk data, showing how the foreign-intelligence mission can quietly acquire information about Americans through the [[data broker]] market.

## Identity

- **Legal name(s):** Central Intelligence Agency.
- **Other names / fronts:** "the Agency"; historically operated through numerous proprietary front companies.
- **Founded:** September 18, 1947, when the National Security Act took effect; successor to the wartime Office of Strategic Services (OSS) and the interim Central Intelligence Group (1946).
- **Parent / owner:** an independent executive-branch agency; since 2004 its director reports to the Director of National Intelligence for community coordination.
- **Registered jurisdiction:** United States; headquartered at Langley, Virginia.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Allen Dulles** — Director of Central Intelligence 1953–1961; oversaw the agency's expansion into large-scale covert action.
- **William Burns** — Director 2021–2025.
- The CIA Director leads the agency; historically the post combined agency leadership with the community-wide "Director of Central Intelligence" role, a function transferred to the DNI in 2004–2005.

## Funding & money flows

The CIA is funded through the classified National Intelligence Program budget; precise figures are not public, though aggregate intelligence-community spending exceeds $90 billion annually. Relevant to this vault: Senator [[Ron Wyden]]'s oversight work and reporting on the intelligence community's commercial-data purchases identified the CIA among agencies acquiring data from brokers, and the agency invests in commercial intelligence ventures through its venture arm, In-Q-Tel.

## Activities

**Foreign intelligence and covert action.** The CIA's core lawful work is overseas: clandestine HUMINT collection, analysis, and presidentially directed covert action. This is outside the vault's domestic focus but establishes the institutional culture.

**Operation CHAOS — the domestic-surveillance violation.** Between 1967 and 1974 the CIA ran Operation CHAOS, a domestic-espionage program established under President Johnson and expanded under President Nixon, targeting American anti-war, civil-rights, and protest movements to find (largely nonexistent) foreign influence. The program directly violated the agency's 1947 charter; subsequent disclosures revealed an index of roughly 300,000 names and detailed files on about 7,200 U.S. citizens. CHAOS, exposed by the Church Committee, is the foundational example of foreign-intelligence capability turned inward — a pattern the vault treats as a recurring risk.

**Commercial-data acquisition.** In the 2020s the CIA has been identified, alongside the [[NSA]] and [[FBI]], as a participant in the intelligence community's purchase of commercially available information from [[data broker|data brokers]]. Because such data is bought rather than compelled, it can include information on U.S. persons without the legal process that would otherwise apply — a modern, market-based echo of the CHAOS problem.

**Backdoor searches.** Like the FBI and NSA, the CIA conducts warrantless queries of [[Section 702]]-acquired data for information about Americans, one of the "thousands" of such searches privacy advocates document each year.

## Successor / related entities

- [[FBI]] — domestic counterpart; the 1947 design assigned internal security to the FBI, not the CIA.
- [[NSA]] — sister signals-intelligence agency, also created/expanded under the post-1947 framework.
- [[DHS]] — later-created (2003) homeland-security department; not a CIA component but part of the same security ecosystem.
- [[National Security Act of 1947]] — the statute that created the CIA.
- In-Q-Tel — the CIA's strategic venture-capital arm, investing in commercial surveillance and data technologies.

## Controversies / investigations

- **Operation CHAOS** — domestic spying in violation of the agency charter; exposed 1975–1976.
- **Church Committee findings** — established the pattern of intelligence-agency abuse against political dissent.
- **Commercial-data purchases (2020s)** — contested as a circumvention of the domestic-spying bar by acquisition rather than collection.
