## Summary

TargetPoint Consulting, Inc. was founded in 2003 by Alexander P. Gage as the first purpose-built Republican microtargeting firm. Contracted by the Bush-Cheney 2004 campaign and the Republican National Committee for a reported $3 million engagement, TargetPoint developed individual-level voter segmentation that broke the electorate into attitude clusters rather than simple demographics. The firm's 2004 work is credited as a central component of the GOP's superior ground game and is documented at length in [[Source - Issenberg 2012 - The Victory Lab]] as the GOP equivalent of what [[Hal Malchow]] and [[Ken Strasma]] were building on the Democratic side.

## Identity

TargetPoint is a full-service public opinion and market research firm that extended commercial consumer-segmentation techniques into electoral politics. Its core innovation was assigning individual voters probability scores based on attitude clusters derived from overlaying voter-file data with commercial consumer databases (purchasing behavior, magazine subscriptions, lifestyle data). This allowed campaigns to deliver highly tailored messages to micro-segments rather than broadcasting to demographic blocs.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Alexander P. Gage** — founder and president; previously worked in commercial market research; died June 2024 at age 74 (noted in Detroit News obituary). Gage is widely described as the creator of political microtargeting in its GOP form.

## Funding & Money Flows

- Bush-Cheney '04: Contracted for approximately $3 million national segmentation project, commissioned by Ken Mehlman.
- Republican National Committee: Between August 2005 and July 2006, the RNC paid TPC approximately $1.05 million for political consulting, $171,000 for microtargeting services, and smaller amounts for voter-ID work.

## Activities

**2004 Bush-Cheney campaign**: TargetPoint divided the electorate into attitude-based clusters — identifying, for instance, 19,000 lower-middle-class Hispanic women in New Mexico who were resistant to Republicans generally but supported Bush's No Child Left Behind education policy. The campaign delivered targeted mail to that specific segment on education, reportedly moving their vote intention. Similar micro-segment work was done on Black voters interested in education and health care in Ohio. The firm provided targeting support across 18 swing states.

**Post-2004**: Continued as an RNC vendor and worked on subsequent Republican campaigns and state party programs. Also, notably, took on bipartisan work — including for the Freedom to Marry campaign (marriage equality) in 2012, illustrating that the firm's technology was ideologically agnostic.

## Successor / Related Entities

No direct successor entity identified. The methodological approach was absorbed into standard GOP campaign practice and later replicated by firms including [[Data Propria]] (Trump 2020 data firm with Cambridge Analytica alumni) and others.

## Controversies / Investigations

No formal investigations or legal proceedings identified as of vault creation date. The TargetPoint model raised early concerns about the use of commercial consumer data in political targeting — the same concern later amplified by the [[Cambridge Analytica]] scandal.
