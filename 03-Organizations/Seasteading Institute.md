# Seasteading Institute

## Summary

The Seasteading Institute (TSI) is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit founded on April 15, 2008, by **Patri Friedman** and **Wayne Gramlich** to facilitate the creation of autonomous, mobile communities on floating platforms in international waters — "seasteads." Patri Friedman is a former Google software engineer and the grandson of economist Milton Friedman; the institute received early seed funding from billionaire [[Peter Thiel]] through his [[Thiel Foundation]] and personal giving (a $500,000 pledge in 2008, ~$1.25 million total by 2011).

The Seasteading Institute is a central node in the vault's "constitutional-capture," "tech-right," and "patron-wing" threads because it expresses the libertarian project in its purest form: **exit rather than reform**. Where the [[Koch Network]] sought to constrain democratic majorities from within the U.S. constitutional system, seasteading proposes leaving that system entirely — building new polities beyond the territorial jurisdiction of any existing state, where founders can experiment freely with novel "social, political, and legal systems." Critics describe it as an escapist fantasy of wealthy secession from shared governance; the institute itself frames seasteading as a way to enable "competitive governance" and let citizens "exit" their states more easily. The same logic — governance as a competitive, opt-in product — connects seasteading to the [[Charter cities]] movement.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Patri Friedman** — co-founder and longtime executive director; former Google engineer; grandson of Milton Friedman and son of economist David Friedman. The intellectual driving force.
- **Wayne Gramlich** — co-founder; an early collaborator who, with Friedman, authored foundational seasteading writings.
- **[[Peter Thiel]]** — principal early funder; bankrolled the institute's launch and early operations.
- **Joe Quirk** — later president and public spokesman, styling himself a "seavangelist"; co-author of the book *Seasteading* (2017).

## Funding & money flows

- **[[Peter Thiel]] / [[Thiel Foundation]]:** Thiel pledged $500,000 to the new institute on April 15, 2008; by 2011 his total giving was reported at roughly $1.25 million — the foundational money behind TSI.
- The institute has otherwise relied on donations from libertarian-aligned individuals and small-dollar supporters; it has never approached the funding scale of the established patron-wing networks.
- Later projects (the French Polynesia "seazone," and the spin-off venture Blue Frontiers) sought additional private investment and cryptocurrency-linked financing.

## Activities

**Engineering and design studies.** In January 2009 the institute patented a design for "ClubStead," a 200-person resort seastead — the first serious engineering analysis in the movement. TSI's early work was largely conceptual: design competitions, feasibility studies, and advocacy writing.

**The French Polynesia "seazone."** TSI's most concrete effort: in September 2016 the institute met officials in French Polynesia, and on January 13, 2017, French Polynesia's Minister of Housing signed a memorandum of understanding with TSI to create a semi-autonomous "seazone" in a sheltered lagoon, with its own governance framework. A spin-off company, Blue Frontiers, was created to build the prototype.

**Collapse of the flagship project.** The French Polynesia project collapsed in 2018 after local protests and government pushback; critics in Polynesia framed it as a form of techno-colonialism imposed on Pacific waters. The MOU lapsed.

**Rhetorical repositioning.** After the 2018 collapse, the institute shifted its messaging — from an explicitly libertarian "escape taxes and government" pitch toward framing seasteading as a "humanitarian necessity" (climate-adaptation housing, sea-level-rise response). Critics note this as a reputational rebrand of an essentially political project.

## Successor / related entities

- **[[Thiel Foundation]] / [[Peter Thiel]]** — seed funder; the seasteading project is part of Thiel's "exit"-focused philanthropy.

## Controversies / investigations

- **Techno-colonialism critique:** Scholars and Pacific commentators argued the French Polynesia project amounted to wealthy outsiders securing autonomous enclaves in another people's waters; this critique helped sink the project. `#claim/disputed` (proponents reject the framing).
- **Anti-democratic critique:** Critics across the political spectrum describe seasteading as a fantasy of secession by the wealthy from the obligations of shared, accountable governance — the practical end-point of the libertarian "exit" logic.
- The institute has never built an operational seastead; its concrete legacy is primarily ideological and as a launchpad for the broader "startup societies" / charter-city movement.
