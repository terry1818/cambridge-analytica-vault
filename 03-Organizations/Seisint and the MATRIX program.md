# Seisint and the MATRIX Program

## Summary

Seisint, Inc. was a Boca Raton, Florida data company founded in 1999 by Hank Asher — a former drug smuggler turned data entrepreneur sometimes described by insiders as "the father of data fusion." Seisint's flagship government project was the **Multistate Anti-Terrorism Information Exchange (MATRIX)**, a post-9/11 data-mining system developed in partnership with the Florida Department of Law Enforcement and subsequently federally funded. MATRIX fused hundreds of millions of private commercial records — driver's license data, property records, criminal histories, vehicle registrations — with government databases, making integrated dossiers searchable by law enforcement officials across participating states. The program ran from approximately 2002 to June 2005, when it was shut down after public outcry and the withdrawal of state and federal funding. Seisint itself was acquired by LexisNexis (a division of Anglo-Dutch publisher Reed Elsevier) in September 2004 for $775 million, merging it into LexisNexis's own data-aggregation subsidiary. MATRIX is a direct institutional ancestor of the commercial data-broker pipeline to government surveillance that followed: it established the architecture (private firm holds data, government queries it), the funding model (federal grants to a commercial operator), and the legal theory (Third-party doctrine avoids Fourth Amendment warrant requirements) that define the [[Hypothesis - Federal database supersedes CA model]].

## Identity

Hank Asher founded Seisint in 1999 by merging two earlier companies. Before data entrepreneurship, Asher had a controversial past that included his own admission of drug smuggling — a background that eventually led to his quiet departure from the MATRIX program under pressure from states questioning his suitability as a government contractor. Despite that controversy, his data architecture attracted serious federal investment. Seisint's core product prior to MATRIX was commercial data aggregation for business and law enforcement clients; MATRIX was an extension of this into the national security domain.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Hank Asher** — founder; drove the MATRIX concept from a personal initiative immediately after 9/11 into a federally funded program; departed Seisint under pressure circa 2003 due to his personal background; later founded TLO (The Last One), another data-aggregation company.
- After Asher's departure, Seisint management continued operations through the LexisNexis acquisition in 2004.

## Funding & Money Flows

MATRIX was almost entirely federally funded:
- **$4 million grant** from the U.S. Department of Justice Bureau of Justice Assistance (December 2002) — initial seed funding
- **$8 million** earmarked by the U.S. Department of Homeland Security for further development
- Additional state-level funding through participating state law enforcement agencies
- Seisint (the commercial parent) was acquired by Reed Elsevier / LexisNexis in **September 2004 for $775 million** in cash

This funding structure — private company operating government-contracted data infrastructure with federal grant money — became a template replicated by successors in the data-broker pipeline.

## Activities

**Data fusion:** Seisint's technical innovation was the integration of disparate datasets into a single queryable layer. MATRIX combined: state driver's license records and photographs, property ownership records, vehicle registration data, criminal history files, incarceration records, and commercial data (address histories, associates, phone records). At peak, the system held approximately **4 billion data records**.

**Terrorism risk scoring:** MATRIX included a "High Terrorism Factor" (HTF) scoring algorithm — a proprietary formula Asher developed — that ranked individuals by likelihood of terrorist involvement based on behavioral and associational pattern-matching. Civil libertarians argued it was a secret blacklisting system with no transparency or appeal mechanism.

**Post-9/11 deployment:** Within days of the September 11 attacks, Asher ran his data system against 450 million individual records, generating a list of 419 "high-scoring" suspects. This list reportedly contained associates of the hijackers and contributed to early investigative leads and detentions. He also assisted FBI investigators during the 2002 Washington D.C. Beltway sniper case, helping link evidence to the identities of John Allen Muhammad and Lee Boyd Malvo.

**State consortium:** At its peak, MATRIX operated as a consortium of participating states. Michigan and New York withdrew early over privacy concerns; ultimately only Florida and a handful of other states remained active through shutdown.

**Law enforcement access:** State and federal law enforcement officers could submit queries against the MATRIX database through a web interface — effectively turning commercial data into a law-enforcement intelligence layer without requiring agencies to maintain or process the data themselves. This is structurally identical to how modern brokers like [[Venntel]] operate.

## Successor / Related Entities

- **LexisNexis Risk Solutions** — direct successor; absorbed Seisint's data assets and staff into its own data-aggregation and law-enforcement analytics business, which continues to operate today
- **TLO (The Last One)** — Hank Asher's subsequent company, founded after his departure from Seisint; eventually acquired by TransUnion
- **ChoicePoint** — contemporaneous commercial data broker serving law enforcement; later acquired by LexisNexis
- **[[Palantir Technologies]]** — founded 2004, same era, competing for the same federal data-fusion market but with a software-platform rather than data-ownership model
- **[[Venntel]]**, **[[X-Mode Social / Outlogic]]**, **[[Premise Data]]** — later-generation commercial data-broker pipeline to government surveillance; MATRIX established the structural and legal precedents they exploit

## Controversies / Investigations

**ACLU opposition:** The ACLU was among the most vocal critics of MATRIX, calling it "essentially an electronic file on everyone whether or not they are suspected of criminal activity." The ACLU challenged the program's lack of transparency, particularly the HTF scoring algorithm, and the absence of any mechanism for individuals to know they had been scored or to contest inaccurate records. ACLU documents from this period (available at ACLU.org) provide detailed critiques.

**Hank Asher's background:** Press reporting surfaced Asher's admission of drug-smuggling in his youth, creating a political liability for the program. States that had been wavering over privacy concerns used this as grounds to withdraw, accelerating the program's collapse. The episode illustrates the tension between the government's need for private data infrastructure and the reputational risks of the specific entrepreneurs who build it.

**Fourth Amendment concerns:** Legal scholars and civil libertarians argued that MATRIX violated the Fourth Amendment by enabling government surveillance without warrants, exploiting the "third-party doctrine" — the legal principle that data voluntarily shared with third parties (like DMV or commercial databases) loses Fourth Amendment protection. This same doctrine was later invoked to justify purchases from [[Venntel]] and [[X-Mode Social / Outlogic]], until *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) began to erode it.

**DHS Privacy Office review:** The DHS Privacy Office issued a public report on MATRIX in December 2006 (after shutdown), acknowledging privacy concerns and the inadequacy of oversight mechanisms during the program's operation.
