# DHS

## Summary

The Department of Homeland Security is a U.S. cabinet department created in 2002–2003 in response to the September 11 attacks, consolidating 22 previously separate agencies into a single homeland-security and immigration-enforcement bureaucracy. It is the third-largest cabinet department and the institutional home of [[ICE]], [[CBP]], [[TSA]], USCIS, the Secret Service, and CISA. For this vault DHS is a central node because it is the most aggressive government participant in the commercial-data surveillance market: its components have spent millions buying bulk cell-phone location data from [[data broker|data brokers]] such as Venntel and Babel Street, and its immigration-enforcement mission supplies the political will and legal cover for repurposing surveillance infrastructure against immigrants, activists, and — civil-liberties advocates warn — ordinary Americans. DHS thus sits where the surveillance-state thread, the data-broker thread, and the political-enforcement thread converge.

## Identity

- **Legal name(s):** United States Department of Homeland Security.
- **Other names / fronts:** none.
- **Founded:** The Homeland Security Act was signed November 25, 2002; the department began operations March 1, 2003.
- **Parent / owner:** a standalone cabinet department of the U.S. executive branch.
- **Registered jurisdiction:** United States; headquartered in Washington, D.C.
- **Subsidiaries:** [[ICE]], [[CBP]], [[TSA]], USCIS, U.S. Secret Service, U.S. Coast Guard, FEMA, CISA, and others.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Tom Ridge** — first Secretary of Homeland Security, 2003–2005.
- **Kristi Noem** — Secretary from 2025; her tenure has overseen an aggressive expansion of immigration-enforcement surveillance.
- The Secretary heads the department; component-agency heads (ICE Director, CBP Commissioner, TSA Administrator) report up to the Secretary.

## Funding & money flows

DHS receives roughly $60+ billion in annual appropriations. Vault-relevant money flows run through its components into the commercial-surveillance market: [[CBP]] entered Venntel contracts in 2019–2020 totaling over $2 million for cell-phone location data; [[ICE]] held its own broker contracts; and 2025–2026 reporting documented DHS components buying access to real-time location data through vendors such as PenLink and its "PLX" product. The department also funds large surveillance-technology procurements, including [[CBP]]'s Autonomous Surveillance Tower program (Anduril) and its Clearview AI facial-recognition pilot.

## Activities

**Buying bulk location data.** DHS components — principally [[ICE]] and [[CBP]] — purchased access to vast quantities of cell-phone location data from brokers Venntel (a Gravy Analytics subsidiary) and Babel Street. ACLU records described the volumes as "staggering." A DHS Inspector General review found ICE used location data without a required privacy impact assessment, prompting the Biden administration to ban bulk location-data purchases in 2024. Reporting in 2026 indicated DHS components were resuming mass phone-tracking through new vendors.

**Immigration-enforcement surveillance.** Through [[ICE]], DHS operates social-media monitoring hubs and integrates data via the [[Palantir]]-built ImmigrationOS platform, which centralizes records from the IRS, Social Security Administration, [[FBI]], DEA, ATF, and SEVIS to identify and prioritize deportation targets. Civil-liberties groups warn the system's scope is not technically limited to undocumented people.

**Border and biometric surveillance.** Through [[CBP]], DHS runs facial-recognition entry/exit programs, a network of surveillance towers along the U.S.-Mexico border, and biometric collection from foreign travelers. Through [[TSA]], it operates the airport facial-recognition program.

## Successor / related entities

- [[ICE]] — DHS component; lead interior immigration-enforcement and surveillance agency.
- [[CBP]] — DHS component; border, biometric, and surveillance-tower programs.
- [[TSA]] — DHS component; airport facial-recognition program.
- [[FBI]] — partner agency; shares data-broker contractors and feeds data into ICE platforms.
- [[Palantir]] — contractor building ICE's ImmigrationOS data-integration platform.

## Controversies / investigations

- **Bulk location-data purchases** — the central controversy; DHS IG found a privacy-assessment failure, and the FTC ruled against its contractor Venntel.
- **ImmigrationOS scope creep** — civil-liberties warning that the deportation-targeting system could extend to any American.
- **Surveillance against protesters and activists** — ICE social-media monitoring documented as scanning activist as well as immigrant accounts.
