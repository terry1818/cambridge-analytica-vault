## Summary

The Stanford Persuasive Technology Lab — now renamed the Stanford Behavior Design Lab — was founded in 1998 by [[B.J. Fogg]] and is the academic institution most directly responsible for formalizing the field of persuasive technology (or "captology"). Its research produced the Fogg Behavior Model and trained a generation of Silicon Valley product designers who went on to apply engagement-maximizing, behavior-change design at massive scale. Roger McNamee's *Zucked* identifies the lab as an upstream causal node in the architecture of attention-capture that made [[Facebook]], Instagram, and similar platforms addictive — and thus made them potent vectors for the kind of political influence operations documented in this vault.

## Identity

The lab was established under the name "Captology Lab" or "Persuasive Technology Lab" in 1998. Its founding premise was that computers, websites, and mobile technologies could be designed deliberately to change user attitudes and behaviors — and that this warranted systematic academic study. Fogg coined "captology" from the acronym Computers As Persuasive Technologies. The lab was later renamed the Behavior Design Lab, reflecting a shift in emphasis from studying persuasion to designing beneficial habits.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **[[B.J. Fogg]]**: Founder and long-time director. His Fogg Behavior Model (B=MAP) is the lab's signature intellectual output.
- **Mike Krieger**: Co-founder of Instagram; took Fogg's course at Stanford. Instagram's engagement mechanics — likes, follower counts, notification design — reflect captology principles.
- Multiple other Silicon Valley founders and designers are documented as having passed through Fogg's courses or lab.

## Funding & money flows

The lab operates within Stanford University's academic structure. Fogg has also offered commercial workshops and training outside the university context. Funding sources beyond standard academic channels are not fully public; some industry partnerships have been reported.

## Activities

- Academic research into behavior change via technology.
- Stanford courses that attracted Silicon Valley students and founders.
- Production of the Fogg Behavior Model — a framework widely adopted in product design.
- Fogg's 2003 book *Persuasive Technology* and 2019 *Tiny Habits* disseminated the lab's frameworks.
- Hosting of the annual Persuasive Technology conference (international academic conference initiated by Fogg).

## Controversies / investigations

- The lab's frameworks have been criticized for enabling the addictive design of social media platforms. Critics argue that persuasive technology, when deployed at commercial scale, crosses from behavior design into psychological manipulation.
- Fogg himself has acknowledged concern about misapplication; however, the direct lineage from his academic work to Silicon Valley engagement-maximization is documented.
- No formal investigations or regulatory actions have targeted the lab directly; the regulatory and congressional attention has focused on the platforms that applied its methods.

## Open questions

- Whether academic institutions bear any responsibility for the downstream application of dual-use behavioral research frameworks.
- The lab's current research agenda and how it navigates the ethics of behavior design for commercial vs. beneficial purposes.
