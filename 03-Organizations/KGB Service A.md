# KGB Service A

## Summary

Service A — formally a department within the KGB's First Chief Directorate (foreign intelligence) — was the Soviet state's dedicated active-measures and disinformation unit. Its mission was political warfare: producing and distributing forged documents, planting fabricated stories in foreign media, managing front organizations, running agents of influence, and coordinating disinformation campaigns across the Warsaw Pact and beyond. Originally designated Service D (for *dezinformatsia*), it was reorganized and expanded under KGB Chairman Yuri Andropov. By the early 1980s Service A had approximately 80 officers at Yasenevo, but its reach extended through the entire 15,000-officer First Chief Directorate and Warsaw Pact liaison services. Thomas Rid's *Active Measures* (2020) treats Service A as the institutional spine of the Soviet active-measures system.

## Identity

Service A was not an independent organization but a specialized directorate embedded in the KGB's foreign intelligence arm. The "A" designation stood for *aktivnyye meropriyatiya* (active measures). Unlike the First Chief Directorate's collection directorates, Service A's output was offensive: shaping foreign political environments rather than gathering intelligence. Its predecessors trace to the GPU's "special disinformation office" established as early as 1923.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Yuri Andropov**: as KGB Chairman (1967–1982) he was the primary patron of Service A's expansion, believing in active measures' utility from his experience suppressing the 1956 Hungarian uprising.
- **Genrikh Borovik**: journalist and agent-of-influence handler associated with Service A operations in the West.
- Officers at Yasenevo headquarters expanded from approximately 50 (mid-1970s) to approximately 80 (mid-1980s).

## Funding & Money Flows

Service A operated with a multi-million-dollar budget (precise figures unavailable in open-source material). Funding supported media-placement fees, front-organization subsidies, forgery production, and coordination payments to Warsaw Pact partner services.

## Activities

Service A's toolkit included:
- **Forgery**: producing fake documents, letters, and academic papers — see [[Operation NEPTUNE]] (forged Nazi documents, 1964)
- **Media placement**: seeding disinformation in foreign newspapers via front outlets (e.g., planting [[Operation INFEKTION]] claims through the Patriot, New Delhi)
- **Front organizations**: subsidizing peace movements, cultural organizations, and anti-war groups, particularly in Western Europe
- **Agents of influence**: cultivating journalists, academics, and politicians who would unknowingly or wittingly advance Soviet narratives
- **Coordination**: directing Warsaw Pact partner services (Stasi, Bulgarian DS, StB) in joint operations

## Successor / Related Entities

Service A's functions were absorbed into the SVR (Foreign Intelligence Service) after the KGB's 1991 dissolution. Analysts including Rid argue that SVR active-measures capabilities — demonstrated in operations like [[Doppelganger]] and interference in the 2016 US election — are institutional descendants of Service A doctrine and tradecraft. [[Cozy Bear (APT29)]], attributed to the SVR, represents the cyber-era successor to Service A's intelligence-gathering and influence functions.

## Controversies / Investigations

- The US Reagan administration's **Active Measures Working Group** (established early 1980s) was specifically created to counter Service A operations, and published declassified analyses of Soviet forgeries.
- Congressional testimony from KGB defectors (including Stanislav Levchenko) provided Western intelligence with detailed accounts of Service A's methods.

## Open Questions
