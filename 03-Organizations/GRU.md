# GRU (Main Directorate)

## Summary

The GRU — still widely used despite its official renaming to simply "Main Directorate" (GU) in 2010 — is Russia's foreign military intelligence service, reporting directly to the General Staff of the Armed Forces rather than to civilian leadership. This structural independence distinguishes it from the FSB (domestic, border) and SVR (foreign civilian intelligence) and has historically given it greater operational latitude. Thomas Rid's *Active Measures* (2020) frames the GRU as the primary institutional vehicle for Russia's "active measures" tradition — the doctrine of subversive influence operations extending back to Soviet Maskirovka and dezinformatsiya campaigns.

In contemporary context the GRU is most significant to this vault for housing the two cyber units — [[Fancy Bear (APT28)]] (Unit 26165) and Sandworm (Unit 74455) — attributed by the Mueller Special Counsel investigation, the July 2018 US federal grand jury indictment (*US v. Netyksho et al.*), and multiple Western intelligence agencies to the hack-and-leak operations targeting the [[2016 US Election]].

## Identity

Founded 21 October 1918 by order of Red Army Commander-in-Chief Jukums Vacietis and Deputy Commissar Ephraim Sklyansky, under the sponsorship of Leon Trotsky, to serve the intelligence needs of the newly formed Red Army. Its modern form dates to a February 1942 Stalin reorganisation. After the Cold War, unlike most Soviet intelligence structures, the GRU survived largely intact through the 1991 dissolution; it lost funding and personnel after failures in the 2008 Russo-Georgian War but was subsequently rebuilt.

The GRU operates globally via military attaches with diplomatic cover ("legal" residencies), illegal officers without cover, and signals/cyber capabilities. Its mandate is military intelligence — understanding the military capabilities and intentions of foreign states — but in practice it has long conducted active measures, assassinations, and political interference operations well beyond purely military intelligence.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Igor Korobov**: Director of the GRU from late 2016 until his death in November 2018. Served during the period when the 2016 US election interference operations and subsequent US indictments occurred.
- **Igor Kostyukov**: Appointed Director November 2018 following Korobov's death; still serving as of 2026.
- **Viktor Borisovich Netyksho**: Named as the lead defendant in the July 2018 Mueller grand jury indictment (*US v. Netyksho et al.*) as commander of Unit 26165 during the DNC/DCCC hack operations.
- **Anatoliy Kovalev**: Named defendant (Unit 26165) in the 2018 indictment; charged with compromising election infrastructure and state election boards in 2016.
- **Yuriy Andrienko, Sergey Detistov, Pavel Frolov, Artem Ochichenko, Petr Pliskin**: Named in the October 2020 US federal indictment as Unit 74455 officers responsible for NotPetya, the 2018 Olympics attack, and other destructive cyber operations.

## Funding & Money Flows

Funded through the Russian federal defence budget; exact figures classified. Post-2008 reforms included significant reinvestment in cyber capabilities. The GRU is known to conduct operations with commercial cover companies and through third-party proxies.

## Activities

The GRU's documented activities in the domain of information warfare and cyber operations include:

**Cyber espionage and hack-and-leak**:
- Unit 26165 (APT28/Fancy Bear) has conducted cyber espionage against NATO governments, election infrastructure, and civil society targets. See [[Fancy Bear (APT28)]] for the full operations record.
- The DNC/DCCC hack (March–June 2016) and subsequent leak via Guccifer 2.0 persona and DCLeaks platform are attributed to Units 26165 and 74455 respectively in the *US v. Netyksho* indictment (Tier-1).

**Destructive cyber operations (Sandworm / Unit 74455)**:
- December 2015 and December 2016: cyberattacks on Ukrainian power grid causing blackouts (Black Energy/Industroyer malware).
- June 2017: NotPetya malware released via Ukrainian accounting software; caused an estimated $10+ billion in global damage — the most destructive and costly cyberattack on record as of its time.
- February 2018: attack on IT systems of the Pyeongchang Winter Olympics opening ceremony (Olympic Destroyer malware).
- Ongoing interference in European elections and infrastructure.

**Human intelligence and special operations**:
- The GRU's Spetsnaz forces and Unit 29155 (separate from cyber units) have been attributed to assassination attempts: Sergei Skripal poisoning (Salisbury, UK, March 2018) and attempted poisoning of Bulgarian arms dealer Emilian Gebrev.
- GRU operatives (under cover of "little green men") played a central role in the covert takeover of Crimea in February–March 2014.

**Signals intelligence**: The GRU operates extensive SIGINT collection capabilities, including satellite intelligence.

## Successor / Related Entities

- **SVR** (Foreign Intelligence Service): Russia's civilian foreign intelligence; overlapping mission but separate chain of command.
- **FSB** (Federal Security Service): Domestic/counterintelligence; also has external cyber capacity (Cozy Bear/APT29).
- **Unit 29155**: GRU assassination/destabilisation unit, distinct from cyber units; attributed to Salisbury and European assassination plots.

## Controversies / Investigations

- **Mueller Report (2019)** and underlying *US v. Netyksho et al.* indictment (July 2018): The most detailed public accounting of GRU cyber operations in the 2016 election cycle. Charges include conspiracy to commit computer crimes, identity theft, and money laundering. All 12 defendants remain at large in Russia. (Tier-1 — federal grand jury indictment, evidence presented under oath)
- **NotPetya formal attribution (2018)**: US, UK, Australian, Canadian, and New Zealand governments jointly attributed NotPetya to the GRU in February 2018. (Tier-1 — formal government attribution)
- **Salisbury poisoning**: UK government and OPCW attributed the Novichok attack on Sergei and Yulia Skripal to the GRU; two identified operatives (travelling as "Alexander Petrov" and "Ruslan Boshirov") were later identified as GRU officers by Bellingcat. (Tier-1 for attribution; Tier-2 for officer identity)
- **Bundestag hack (2015)**: German authorities attributed a cyberattack on the German Bundestag to APT28/Unit 26165.
- **WADA hack (2016)**: GRU officers hacked World Anti-Doping Agency systems and released athletes' medical records; part of the 2018 US indictment.

## Open Questions

- The full scope of GRU Unit 29155's assassination programme in Europe and the degree to which it operates independently of central command versus on specific political authorisation remains incompletely documented.
- The operational coordination (or lack thereof) between GRU cyber units and the [[Internet Research Agency]] troll operations during 2016 — whether these tracks were independently ordered or jointly managed — is not fully established in public record.
- Whether [[Vladislav Surkov]]'s Ukraine political-technology operations were coordinated with GRU Donbas activities is not publicly documented.
