## Summary

Clearview AI is a New York-based facial-recognition company that built a database of tens of billions of images scraped from social media, news sites, and other public web sources — without the consent of the people pictured — and used those images to train an identification algorithm sold primarily to law enforcement agencies and government departments. Founded in approximately 2017 and publicly exposed in a January 2020 New York Times investigation, Clearview rapidly became one of the most contested companies in the US privacy landscape. Its technology represents a commercialized, privately operated layer of the biometric-surveillance infrastructure that complements and extends the data-broker ecosystem documented in [[Source - Tau 2024 - Means of Control]]. Clearview is directly relevant to the vault's [[Hypothesis - Federal database supersedes CA model]]: it demonstrates how private companies can construct government-grade surveillance capacity from scraped public data and sell it back to the state.

## Identity

Clearview's core product is a facial-recognition search engine: users upload a photograph of a face, and the system returns potential matches drawn from a database of scraped images, together with the URLs of the source pages. As of 2025, that database contains approximately 60 billion images — a scale that dwarfs any government-held biometric database. The company initially marketed exclusively to law enforcement, but announced plans in 2025 to expand to the private sector.

## Leadership & key personnel

Hoan Ton-That, an Australian entrepreneur, co-founded and led Clearview as CEO until his resignation in December 2024. Richard Schwartz and Hal Lambert were appointed co-CEOs in February 2025, signaling a pivot toward federal government contracting under the second Trump administration.

## Funding & money flows

Clearview's investor backing and revenue figures are not fully public. Government contracts are a primary revenue source. Documented federal contracts include:
- ICE (Homeland Security Investigations): $2.3 million enterprise license (2021); $9.2 million contract (September 2025)
- CBP: contract for access to 60 billion-image database (~2024–2025)
- Documented users include FBI, Department of the Army, Air Force, and DOJ

## Activities

- Scraping public web images at scale without consent or licensing agreements
- Training and operating a facial-recognition matching algorithm
- Selling identification services to law enforcement (local, state, federal) and government agencies
- Expanding into private-sector markets (2025 onward)

## Controversies / investigations

Clearview's central controversy is its scraping model: it harvested biometric data at massive scale from unwilling individuals to create a for-profit surveillance tool. Regulatory and legal actions span multiple jurisdictions:
- US state BIPA litigation (Illinois) resulting in $51.75 million settlement (2024/2025) structured as equity stake rather than cash
- UK ICO fine (2022), Australian Privacy Commissioner order to delete data, Italian, French, and Greek DPA enforcement actions
- Platform bans: Facebook, Twitter/X, Google, LinkedIn, Venmo all sent cease-and-desist letters; Clearview stopped complying with removal requests
- Civil liberties groups characterize the company as an example of private actors building mass-surveillance infrastructure that is then contracted back to the state, exploiting the absence of a federal biometric privacy law
