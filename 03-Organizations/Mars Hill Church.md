## Summary

Mars Hill Church was a Seattle, Washington megachurch co-founded by Mark Driscoll in 1996 that grew to 15 branches in five states with approximately 13,000–15,000 Sunday attendees at its peak. Kristin Kobes Du Mez's *Jesus and John Wayne* (2020) treats Mars Hill as the flagship institution of a distinct cultural moment in evangelical masculinity — young, urban, Calvinist, deliberately transgressive, and explicitly militarized — and Driscoll as the primary popularizer of the "cage-stage Calvinist" warrior-pastor model for millennial evangelicals. Mars Hill dissolved in January 2015 following Driscoll's resignation in October 2014 amid sustained allegations of bullying, plagiarism, financial mismanagement, and spiritual abuse.

The 2021 *Christianity Today* podcast "The Rise and Fall of Mars Hill" became one of the most-downloaded Christian podcasts ever, renewing scrutiny of the institutional patterns that enabled Driscoll's abuses.

## Identity

Mars Hill under Driscoll combined several unusual features: a conservative Calvinist theology delivered in deliberately crass, confrontational language; an aesthetic that appropriated indie-rock and urban culture; an explicit doctrine of masculine headship framed as edgy rebellion against perceived cultural feminization of Christianity; and a church governance structure that concentrated authority in Driscoll and his inner circle while systematically marginalizing dissent.

Du Mez situates Mars Hill as the inheritor and amplifier of themes running from the muscular Christianity movement through [[Promise Keepers]] and [[Christian Men's Network]]: the premise that Christianity had been feminized, that men needed to reclaim warrior identity, and that male headship was both spiritually mandated and culturally necessary. Driscoll's innovation was to package this ideology for an urban demographic previously resistant to evangelical culture.

Acts 29, the church-planting network Driscoll co-founded, extended the Mars Hill model globally; Acts 29 has continued operating after separating from Driscoll in 2014.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Mark Driscoll**: Co-founder and senior pastor; no seminary training; built the church from a small Bible study; resigned October 2014; subsequently founded Trinity Church in Scottsdale, AZ
- **Lief Moi**: Co-founder
- **Mike Gunn**: Co-founder

## Funding & Money Flows

Mars Hill was a 501(c)(3) nonprofit church. At its peak it operated on a multi-million-dollar annual budget generated through tithes and offerings from its large congregation. A 2014 investigation found that the church spent approximately $200,000 on a "Result Source" campaign to artificially boost Driscoll's book *Real Marriage* onto the *New York Times* bestseller list — a decision the church board later characterized as a mistake, though Driscoll denied personal knowledge. Specific donor networks are not publicly documented beyond member contributions.

## Controversies / Investigations

- Multiple former staff and members documented a culture of bullying, shunning, and spiritual abuse under Driscoll
- 2013: Plagiarism accusations following the Janet Mefferd radio confrontation; subsequent investigation confirmed multiple instances
- 2014: Internal documents leaked showing Driscoll's response to elder accountability processes as retaliatory
- 2014: Acts 29 Network (which Driscoll co-founded) asks Driscoll to step down and removes Mars Hill from the network
- Result Source campaign revealed: ~$200,000 spent to artificially place *Real Marriage* on bestseller lists
- Investigation found "patterns of persistent sin in the areas of arrogance, a quick temper, and domineering leadership"
- *Christianity Today* podcast (2021) reached millions of listeners; became a landmark documentation of authoritarian church culture
