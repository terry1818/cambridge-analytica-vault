## Summary

The Full Gospel Business Men's Fellowship International (FGBMFI) was founded in 1952 by Demos Shakarian, a California dairy farmer and Armenian-American Pentecostal, with the spiritual endorsement of evangelist Oral Roberts. It served a specific and important function in the mid-20th-century religious right: it brought Pentecostal and charismatic Christianity — with its emphasis on faith healing, speaking in tongues, and direct divine intervention — into contact with the business class. Where [[J. Howard Pew]] and [[James Fifield]] operated in mainline Protestant and Reformed evangelical circuits, FGBMFI penetrated the entrepreneurial and small-business world, making it a significant channel for the spread of prosperity theology and charismatic political religion. Darren Dochuk identifies it as a key organizational node in the oil-industry-Pentecostal-charismatic nexus that shaped the southwestern United States in the postwar period.

## Identity

FGBMFI operated through local chapters that held regular breakfast or dinner meetings at which businessmen shared personal testimonies of conversion, healing, and economic blessing. This format — informal, masculine, entrepreneurial — was deliberately designed to make Pentecostalism accessible to men who found traditional church settings uncongenial. The implicit theology was proto-prosperity gospel: business success as evidence of divine favor, spiritual gifts as practical assets.

The organization represents a distinct strand of the religious-right infrastructure: not clergy-driven like [[Spiritual Mobilization]], not denominationally anchored like the [[National Association of Evangelicals]], but lay businessmen organized around charismatic religious experience and entrepreneurial culture.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Demos Shakarian** (1913–1993): founder; Armenian-American dairy farmer whose family had been Pentecostal for generations; his vision was of an organization where businessmen could share faith experiences outside formal church structures.
- **Oral Roberts**: did not found the organization but prayed at its inaugural meeting and remained closely associated; his healing crusades provided the spiritual energy that made FGBMFI's early growth possible.

## Funding & money flows

- Funded through member dues, chapter fees, and donations from member businessmen.
- No single corporate patron on the scale of Pew or the Spiritual Mobilization donors; instead, distributed funding from a large network of small and medium business owners.
- A $1,000 donation from an early supporter funded the first issue of the *Full Gospel Business Men's Voice* publication, which became the organization's key outreach tool.
- Evidence tier: organizational self-reporting and academic history; comprehensive financial records not available for early period.

## Activities

- Organized local chapter meetings (by peak membership in the 1970s–80s, chapters existed in 90+ countries with 3,000+ chapters and 300,000 members by 1972).
- Published the *Full Gospel Business Men's Voice* magazine, which served as a testimony platform and organizational newsletter.
- Hosted regional and national conventions featuring prominent charismatic and Pentecostal speakers.
- Served as a networking vehicle connecting charismatic Christians in business — including many in the oil-and-gas sector of Texas and Oklahoma — with political and religious causes.
- Was instrumental in spreading charismatic Christianity into mainline Protestant denominations in the 1960s–70s (the "charismatic renewal" movement).

## Open questions

- The degree to which FGBMFI meetings served as explicit political organizing venues (as opposed to religious testimony settings) in the 1960s–70s has not been fully documented.
- Connections between FGBMFI and [[H.L. Hunt]]'s political circles in Texas warrant further investigation; they operated in overlapping social milieus.
