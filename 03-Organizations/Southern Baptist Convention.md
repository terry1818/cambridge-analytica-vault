# Southern Baptist Convention

## Summary

The Southern Baptist Convention (SBC) is the largest Protestant denomination in the United States and the second-largest Christian body after the Catholic Church, with roughly 13 million members at its 2006 peak and approximately 12.7 million as of recent reporting. Founded in 1845 in Augusta, Georgia, after Baptists in the U.S. South split from Northern Baptists over whether slaveholders could serve as missionaries, the SBC carries an origin story rooted in the defense of slavery — a history the convention formally apologized for in 1995. The denomination matters to this vault because of the **conservative resurgence**: a sustained, deliberate campaign from 1979 to roughly 2000 to capture the convention's presidency, boards, agencies, and six seminaries for a biblical-inerrancy faction. That capture converted the largest white Protestant denomination into a reliable institutional pillar of the Republican Party and the [[Christian nationalism]] movement, supplying voters, clergy, and a public-policy arm — the Ethics & Religious Liberty Commission — to the broader religious right.

## Identity

- **Legal name(s):** Southern Baptist Convention
- **Other names / fronts:** SBC
- **Founded:** 1845, Augusta, Georgia (split from Northern Baptists over slavery and missions)
- **Dissolved / restructured:** Active; restructured agency portfolio in 1997 ("Covenant for a New Century")
- **Parent / owner:** None — a cooperative of autonomous local churches
- **Subsidiaries:** International Mission Board; North American Mission Board; Ethics & Religious Liberty Commission (ERLC); LifeWay (now Lifeway Christian Resources); six seminaries
- **Registered jurisdiction:** United States; headquartered in Nashville, Tennessee

## Leadership & key personnel

- **[[Paul Pressler]]** — Houston judge and former Texas state representative; co-architect of the resurgence's "takeover" strategy; later faced sexual-abuse allegations.
- **[[Paige Patterson]]** — theologian and seminary president; co-architect with Pressler; removed from Southwestern Baptist Theological Seminary in 2018 over his handling of abuse allegations.
- **Adrian Rogers** — Memphis megachurch pastor; elected SBC president in 1979, the first resurgence victory.
- **W. A. Criswell** — Dallas First Baptist pastor; movement patriarch and inerrancy standard-bearer.
- **Richard Land** — president of the ERLC (then the Christian Life Commission) 1988–2013; built the SBC's Washington political profile.
- **Russell Moore** — ERLC president 2013–2021; clashed with the pro-Trump majority and resigned, signaling internal fracture.

## Funding & money flows

The SBC funds shared work through the **Cooperative Program**, a pooled-giving channel into which local churches contribute and from which seminaries, mission boards, and the ERLC are funded. The convention does not depend on a single patron; its political weight derives from membership scale and clergy reach rather than from donor concentration. The ERLC is the policy node that interfaces with the wider donor-funded religious-right ecosystem.

## Activities

For its first 130 years the SBC was theologically conservative but not monolithically partisan, containing a moderate wing in its seminaries and agencies. The **conservative resurgence** changed that. Pressler and Patterson met at the 1979 New Orleans convention and earlier with W. A. Criswell, Adrian Rogers, and allied pastors near the Atlanta airport to design a strategy: because the SBC president appoints the committees that in turn appoint agency trustees, electing a string of inerrantist presidents would, over a decade or more, hand the faction control of every institution. It worked. From Adrian Rogers's 1979 election onward, a moderate never again won the presidency. By the late 1990s the faction controlled the seminaries, the mission boards, and the policy commission, and moderates had largely departed (many forming the Cooperative Baptist Fellowship in 1991).

The resurgence is officially described as a fight over **biblical inerrancy** — and that doctrinal claim is genuine. But, as historian and journalistic accounts (and Pressler's own later record as a GOP power broker) document, the resurgence also functioned as a political project: it aligned the denomination with anti-abortion politics, opposition to LGBTQ rights, and a complementarian gender theology, and made the SBC a dependable bloc within the Republican coalition. The ERLC became the convention's Washington-facing advocacy arm. The convention's clergy network is a major distribution channel for the kind of church-based voter mobilization run by entities such as [[Faith Wins]].

In 2022, an independent Guidepost Solutions investigation, commissioned after Houston Chronicle reporting, found that SBC leaders had for two decades mishandled and concealed sexual-abuse allegations across the denomination — a crisis that has run alongside the abuse allegations against Paul Pressler himself.

## Successor / related entities

- **Cooperative Baptist Fellowship** — moderate breakaway formed by Baptists who left during the resurgence.
- **Ethics & Religious Liberty Commission** — the SBC's in-house political/policy arm.
- **Southwestern Baptist Theological Seminary** — a captured institution and resurgence flashpoint.

## Controversies / investigations

- 2022 Guidepost Solutions investigation into systemic mishandling of sexual abuse.
- Sexual-abuse allegations against resurgence architect [[Paul Pressler]].
- Internal split over Donald Trump and Christian nationalism, marked by Russell Moore's 2021 departure.
