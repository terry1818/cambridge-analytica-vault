## Summary

Bank Rossiya is a Russian private bank based in St. Petersburg, founded in 1990 out of the Communist Party's financial infrastructure. It became the central financial vehicle of [[Vladimir Putin]]'s inner circle after Putin rose to the presidency. The US Treasury, in its March 2014 sanctions designation, described it as "the personal bank for senior officials of the Russian Federation" and as Putin's personal cashbox — language uncharacteristic of regulatory filings and conveying the Treasury's assessment that the bank functions as a private treasury for the ruling group rather than a normal commercial institution. Catherine Belton's *Putin's People* (2020) traces the bank's transformation from a mid-tier regional institution to the hub of the Ozero-connected financial network.

## Identity

Founded from assets of the Leningrad regional Communist Party committee, Bank Rossiya was a minor institution through the 1990s. After 2000, as Putin associates gained control of its shareholding, it grew rapidly — not through commercial competition but through the redistribution of state-adjacent assets to its principal owners. By the mid-2000s it held stakes in or controlled assets across the gas, media, and insurance sectors. The bank itself operates primarily as a holding and investment vehicle; its listed banking operations are secondary to its function as a capital pool for the Ozero network.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **[[Yury Kovalchuk]]**: Became largest shareholder and chairman of the board in May 2004; described as Putin's personal banker. Controls National Media Group through bank affiliates.
- **Nikolay Shamalov**: Co-founder of [[Ozero Cooperative]]; long-time co-shareholder; son Kirill married Putin's daughter Katerina Tikhonova.
- **Mikhail Shelomov**: Described in investigative reporting as a distant relative of Putin; shareholder.
- **[[Sergei Roldugin]]**: Cellist and Putin confidant; listed as beneficial owner of offshore companies linked to the bank's financial flows in Panama Papers.

## Funding & Money Flows

The bank's growth followed a pattern documented by Belton: state-connected contracts and privatizations were steered toward entities in which Bank Rossiya principals held interests. The bank's offshore network — partly traced through the 2016 Panama Papers — routed funds through companies nominally held by [[Sergei Roldugin]] and others. The OCCRP's "Panama Papers: Russia — Banking on Influence" investigation found evidence of at least $2 billion moved through these structures. Separately, the Troika Laundromat investigation (OCCRP, 2019) found Roldugin-linked entities receiving payments from the scheme.

## Activities

- **Media control**: Through National Media Group, bank-affiliated entities control or hold stakes in Channel One Russia, REN TV, STS, and the newspaper *Izvestia* — giving the Kovalchuk/Rossiya network dominant influence over Russian state media messaging.
- **Insurance**: SOGAZ, one of Russia's largest insurers, passed through Bank Rossiya control before being transferred; the mechanism is documented in Belton and ICIJ reporting.
- **Offshore structuring**: Nominee ownership of offshore companies, attributed to Roldugin and others, used to hold assets away from official ownership registers.

## Controversies / Investigations

- **US Treasury SDN designation (20 March 2014)**: First designation of a major Russian bank; Treasury statement explicitly described it as Putin's "personal cashbox." This is an adjudicated government finding, evidence tier T1.
- **Panama Papers (2016)**: ICIJ/OCCRP investigation linked bank-adjacent offshore structures to nominee ownership by [[Sergei Roldugin]] and others; found at minimum $2 billion in flows. Evidence tier: T2 (investigative journalism; primary leaked documents).
- **Troika Laundromat (OCCRP, 2019)**: Further documented flows through Roldugin-connected entities. Evidence tier: T2.
- **EU and UK sanctions**: Bank Rossiya is also listed under EU and UK sanctions regimes following February 2022 invasion of Ukraine.
