## Summary

The Center for an Informed Public (CIP) is a cross-disciplinary research center at the University of Washington, established in July 2019 with a $5 million founding grant from the John S. and James L. Knight Foundation and a supplementary $600,000 from the William and Flora Hewlett Foundation. Its mission is to resist strategic misinformation, promote an informed society, and strengthen democratic discourse through research, policy engagement, and public education. The CIP is co-housed across the Information School and the Department of Human Centered Design & Engineering (HCDE). It became a high-profile target during the [[Congressional campaign against disinformation research]] (2023–2024), primarily because of its participation in the Election Integrity Partnership.

## Identity

The CIP represents the institutionalization of crisis informatics and computational social science research on misinformation — bringing together researchers from engineering, information science, law, and communications under a shared mission. It distinguishes itself from single-PI research groups by operating a community-facing model: Community Labs in public libraries, policy outreach, and practitioner training.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **[[Kate Starbird]]** — Professor of HCDE; principal investigator and public face; co-founder. Named ACM Fellow in January 2026.
- **Ryan Calo** — Co-founder; Professor of Law, UW School of Law; focuses on legal dimensions of misinformation and platform regulation.
- **Chris Coward** — Co-founder; Information School; focus on media literacy interventions.
- **Emma Spiro** — Co-founder; Information School; computational social science.
- **Jevin West** — Co-founder; Information School; co-creator of the "Calling Bullshit" course and book; data literacy focus.

## Funding & money flows

- **Founding (2019):** $5 million, Knight Foundation; $600,000, Hewlett Foundation.
- Subsequent funding sources include additional Knight Foundation investments in the broader technology-democracy research field.
- No documented government funding that would create the conflict-of-interest claimed by congressional critics; funding has been transparent and philanthropic.

## Activities

- Academic research on misinformation and disinformation during elections, crises, and public health emergencies (including COVID-19 infodemic research).
- Participation in the **Election Integrity Partnership (EIP)** — a cross-institutional collaboration with the [[Stanford Internet Observatory]], Graphika, and the University of Washington for monitoring election-related disinformation in 2020 and 2022.
- Operation of Community Labs in public libraries to build media literacy capacity.
- Policy engagement and practitioner outreach.
- Publication and conference participation in CSCW, HCI, and related fields.

## Successor / related entities

- Operates independently of the [[Stanford Internet Observatory]] but was a co-participant in the Election Integrity Partnership alongside SIO. The SIO wound down its election research in 2024; CIP's status remains active.

## Controversies / investigations

- The CIP and its Election Integrity Partnership participation became central to the House Judiciary Committee's investigation (chaired by [[Jim Jordan]]) into alleged government-directed "censorship" of social media content. The committee alleged that EIP work amounted to government-coordinated suppression of conservative speech.
- CIP and its founders dispute this characterization; their work was philanthropically funded academic research, not government-directed action.
- The investigation had a documented chilling effect on researchers and institutions (per Washington Post, Inside Higher Ed, September–December 2023).

## Open questions

- Whether the EIP model — cross-institutional real-time election misinformation monitoring — will be revived for future elections given the political climate.
- Whether philanthropic funders will sustain support in the face of continued congressional pressure.
