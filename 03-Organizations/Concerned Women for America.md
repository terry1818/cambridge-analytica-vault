# Concerned Women for America

## Summary

Concerned Women for America (CWA) is a conservative women's advocacy organization founded in 1979 by [[Beverly LaHaye]] in San Diego, California, and now headquartered in Washington, D.C. LaHaye created CWA as an explicit religious-right counterweight to second-wave feminism — she has said she was moved to act after watching a Barbara Walters interview with [[Betty Friedan]], founder of the National Organization for Women, and concluding that feminism did not speak for the "millions of women" who held traditionalist religious values. CWA describes its mission as protecting and promoting "Biblical values" through prayer, education, and political influence. It grew rapidly — 52 chapters in 14 states within its first year — and became a durable grassroots vehicle for the Christian right, mobilizing church-based women's networks on abortion, LGBTQ rights, education, and family policy. It matters to this vault as one of the oldest continuously operating mass-membership organizations of the theocratic political wing, and as a model for faith-based grassroots mobilization that newer operations have echoed.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **[[Beverly LaHaye]]** — founder; president until 2010; chairman of the board of trustees until 2019. Wife of [[Tim LaHaye]], the influential evangelical author (co-author of the *Left Behind* series) and religious-right organizer — a marriage that placed CWA inside the inner organizational network of the modern Christian right from the start.
- **Penny Nance** — longtime CEO and president in the modern era; a prominent religious-right media voice.
- A national field structure of state directors and local prayer/action chapters.

## Funding & money flows

CWA is donor- and member-supported, sustained by individual contributions, membership, and conservative foundations. It does not depend on a single named patron in the manner of a donor-vehicle organization; its political asset is its claimed mass membership and its chapter network. Detailed funding breakdowns are not consolidated in reviewed public sources.

## Activities

CWA's core activity is **faith-based grassroots mobilization of conservative women**. Built on a model of local prayer/action chapters, it monitors legislation at the state and federal levels, organizes constituent contact for bills aligned with its agenda, and trains activists. Its policy program is the standard religious-right portfolio: opposition to abortion, same-sex marriage, what it calls secular education, pornography, and (in its framing) the broader cultural agenda of the left; support for "biblical" family structure and religious liberty.

Historically, CWA was an early and effective entrant in the institution-building wave of the late-1970s religious right alongside the Moral Majority and the Eagle Forum. It strongly backed Ronald Reagan; Reagan publicly credited LaHaye with helping "change the face of American politics." The organization's longevity is its distinguishing feature: where many religious-right groups have risen and faded, CWA has operated continuously for more than four decades, adapting to each political era — anti-ERA and anti-abortion organizing in the 1980s, "family values" politics in the 1990s and 2000s, and alignment with the Trump-era Republican coalition more recently. Its CWALAC arm conducts the explicit lobbying and electoral-issue work.

In the vault's analytical frame, CWA represents the *durable grassroots layer* of the theocratic wing — the patient, decades-long organizing infrastructure that supplies activists, normalizes religious-right framing of policy questions, and connects church communities to electoral politics. It is the kind of grassroots base that church-mobilization operations like [[Faith Wins]] and the broader Christian-nationalist movement build on.

## Successor / related entities

- **Concerned Women for America Legislative Action Committee (CWALAC)** — CWA's lobbying/electoral arm.
- **Eagle Forum** — Phyllis Schlafly's contemporaneous anti-feminist organization; a parallel and overlapping religious-right women's vehicle.
- **[[Tim LaHaye]] organizations** — the broader LaHaye family network within the religious right.

## Controversies / investigations

- CWA has been criticized by LGBTQ-rights and civil-liberties organizations for its opposition to LGBTQ legal protections; some watchdogs have characterized aspects of its advocacy as anti-LGBTQ.
- Its self-reported membership figures have been questioned by critics as difficult to verify.
