# Opus Dei

## Summary

Opus Dei (Latin: "Work of God") is a personal prelature of the Roman Catholic Church founded in Madrid, Spain, on October 2, 1928, by Josemaría Escrivá, a Spanish priest. Its central charism is the teaching that ordinary people can achieve holiness through their everyday work and professional life — in contrast to religious orders that emphasize separation from the world. Escrivá was canonized by Pope John Paul II in 2002.

In 1982, Pope John Paul II elevated Opus Dei to a personal prelature through the apostolic constitution *Ut sit* — a canonical status that means its members, wherever they live globally, fall under the jurisdiction of the Prelate of Opus Dei rather than their local bishop for matters pertaining to the prelature's mission. This is a canonical category held uniquely by Opus Dei within the global Church.

In the United States, Opus Dei operates in more than 25 cities, arrived in Chicago in 1949, and has over time developed a documented presence in Washington, DC legal and political circles. The organization itself describes its mission as spiritual and educational; critics and investigative journalists — including sources cited in [[Source - Stewart 2025 - Money Lies and God]] — describe a pattern of influence in conservative judicial appointments, media, and politics that goes beyond the organization's stated scope.

## Identity

Opus Dei's membership is composed predominantly of laypeople (numeraries, associates, supernumeraries) with a smaller clergy component (members of the Priestly Society of the Holy Cross). Numeraries — the most committed lay members — typically live in Opus Dei centers and practice corporal mortification. Supernumeraries live ordinary family lives while following a structured plan of spiritual formation.

The organization is notable for operating with considerable opacity regarding membership lists. Public figures who are members rarely identify themselves as such voluntarily. This opacity has been a source of ongoing controversy and is noted by researchers tracking the organization's political footprint.

Josemaría Escrivá's writings — particularly *The Way* (1939) — emphasize obedience, apostolate in professional life, and the "sanctification of work." Critics have characterized the organization's structure as hierarchical to the point of being cultic; the organization and its defenders strongly contest this framing.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Josemaría Escrivá** (founder): Spanish priest, b. 1902, d. 1975; founded Opus Dei in Madrid in 1928; expanded it globally after World War II; beatified 1992, canonized 2002.
- **Fernando Ocáriz** (current Prelate, from 2017): Appointed by Pope Francis; theologian; longtime Opus Dei official.
- **C. John McCloskey**: Opus Dei priest who ran the **Catholic Information Center** (CIC) in Washington, DC, during the 1990s and early 2000s. Under McCloskey, the CIC at 15th and K Street (two blocks from the White House) became a documented meeting point for high-profile conservative Catholics. McCloskey received a financial settlement from the Catholic Church in 2018 following a sexual misconduct allegation; details were reported by the Washington Post.
- **[[Leonard Leo]]**: Longtime executive vice president of the [[Federalist Society]]; Opus Dei-affiliated; served on the CIC board; architect of the conservative Supreme Court transformation. Leo received the CIC's John Paul II New Evangelization Award in 2022.
- **William Barr**: Former US Attorney General; served on the CIC board.

## Funding & Money Flows

Opus Dei does not publish consolidated financial accounts. Individual apostolic works (schools, university residences, the CIC) operate as separate nonprofits under US law. Funding comes from membership donations, institutional fees, and gifts from benefactors. The organization's global consolidated financial picture is not publicly available.

The Catholic Information Center in Washington receives donations from prominent conservative Catholics and has been a venue connecting Opus Dei-affiliated clergy with the donor-funder wing of the right. Evidence of specific grant flows between Opus Dei entities and the broader conservative dark-money network (e.g., [[Donors Trust]], [[DonorsTrust]], [[National Christian Foundation]]) has not been confirmed in available sources and should not be asserted.

## Activities

- Operates schools (68% of Corporate Works globally), technical training centers (26%), and university residences.
- University of Navarra (Spain) is the flagship Opus Dei university; IESE Business School (Barcelona) is a globally ranked Opus Dei-affiliated institution.
- In the US, the **Catholic Information Center** (Washington, DC) has been the most politically significant apostolate — a bookstore and meeting space that hosted figures including Supreme Court justices, senior government officials, and conservative movement leaders.
- Opus Dei priests have served as chaplains to prominent political figures.
- Multiple sources (Rolling Stone, Slate, investigative journalism) have documented Opus Dei's presence in the networks surrounding the conservative judicial appointment pipeline, including connections to several sitting US Supreme Court justices raised Catholic and confirmed with [[Leonard Leo]]'s assistance.

## Controversies / Investigations

- **Opacity of membership**: Opus Dei does not publish membership lists. Researchers and journalists have documented this as enabling undisclosed influence in professional and political contexts. The organization says members are not required to disclose membership.
- **McCloskey misconduct (2018)**: The Washington Post reported that the Catholic Church paid a financial settlement to a woman who alleged sexual misconduct by Fr. C. John McCloskey in the 1990s. McCloskey was reassigned and eventually withdrew from public ministry.
- **Judicial pipeline allegations**: Multiple investigative pieces (Rolling Stone 2023, Slate 2024, Dem Labs 2024) have argued that the Opus Dei-affiliated network, centered on the CIC and Leonard Leo, played a central role in building the conservative supermajority on the US Supreme Court. *Note: The organization denies a coordinated political agenda; the specific causal claims vary in evidentiary strength and should be evaluated source by source.*
- **Project 2025**: Some analyses have traced Opus Dei-affiliated figures' involvement in Project 2025 planning documents. Evidence tier for specific organizational involvement is Tier-3 (advocacy/watchdog; not independently confirmed by major investigative outlets as of this writing).
- **Historical association with Franco**: Opus Dei expanded significantly in Francoist Spain, and several early members held positions in the Franco government. The organization has described these figures as acting in personal capacities. This historical association is documented; its interpretation is contested.
