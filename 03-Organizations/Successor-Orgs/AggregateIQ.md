## The Canadian arm of the operation

[[AggregateIQ]] (AIQ) is a Victoria, British Columbia-based digital advertising and software-development firm. The vault treats it as an operational arm of the SCL / CA group rather than as an independent vendor that happened to share clients. The evidentiary basis for that treatment: [[Christopher Wylie]] (formerly of both SCL and AIQ) testified that AIQ was effectively SCL Canada and was set up at SCL's instruction in 2013-2014; AIQ's [[Ripon]] software was built to specifications that matched CA's needs; AIQ shared office space, staff, and project management with SCL/CA; AIQ's billing records (subpoenaed by the ICO) showed deep operational integration with CA work.

## Documented client work

AIQ's most consequential work was for [[Vote Leave]] (the official UK Leave campaign during the 2016 [[Brexit Referendum 2016]]). AIQ received approximately GBP 3.5 million from Vote Leave, plus a separate GBP 625,000 from [[BeLeave]] that the Electoral Commission later found was unlawfully coordinated. AIQ also worked for [[DUP Vote to Leave]] and the [[Veterans for Britain]] sub-campaigns. In total, AIQ's documented Leave-side billings exceeded 40 percent of the entire Vote Leave reported budget.

Outside the UK, AIQ did work for Ted Cruz's 2016 presidential primary campaign, for the Trump campaign at certain points, for the BC Liberals in Canadian provincial politics, and for various less-documented international clients.

## Regulatory consequences

The ICO issued AIQ an enforcement notice in July 2018 for unlawful processing of UK personal data. AIQ initially refused to comply, was issued a second notice, and ultimately complied. AIQ has continued to operate post-2018; it was not dissolved and it was not subject to criminal prosecution in Canada. Canadian privacy regulators (federal Privacy Commissioner and BC's OIPC) jointly investigated AIQ and produced a 2019 report finding violations of PIPEDA and BC's PIPA.

## Why AIQ matters as a successor / parallel operation

AIQ is the clearest documented case of CA-style infrastructure surviving the CA bankruptcy intact: the firm exists, the staff exist, the software exists, the methodology exists, and the regulatory record concluded with enforcement notices rather than dissolution. The vault treats AIQ as a working case study in the limits of post-CA accountability: the headline firm (CA) collapsed, the operational arm (AIQ) continued.

## Detailed corporate history and SCL relationship

[[AggregateIQ]] (AIQ) was incorporated in Victoria, British Columbia in 2013 by Zackary Massingham and Jeff Silvester. The corporate founding presented as a standard digital-advertising-and-software-development consultancy, with no public corporate-relationship disclosure to SCL Group at that time. The relationship was disclosed under the [[ICO Investigation]] and the Canadian Privacy Commissioner investigation, where it became clear that AIQ had been set up at SCL's instruction in late 2013, that the founders had pre-existing SCL relationships (Massingham had done work for SCL through other channels), and that AIQ's actual operational structure was functionally an SCL Canada arm rather than an independent vendor.

The structural evidence: AIQ's office space was reportedly shared with SCL personnel for portions of the 2014-2017 period; AIQ's principal software product [[Ripon]] was built to specifications matching CA's modelling-and-targeting requirements; AIQ's billing records (subpoenaed by the ICO during the 2018 investigation) showed deep operational integration with CA workflow; AIQ's staff included individuals who had previously worked for SCL or who moved between AIQ and SCL/CA during the relationship period; and the Wylie testimony to the DCMS Disinformation Inquiry described AIQ explicitly as "essentially SCL Canada."

## The Brexit deployment in detail

AIQ's most operationally consequential work was the [[Brexit Referendum 2016]] Leave-side digital operation. The corporate-finance picture, as the [[UK Electoral Commission]] and the [[ICO Investigation]] reconstructed it:

- [[Vote Leave]] (the officially designated Leave campaign, led by Dominic Cummings): approximately GBP 3.5 million to AIQ, representing roughly 40 percent of Vote Leave's reported referendum-period budget.
- [[BeLeave]] (a small youth-targeted Leave group): GBP 625,000 to AIQ, transferred via Vote Leave in what the Electoral Commission later found was unlawful coordination that effectively exceeded Vote Leave's spending cap.
- DUP Vote to Leave (the Democratic Unionist Party's Leave campaign in Northern Ireland): smaller direct payments to AIQ.
- Veterans for Britain (a smaller Leave-aligned organisation): also a direct AIQ client.

The aggregate Leave-side AIQ billings exceeded 40 percent of the entire Vote Leave reported budget, which is the operationally significant figure: a single Canadian vendor was the principal digital operational arm of the UK Leave campaign in a referendum that produced one of the most consequential political outcomes of the 21st century.

## Regulatory consequences

The [[ICO Investigation]] issued AIQ an enforcement notice in July 2018 for unlawful processing of UK personal data. AIQ initially refused to comply, was issued a second notice, and ultimately complied. The Canadian Privacy Commissioner and BC's Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner (OIPC) jointly investigated AIQ and produced a 2019 report finding violations of PIPEDA (federal) and BC's PIPA (provincial). The Canadian investigations did not produce criminal proceedings; the principal formal consequences were the enforcement notices and the public-record findings.

The UK Electoral Commission also acted on the Vote Leave / BeLeave coordination finding, fining Vote Leave GBP 61,000 (the statutory maximum at the time) and referring the matter to the Metropolitan Police. The Metropolitan Police investigation did not result in criminal charges; the case was closed in September 2019.

## Post-2018 status

AIQ has continued to operate post-2018 from the same Victoria, BC offices. The firm has been less publicly visible than before 2018, has substantially reduced its political-consulting profile, and has continued operating principally on the commercial-digital-marketing side. The corporate entity exists; no criminal prosecution has occurred; the Canadian regulatory record is the principal formal consequence.

The post-2018 AIQ continuation is the cleanest documented case in the vault of CA-style infrastructure surviving the CA bankruptcy intact: the firm exists, the staff exist, the software exists, the methodology exists, and the regulatory record concluded with enforcement notices rather than dissolution. The vault treats AIQ as a working case study in the limits of post-CA accountability.
