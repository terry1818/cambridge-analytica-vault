# Oath Keepers

The Oath Keepers is a US militia network founded in 2009 by Stewart Rhodes (Yale Law graduate, former Army paratrooper) as a coalition of current-and-former military, law enforcement, and first responders who took an oath to "defend the Constitution against all enemies, foreign and domestic." The organisation's central operational claim is that its members are obligated to refuse orders the organisation judges unconstitutional.

## Pre-2021 operational trajectory

Oath Keepers grew through 2009-2020 from a small online community into a substantial network with claimed membership in the tens of thousands (actual paid membership and activity-level membership were substantially smaller than claimed figures). Specific operational episodes included:

- 2014 Cliven Bundy standoff at Bunkerville, Nevada
- 2016 Malheur National Wildlife Refuge occupation (Oath Keepers were peripherally involved)
- Various armed protest-and-counter-protest deployments during 2014-2020

## January 6 Capitol attack

Oath Keepers had substantial documented operational participation in the January 6, 2021 Capitol attack. Approximately 20 Oath Keepers were charged with federal crimes including seditious conspiracy. Stewart Rhodes was convicted of seditious conspiracy in November 2022 and sentenced to 18 years federal prison in May 2023.

The seditious-conspiracy conviction was the most significant single legal outcome for any January 6 defendant and the principal legal precedent for prosecuting militia leadership for January 6-related conduct.

## Post-conviction status

Following Rhodes's conviction and the broader leadership prosecutions, Oath Keepers has been substantially reduced in operational scale. The organisation has not been formally disbanded; lower-tier organisational activity has continued under reorganised leadership. The 2024-2026 operational footprint is substantially smaller than the pre-2021 footprint.

The Trump 2.0 administration's pardons of January 6 defendants (issued January 2025) freed multiple Oath Keepers including Rhodes; the operational consequences of those pardons for the organisation's future trajectory remain a continuing thread.
