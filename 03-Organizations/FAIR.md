# FAIR

## Summary

The Federation for American Immigration Reform (FAIR) is a Washington, D.C.-based advocacy organization founded on January 2, 1979, that campaigns for sharp reductions in both unauthorized and legal immigration to the United States. It is the flagship and oldest organization of the modern American immigration-restriction movement, and the anchor of what is commonly called the "Tanton network" — a cluster of restrictionist groups founded or seeded by its creator, Michigan ophthalmologist [[John Tanton]]. FAIR's principal sister organizations are the [[Center for Immigration Studies]] (a think tank), NumbersUSA (a grassroots lobbying group), and its own affiliated legal arm, the [[Immigration Reform Law Institute]] (IRLI).

FAIR matters to this vault as the institutional core of the immigration-restriction legal and political movement. Its founder John Tanton was a documented eugenicist and white nationalist, and the Southern Poverty Law Center classifies FAIR as a hate group — a designation FAIR rejects. Despite this, FAIR became a mainstream policy player: it has testified before Congress hundreds of times, supplied model legislation, and — especially through IRLI — placed personnel and legal strategy at the center of immigration policy during the Trump administrations. FAIR's early funding included seed money from the Charles Stewart Mott Foundation, and (controversially) the organization for years accepted grants from the Pioneer Fund, a foundation with a documented history of funding race-science research.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **[[John Tanton]]** — founder; ophthalmologist, former Zero Population Growth president; later documented as a eugenicist and white nationalist; founder of the broader Tanton network.
- **Dan Stein** — long-serving President of FAIR; the organization's primary public spokesman for decades.
- **Otis Graham** — co-founder; environmental historian.
- **Sidney Swensrud** — co-founder; former Gulf Oil chief executive; early funder.

## Funding & money flows

- **Charles Stewart Mott Foundation** — provided early seed money around FAIR's 1979 founding.
- **Pioneer Fund** — FAIR accepted grants over many years from this foundation, which has a documented history of underwriting race-science and eugenics-adjacent research; this is among the most-cited controversies about FAIR's funding.
- FAIR is also sustained by a large base of individual donors and some larger conservative foundations; precise current donor breakdowns are not fully public.

## Activities

FAIR lobbies for restrictionist immigration policy, produces research and cost estimates (e.g., claimed fiscal costs of unauthorized immigration), testifies before Congress, supplies model legislation to state and federal lawmakers, runs media operations, and — through its legal affiliate [[Immigration Reform Law Institute]] — drafts and litigates immigration measures. It functions as the policy and organizing hub while CIS supplies think-tank research, NumbersUSA supplies grassroots lobbying, and IRLI supplies legal capacity.

## Timeline

- `1979-01-02` — FAIR founded by John Tanton with seed money from the Mott Foundation
- `1985` — Tanton founds U.S. English; the Tanton network begins to expand
- `1986` — FAIR establishes the [[Immigration Reform Law Institute]] as its legal arm
- `1985` — Center for Immigration Studies spun off as a FAIR-linked research organization
- `1996` — NumbersUSA founded within the Tanton network
- `2007` — SPLC designates FAIR a hate group, citing Tanton's writings and the Pioneer Fund ties
- `2017–2021` — Tanton-network personnel, including via IRLI, occupy influential roles in the first Trump administration
- `2019-07-16` — John Tanton dies
- `2025–2026` — FAIR and IRLI remain active in immigration policy and litigation under the second Trump administration

## Controversies / investigations

- **Hate-group designation** — the SPLC classifies FAIR as a hate group, citing founder John Tanton's eugenicist and white-nationalist writings and FAIR's leadership ties. FAIR disputes the designation as politically motivated. `#claim/disputed`
- **Pioneer Fund grants** — FAIR's acceptance of funding from the Pioneer Fund, a foundation with documented race-science history, is among the most-cited objections to the organization.
- **Tanton's archive** — Tanton's personal papers (held at the University of Michigan, with portions sealed) contain correspondence and memos that critics cite as evidence of explicitly racial motives behind the restriction movement.
