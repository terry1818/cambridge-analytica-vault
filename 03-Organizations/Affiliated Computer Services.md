## Summary

Affiliated Computer Services (ACS) was a Dallas-based information technology and business-process outsourcing company founded in 1988 by Darwin Deason. By the mid-2000s it was one of the largest government IT contractors in the United States, with approximately 40% of revenues from public-sector clients. ACS held the IBM-subcontracted role in Indiana's 2006 welfare privatization scheme — the [[Indiana welfare automation]] — and was the primary technology implementer on the ground. The project, which aimed to replace face-to-face caseworker eligibility determinations with automated call centers and online applications, became a documented failure: Indiana cancelled the $1.37 billion contract in 2009, and the Indiana Supreme Court later ruled IBM (the prime contractor) had breached its obligations, awarding Indiana $78 million. ACS was acquired by Xerox Corporation in 2010 for $6.4 billion; the welfare-related business was ultimately spun off in 2017 as Conduent.

## Identity

ACS positioned itself as a business-process outsourcing leader that could bring private-sector efficiency to government service delivery. The Indiana welfare contract embodied the ideology of the Bush-era "compassionate conservatism" approach to social services: privatize eligibility determination, replace human judgment with automated rules, and reduce costs by automated denials. Virginia Eubanks's *Automating Inequality* treats ACS and IBM jointly as the operators of the [[Digital poorhouse]] model in Indiana.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Darwin Deason**: Founder (1988); built ACS into a government outsourcing powerhouse; sold to Xerox 2010.

## Funding & money flows

- Indiana contract (prime: IBM, subcontractor work: ACS): valued at approximately $1.37 billion over 10 years (signed 2006).
- US Food and Nutrition Service (FNS) fined Indiana $1–2 million for errors in food stamp benefit calculations attributable to the automated system.

## Activities

- Operated automated call centers that replaced Indiana's county caseworkers for food-stamp, Medicaid, and cash-assistance eligibility.
- Processed welfare applications through document-scanning and algorithmic eligibility rules.
- Similar contracts in other states: Medicaid claims processing, food-stamp EBT card administration.
- Pioneered putting food-stamp and child-support payments onto electronic benefit transfer (EBT) cards across multiple states.

## Controversies / investigations

- **Indiana welfare failure**: The automated eligibility system lost documents, delayed and denied benefits to eligible recipients, and produced systematic food-stamp calculation errors. Indiana fired IBM (prime contractor) in 2009; IBM and Indiana then sued each other. The Indiana Supreme Court ultimately ruled IBM breached the contract.
- **Virginia Eubanks documentation**: Eubanks documented in *Automating Inequality* how the system flagged any application error as "failure to cooperate," resulting in approximately 1 million benefit denials in Indiana.
- **Structural critique**: The model replaced caseworker discretion with rigid algorithmic rules that could not handle the complexity of real applicants' lives, disproportionately harming the most vulnerable.
