## Summary

Christianity Today was founded in October 1956 as a deliberate counter-publication to *The Christian Century*, the dominant voice of mainline liberal Protestantism. Billy Graham, awakened in the early 1950s by a vision for a magazine that could unify and intellectually equip conservative evangelicals, partnered with his father-in-law L. Nelson Bell and theologian Carl F.H. Henry — and critically, with [[J. Howard Pew]], the Sun Oil president who provided both funding and institutional direction. Christianity Today thus represents the convergence of evangelical theological ambition and oil-industry capital that Darren Dochuk traces throughout his analysis of the petrostate-Christian right alliance. It remains the most widely read evangelical magazine in the United States.

## Identity

Christianity Today occupies a specific institutional niche: it is neither a denominational publication nor a purely popular outlet, but the intellectual journal of record for educated evangelicals. Graham's stated intent was to "plant the evangelical flag in the middle of the road, taking the conservative theological position but a definite liberal approach to social problems" — though Pew's influence pushed the early magazine toward more explicit economic conservatism than Graham initially intended.

The magazine has evolved substantially since its founding, and by the 2010s–20s it notably diverged from the Trumpist trajectory of much of the evangelical world (publishing an editorial calling for Trump's removal from office in 2019). Its founding history and its present-day identity are thus somewhat in tension.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Billy Graham**: conceived the magazine; provided the brand and the evangelical coalition.
- **L. Nelson Bell**: Graham's father-in-law; Presbyterian lay leader; operational co-founder.
- **Carl F.H. Henry**: first editor (1956–1968); Fuller Seminary theologian; gave the magazine its intellectual framework in *The Uneasy Conscience of Modern Fundamentalism* (1947).
- **[[J. Howard Pew]]**: early board director and major funder; attempted to steer editorial content toward explicit pro-business positions; relationship with Henry was sometimes contentious.

## Funding & money flows

- [[J. Howard Pew]] was the largest single early patron; his investment in the magazine reflected his broader strategy of using evangelical institutions to advance Christian-libertarian ideology.
- Billy Graham's association brought in additional evangelical donor support.
- The early funding relationship between Pew and the magazine is documented in peer-reviewed scholarship (Worthen, 2014, *Enterprise and Society*; archived at Cambridge Core).
- Evidence tier: **documented in multiple academic sources** including Kruse (2015) and Cambridge Core peer-reviewed article.

## Activities

- Published theological content defending orthodox evangelical doctrine.
- Provided a platform for neo-evangelical intellectual figures (Henry, Harold Lindsell, etc.) to articulate a conservative Protestant alternative to both fundamentalist separatism and liberal modernism.
- Covered evangelical political engagement, missions, and cultural issues.
- In the founding era, carried implicit and sometimes explicit endorsement of free-market economics consistent with Pew's funding interests.
- Has since 2016–2019 become a voice for evangelical dissent from Christian nationalism, publishing critiques of the Trump alignment of evangelical leaders.

## Successor / related entities

- No formal successors; Christianity Today continues.
- Broadly, the evangelical intellectual tradition it represents is carried by outlets like *First Things* (Catholic-adjacent), *The Gospel Coalition*, and *Mere Orthodoxy*, though these are distinct and sometimes competing voices.

## Controversies / investigations

- J. Howard Pew's attempt to direct editorial content toward explicit free-market advocacy was a recurring tension with editor Carl Henry, who resisted turning the magazine into a corporate propaganda organ.
- The magazine's 2019 Trump-removal editorial generated immediate backlash from evangelical Trump supporters; Franklin Graham (Billy's son) publicly condemned it.

## Open questions

- The full extent of Pew's financial leverage over editorial content in the early years — and how much it shaped the magazine's theological-political framing — is not fully documented in public sources.
