# Thomas More Law Center

## Summary

The Thomas More Law Center (TMLC) is a Christian-right public-interest law firm, founded in 1999 in Ann Arbor, Michigan, by Domino's Pizza founder [[Tom Monaghan]] and Richard Thompson, a former Oakland County, Michigan, prosecutor. It describes its goals as preserving "America's Judeo-Christian heritage," defending the religious freedom of Christians, restoring "time-honored moral and family values," protecting "the sanctity of human life," and promoting "a strong national defense and a free and sovereign United States." It litigates without charging clients, funded by donations and conservative foundations. **The Thomas More Law Center is a separate organization from the [[Thomas More Society]]** — the Chicago-based conservative Catholic firm founded in 1997 — despite the near-identical name and shared Catholic-saint namesake; this vault builds them as two distinct nodes. TMLC matters to this vault as a long-running litigation arm of the Christian right, and as one of several institutions in the orbit of Tom Monaghan, whose fortune underwrote a network of conservative Catholic and Christian-right organizations.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **[[Tom Monaghan]]** — co-founder and founding funder. Monaghan built Domino's Pizza, sold his stake in 1998, and turned to financing conservative Catholic and Christian-right causes; TMLC, [[Legatus International]], and Ave Maria University are part of his institutional legacy.
- **Richard Thompson** — co-founder; longtime president and chief counsel. A devout Catholic and former Oakland County prosecutor, Thompson founded TMLC after concluding that the courts were contributing to what he described as America's moral decline. Thompson is the operational face of the firm.

## Funding & money flows

TMLC does not charge clients for legal services and relies on tax-deductible donations from individual supporters and charitable foundations. Tom Monaghan's funding was central to the firm's launch. As a conservative Christian litigation organization, TMLC is a candidate recipient for funds routed through conservative donor-advised vehicles such as the [[National Christian Foundation]]. Detailed donor breakdowns are not consolidated in reviewed public sources.

## Activities

TMLC's core activity is **Christian-right public-interest litigation**. Its docket centers on what it frames as defending Christians and Judeo-Christian heritage against secularization. Recurring case categories include:

- **Religious expression in public schools** — e.g., a 2004 suit on behalf of a student challenging a New Jersey school district's restriction on traditional Christmas music.
- **Public religious displays and monuments** — e.g., defending a Ten Commandments monument in Duchesne City, Utah (2003).
- **Anti-abortion advocacy** — defending "pro-life" sidewalk counselors against legal challenges, including a notable defense of counselors sued in a New York federal court.
- **Opposition to same-sex marriage, the Affordable Care Act, and the HHS contraceptive mandate**, and a portfolio of "religious liberty" and culture-war litigation.

TMLC was also involved in one of the most cited church-state cases of the 2000s: it represented the school board in *Kitzmiller v. Dover Area School District* (2005), the landmark trial in which a federal court ruled that teaching "intelligent design" in public-school science classes was unconstitutional — a defeat for TMLC and a significant precedent against creationist instruction. (This case is well documented and is central to TMLC's litigation history; it illustrates that the firm's docket extends to challenging the boundary between religion and public education.)

In the vault's analytical frame, TMLC is part of the **litigation layer** of the religious right — operating alongside firms like the [[Thomas More Society]], [[First Liberty Institute]], and Alliance Defending Freedom — pursuing, case by case, a more religion-favoring interpretation of constitutional law and public policy. It is a smaller and more regionally rooted firm than the largest players, but it is one of the longest-running, and its Monaghan lineage ties it into the conservative-Catholic institutional network.

## Controversies / investigations

- TMLC's representation of the losing side in *Kitzmiller v. Dover* — a high-profile defeat for the intelligent-design movement.
