## Summary

First Baptist Church of Dallas is one of the most historically significant Southern Baptist congregations in the United States. Founded in 1868, the church grew to national prominence under pastor W. A. Criswell (1944–1991), who championed Biblical inerrancy and helped anchor the SBC's conservative resurgence. Under current senior pastor [[Robert Jeffress]] (2007–present), the church became a flagship institution of the Trump-aligned evangelical right. With approximately 16,000 members and a campus covering multiple city blocks in downtown Dallas, it commands a media platform — Pathway to Victory — that reaches internationally. Timothy Alberta's *The Kingdom, the Power, and the Glory* (2023) portrays First Baptist Dallas as emblematic of megachurch Christianity's political capture.

## Identity

- Southern Baptist congregation; adheres to the Baptist Faith and Message 2000 statement.
- Positions itself as a conservative evangelical voice on culture-war issues: abortion, LGBTQ rights, religious liberty, Israel.
- Historically associated with the SBC's conservative resurgence movement; W. A. Criswell's presidency of the SBC (1968–1970) and his advocacy for Biblical inerrancy directly shaped the denomination's rightward theological trajectory in the 1980s–90s.
- Under Jeffress, the church operates an explicit political posture, with frequent invitations to Republican officeholders and candidates.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **[[Robert Jeffress]]** (2007–present): senior pastor; Fox News contributor; Trump Evangelical Advisory Board member; delivered inaugural-eve service January 19, 2017.
- **W. A. Criswell** (1944–1991): towering figure in SBC conservatism; championed Biblical inerrancy; his 1968 SBC presidential address opposing racial integration later became a matter of historical controversy.
- **George W. Truett** (1897–1944): built the congregation from 797 to 7,804 members; nationally known preacher; president of the Baptist World Alliance 1934–1939.

## Funding & money flows

Specific financial data not publicly disclosed beyond 501(c)(3) status. The church completed a major campus redevelopment — described by Church Executive magazine as "the largest Protestant church building project in modern history" — in the 2010s, covering a full city block in downtown Dallas. Funding came from congregational giving. No confirmed PAC or political committee affiliations; Jeffress's political appearances are conducted in his personal capacity.

## Activities

- Pathway to Victory broadcast: airs on 1,200+ television stations; 900+ radio stations; 195 countries.
- Annual events, conferences, and community programming aligned with conservative evangelical priorities.
- Regular platform for Republican political figures; Jeffress regularly hosts or appears alongside elected officials.
- Hosts National Prayer Luncheons and similar civic-religious events.

## Controversies / investigations

- W. A. Criswell in 1956 delivered a sermon opposing racial integration of churches; he later partially walked back these views in the 1990s.
- Jeffress's repeated characterizations of Catholicism, Islam, Judaism, and Mormonism as false religions have generated sustained public criticism but no formal investigation.
- The church's overt political engagement raises recurring questions about its 501(c)(3) status and the Johnson Amendment, though no IRS action has been publicly reported.
