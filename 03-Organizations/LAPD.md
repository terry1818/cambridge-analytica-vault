# LAPD

## Summary

The Los Angeles Police Department is the municipal police force of the City of Los Angeles and the third-largest local law-enforcement agency in the United States. For this vault the LAPD is the key local-government case study: a department that, for nearly two decades, has been an early and aggressive adopter of surveillance technologies originally associated with the federal [[national security state]] — predictive-policing algorithms, [[Palantir]]'s Gotham data-fusion platform, mass automated license-plate reading, social-media monitoring, and facial recognition. The LAPD demonstrates the diffusion pattern the vault tracks: capabilities developed for counterterrorism and intelligence migrate downward into routine policing of ordinary residents, disproportionately in communities of color, and frequently without public knowledge, candid disclosure, or effective oversight. Its history of data-driven policing being audited and found ineffective — yet retained — is itself an analytically important pattern.

## Identity

- **Legal name(s):** Los Angeles Police Department.
- **Other names / fronts:** none; "LAPD."
- **Founded:** the department traces to 1869; the modern force took shape in the early 20th century.
- **Parent / owner:** City of Los Angeles; overseen by the civilian Board of Police Commissioners.
- **Registered jurisdiction:** United States; City of Los Angeles, California.

## Leadership & key personnel

- The department is led by a Chief of Police appointed by the Mayor with Police Commission consent.
- The **Board of Police Commissioners** is the civilian oversight body; in January 2021 it imposed new rules on the department's use of surveillance technology, including facial recognition, after press disclosures.

## Funding & money flows

The LAPD's budget exceeds $3 billion annually, funded by the City of Los Angeles. Vault-relevant procurement and partnerships include its contract with Palantir Technologies for the Gotham data-fusion platform; its earlier contract with PredPol for predictive-policing software; and its accumulation of automated license-plate-reader infrastructure. The department has also historically received federal homeland-security grants that subsidized surveillance-technology purchases, a funding channel that links municipal policing to the federal security apparatus.

## Activities

**Predictive policing.** The LAPD began implementing predictive models to identify crime "hotspots" around 2008 and contracted with PredPol, founded by a researcher tied to Santa Cruz's predictive-policing program. A 2019 LAPD internal audit conceded that the program could not be shown to have "helped reduce crime." A parallel person-based program, Operation LASER, scored individuals for surveillance attention and was discontinued after criticism that it amplified racial bias.

**Palantir data fusion.** The LAPD adopted [[Palantir]]'s Gotham platform, which merges crime and arrest reports, automated license-plate-reader scans, rap sheets, field-interview cards, and other sources into a single searchable system. Investigative reporting (The Intercept, 2021) argued the system operationalized and lent a veneer of objectivity to racially disparate policing.

**Automated license-plate readers.** Per a 2020 California State Auditor report, the LAPD alone had accumulated more than 320 million license-plate scans; the audit found 99.9 percent of stored ALPR images were of vehicles not on any "hot list" when scanned — i.e., overwhelmingly innocent drivers under persistent location tracking.

**Facial recognition.** A Los Angeles Times investigation found LAPD officers had used facial-recognition technology roughly 32,000 times over the years, despite prior departmental denials. The 2021 Police Commission rules now restrict use to a single Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department platform comparing images only to mugshots.

**Networked camera footage.** Like more than 1,800 U.S. law-enforcement agencies, the LAPD has accessed footage from privately owned cameras (such as Ring doorbell devices), extending its visual surveillance reach through residents' own devices.

## Successor / related entities

- City of Los Angeles — governing parent.
- [[Palantir]] — surveillance-technology contractor (Gotham platform).
- [[DHS]] — federal department whose homeland-security grants have subsidized municipal surveillance technology.
- [[FBI]] — federal partner; joint task forces and data-sharing arrangements.
- Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department — operator of the facial-recognition system the LAPD is now restricted to using.

## Controversies / investigations

- **Predictive policing** — discontinued/curtailed after audits found no demonstrable crime reduction and critics documented racial-bias amplification.
- **Undisclosed facial-recognition use** — LA Times exposed ~32,000 uses after departmental denials, prompting 2021 Commission rules.
- **Mass ALPR retention** — State Auditor faulted the indefinite retention of hundreds of millions of scans of innocent drivers.
