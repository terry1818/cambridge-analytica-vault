## Summary

The John M. Olin Foundation was a conservative American grant-making foundation that became one of the most strategically consequential funders in the late-twentieth-century conservative movement. Over its 52-year life it disbursed more than $370 million, concentrating resources on law schools, economics departments, think tanks, and judicial education programs in ways that measurably shifted mainstream legal and economic scholarship to the right. It is the institutional locus of the [[Law and Economics movement]], the principal early funder of the [[Federalist Society]], and a central pillar of the [[Weaponizing philanthropy]] strategy documented in [[Source Jane Mayer Dark Money]].

## Identity

Founded in 1953 by John M. Olin — president of Olin Industries, a chemicals and munitions conglomerate — the foundation lay largely dormant until 1968, when Olin, then 80, decided his wealth must be used to defend the free-market system he believed was under assault on university campuses. The foundation adopted a deliberate sunset clause: all assets were to be spent within a generation of Olin's death to prevent mission drift and preserve donor intent. Olin died in 1982; the foundation made its last grant in summer 2005 and formally dissolved on 29 November 2005.

This spending-down model was itself influential, inspiring other conservative donors to emulate the disciplined, mission-focused approach rather than creating permanent endowments vulnerable to later ideological capture.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

William Simon, Treasury Secretary under Nixon and Ford, served as foundation president and articulated its combative theory of change in his 1978 manifesto *A Time for Truth*, calling for business leaders to fund a counter-intelligentsia. James Piereson served as executive director from 1985 until dissolution and is credited with the disciplined programmatic focus that made the foundation unusually effective per dollar.

## Funding & Money Flows

Total disbursements exceeded **$370 million** across the foundation's active grantmaking period (late 1960s–2005). Key funding streams included:

- **Law and Economics programs**: first grant in 1974 ($100,000 to Henry Manne's center at the University of Miami); ultimately funded Olin Law and Economics programs at Harvard, Chicago, Yale, Stanford, Columbia, Cornell, Michigan, and Virginia, among others.
- **Federalist Society**: provided crucial early support from 1982 founding; the Society became the primary pipeline for conservative federal judges.
- **Think tanks**: Heritage Foundation, American Enterprise Institute, Hoover Institution, Manhattan Institute, Hudson Institute.
- **Judicial education**: co-funded the Economics Institute for Federal Judges (associated with Henry Manne), through which an estimated 40 percent of the federal judiciary received Law and Economics training by the mid-1990s.

## Activities

The foundation's signature achievement was creating a self-replicating pipeline: fund academic programs → produce scholars → place them in think tanks and clerkships → shift the Overton window in law and economics → produce judges and policymakers shaped by those ideas. Jane Mayer's *Dark Money* describes this as a "counter-intelligentsia" strategy first articulated by Lewis Powell's 1971 memo to the US Chamber of Commerce. The Olin Foundation operationalized that blueprint more systematically than any other single institution.

## Successor / Related Entities

The foundation had no direct successor, but its intellectual legacy was carried forward by:
- [[Federalist Society]] — remains the dominant conservative legal network
- [[Heritage Foundation]] — continued independent of Olin but shaped by Olin funding
- [[Mercatus Center]] — Koch-funded successor to Olin's George Mason investments
- [[Koch Network]] — adopted the counter-intelligentsia model at larger scale
- [[Bradley Foundation]] — explicitly modeled its "weaponized philanthropy" strategy on Olin

## Controversies / Investigations

The "judicial junket" programs — all-expenses-paid retreats in luxury resorts where federal judges received Law and Economics training — attracted scrutiny from legal ethics scholars and congressional critics who argued they constituted impermissible ex parte education of sitting judges on contested economic doctrine. No formal sanctions resulted.
