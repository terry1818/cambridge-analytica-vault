# Regent University

## Summary

Regent University is a private Christian university in Virginia Beach, Virginia, founded in 1977 by televangelist and Christian Broadcasting Network founder [[Pat Robertson]]. It opened in 1978 as **CBN University** — an outgrowth of Robertson's broadcasting empire — and was renamed Regent University in 1990. Regent's significance to this vault is as a deliberate **talent pipeline** for the religious right: Robertson built it to train conservative Christians for leadership and influence across the spheres of law, government, journalism, education, and business. Its motto — "Christian Leadership to Change the World" — captures the dominionist project of seeding conservative Christians into the institutions of American public life. The Regent University School of Law became the most consequential element of that project: during the George W. Bush administration, a notable cohort of Regent law graduates obtained federal government positions, including in the Department of Justice, drawing national attention to the school as an engine for placing movement-aligned lawyers into government.

- **Dissolved / restructured:** Active

- **Subsidiaries:** Multiple schools — Law, Government, Divinity, Business & Leadership, Communication & the Arts, Education, Psychology & Counseling
- **Registered jurisdiction:** United States (Virginia)

## Leadership & key personnel

- **[[Pat Robertson]]** — founder, chancellor, and CEO until his death in 2023; the dominant figure of the institution. Robertson also founded the [[Christian Broadcasting Network]] (1960), the long-running *700 Club* program, the American Center for Law & Justice, and ran for the Republican presidential nomination in 1988.
- Regent has a conventional university administration (president, provost, deans) operating under Robertson's chancellorship and, since 2023, his successors.

## Funding & money flows

Regent was capitalized and sustained through Robertson's broadcasting and fundraising apparatus, the Christian Broadcasting Network, and conservative Christian donors, alongside ordinary tuition revenue. Robertson's personal wealth and the CBN donor base gave the institution unusual financial backing for a new religious university. Detailed endowment and donor figures are not consolidated in reviewed public sources.

## Activities

Regent's core activity is higher education delivered with an explicitly Christian, conservative worldview across its degree programs. But the institution was designed with a strategic purpose beyond ordinary education: to produce credentialed conservative Christians who could enter and influence elite professional fields. That design is clearest in two schools:

- **School of Law.** Regent Law trains attorneys within a conservative Christian framework. During the George W. Bush administration, the placement of Regent law graduates in federal posts — including in the Department of Justice — became a widely reported story, illustrating the school's function as a feeder for movement-aligned legal personnel. This connects Regent to the broader project, tracked in this vault, of staffing the legal and judicial machinery of government with ideologically committed lawyers.

- **Robertson School of Government.** Trains conservative Christians for careers in public policy, government, and politics.

Regent has also been a venue and platform for conservative and Christian-right political figures, hosting speakers and conferences and granting honorary recognition to movement allies. As an institution it embodies the cultural-influence strategy associated with dominion theology and the broader Christian-nationalist movement: rather than only preaching, build the schools that credential the next generation of judges, officials, journalists, and executives. In the vault's analytical frame, Regent is **infrastructure** — a long-horizon investment in personnel, comparable in logic (if not scale or ideology) to other movements' institution-building.
