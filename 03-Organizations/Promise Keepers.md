## Summary

Promise Keepers is an evangelical Christian men's organization founded in 1990 by Bill McCartney, then head football coach at the University of Colorado. At its 1990s peak, PK was arguably the largest organized men's movement in American history, with an estimated 3.5 million men attending stadium rallies over the decade. Its 1997 Stand in the Gap assembly on the National Mall drew between 600,000 and 800,000 men. Kristin Kobes Du Mez's *Jesus and John Wayne* (2020) treats Promise Keepers as a central institution in the decades-long construction of militant evangelical masculinity — the theological model of Christian manhood as warrior, protector, and spiritual head that Du Mez argues primed white evangelical men for Donald Trump.

## Identity

Promise Keepers organized men around seven promises: honor to Jesus Christ, vital male relationships, sexual and moral purity, strong marriages and families, support for the church, racial reconciliation, and influence in the world. The theological center was a model of male headship that called men to "take back" spiritual leadership in their families — a framing that simultaneously valorized masculine authority and presented that authority as service and sacrifice rather than domination.

Du Mez analyzes Promise Keepers as part of a longer lineage of attempts to "re-masculinize" American Christianity, running from the muscular Christianity of the early 20th century through [[Christian Men's Network]] founder Edwin Louis Cole (who McCartney acknowledged as an influence) and into the post-9/11 militarized warrior-faith models promoted by figures like Mark Driscoll at [[Mars Hill Church]].

The racial reconciliation component — genuine in McCartney's framing, and prominently featured at PK events with diverse speakers — was controversial within white evangelical culture and is cited by some observers as a factor in PK's mid-1990s decline among some constituencies.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Bill McCartney** (1940–2025): Founder; University of Colorado head football coach 1982–1994; committed to PK full-time after resigning coaching post; died January 2025
- **Randy Phillips**: Served as president through PK's 1990s peak

## Funding & Money Flows

Promise Keepers generated substantial revenue from stadium event admissions and merchandise during its 1990s peak. Financial difficulties followed the 1997 Stand in the Gap event, after which PK eliminated admission charges to conferences; this precipitated a cash crisis and layoffs of approximately 400 staff in 1998. The organization subsequently scaled back dramatically but survived. Specific major donor information is not publicly documented.

## Activities

- Large-scale stadium rallies (1990s peak: dozens of events per year)
- Stand in the Gap Sacred Assembly, National Mall, October 4, 1997 (600,000–800,000 attendees)
- Small-group discipleship programs
- Racial reconciliation programming
- Revival of large events in subsequent decades at reduced scale
- Trump-era resurgence: PK positioned itself within MAGA-adjacent evangelical men's organizing

## Controversies / Investigations

- Feminist critics argued PK's headship theology reinforced patriarchal family structures; groups like NOW organized counter-events at PK rallies
- The racial reconciliation framing was criticized both by those who found it insufficient and by white evangelicals who resisted it
- PK's male-only format and martial imagery raised questions about gender essentialism in evangelical culture
- Du Mez documents how PK's model of "servant leadership" masked the reinforcement of male hierarchical authority
