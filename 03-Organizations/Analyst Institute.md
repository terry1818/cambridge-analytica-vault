## Summary

The Analyst Institute is a Democratic-aligned research clearinghouse established in 2007 to compile, run, and share results from randomized field experiments on voter contact, persuasion, and turnout. It was co-founded by [[Mike Podhorzer]] (AFL-CIO), [[Hal Malchow]] (MSHC Partners), and [[Todd Rogers]] (then completing his doctorate at Harvard), with academic affiliations to [[Gerber and Green field experiments]] researchers at Yale. [[Source - Issenberg 2012 - The Victory Lab]] characterizes the Institute as the institutional solution to a collective-action problem: left-of-center organizations were each running small, proprietary experiments with no mechanism to aggregate learnings, so campaigns kept repeating one another's mistakes. The Institute created a shared evidence base.

## Identity

The Analyst Institute operates as a data research and consulting organization working exclusively with progressive-aligned clients — Democratic campaigns, labor unions, advocacy nonprofits, and voter-registration groups. It does not work for Republican campaigns or commercial clients. Its primary function is to:

1. Design and run randomized controlled trials on behalf of member organizations
2. Compile meta-analyses across multiple experiments to identify consistent effects
3. Maintain a proprietary database of what works and what does not in voter contact

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Mike Podhorzer** — Co-founder and longtime chair; provided AFL-CIO institutional home and convened the predecessor "Analyst Group" (2004–2007)
- **Hal Malchow** — Co-founder; brought practitioner direct-mail expertise and motivation to test claims empirically
- **Todd Rogers** — Co-founder and behavioral-science perspective; provided academic rigor from Harvard research tradition
- **Alan Gerber and Donald Green** — Listed as founding affiliated academics; their Yale GOTV experiments (1998–) provided the theoretical and methodological template

## Funding & Money Flows

Specific funding sources are not publicly disclosed. The Institute operates on a subscription/consortium model — member organizations pay for access to research and for the Institute to run experiments on their programs. Likely funders include labor unions (AFL-CIO affiliates), major Democratic donor networks, and advocacy organizations. No public tax filings have been verified for this note.

## Activities

- **Randomized experiment library**: Has run hundreds of randomized tests for member organizations covering canvassing scripts, mail content, phone-banking approaches, digital contact, and social-pressure interventions.
- **Best-practices synthesis**: Compiled meta-analyses of GOTV tactics, distributed to members as evidence-based guidance. The 2008 Obama ground game and subsequent Democratic campaigns drew on this evidence base.
- **Collaborative data model**: Member organizations share experimental results back to the Institute, building cumulative knowledge rather than siloed proprietary tests.
- **Behavioral science integration**: [[Todd Rogers]]'s involvement brought identity-based framing ("you're the kind of person who votes") and social-norm messaging into the research agenda, moving beyond simple contact-method evaluation.

## Controversies / Investigations

No formal legal controversies on record. The Institute's deliberate opacity about its membership and research results has drawn some criticism as an example of Democrats keeping successful tactics proprietary rather than publishing in peer-reviewed journals where Republicans could also learn from them.
