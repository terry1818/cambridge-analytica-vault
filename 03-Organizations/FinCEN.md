## Summary

The Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN) is a bureau of the US Department of the Treasury established in 1990. Its mandate is to safeguard the financial system from illicit use by collecting, analyzing, and disseminating financial intelligence and imposing AML compliance requirements on financial institutions. For this vault's purposes, FinCEN is significant as the regulatory body that identified — and attempted to close — the [[All-cash LLC real-estate laundering]] loophole: the use of anonymous shell companies to purchase US real estate with cash, enabling the placement of funds of uncertain provenance (including alleged Russian organized crime and oligarchic capital) into the US property market.

FinCEN administers the Bank Secrecy Act (BSA), which requires banks and other financial institutions to file Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) and Currency Transaction Reports (CTRs). Real estate professionals were historically exempt from these requirements — a gap that FinCEN has worked to close through Geographic Targeting Orders and proposed rulemaking.

## Identity

FinCEN is a regulatory and intelligence agency within Treasury, not a law enforcement body; it shares intelligence with the FBI, IRS Criminal Investigation, DEA, and other agencies rather than making arrests itself. Its legal authorities derive primarily from the Bank Secrecy Act. The agency receives and analyzes approximately 20 million filings per year from financial institutions.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

Directors are political appointees confirmed by the Senate or serving in an acting capacity; specific current director not listed here as these change with administrations.

## Funding & Money Flows

FinCEN is a federal agency funded through Congressional appropriations. It does not directly handle financial flows; it requires covered institutions to report on them and maintains databases (the BSA database) used by law enforcement.

## Activities

**Relevant to this vault:**

- **Geographic Targeting Orders (GTOs)**: Beginning in January 2016, FinCEN issued temporary orders requiring title insurance companies to identify the beneficial owners behind LLC and other entity purchases of high-value residential real estate in specific metropolitan areas. Initially limited to Manhattan and Miami, expanded to 12+ metro areas by 2018. Finding: approximately 30% of covered transactions involved a buyer or representative who was also the subject of a prior SAR. This is the primary documented evidence of the scale of suspicious all-cash real estate activity.
- **Residential Real Estate Rule (2026)**: FinCEN finalized a permanent nationwide rule requiring title agents and settlement companies to report beneficial ownership on all all-cash residential purchases by entities or trusts. Effective March 1, 2026; vacated by a federal district court in Texas on March 19, 2026 on the grounds that FinCEN exceeded its statutory authority. The rule's fate remained uncertain as of mid-2026.
- **SARs on Trump-related transactions**: FinCEN data on suspicious activity reports related to Trump-associated entities was reported on by BuzzFeed News (2019) and others as part of the "FinCEN Files" — leaked SAR data showing that major banks had filed suspicious activity reports on transactions involving individuals connected to Trump, Russian organized crime, and related parties.

**Other major FinCEN work:**
- Enforcement against banks (e.g., HSBC, Deutsche Bank) for AML failures
- Administration of the Corporate Transparency Act beneficial ownership registry (2024)

## Controversies / Investigations

- **FinCEN Files (2019)**: BuzzFeed News obtained and published leaked SAR data — 2,100 SARs totaling $2 trillion in transactions — showing major banks filing suspicious activity reports on individuals and entities connected to Russian organized crime, kleptocratic networks, and related parties, including Trump associates. The leak raised questions about whether FinCEN adequately acted on the SARs it received.
- **Slow real estate regulation**: Critics note that FinCEN identified the all-cash LLC real estate problem no later than 2016 but took a decade to finalize a permanent rule, which was then vacated. The industry lobbying that slowed regulation is well-documented.
