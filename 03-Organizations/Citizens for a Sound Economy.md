## Summary

Citizens for a Sound Economy (CSE) was the principal Washington-based advocacy vehicle created by Charles and David Koch in 1984 to advance free-market and deregulatory policy. Founded with a 110-page strategic business plan authored by Richard Fink, CSE combined corporate lobby-group tactics with grassroots astroturfing techniques years before the Tea Party popularized the approach. In 2004 it split into two successor organizations: **FreedomWorks** (retaining the CSE name then rebranded, led by Dick Armey, focused on grassroots mobilization) and **Americans for Prosperity** (retaining the CSE Foundation, chaired by David Koch, focused on electoral and policy infrastructure). Both successors played central roles in the Tea Party movement beginning in 2009. Christopher Leonard's *Kochland* documents CSE as the template for Koch political activism and the bridge between Koch Industries' corporate interests and mass-movement politics.

## Identity

CSE was established in 1984 by Charles Koch, David Koch, J. P. Humphries, and Richard Fink, with Ron Paul as its first chairman. The organization pursued an explicitly Washington-focused strategy: appearing as a citizen advocacy group while being substantially funded by corporate interests. Its stated mission was to promote free markets, limited government, and deregulation. It operated alongside the CSE Foundation, a 501(c)(3) entity.

The Kochland account portrays CSE as the organizational expression of Charles Koch's [[Market-Based Management]] philosophy applied to politics: decentralized, incentive-driven, and oriented toward achieving concrete policy victories rather than mere intellectual influence.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Richard Fink**: architect of the founding business plan; later became Koch Industries' chief political strategist and a director of [[Americans for Prosperity]]
- **Ron Paul**: first chairman (1984–1986), establishing the libertarian credibility of the organization
- **Dick Armey**: later chairman; joined from Congress; split with David Koch in 2004 and took the FreedomWorks vehicle
- **Matt Kibbe**: co-founder; became president of FreedomWorks post-split

## Funding & Money Flows

CSE received nearly **$5 million** from Koch foundations between 1986 and 1990 alone. Additional major funders included tobacco companies (Philip Morris), oil companies (ExxonMobil predecessor entities), and other industries seeking deregulation. David Koch and several Koch Industries employees served as directors. The tobacco industry's funding of CSE's anti-regulation campaigns is documented in tobacco industry internal documents; CSE ran campaigns against cigarette taxes and Clean Air Act regulations while receiving tobacco money.

## Activities

CSE's core activities included:
- Lobbying against energy regulations, environmental rules, and tax increases
- Coordinating "grassroots" letter-writing and phone-banking campaigns for corporate policy goals
- Commissioning and distributing economic research favorable to deregulation
- Building a state-chapter network that prefigured later Tea Party organizing infrastructure
- Running campaigns against Clinton-era health reform (1993–1994)

The anti-Clinton health reform campaign is widely cited as CSE's most visible national campaign, deploying what Greenpeace later called a "fraudulent grassroots" operation: corporate-funded mass-contact campaigns presented as citizen initiatives.

## Successor / Related Entities

- [[Americans for Prosperity]] — direct policy/electoral successor; remains a primary Koch political vehicle as of 2026
- [[FreedomWorks]] — grassroots mobilization successor; dissolved 2023 after internal conflicts
- [[Koch Network]] — broader funding and coordination infrastructure

## Controversies / Investigations

- **Tobacco industry coordination**: Internal tobacco documents (released via litigation) show Philip Morris funding CSE's anti-cigarette-tax campaigns while CSE presented itself as a citizen group.
- **Astroturfing**: Greenpeace and other watchdog organizations documented CSE as an early template for corporate-funded fake grassroots campaigns.
