# National Association of Manufacturers

## Summary

The National Association of Manufacturers (NAM) is the oldest and largest manufacturing trade association in the United States, founded in 1895 in Cincinnati. It is included in this vault not for its routine modern lobbying but for a specific, formative episode: its sustained **propaganda campaign of roughly 1935–1945**, in which NAM pioneered the mass-communication techniques that American business has used ever since to shape public opinion. In the 1930s NAM became the focal point of organized business opposition to Franklin D. Roosevelt's New Deal, and it mounted what scholars treat as one of the first modern corporate public-relations campaigns — a deliberate, well-funded effort to win the ideological argument over the role of government in the economy.

NAM matters to the vault's "constitutional-capture," "patron-wing," and "disinformation" threads as the **historical prototype**. Decades before the [[William Volker Fund]] funded libertarian economists or the [[Koch Network]] built its donor apparatus, NAM had already developed the core method: use concentrated business money to manufacture a public belief that free-market capitalism and American freedom are one and the same — and that government intervention is therefore un-American. NAM's campaign promoted what scholars call the "indivisibility thesis" (free enterprise is inseparable from democracy and political liberty) and the "tripod of freedom" narrative. The campaign's institutional vehicle was NAM's main propaganda agency, the National Industrial Information Council (NIIC).

## Leadership & key personnel

- NAM was founded in 1895 by manufacturing industrialists; in the 1930s its leadership was dominated by executives of large industrial corporations who drove the anti–New Deal turn.
- The New Deal–era public-relations campaign was orchestrated through the **National Industrial Information Council (NIIC)** and NAM's public-relations staff.
- In its modern form NAM is led by a president/CEO and a board drawn from major U.S. manufacturers.

## Funding & money flows

NAM is funded by membership dues and contributions from manufacturing companies, historically dominated by large industrial corporations. In the New Deal era, scholars note that NAM's propaganda activities became its single most expensive function — absorbing, by some accounts, more time, effort, and money than all the association's other functions combined. The campaign was financed by member corporations seeking to roll back the New Deal regulatory and labor settlement.

## Activities

**The 1935–1945 propaganda campaign.** NAM's New Deal–era campaign aimed to delegitimize the New Deal and organized labor and to entrench a pro-business, "free enterprise" worldview. Its methods, strikingly modern, included:

- **Advertising campaigns** in newspapers and magazines.
- **Leaflets and brochures** distributed to schools, libraries, religious leaders, and women's clubs.
- **A nationally syndicated radio program.**
- **Visual propaganda** — posters and billboards placed in workplaces and public spaces (the Hagley Library holds NAM's poster series).
- **Workplace messaging** aimed directly at employees.

The ideological payload was the "indivisibility thesis": the claim that free enterprise, democracy, and personal liberty form a single indivisible whole, so that any expansion of government economic role is a threat to freedom itself. This framing — government as the enemy of liberty — is the through-line connecting NAM's 1930s campaign to the libertarian movement the vault tracks.

**Anti-labor lobbying.** In the later 1930s and 1940s NAM worked to repeal the National Labor Relations Act (Wagner Act), which guaranteed the rights to organize, bargain collectively, and strike. In the 1940s–1950s NAM lobbied for federal laws restricting union power and internal governance (it supported the 1947 Taft-Hartley Act).

**Modern NAM.** Today NAM functions as a conventional, large-scale trade-association lobby on tax, trade, regulatory, and labor policy — but its historical significance for this vault is as the originator of the corporate-propaganda model.

## Successor / related entities

- **National Industrial Information Council (NIIC)** — NAM's historical propaganda subsidiary.
- **[[Foundation for Economic Education]] / [[William Volker Fund]]** — post-war libertarian institutions that carried forward the "free enterprise = freedom" argument NAM popularized.
- **[[Koch Network]] / [[Americans for Prosperity]]** — modern, vastly larger versions of the business-funded ideological campaign NAM prototyped.
- **U.S. Chamber of Commerce** — parallel and overlapping business-lobby organization.

## Controversies / investigations

- NAM's New Deal–era campaign is studied by historians (notably in *The Disinformation Age*, ed. W. Lance Bennett and Steven Livingston) as an early case of organized corporate disinformation — the deliberate, paid manufacture of a self-serving public belief presented as patriotic common sense.
- In 1938 NAM and other business groups were investigated in connection with anti-labor activity; the La Follette Civil Liberties Committee scrutinized industrial espionage and union-busting by NAM-aligned firms.

## Open questions
