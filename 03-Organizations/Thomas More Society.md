# Thomas More Society

## Summary

The Thomas More Society is a conservative Roman Catholic public-interest law firm based in Chicago, founded in 1997 by attorney Tom Brejcha. It built its reputation on anti-abortion litigation, most notably winning two U.S. Supreme Court victories against the National Organization for Women in the *Scheidler v. NOW* cases (2003 and 2006). It is **distinct from the [[Thomas More Law Center]]** — a separate Michigan-based Christian-right firm founded in 1999 by Tom Monaghan and Richard Thompson — despite the similar name; both are named for the Catholic martyr-saint Thomas More. The Thomas More Society matters to this vault because, after the 2020 election, it created and housed the **Amistad Project**, one of the most consequential legal vehicles in Donald Trump's failed campaign to overturn the result. Through the Amistad Project, the firm filed sweeping lawsuits across the battleground states, advanced election-fraud claims, and helped seed durable doubts about election integrity — placing a Catholic public-interest law firm directly inside the machinery of post-2020 election denial.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Tom Brejcha** — founder, president, and chief counsel; built the firm from the *Scheidler v. NOW* anti-abortion litigation.
- **Phill Kline** — director of the Amistad Project; former Kansas Attorney General whose law license had been suspended; later a Liberty University faculty member. Kline directed the post-2020 election-litigation effort.
- **[[Jenna Ellis]]** — senior legal adviser to the Trump campaign and special counsel to the Thomas More Society; later pleaded guilty in the Georgia election-interference prosecution.

## Funding & money flows

The Thomas More Society is donor-funded, supported by conservative Catholic and other right-leaning individuals and foundations. Its election-related work through the Amistad Project drew on conservative donor funding; reporting (American Oversight, ProPublica, Raw Story) examined the financing and coordination of the Amistad Project's activities, including its influence on partisan election "investigations" such as the one in Wisconsin. Detailed donor breakdowns are not fully consolidated in reviewed public sources; like peer litigation firms it is a candidate for funds routed through donor-advised vehicles such as the [[National Christian Foundation]].

## Activities

For its first two decades the Thomas More Society's core activity was **conservative Catholic public-interest litigation**, centered on anti-abortion cases (its *Scheidler v. NOW* wins), religious-liberty disputes, and "pro-life" sidewalk-counseling defense. That work placed it within the standard religious-right litigation ecosystem.

The firm's defining turn for this vault came after the November 2020 election. The Society established the **Amistad Project** and, under Phill Kline, filed lawsuits in the swing states Joe Biden won — Arizona, Georgia, Michigan, Pennsylvania, and Wisconsin — on behalf of nominally "grassroots" plaintiffs with names such as the "Wisconsin Voters Alliance" and "Pennsylvania Voters Alliance." On December 22, 2020, after the Electoral College had voted, the Amistad Project sued in federal court in Washington, D.C. on behalf of voters, organizations, and state legislators, naming Vice President Mike Pence, governors, election officials, both houses of Congress, and the Electoral College itself, and asking the court to declare longstanding federal elector-counting statutes unconstitutional. Every suit was rejected or dismissed.

Beyond the courtroom, reporting documented that the Amistad Project's work was part of a **long-term strategy** of "sowing doubts about election integrity" — and that it influenced partisan post-election "investigations," including Wisconsin's. The connection ran into the criminal sphere as well: Jenna Ellis, a Trump campaign adviser who was also special counsel to the Thomas More Society, was later indicted in Georgia's election-interference case and pleaded guilty. In the vault's analytical frame, the Thomas More Society is a case study in how an established religious-right litigation institution lent its legal infrastructure, personnel, and respectability to election denial and the broader project of democratic backsliding.
