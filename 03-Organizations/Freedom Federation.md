## Summary

The Freedom Federation is a coalition of religious-right and Christian nationalist organizations convened around 2009–2010, with Mat Staver of Liberty Counsel described in contemporaneous reporting as its chairman. The coalition bridges what Susan Posner's *Unholy* (2020) identifies as distinct strands of the American religious right: mainline evangelical lobbying organizations ([[Family Research Council]], [[Liberty Counsel]]) and charismatic / dominionist networks ([[The Call (prayer movement)]], associated with Lou Engle). The Freedom Federation became publicly visible around healthcare reform opposition in 2009–2010, framing the Affordable Care Act debate in explicitly theological terms. It represents the kind of cross-network coalition infrastructure that allows different wings of the theocratic movement to coordinate on legislative campaigns.

## Identity

- A coalition structure rather than a standalone organization; member organizations retain their own identities and leadership.
- Theologically ecumenical within Christian conservatism: includes both Reformed and charismatic/Pentecostal streams, unusual for formal institutional alignment.
- Political orientation: explicitly anti-abortion, anti-LGBTQ, anti-secularism; engaged in healthcare reform opposition as theological issue.
- Mat Staver, dean of Liberty University School of Law, was described as chairman in 2009–10 coverage; Staver framed the coalition as unified cross-denominational Christian political voice.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Mat Staver**: Chairman (per 2009–10 reporting); founder of Liberty Counsel; dean, Liberty University School of Law.
- **Lou Engle**: Founder of [[The Call (prayer movement)]]; charismatic intercessory prayer movement leader; associated with [[New Apostolic Reformation]] networks.
- **Tony Perkins**: President, [[Family Research Council]]; provided mainline evangelical lobbying credibility.
- **[[Brigitte Gabriel]]**: [[ACT for America]] — potential participation confirmed in overlapping religious-right coalition events.
- Other reported members include leaders from the American Family Association, Concerned Women for America, and related organizations.

## Funding & money flows

No independent financial disclosures identified for the Freedom Federation as a standalone entity. Member organizations maintain their own fundraising operations. The coalition's function appears to be coordination and joint messaging rather than pooled funding.

## Activities

- Convened summits and prayer events framing political opposition in theological terms.
- Healthcare reform opposition (2009–10): Freedom Federation explicitly invoked divine opposition to the ACA; framed universal healthcare as "Herod" — a reference to the biblical slaughter of innocents, conflating the ACA with abortion funding.
- Joint declarations and statements on cultural issues.
- Networking between charismatic prayer networks and formal lobbying organizations — bridging structures that typically operate in separate silos.

## Controversies / investigations

- The Freedom Federation's healthcare opposition framing — invoking divine and biblical opposition to government healthcare — was criticized as evidence of theocratic political theology entering mainstream Republican discourse.
- AFA (American Family Association) blog posts questioned whether the coalition's association with charismatic/prophetic figures (including those associated with the NAR) compromised doctrinal integrity — illustrating internal theological tension even within the religious right.
