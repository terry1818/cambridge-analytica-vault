# Catalist

## Summary

Catalist is a for-profit voter-data and analytics company, founded in 2006, that builds and licenses a centralized national database of U.S. voters and consumers for Democratic Party committees, progressive organizations, labor unions, and aligned campaigns. It was created by Democratic operatives [[Harold Ickes]] and Laura Quinn — originally incorporated as Data Warehouse, LLC — in direct response to a perceived Republican advantage in data-driven campaigning after the 2004 election. The decision to build it as a *business*, with investors and paying customers, rather than as a party organ, was deliberate: the for-profit LLC structure let wealthy Democratic-aligned donors fund left-of-center voter modeling and turnout work without triggering Federal Election Commission contribution limits, since clients pay for a commercial service. Catalist reportedly received early seed funding from a group of major Democratic donors associated with [[George Soros]]. In this vault, Catalist is the Democratic mirror image of the political-data industry — useful for showing that micro-targeted, data-rich electioneering is a bipartisan infrastructure, not a uniquely Republican or Cambridge Analytica-specific one.

## Leadership & key personnel

- [[Harold Ickes]] — co-founder; longtime Democratic operative and former Clinton White House deputy chief of staff.
- Laura Quinn — co-founder and longtime CEO; previously a senior Democratic strategist.
- Vijay Ravindran — co-founding technologist who helped build the original data architecture.

## Funding & money flows

- Seed funding came from a pool of high-profile Democratic-aligned donors; reporting has described the funding group as led by [[George Soros]].
- Revenue model: clients (party committees, c3/c4 nonprofits, unions, campaigns) pay licensing and analytics fees for access to the database — the commercial structure that distinguishes contributions from service payments under FEC rules.
- The founding context is notable: Ickes had previously led America Coming Together and the Media Fund, organizations later fined a combined ~$1.35 million by the FEC; Catalist's business structure was in part designed to operate cleanly within campaign-finance law.

## Activities

Catalist maintains a continuously updated database covering essentially the entire U.S. voting-age population, integrating public voter files with commercial and modeled data. It provides voter-contact data, predictive models (turnout propensity, partisanship, issue support), and — increasingly — post-election analysis: Catalist's "What Happened" reports have become a widely cited account of the electorate after recent national elections. Its core function is to give the fragmented universe of Democratic and progressive organizations a shared, persistent data backbone, so that voter intelligence is not rebuilt from scratch each cycle.

## Timeline

- `2004` — Democrats lose the presidential election; the board of America Coming Together resolves to create a persistent Democratic voter database.
- `2006` — the firm is founded as Data Warehouse, LLC by Ickes, Quinn, and Ravindran; later branded Catalist.
- `2008-2012` — Catalist data underpins Democratic and progressive voter-targeting operations during the Obama campaigns era.
- `2014-2020` — Catalist becomes standard infrastructure across Democratic-aligned campaigns and nonprofits; publishes influential post-election analyses.
- `2024-2026` — continues operating as the principal shared data vendor for the political left; "What Happened" analyses widely cited.

## Successor / related entities

- [[NGP VAN]] — separate Democratic data/technology vendor operating in adjacent space (voter contact, fundraising software).
- [[i360]] / [[Data Trust]] — Republican-side data infrastructure (Koch-network and RNC-aligned respectively); structural counterparts to Catalist.

## Controversies / investigations

- Conservative research outlets characterize Catalist's LLC structure as a vehicle that lets donors evade campaign-finance limits by routing money as service payments; defenders argue paying a commercial vendor for data is entirely lawful and ordinary. `#claim/disputed`
- The Soros funding link is frequently invoked in right-wing media; the documented fact is early donor backing associated with Soros, not operational control.
