# Raytheon

## Summary

Raytheon is one of the largest U.S. aerospace and defense contractors, founded in 1922 and today operating as the Raytheon business segment of **RTX Corporation** — the holding company formed in 2020 by the merger of Raytheon Company and United Technologies, and renamed from "Raytheon Technologies" to "RTX" in 2023. RTX describes itself as the world's largest aerospace and defense company, organized into three businesses: Collins Aerospace, Pratt & Whitney, and Raytheon. The Raytheon segment makes the Patriot air-defense system, the SM-3 and SM-6 naval missiles, the AMRAAM and other air-to-air missiles, the Evolved SeaSparrow Missile, and a large portfolio of radar and sensor systems. For this vault, Raytheon matters as a pillar of the U.S. military-industrial complex and, more specifically, as a major builder of **surveillance and reconnaissance hardware** — the radars and sensors that constitute the physical layer of the surveillance state — and as a participant in the broader fusion of defense contracting, AI, and missile-defense politics around initiatives like "Iron Dome for America."

## Funding & money flows

RTX's revenue — on the order of $80 billion annually across its three segments — is driven heavily by U.S. and allied defense spending. Representative recent Raytheon-segment money flows:

- A **$5.6 billion** contract for Patriot GEM-T missiles from NATO
- Growth driven by higher volume on land and air-defense systems, including international Patriot and naval programs (SM-6, Evolved SeaSparrow Missile)
- Positioning for the "Iron Dome for America" homeland missile-defense initiative

## Activities

**Missiles and air defense.** The Raytheon segment is one of the world's principal missile makers: the Patriot surface-to-air system, the Standard Missile family (SM-3, SM-6), AMRAAM air-to-air missiles, the Evolved SeaSparrow, and more. Patriot in particular has been central to allied air defense and to the Russia–Ukraine war.

**Surveillance radar and sensors.** In October 2025 Raytheon began initial production of its new **SharpSight** multi-domain surveillance radar — a next-generation system providing high-altitude, real-time, high-resolution imaging with wide-area search and tracking for land and maritime surveillance, day or night, in any weather. SharpSight is emblematic of Raytheon's role in building the sensor layer of modern surveillance and reconnaissance.

**Homeland missile defense.** RTX has positioned its portfolio to play a role in the "Iron Dome for America" initiative — a politically prominent homeland-defense program — connecting the company to the missile-defense politics that recur in U.S. national-security debates.

## Controversies / investigations

- **Revolving door and procurement capture:** RTX, like Lockheed Martin, exemplifies the personnel exchange between defense contractors and the Pentagon that critics argue distorts procurement. `#claim/established`
- **Foreign Corrupt Practices / overcharging settlements:** RTX and predecessor Raytheon entities have faced government settlements over contracting and foreign-bribery allegations in the 2020s.
- **Arms-export politics:** Raytheon missile sales to parties in active conflicts (including the Middle East) have drawn human-rights criticism.
- **Surveillance hardware:** SharpSight and similar systems extend persistent wide-area surveillance capability, raising the same civil-liberties questions the vault tracks across the surveillance-state cluster.
