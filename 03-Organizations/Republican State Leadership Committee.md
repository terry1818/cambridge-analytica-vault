# Republican State Leadership Committee (RSLC)

## Summary

The Republican State Leadership Committee is a national partisan committee of the Republican Party focused exclusively on electing Republicans to state-level offices: state legislatures, attorneys general, lieutenant governors, secretaries of state, and state supreme court judges. Founded in 2002, the RSLC became the vehicle for the [[REDMAP]] redistricting strategy beginning in 2009, transforming from a low-profile fundraising clearinghouse into a $30-million precision operation that reshaped American Congressional representation for the decade following the 2010 Census.

## Identity

The RSLC operates as a 527 political organization, allowing it to accept unlimited contributions. It does not field candidates directly but bundles and deploys money to targeted state legislative races. Its explicit theory of change — articulated in the [[REDMAP]] project — is that controlling state legislatures controls Congressional maps, which controls Congress. The organization is explicitly strategic-institutional rather than purely electoral.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

**Ed Gillespie** served as RSLC national chairman beginning January 2010, bringing with him the strategic vision of targeting redistricting-year state races. Gillespie was the former RNC chairman (2003–2005) and a veteran of the Bush White House; his involvement dramatically elevated the RSLC's donor profile.

**Chris Jankowski** served as executive director and later president. Jankowski was the operational architect of REDMAP — he identified the specific state chambers to target, managed the spending decisions, and oversaw the 2010 cycle. He ran the RSLC through approximately 2013–2014, then returned to private consulting in Richmond, Virginia.

**Thomas Hofeller**, while not an RSLC employee, was the organization's primary mapmaker consultant in the post-2010 redistricting round. His files, later discovered by his daughter and made public in 2020, documented his central role.

## Funding & Money Flows

In the 2010 cycle, the RSLC raised approximately $30 million — a dramatic increase over prior cycles. Key donors included corporations and dark-money vehicles. The organization is not required to disclose individual donors in the same manner as candidate committees. ProPublica documented that the 2010 spending included significant contributions from interests with stakes in state regulatory decisions, effectively purchasing favorable legislative majorities.

In the 2020 cycle (REDMAP 2020), the RSLC again organized a major redistricting-focused effort, but this time faced a counterpart in the National Democratic Redistricting Committee (NDRC), founded in 2017 by former U.S. Attorney General Eric Holder.

## Activities

- **State legislature targeting:** Identifies chambers where Republican control is within reach and concentrates spending in those races.
- **REDMAP (2010–present):** The redistricting strategy that made the RSLC nationally significant.
- **Judicial elections:** The RSLC has expanded into state supreme court and appellate court races, recognizing that courts adjudicate redistricting and voting challenges.
- **Attorneys general:** Funds AG races as AGs control election litigation and enforcement.
- **REDMAP 2020:** A repeat of the 2010 strategy for the 2020 Census cycle.

## Timeline

- **2002:** RSLC founded.
- **2009:** Gillespie and Jankowski join; REDMAP concept developed.
- **2010:** RSLC spends ~$30 million; Republicans gain ~680 state legislative seats and 20 additional legislative chambers.
- **2011–2012:** Post-Census maps drawn by Republican-controlled legislatures using RSLC/REDMAP advantage.
- **2012:** Republicans retain House 33-seat majority despite losing popular vote by 1.4 million.
- **2017:** Democrats found NDRC as direct counter.
- **2020:** RSLC executes REDMAP 2020 with mixed results; several targeted chambers remain contested.
- **2021–2022:** New maps drawn; RSLC advantage persists in several key states.

## Successor / Related Entities

The RSLC's judicial-elections arm, the **Republican State Leadership Committee Judicial Fairness Initiative**, operates separately. The RSLC's model has been studied and partially replicated by Democratic-aligned organizations including the [[National Democratic Redistricting Committee]].

## Controversies / Investigations

The RSLC's dark-money structure has attracted scrutiny from campaign finance researchers. The organization is not itself illegal, but critics argue its pooled spending for redistricting control amounts to a systematic effort to entrench minority-rule outcomes by insulating legislative majorities from popular-vote shifts.
