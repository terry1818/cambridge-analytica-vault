## Founding and the judicial-pipeline operating model

The [[Federalist Society]] for Law and Public Policy Studies was founded in 1982 by Steven Calabresi, David McIntosh, and Lee Liberman Otis as a network of conservative and libertarian law students. The founding conditions: the law-school environment of the early 1980s was perceived by founders as overwhelmingly liberal; the prospect of substantial Republican executive-branch judicial-nomination capacity was emerging with the Reagan administration; and there was no existing institutional infrastructure that could supply vetted conservative legal candidates for federal court appointments.

The operating model that the Federalist Society has built since 1982 is the most operationally durable institutional infrastructure in the entire vault. It is not a litigation organisation (it does not file briefs or pursue cases). It is not a policy-advocacy organisation (it does not lobby for legislation). It is a credentialing-and-pipeline organisation: it provides the institutional context within which conservative legal thinkers develop, network, build credentials, and become candidates for the federal judiciary.

## Membership and reach

The Federalist Society has approximately 70,000 members across student chapters at 200+ law schools, lawyer chapters in major US cities, and an international footprint. Its annual budget is in the $30-40M range, with funding from the [[Koch Network]], the [[Mercer]] family historically, the [[Bradley Foundation]], the [[Scaife Foundation]], and the broader conservative-legal donor architecture.

Most consequentially, virtually every Republican-appointed federal judge since the early 1990s has had Federalist Society involvement before nomination. The current US Supreme Court includes five Federalist Society-affiliated justices (Roberts, Alito, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, Barrett). The federal circuit courts and district courts include hundreds of FedSoc-affiliated judges. The pipeline effect is real and measurable.

## The Leonard Leo operational role

[[Leonard Leo]] (Federalist Society co-chair, longtime executive vice president before stepping into the broader donor-coordinator role) is the principal operational figure connecting the Federalist Society to the Republican executive-branch judicial-nomination process. Leo's role through the Trump first-term judicial nominations was substantially documented: he was the principal external advisor on judicial selection, and the Trump-administration nominee list reflected Federalist Society pre-vetting.

Leo's post-2020 role expanded beyond the Federalist Society proper. The "Marble Freedom Trust" (a $1.6 billion donation Leo helped structure in 2020-2021) gave Leo control over what is reportedly the largest single conservative-movement donor vehicle in US history. The Marble Freedom Trust funding has flowed to a network of conservative-legal and conservative-movement organisations including the [[Concord Fund]] (formerly Judicial Crisis Network), [[The 85 Fund]] (formerly Judicial Education Project), and the broader conservative-legal infrastructure that complements the Federalist Society proper.

## Why the Federalist Society matters for the broader vault

The vault treats the Federalist Society as one of the three load-bearing institutional pillars of contemporary US conservative power (alongside [[Heritage Foundation]] on policy and [[Council for National Policy]] on coordination). The three pillars are operationally complementary: Heritage produces the policy blueprint; CNP coordinates the donor and leadership network; the Federalist Society supplies the judicial machinery that implements and durably entrenches both.

Without the Federalist Society pipeline, the Republican-administration judicial-nomination capacity would not exist at the scale or quality it does. Without that judicial capacity, the [[Project 2025]] policy programme would face much higher reversal risk from federal-court challenges to its EO and regulatory components. The Federalist Society is the operational answer to the question "how do we make the policy changes stick?"
