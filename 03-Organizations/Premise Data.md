# Premise Data

## Summary

Premise Data Corporation is a San Francisco-based technology company founded in 2012 that operates a globally distributed crowdsourcing platform. The platform pays ordinary people ("contributors") small per-task fees — typically $0.05–$0.10 — via a smartphone app to photograph storefronts, survey prices, count ATMs, map wireless signals, or perform other short ground-level data-collection tasks. Premise publicly presents this as economic and market research intelligence. However, reporting by the Wall Street Journal and subsequent investigation by researcher Jack Poulson (Tech Inquiry) established that Premise held undisclosed contracts with U.S. military special operations forces and intelligence agencies. An internal Premise slide deck described the company as "A Dynamically Re-taskable, Global System for Persistent Ground [Intelligence, Surveillance, and Reconnaissance]." Premise subsequently confirmed the existence of its covert military and intelligence contracts in a court filing, while seeking to shield the details from public disclosure. The company's architecture — civilian crowdworkers performing intelligence-collection tasks without knowing the ultimate client — represents a novel model for outsourcing human intelligence (HUMINT) collection to the commercial sector, with implications both for the contributors' safety and for the legal and ethical frameworks governing intelligence activity.

## Identity

Premise was co-founded in 2012 by David Soloff and Joseph Reisinger, with early positioning as a humanitarian/economic data platform — tracking price indices, food security indicators, and market conditions in developing countries. This framing attracted development-organization clients and made the company appear primarily as an NGO-adjacent research firm. The appointment of Maury Blackman as CEO in 2018 — an executive with U.S. Army Field Artillery Officer background — shifted the company's profile toward government and national security markets. Poulson's Tech Inquiry investigation revealed that the DARPA "More Eyes" crowdsourcing program, developed at "The Taj" (a DARPA facility), was a conceptual precursor to Premise, and that Rajiv Shah — a former USAID Administrator who reportedly requested DARPA initiate More Eyes — later served as a public advisor to Premise. This lineage traces Premise's architecture directly to a U.S. government research program.

## Funding & Money Flows

Premise reached reported revenues of approximately $20 million with a valuation exceeding $400 million (Series E, per media reporting). Specific investor names are not reliably documented in reviewed public sources. Government contract funding is partially documented:
- Since 2017, at least **$5 million** in documented contracts with U.S. military projects including the Air Force and Army (per federal spending records cited by the Wall Street Journal)
- Subcontracts under JSOC (Joint Special Operations Command) primes — Poulson's investigation identified Premise and IST Research as co-subcontractors under the same ongoing JSOC prime contract; the total value of these contracts is not publicly disclosed
- Premise sought to protect specific contract details from public disclosure in litigation, suggesting the government-revenue stream is materially significant and operationally sensitive

## Activities

**Crowdsourced ground-level data collection:** Premise's platform divides intelligence tasks into small, discrete micro-tasks assigned to contributors via the app. Standard commercial tasks include: photographing product prices and availability in retail stores, documenting infrastructure condition, mapping wifi/cellular signal strength, conducting brief surveys about local economic conditions. Contributors are paid per completed task; in developing countries, per-task fees represent meaningful income.

**Military and intelligence tasking:** The Wall Street Journal's reporting and Poulson's subsequent investigation established that some tasks are not economic research but military intelligence collection — routing contributors through specific paths to collect data on wireless signals, photographing specific infrastructure, or gathering information useful to Special Operations planning. Contributors performing these tasks are not informed they are working for military or intelligence clients. Poulson's characterization of this as a "gigwork human intelligence" model — adapting the gig-economy labor model to HUMINT collection — is analytically apt.

**"Dynamically re-taskable" architecture:** Premise's own internal marketing materials used the phrase "Dynamically Re-taskable, Global System for Persistent Ground [Intelligence, Surveillance, and Reconnaissance]." This language, revealed in a leaked slide deck, describes capabilities that go well beyond economic research: the system can be redirected from commercial tasks to surveillance tasks in any geography where Premise has active contributors.

**Safety risks to contributors:** In regions with active conflict or authoritarian governments, contributors unwittingly collecting data for U.S. military clients face serious physical risks if identified by local authorities or adversaries. The Wall Street Journal raised this concern explicitly; Premise disputed the characterization but did not deny the military contracts.

## Controversies / Investigations

**Wall Street Journal Investigation (2021):** The WSJ published a report that described Premise's military ties, the potential risks to contributors in unstable regions, and the gap between the company's public humanitarian positioning and its intelligence-collection activities. The report included an internal slide deck with the "Persistent Ground ISR" language. This was the triggering event for most subsequent scrutiny.

**Tech Inquiry / Jack Poulson Investigation:** Poulson's ongoing Tech Inquiry research traced the institutional lineage from DARPA's More Eyes program to Premise, documented the JSOC subcontracting structure, and identified the Rajiv Shah advisory role. Poulson's Substack reporting includes primary-source procurement database analysis; the research is methodologically careful and relies on verifiable government contract records. Evidence tier: Tier-2 (investigative research drawing on Tier-1 procurement records).

**Court-Filing Disclosure:** Premise's confirmation of its covert military and intelligence contracts in a court filing — while simultaneously seeking protective orders to prevent their public disclosure — is itself significant. It establishes that the contracts exist and are sensitive, without providing details. The litigation context is not fully described in reviewed sources; the specific case is documented at Jack Poulson's Substack (cited below).

**Ethical Issues — Informed Consent and "Unwitting" Collection:** The core ethical controversy is whether contributors meaningfully consent to performing intelligence work on behalf of the U.S. military or intelligence community. Premise's terms of service state that data may be shared with government clients, but critics argue that generic ToS language does not constitute meaningful, specific consent to HUMINT tasking in dangerous operational contexts.
