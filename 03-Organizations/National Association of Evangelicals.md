## Summary

The National Association of Evangelicals (NAE) was founded on April 7, 1942, when Harold John Ockenga addressed 150 delegates at the Hotel Coronado in St. Louis to launch a new evangelical umbrella body. The NAE was conceived as a deliberate alternative to both the militantly separatist fundamentalism of the 1920s–30s and the liberal theological drift of the mainline Federal Council of Churches. It provided the organizational infrastructure through which neo-evangelicalism — theologically conservative but culturally engaged — became a mass movement. [[J. Howard Pew]] was among the key corporate patrons who gave the NAE credibility and resources in its early years, making it a central node in the oil-industry-to-Christian-right patronage network documented by Darren Dochuk and Kevin Kruse.

## Identity

The NAE defines itself as a fellowship of evangelical Protestant churches and organizations committed to conservative biblical theology while rejecting the cultural isolation of fundamentalism. In practice it has served as:
- A credentialing body for evangelical organizations seeking mainstream recognition
- A lobbying presence in Washington representing the evangelical community's policy interests
- A platform for theological and cultural projects including [[Christianity Today]] and, eventually, formal political advocacy

The NAE's founding in 1942 represented the moment evangelical Christianity began deliberately constructing institutional infrastructure parallel to (and in competition with) the mainline Protestant establishment.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Harold Ockenga** (1905–1985): founding president and primary architect; pastor of Park Street Church, Boston; coined the term "neo-evangelicalism."
- **Carl F.H. Henry**: theologian; first editor of [[Christianity Today]]; articulated the intellectual framework of engaged evangelicalism.
- **Billy Graham**: not an NAE official but the most prominent public face of the neo-evangelical movement that NAE incubated.
- **[[J. Howard Pew]]**: corporate patron; provided direct connections between NAE leadership and the libertarian-Christian funding networks of the Foundation for Economic Education.

## Funding & money flows

- Early funding came from evangelical denominations and individual donors within the coalition.
- [[J. Howard Pew]] and other corporate evangelicals provided significant early patronage.
- The NAE has historically been more diffuse in its funding than organizations like [[Spiritual Mobilization]]; it is an umbrella body whose member organizations raise their own funds.
- Evidence tier: Dochuk (2019) and Kruse (2015) document Pew's patronage; comprehensive NAE financial records are not fully public for the early period.

## Activities

- Organized the 1943 constitutional convention in Chicago (1,000+ participants, ~50 denominations, estimated constituency of 15 million Christians).
- Served as the platform from which [[Christianity Today]] was conceived and launched.
- Provided organizational legitimacy for evangelicals' engagement with Cold War politics, opposing communism while resisting the separatist fundamentalists.
- Lobbied for evangelical interests in broadcasting regulation (opposing FCC policies that favored mainline denominations).
- From the 1970s onward, became increasingly aligned with the emerging Religious Right political movement (Moral Majority era and beyond).

## Successor / related entities

- [[Christianity Today]] — the NAE's flagship intellectual publication (indirectly).
- [[Moral Majority]] — the political mobilization arm that emerged from the same evangelical ecosystem.
- [[Christian Coalition]] — Ralph Reed's successor to Moral Majority; NAE members overlapped.
- [[Council for National Policy]] — overlapping elite networks.

## Open questions

- The degree to which NAE formally coordinated with [[Spiritual Mobilization]] versus operating as a parallel institution in the same ideological space has not been fully mapped.
- NAE's internal debates over political engagement (some members resistant to overt political alignment) are an underexplored dimension of its history.
