# Ku Klux Klan

## Summary

The Ku Klux Klan is an American white-supremacist movement, first founded in 1865 in Pulaski, Tennessee, that has existed in three distinct major waves and a long tail of small successor groups. It is best understood not as a single continuous organization but as a recurring movement-brand: a name, a set of symbols (white robes, the burning cross), and an ideology of white Protestant supremacy that different organizations have repeatedly revived. Across all its forms the Klan combined racial terrorism — lynching, arson, assault, intimidation — with a political program of suppressing Black political participation and enforcing a racial and religious hierarchy.

The Klan belongs in this vault as the historical template for religiously framed ethnonationalism in America. Its second wave in the 1920s, in particular, fused white supremacy with militant Protestant nativism — anti-Catholic, anti-Jewish, anti-immigrant — and a self-presentation as the defender of "100% Americanism" and Christian civilization. That fusion of racial hierarchy, Protestant identity, and the claim to be restoring a Christian nation is the direct ancestor of the [[Christian nationalism]] and exclusionary-nationalism currents the vault traces forward. The Klan also pioneered, in its 1920s wave, the use of mass media, fraternal-style recruitment, and political mobilization at scale — making it a historically important case of movement infrastructure and propaganda, not merely night-riding violence.

## Identity

- **Legal name(s):** "Ku Klux Klan" (the name itself derives from the Greek *kyklos*, "circle," plus "clan"); over time dozens of separate corporate entities have used the name.
- **Other names / fronts:** "The Invisible Empire," "Knights of the Ku Klux Klan," and many regional variants.
- **Founded:** 1865, Pulaski, Tennessee.
- **Dissolved / restructured:** No single dissolution; the movement has fractured and revived repeatedly. Numerous small Klan groups remain active and are tracked by civil-rights monitors.
- **Registered jurisdiction:** United States.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Nathan Bedford Forrest** — Confederate general, named first "Grand Wizard" of the first-era Klan (c. 1867).
- **William Joseph Simmons** — revived the Klan atop Stone Mountain, Georgia, in 1915.
- **Hiram Wesley Evans** — Imperial Wizard who led the Klan at its 1920s peak.
- **David Duke** — Louisiana Klan leader of the 1970s who later sought to "mainstream" the movement and ran for office.

## Funding & money flows

The 1920s Klan was, among other things, a financial enterprise. It generated revenue through membership dues, the sale of regalia (robes and hoods), and a multi-level recruitment system in which organizers ("Kleagles") kept a share of every initiation fee — an early pyramid-style funding model. This commercialization helped drive the second wave's explosive growth and also contributed to the corruption scandals that hastened its collapse.

## Activities

**First wave (1865–1871):** Founded as Reconstruction-era racial terrorism in the defeated South, the Klan used violence and intimidation to suppress Black freedmen's political and economic rights and to restore white Democratic control. Federal Enforcement Acts (the "Ku Klux Klan Act" of 1871) and prosecutions largely suppressed it.

**Second wave (1915–1944):** Revived in 1915 — inspired in part by the film "The Birth of a Nation" — the second Klan grew into a national mass movement claiming millions of members at its mid-1920s peak, with strength well beyond the South. It targeted not only Black Americans but Catholics, Jews, and immigrants, and presented itself as the guardian of Protestant "Americanism." It exercised real electoral and political influence in several states before internal scandal and exposure collapsed it.

**Third wave (1950s–1960s):** Revived in violent opposition to the civil-rights movement, this Klan carried out bombings, assassinations, and murders, including the 1963 Birmingham church bombing and the 1964 murders of civil-rights workers in Mississippi.

**Post-1970s tail:** Since the 1970s the Klan has been a fragmented set of small groups; David Duke attempted a media-savvy "mainstreaming" strategy. Klan symbols and personnel overlap with the broader contemporary white-nationalist and neo-Nazi milieu.

## Successor / related entities

- Contemporary fragmented Klan groups — tracked by civil-rights monitors as part of the broader U.S. hate-group landscape.
- The broader white-nationalist and "alt-right" milieu — ideologically continuous with, and partly overlapping in personnel with, the Klan.

## Controversies / investigations

- The 1871 federal Enforcement Act prosecutions.
- The 1920s corruption and sexual-violence scandals (notably D. C. Stephenson) that discredited the second wave.
- FBI investigations of third-wave Klan violence during the civil-rights era ("Mississippi Burning").
