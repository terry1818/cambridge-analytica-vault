# U.S. Term Limits

## Summary

U.S. Term Limits (USTL) is a non-profit, self-described non-partisan organization founded in 1992 with the single goal of enacting term limits for elected officials at every level of U.S. government. In its first decade, it ran state-ballot-initiative campaigns that placed congressional term limits on the delegations of 23 states — all of which were invalidated by the Supreme Court in *U.S. Term Limits v. Thornton*, 514 U.S. 779 (1995). That judicial defeat redirected the organization toward the [[Article V Convention]] mechanism: only a constitutional amendment can limit congressional terms, and only Article V can produce one without Congress's cooperation. USTL's Article V campaign is distinct from [[Convention of States Action]] in that it seeks a *single-subject* convention limited to a congressional term-limits amendment. The organization is one of several independently operating Article V campaigns — alongside the [[Balanced Budget Amendment Task Force]] — that collectively complicate the [[Article V aggregation problem]] by seeking differently-scoped conventions.

## Identity

USTL presents itself as nonpartisan, though its core policy objective — limiting incumbency — aligns structurally with insurgent and anti-establishment politics across the spectrum. In practice, the organization's Article V project, launched formally in 2016 as the "Term Limits Convention," has operated within the broader conservative Article V coalition that includes Heritage Foundation affiliates and ALEC networks, even if USTL explicitly distances itself from the COS single-organization framework.

## Activities

- **State legislative lobbying**: USTL's Article V project deploys model legislation in state legislatures, seeking resolutions calling for a convention limited to term limits. As of the mid-2020s, multiple states had passed such resolutions.
- **Voter pledge campaigns**: USTL has run grassroots pledge campaigns asking voters to support only candidates who commit to term limits.
- **Coalition building with other Article V campaigns**: USTL coordinates with but maintains its single-subject independence from COS and BBATF.
- **Legal/educational advocacy**: Publishes arguments that an Article V convention is the only viable path to congressional term limits.

## Timeline

- **1992**: Founded.
- **Early 1990s**: Ballot initiative campaigns place term limits on 23 state congressional delegations.
- **1995**: *U.S. Term Limits v. Thornton* (SCOTUS) strikes down state-imposed congressional term limits as unconstitutional; organization pivots to Article V strategy.
- **2016**: Formally launches the "Term Limits Convention" project — a dedicated Article V application campaign.
- **2020s**: Multiple state legislatures have passed USTL-backed applications; specific count varies by source and year.

## Controversies / Investigations

1. **Scope creep / aggregation risk**: Critics including Feingold and Prindiville note that USTL's "single-subject" approach does not legally guarantee a convention would stay on-topic. Once delegates assemble, there is no enforceable mechanism to prevent broader deliberation — the [[Runaway Convention]] concern.
2. **Application aggregation**: USTL's applications are worded differently from COS and BBATF applications. This means they cannot be aggregated toward a single convention threshold; each set of applications effectively advances toward a separate convention, or Congress must adjudicate which applications are commensurable. This is a core dimension of the [[Article V aggregation problem]].
3. **Post-*Thornton* strategy shift**: Some critics argue that the Article V convention path USTL adopted after *Thornton* carries risks that dwarf those of the original state-ballot approach.

## Open Questions

- Do USTL-backed term-limits applications count toward the same 34-state threshold as BBA or COS applications, or do they advance a separate convention?
- If Congress were to call a convention based on mixed applications from USTL, BBATF, and COS, would delegates from each set of applications be empowered on different topics?
