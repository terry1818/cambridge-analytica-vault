## Origin and structural innovation

[[The Fellowship]] (also called the Family, the Fellowship Foundation, and the International Foundation) was founded in 1935 by Abraham Vereide as a Christian-businessmen's prayer-and-policy network operating principally in Seattle. Vereide's structural innovation, refined through the 1940s and 1950s, was to organise around small-group prayer cells of legislators and government officials rather than mass-public preaching. The prayer-cell model meant the organisation's reach was concentrated at the leadership level: a small number of carefully cultivated relationships at the top of government were treated as more valuable than a much larger number of relationships at the base.

The Fellowship's operating premise -- articulated principally by Doug Coe, who succeeded Vereide as the dominant leader from the 1960s through his death in 2017 -- is that political authority comes from God, that political leaders are God's agents whether or not they personally identify as Christian, and that the Fellowship's role is to support and pray for those leaders without requiring public Christian affiliation or alignment on specific policy questions. The premise allowed the Fellowship to build relationships across partisan lines, across denominational lines, and across international boundaries in ways that more conventional Christian-political organisations could not.

## The National Prayer Breakfast

The principal public-facing institution the Fellowship produces is the [[National Prayer Breakfast]], held annually in Washington since 1953. The Breakfast is technically a separate event hosted by a bipartisan congressional committee, but the Fellowship has organised and operationally directed it from the start. Presidents from Eisenhower onward have attended. The event's importance is the side meetings: foreign dignitaries, US legislators, business leaders, and movement figures use the Breakfast week as a coordination opportunity that the principal public event provides cover for.

The international dimension of the Breakfast (foreign-government officials attending, often using Fellowship-facilitated side meetings to conduct diplomacy outside formal channels) is one of the principal operational mechanisms by which the Fellowship's reach extends beyond US politics. [[Source NPR Fresh Air Sharlet Uganda]] documents the Uganda-specific aspect of this dynamic; the underlying pattern operates more broadly.

## Documented members and operational figures

The Fellowship's membership is not officially disclosed and individual members have generally not confirmed affiliation publicly. The investigative record (principally [[Sharlet 2008 The Family Book]] and the [[The Family Netflix 2019]] documentary) has documented affiliations including:

- Doug Coe (longtime leader, died 2017)
- Sam Brownback (US Senate, then Kansas governor, then ambassador-at-large for international religious freedom)
- Mike Pence (the most operationally consequential Fellowship-adjacent figure in recent US politics)
- Pat Robertson (founding-era adjacency)
- Numerous House and Senate members across multiple administrations
- International figures including African heads of state across multiple decades

The C Street house in Washington, owned and operated by Fellowship-adjacent entities, has been the documented residence of multiple US legislators across multiple decades. The [[Sharlet 2010 C Street Book]] is the principal investigative reconstruction.

## Operational principle: the off-the-record model

Like [[Council for National Policy]] but to an even greater degree, the Fellowship operates on a no-press-coverage principle. Members typically do not confirm affiliation; meetings are off-the-record; the organisation's financial structure (the Fellowship Foundation as 501(c)(3), with various affiliated entities) is documented in IRS filings but the operational relationships those filings represent are largely opaque from outside.

The off-the-record principle has been substantially eroded by the Sharlet reporting and the Netflix documentary, but the operational model continues. The Fellowship's value to its members is precisely that it is not visible: visibility would constrain the political relationship-building it exists to facilitate.

## Connection to [[Capitol Ministries]]

[[Capitol Ministries]] (founded by [[Ralph Drollinger]]) is the contemporary analogue to the Fellowship's congressional-prayer-cell model, with the difference that Capitol Ministries operates explicitly within Christian-Right policy alignment rather than the Fellowship's pan-ideological model. The two organisations are operationally adjacent but theologically and politically distinct: the Fellowship cultivates relationships across the political spectrum; Capitol Ministries cultivates explicitly Right-aligned Bible study programmes that have produced direct policy outputs in both Trump administrations.

The vault treats the two as complementary rather than competitive: the Fellowship handles the longstanding leadership-network coordination; Capitol Ministries handles the explicit Christian-nationalism-adjacent policy formation within Republican administrations.

## Why the vault treats the Fellowship as a top connector

The Fellowship is the longest-running and most operationally durable religious-political network in the vault. Its 90-year operational history predates and substantially exceeds [[Council for National Policy]] (founded 1981) and [[Heritage Foundation]] (founded 1973). Its relationship-network reaches into US legislative leadership, into foreign-government leadership, and into the broader business-and-policy elite in ways that more visible organisations cannot match.

For the vault's analysis of how the religious-right cluster maintains coherence across multiple decades and multiple political environments, the Fellowship is the load-bearing structural answer.
