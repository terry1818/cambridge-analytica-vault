# Council for National Policy (CNP)

## Summary

**Founded 1981** in a Washington suburb by ~50 Republican operatives, evangelicals, oil barons, and gun lobbyists to **coordinate the conservative coalition's attack on civil liberties and the social safety net**. Co-founded by **[[Tim LaHaye]]** (Christian-right organizer) and **[[Paul Weyrich]]** (also co-founder of [[The Heritage Foundation]] and [[American Legislative Exchange Council|ALEC]]). **Operates in extreme secrecy** â€" members instructed not to reveal their membership or name the group; meets 3x/year at undisclosed locations. **Functional role: the coordinating nerve center** that connects the religious right's manpower, oligarch capital, and Republican-political-operative strategy. **Key incubator of Trump's 2016 campaign** and its administration's policies; **June 2016 CNP meeting** was where Trump secured evangelical-coalition support in exchange for the Evangelical Advisory Board promise.

**Per Anne Nelson's [[Source Anne Nelson Shadow Network 2019|Shadow Network]] (2019):** CNP is **"the secret hub of the radical right"** â€" the most consequential single coordinating institution in US conservative politics.

The June 2016 CNP meeting was the pivot point in Trump's relationship with the religious right. Per multiple sources, **Trump secured CNP backing by promising:**
- An Evangelical Advisory Board featuring CNP members
- CNP-aligned input on Republican party platform

These promises were honored under Trump 1.0 and have continued / expanded under Trump 2.0. **The CNP-Heritage-ALEC nexus is the institutional spine of the conservative coalition.**

## Founding and operating principle

The [[Council for National Policy]] was founded in 1981 by [[Tim LaHaye]] (founder of the Pre-Trib Research Center and author of the Left Behind series) with co-founders including [[Paul Weyrich]] (also a founder of [[Heritage Foundation]] and the [[Free Congress Foundation]]) and Texas oilman T. Cullen Davis. The founding rationale was explicit: to create an off-the-record coordination forum for the leadership of the conservative-Christian and conservative-political movement, away from press scrutiny, where strategy could be aligned across organisations without the corporate-PR-and-501(c)(3)-compliance constraints that direct inter-organisational coordination would face.

The CNP operating principle is the no-press-coverage rule. Membership is by invitation only; the membership list is not officially published; meetings are off-the-record; coverage of CNP events is permitted only with the organisation's specific consent. The principal exception to the no-press rule is the periodic disclosure of membership rosters by investigators and former members ([[Source Anne Nelson Shadow Network 2019]], [[Source SourceWatch CNP]], [[Source SPLC CNP Behind the Curtain]], and various individual investigative pieces).

## How CNP coordination actually works

The operational mechanism is the multi-day off-the-record meeting (typically three to four meetings per year), the affiliated CNP Action (the 501(c)(4) advocacy vehicle that can do explicit electoral work the 501(c)(3) Council cannot), and the standing committee structure that does substantive policy and personnel coordination between meetings. Specific operational outputs documented in the investigative record include: coordinated endorsement strategy for primary elections, coordinated personnel recommendations for Republican administrations, coordinated funding allocation across member-organisation initiatives, and coordinated messaging on legislative-priority issues.

The 2016-cycle CNP coordination was reportedly central to consolidating evangelical-leadership endorsement of Trump (over alternative Republican primary candidates) and to organising the Vice Presidential selection process that produced Mike Pence. The 2024-cycle CNP coordination was similarly central to the [[Project 2025]] launch and to the personnel-pipeline coordination that produced the Trump 2.0 cabinet.

## Funding and resources

The CNP itself operates on a relatively modest budget (the (c)(3) Council is reported in the $3-5M range annually) but the operational reach is much larger because the member organisations contribute their own resources to coordinated initiatives. CNP Action ((c)(4)) operates on a larger budget that varies by cycle.

The funder base is the broader conservative-movement donor architecture: the same families and family offices that fund Heritage, the Federalist Society, the religious-right policy infrastructure, and the broader movement-conservative ecosystem.

## Why CNP is operationally distinctive

Most US-Right institutional infrastructure is organised by issue (Federalist Society on judicial nominations, ADF on religious-liberty litigation, FRC on social-policy advocacy, Heritage on policy formulation). CNP is organised by leadership convergence across all of those issues. It is the meta-coordination layer that lets the various single-issue organisations align without each having to negotiate bilateral relationships with every other organisation.

This is why CNP membership is so often the most predictive variable for understanding how a movement-conservative figure will behave on a specific issue: it indicates which coordination forum they are inside, not just which single-issue organisation they primarily work for.

## Why the vault treats CNP as a top connector

CNP is the highest-degree node in the religious-right and movement-conservative network. Removing it from the graph would force the coordination that currently happens through CNP into bilateral relationships across dozens of organisations, with a substantial loss of efficiency and alignment. The vault treats CNP as the institutional answer to the question "how do all these otherwise-separate organisations stay aligned?"
