# Concord Fund

The Concord Fund (formerly the **Judicial Crisis Network / JCN**) is the **principal 501(c)(4) political-advocacy vehicle** in [[Leonard Leo]]'s dark-money network — the same network whose other components include **[[The 85 Fund]]** (501(c)(3) sister), the **[[Federalist Society]]**, the **[[Honest Elections Project]]**, and the **[[Marble Freedom Trust]]** (the $1.6B Barre Seid funding source that effectively underwrites the entire operation). The Concord Fund is the **direct conduit** through which Leo-network money buys judicial nominations, judicial confirmation ad campaigns, and right-wing legal infrastructure.

## Formation and rebrand

- **Original entity:** Judicial Crisis Network (JCN), founded 2005
- **Rebranded to Concord Fund:** 2020 — coinciding with Leo's strategic shift from a narrow judicial-confirmation focus to a broader political-advocacy posture
- **Why rebrand:** "Judicial Crisis Network" had become a reputational liability after extensive press coverage tied it to specific Supreme Court confirmation campaigns (Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, Barrett). The Concord Fund name allowed the entity to broaden its public-facing political work without dragging the JCN baggage.

## Financial scale and Marble Freedom flow

The Concord Fund received **$55,500,000 from the [[Marble Freedom Trust]]** — the single largest known transfer from Leo's $1.6B Seid bequest to a downstream advocacy entity. This is the financial spine of the network's political operations.

The flow is roughly:

```
Barre Seid bequest ($1.6B, 2021)
  ↓
Marble Freedom Trust (Leo-controlled holding entity)
  ↓
The 85 Fund (501(c)(3), $134M distributed in 2022) ───┐
                                                      ↓
Concord Fund (501(c)(4)) ←────────────────────────────┘
  ↓
Specific political advocacy: judicial confirmations, ad campaigns,
state-level litigation funding, Project 2025 coalition members
```

## 2025 restructuring (the disappearing-name pattern)

In **December 2025**, the Concord Fund was **dissolved** — a notable choice given its scale and operational tempo. Days before dissolution, another Leo-linked nonprofit, **The Lexington Fund**, registered several alternative names including "Judicial Crisis Network" and "Honest Election Project Action." A new Tennessee-based nonprofit, **The Yorktown Fund**, also surfaced in December.

This is a recurring pattern in the Leo network: a high-profile entity gets retired and its operational functions are redistributed across newly-named successor vehicles, complicating press coverage, donor tracing, and legal challenges. The same pattern previously moved JCN → Concord Fund; now Concord Fund → Lexington Fund / Yorktown Fund.

The Maine super PAC lawsuit (April 2026) is the first major legal action attempting to pierce this restructuring — see [[Source Maine Morning Star 2026-04-27 Leonard Leo dark money]].

## Operational use

The Concord Fund has been the principal funder of:

- **Supreme Court confirmation ad campaigns** for Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, Barrett (originally as JCN)
- **State-level judicial races** in swing states
- **[[Project 2025]] coalition** orgs (multiple grants disclosed in 990s)
- **[[Honest Elections Project]]** voter-suppression litigation
- **Stop the Vote** and related election-administration challenges in 2020–2024
- **Anti-DEI and anti-affirmative-action litigation** post-SFFA v Harvard

## Why this matters

The Concord Fund is the operational answer to "who paid for the Trump-era judicial transformation." Three SCOTUS seats (Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, Barrett) and ~230 federal judicial confirmations were not organic political outcomes — they were the product of a sustained, well-funded campaign run through this entity (under both its names). The same machine is now being repurposed for state-level judicial races and election-administration battles in 2026.

For the [[Political App prototype]]'s defensive purpose, Concord Fund / Lexington Fund / Yorktown Fund is the single most important dark-money node to surveil: if a state-level judicial race or election administration challenge has unusual ad spending, this is statistically the first place to look.
