## Detailed operational mechanics

Convention of States Action (COSA, the 501(c)(4) advocacy and electoral arm) operates alongside [[Citizens for Self-Governance]] (CSG, the 501(c)(3) charitable parent) as the two-vehicle structure that allows the broader COS movement to do both charitable-educational work and explicit political-and-lobbying work without violating IRS rules separately governing each entity type.

COSA's operational model: state-by-state legislative-organising, model-legislation drafting (the Article V state-application resolution text that 28+ states have now adopted), volunteer-cell organising at the state-legislative-district level, lobbying-disclosure-compliant in-state advocacy operations, and coordinated electoral support for state legislators who vote for the Article V application.

The state-application strategy has reached 28 of the required 34 states (counts vary slightly depending on which legislative-resolution version is considered to qualify). The remaining 6+ states needed are principally in the Northeast and the Pacific Northwest, where state legislatures have been less receptive to the COS legal theory.

## The Meckler-Farris leadership in detail

[[Mark Meckler]] (CSG / COSA president since 2010) and [[Michael Farris]] (COSA chair, longtime homeschool-legal-defense and Alliance Defending Freedom leadership) are the principal operational figures. Meckler's prior career included co-founding the Tea Party Patriots in 2009; his transition from Tea-Party-era organising to COS-era organising represents the operational continuity of conservative-grassroots organising infrastructure across the past 15 years.

Farris's role brings the legal-pipeline and religious-right adjacencies that COSA operationally requires: Federalist Society-adjacent legal credibility for the Article V state-application strategy, [[Alliance Defending Freedom]] / religious-right movement endorsement, and the broader [[Council for National Policy]] coordination layer that connects COSA to the rest of the conservative-movement infrastructure.

## Funding structure

COSA / CSG funding has been substantially documented in IRS 990 filings. Major funders include the [[Mercer Family Foundation]] (documented annual giving), the [[DeVos Family Network]] (substantial documented giving), [[Heritage Foundation]] (via institutional partnership and personnel sharing rather than direct grants), and a broader base of mid-tier conservative-movement donors. The annual operating budget has been in the $5-10M range across recent years.

The funding pattern (concentrated large-donor base, substantial overlap with the broader conservative-movement donor architecture) is the standard model for the movement-conservative organising infrastructure.
