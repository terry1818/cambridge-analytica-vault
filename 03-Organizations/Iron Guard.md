## Summary

The Legion of the Archangel Michael, commonly known outside Romania as the Iron Guard, was founded in 1923 by Corneliu Zelea Codreanu as a radical nationalist, antisemitic, and Orthodox Christian mystical movement. It became arguably the third-largest fascist organization in interwar Europe, after the NSDAP and Italian Fascists. It is unique among interwar fascist movements for the centrality of Eastern Orthodox Christian mysticism to its ideology — including a cult of martyrdom, a veneration of death, and the belief that political struggle had a sacred character — which distinguished it from the secular and paganizing tendencies of German National Socialism and Italian Fascism.

**Vault relevance — comparative case:** Robert Paxton in *The Anatomy of Fascism* (2004) uses the Iron Guard as one of several non-German, non-Italian cases to argue that fascism was not a uniquely German or Italian phenomenon but a broader political pathology with variable national-cultural inflections. The Guard illustrates how fascism's emotional core — national rebirth through purifying violence, leader worship, hostility to liberal democracy and Marxism alike — could be grafted onto Orthodox Christianity rather than secular nationalism, raising questions about the relationship between religious radicalism and fascism that are directly relevant to the vault's interest in the US theocratic right.

## Identity

The movement combined:
- **Ultranationalist Romanian ethnic identity** with virulent antisemitism (Jews depicted as corrupting the Romanian nation)
- **Orthodox Christian mysticism**: martyrdom, communion with the dead, ritual self-sacrifice as patriotic duty; Codreanu described the Legion as "a school and an army"
- **Anti-communism and anti-liberalism**: rejection of parliamentary democracy and Marxism as twin corruptions
- **Paramilitary street violence**: assassinations of political opponents including Prime Minister Ion Duca (1933)
- **Mass mobilization**: work camps, marches, hymns; emotional and ritual politics of the kind Paxton identifies as fascism's distinctive mobilizing mode

## Leadership and key personnel

- **Corneliu Zelea Codreanu** (1899–1938): founder and "Captain"; charismatic movement leader; arrested and strangled in prison on orders of King Carol II, November 1938
- **Horia Sima** (1907–1993): took leadership after Codreanu's death; led the movement's brief period in power (National Legionary State, September–January 1940–41)
- **Ion Moța and Vasile Marin**: senior ideologues; volunteered to fight for Franco in Spain; both killed at the Battle of Majadahonda (January 1937); their funerals became major movement spectacles

## Funding and money flows

- Primarily domestic Romanian sources: contributions from sympathetic businessmen, landowners, and middle-class members; work camps were partly self-funding through labor.
- Some contact with Italian Fascist intelligence (OVRA) documented; extent of foreign funding is not fully established in the historical record.

## Activities

- Political violence: assassinations of political opponents (PM Duca, 1933; others)
- Electoral participation: All for the Fatherland party won 15.58% in 1937 elections, making it the third-largest party in Romania
- Work camps: legionaries performed communal labor as a form of national-spiritual service
- Youth and women's organizations
- Brief period in government: National Legionary State (September 1940 – January 1941) in coalition with General Ion Antonescu; ended when Antonescu suppressed the Legionary Rebellion (January 1941)

## Successor and related entities

- Legionary diaspora organizations persisted in exile (Spain, Germany, the Americas) through the Cold War
- Noua Dreaptă (New Right) and other post-communist Romanian far-right organizations have claimed Legionary heritage — these are distinct entities

## Controversies and investigations

- Responsibility for multiple political assassinations (documented, Tier 1)
- Participation in anti-Jewish pogroms, including the Bucharest pogrom of January 1941 (documented, Tier 1)
- Ideological relationship to contemporary Christian nationalism: the guard's synthesis of Orthodox mysticism and fascist politics is studied as a historical precursor to religiously inflected authoritarianism

## Open questions

- Extent of Italian and German financial and organizational support (partially documented, partially disputed)
- Whether the Iron Guard is best classified as "clerical fascism" (a subtype) or a distinctive variant; ongoing historiographical debate
- The relevance of the Legionary model to contemporary far-right movements that blend Christian nationalism with authoritarian politics
