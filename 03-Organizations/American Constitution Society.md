# American Constitution Society

## Summary

The American Constitution Society for Law and Policy (ACS) is a progressive legal organization founded in 2001, headquartered in Washington, D.C. It was created explicitly as a structural counterpart to the [[Federalist Society]] — mirroring its model of law student chapters, lawyer networks, judicial nomination advocacy, and ideological pipeline-building, but from a progressive constitutional perspective. ACS came to prominence in the Article V debate primarily through the presidency of [[Russ Feingold]], who led the organization from March 2020 to early 2025 and co-authored *The Constitution in Jeopardy* (2022) while serving in that role. The book — which this vault cites as [[Source - Feingold 2022 - The Constitution in Jeopardy]] — was explicitly produced within the intellectual and institutional context of ACS's mission to counter right-wing constitutional engineering.

## Identity

ACS describes its mission as supporting "laws and legal systems that redress the founding failures of the Constitution, strengthen democratic legitimacy, uphold the rule of law, and realize the promise of equality for all." It operates through a nationwide network of law student chapters, lawyer chapters, and an associated roster of judges, scholars, and advocates. It hosts conferences, sponsors research, and engages in judicial nominations advocacy.

ACS was founded in response to the Supreme Court's *Bush v. Gore* (2000) decision by Peter J. Rubin, who had served as counsel to Al Gore in that litigation. Its founding represented a strategic recognition by progressive legal professionals that the Federalist Society's decades-long pipeline had decisively shifted the federal judiciary rightward without equivalent liberal infrastructure.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Peter J. Rubin**: Founding figure; counsel to Al Gore in *Bush v. Gore*.
- **Russ Feingold**: President, March 2020 – early 2025. Former U.S. Senator (Wisconsin, 1993–2011). Under his leadership, ACS sharpened its focus on Article V convention risks, culminating in the 2022 book. Announced step-down in late 2024/early 2025.
- Post-Feingold leadership: Not documented in sources consulted at Tier-1.

## Funding & Money Flows

ACS receives funding from progressive foundations and individual donors. Specific funding details are not well-documented in the sources consulted for this note. Unlike the [[Federalist Society]], which benefits from Koch Network, Scaife Foundation, and aligned dark-money infrastructure, ACS has operated on a comparatively smaller budget — a disparity frequently cited in assessments of the progressive-conservative asymmetry in legal infrastructure.

## Activities

- **Law student chapters**: Maintains chapters at law schools across the country, introducing law students to progressive constitutional theory.
- **Lawyer chapters**: Networks of practicing attorneys.
- **Issue advocacy**: Judicial nominations, voting rights, executive power, civil liberties.
- **Article V convention opposition**: Under Feingold's leadership, ACS became a prominent institutional voice against the COS/BBA convention campaign. The 2022 book was the flagship output; ACS also hosted related events, publications, and podcasts.
- **Annual conventions**: National gathering of progressive legal professionals.

## Timeline

- **2001**: Founded by Peter J. Rubin following *Bush v. Gore*.
- **2000s–2010s**: Grew steadily as a Federalist Society counterpart; remained smaller and less funded.
- **March 2020**: Russ Feingold appointed President.
- **2022**: *The Constitution in Jeopardy* (Feingold and Peter Prindiville) published; ACS becomes central institutional home for Article V opposition scholarship.
- **Early 2025**: Feingold announces step-down; institutional future under new leadership not yet documented.

## Controversies / Investigations

No significant controversies documented. ACS is periodically criticized by conservative legal commentators as a partisan counterpart to a Federalist Society they consider to be academically neutral — a claim ACS disputes. The asymmetry in funding and influence between ACS and the Federalist Society is itself a subject of ongoing progressive legal advocacy.
