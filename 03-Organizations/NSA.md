# NSA

## Summary

The National Security Agency is the United States' signals-intelligence (SIGINT) and communications-security organization, a component of the [[DOD|Department of Defense]] headquartered at Fort Meade, Maryland. It intercepts, processes, and analyzes electronic communications worldwide and operates the technical infrastructure on which much of U.S. foreign intelligence depends. For this vault the NSA is the technical heart of the surveillance state: it runs the [[Section 702]] collection that sweeps in Americans' communications "incidentally," and in January 2024 documents released by Senator [[Ron Wyden]] confirmed that the agency also *buys* Americans' internet-browsing and netflow records from commercial [[data broker|data brokers]] without a warrant. The NSA's capabilities — global reach, bulk collection, deep packet visibility — are the baseline against which the vault measures how much surveillance power exists and how readily it can be repointed at domestic political life.

## Identity

- **Legal name(s):** National Security Agency; jointly the National Security Agency/Central Security Service (NSA/CSS).
- **Other names / fronts:** historically nicknamed "No Such Agency" for its secrecy.
- **Founded:** November 4, 1952, by a classified directive from President Truman; succeeded the Armed Forces Security Agency.
- **Parent / owner:** [[DOD|U.S. Department of Defense]]; the Director also commands U.S. Cyber Command (a dual-hat arrangement).
- **Registered jurisdiction:** United States; headquartered at Fort George G. Meade, Maryland.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **General Keith Alexander** — Director 2005–2014; led the agency during the period exposed by the 2013 Snowden disclosures.
- **General Timothy Haugh** — Director from 2024; removed in 2025 amid a politically contested shake-up of intelligence leadership.
- The NSA Director is, by tradition, a serving military officer who simultaneously commands U.S. Cyber Command.

## Funding & money flows

The NSA is funded through the classified Military Intelligence Program and National Intelligence Program budgets; its specific budget is not public. Relevant to this vault, Wyden's 2024 disclosure documented that the NSA spends appropriated funds purchasing Americans' internet records from data brokers — a money flow into the commercial-surveillance market that operates outside the warrant framework.

## Activities

**Signals intelligence and Section 702 collection.** The NSA's core mission is intercepting foreign communications. Under [[Section 702]] of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act it compels U.S. communications providers to assist in collecting the communications of non-U.S. persons abroad. Because targets communicate with people inside the United States, this collection inevitably captures Americans' phone calls, texts, and emails "incidentally." The [[FBI]], [[CIA]], NSA, and National Counterterrorism Center then conduct thousands of warrantless "backdoor searches" of this database each year for Americans' communications.

**Buying Americans' internet records.** In January 2024, after a nearly three-year fight, Senator Wyden released documents confirming the NSA purchases Americans' internet metadata — netflow data and browsing records that reveal which websites people visit and which apps they use, including visits to mental-health, abortion, domestic-violence, and telehealth resources. The data covers wholly domestic communications and mixed domestic-foreign communications. Wyden argued the purchases were unlawful because a December 2023 FTC order held that brokers must obtain informed consent before selling such data — consent the data in question lacked.

**Bulk collection history.** The 2013 Edward Snowden disclosures revealed NSA programs including bulk telephone-metadata collection of Americans' call records under a contested reading of the PATRIOT Act, the PRISM program drawing on major internet companies, and upstream collection from internet backbone cables. The metadata program was curtailed by the 2015 USA FREEDOM Act, but the broader architecture and Section 702 collection persist.

## Successor / related entities

- [[DOD|U.S. Department of Defense]] — parent department.
- [[CIA]] — sister foreign-intelligence agency; both query Section 702 data.
- [[FBI]] — runs the largest share of warrantless backdoor searches of NSA-collected Section 702 data.
- Central Security Service — the NSA's military-cryptologic partner element.
- U.S. Cyber Command — co-located and dual-hatted with the NSA Director.

## Controversies / investigations

- **Project SHAMROCK / MINARET** — Cold War programs intercepting Americans' communications, exposed by the Church Committee.
- **Snowden disclosures (2013)** — revealed bulk metadata collection, PRISM, upstream collection.
- **Section 702 backdoor searches** — ongoing controversy over warrantless access to Americans' communications.
- **Data-broker purchases** — the 2024 Wyden disclosure; contested as an unlawful end-run around the warrant requirement.
