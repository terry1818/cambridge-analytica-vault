# CBP

## Summary

U.S. Customs and Border Protection is the border-security component of the [[DHS|Department of Homeland Security]] and the largest federal law-enforcement agency in the United States by personnel. Created in 2003 by merging legacy customs, immigration-inspection, and Border Patrol functions, CBP controls entry at land borders, airports, and seaports and patrols the borderlands. For this vault CBP matters because it operates the country's densest concentration of surveillance technology: a facial-recognition biometric entry/exit system, a growing network of AI-driven autonomous surveillance towers, and a documented record of buying commercial cell-phone location data from [[data broker|data brokers]]. The legal doctrine that the Fourth Amendment is weaker within 100 miles of the border — a zone containing roughly two-thirds of the U.S. population — makes CBP the agency where new surveillance capabilities are normalized before diffusing into domestic policing. It is a frontline node in the surveillance-state and democratic-backsliding threads.

## Identity

- **Legal name(s):** U.S. Customs and Border Protection.
- **Other names / fronts:** none; commonly "CBP."
- **Founded:** March 1, 2003, on the creation of DHS; consolidated the U.S. Customs Service, the inspection functions of the former Immigration and Naturalization Service, the Border Patrol, and parts of the Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service.
- **Parent / owner:** [[DHS|U.S. Department of Homeland Security]].
- **Registered jurisdiction:** United States; headquartered in Washington, D.C.
- **Subsidiaries:** U.S. Border Patrol, Office of Field Operations, Air and Marine Operations.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Robert Bonner** — first CBP Commissioner, 2003–2005.
- CBP is led by a Commissioner who reports to the Secretary of Homeland Security; the U.S. Border Patrol Chief leads the agency's largest uniformed division.

## Funding & money flows

CBP's appropriations exceed $20 billion annually. Vault-relevant procurement includes Venntel contracts in 2019–2020 totaling over $2 million for cell-phone location data; the purchase and installation of Autonomous Surveillance Towers from Anduril Industries — the defense firm founded by Trump donor Palmer Luckey — with a multi-billion-dollar set-aside ($2.8 billion) reported for border surveillance towers and sensors; and a one-year base contract with Clearview AI for facial-recognition access to a database of more than 60 billion publicly scraped images.

## Activities

**Facial-recognition entry/exit.** CBP runs a biometric entry/exit program that photographs travelers and matches faces against government galleries. A DHS final rule has expanded this to authorize facial-biometric collection from essentially all foreign nationals — including Canadian citizens — at airports, land borders, and seaports, with rollout to all major airports and seaports planned over three to five years. CBP has also published documentation toward extending facial recognition to vehicle occupants at land crossings.

**Surveillance towers.** CBP operates a border-surveillance-tower program mapped by the EFF at 577 towers as of September 2025. It is installing roughly 200 Anduril Autonomous Surveillance Towers controlled by AI software capable of automatically detecting and tracking objects up to 7.5 miles away and classifying objects at 3 miles. This persistent, AI-driven monitoring covers populated borderland communities, not only the line itself.

**Buying location data.** CBP, like sister component [[ICE]], purchased access to bulk cell-phone location data from [[data broker|data brokers]] including Venntel — data harvested from ordinary apps that enables tracking of individuals' movements over time. The FTC ruled against Venntel/Gravy Analytics in December 2024.

**Clearview AI.** CBP began piloting Clearview AI's facial-recognition technology in 2025, gaining access to a database of 60+ billion images scraped from the public internet — applied, per CBP, to "tactical targeting" and "counter-network analysis."

## Controversies / investigations

- **Border facial-recognition expansion** — civil-liberties objection that universal traveler biometrics build a permanent face database; DHS analysis found lower match rates for Black travelers.
- **Surveillance towers over communities** — EFF and residents object that AI towers persistently monitor populated borderland towns.
- **Location-data purchases** — CBP's Venntel buys, like ICE's, exploited the data-broker loophole; the contractor was later sanctioned by the FTC.
