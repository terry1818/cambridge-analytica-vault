# Business Roundtable

## Summary

The Business Roundtable (BRT) is a nonprofit lobbying association headquartered in Washington, D.C., whose members are the chief executive officers of America's largest corporations. Founded in 1972 through the merger of three predecessor corporate groups, the BRT was a direct organizational response to the [[Lewis Powell Memo]]'s call for more aggressive corporate political engagement. Unlike trade associations that lobby on behalf of industries, the BRT was designed from the outset for CEO-level peer lobbying — having chief executives personally meet with legislators and executive branch officials, trading on their economic credibility and the implicit threat of investment decisions. It has been among the most consequential business lobbying organizations in US history, shaping tax, labor, trade, and regulatory policy across Democratic and Republican administrations alike.

## Identity

The BRT differs from think tanks in the patron-class network in that it is an explicitly political lobbying organization rather than an ideological education body. Its power derives from member CEOs' ability to speak credibly about investment, hiring, and economic consequences of legislation. At peak influence in the late 1970s and 1980s, the BRT is credited with defeating labor law reform, shaping the 1981 Reagan tax cuts, and negotiating the terms of multiple trade agreements. Its 2019 "Statement on the Purpose of a Corporation," which replaced a 1997 declaration of shareholder primacy with stakeholder capitalism language, was widely noted as a significant rhetorical shift — though critics argued it produced few concrete policy changes.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **John D. Harper** (Alcoa chairman): Co-founder; served as BRT chairman 1973–1976
- **Fred Borch** (GE CEO): Co-founder and initial co-chairman; GE's presence linked the BRT directly to the [[Boulwarism]] tradition of corporate ideological mobilization
- **Roger Blough** (US Steel CEO): Co-founder; his Construction Users Anti-Inflation Roundtable was one of three predecessor bodies
- BRT is led by a President/CEO drawn from business or government (not a sitting corporate executive)

## Funding & Money Flows

Funded entirely by member corporation dues. Membership is restricted to CEOs of major corporations; in the 2020s, members collectively employ over 19 million people and generate approximately $9 trillion in annual revenues. Dues and organizational finances are not publicly disclosed in detail.

## Activities

- Direct CEO lobbying of Congress and the executive branch
- Development of detailed policy positions on tax, trade, labor, and regulation
- Publication of policy reports and business-climate assessments
- Coordinated corporate responses to major legislation (the BRT was instrumental in the Reagan tax cut of 1981 and in defeating labor law reform in 1978)
- From 2019 onward: stakeholder capitalism rebranding

## Successor / Related Entities

The BRT sits at the apex of a tiered corporate political infrastructure. Below it are the US Chamber of Commerce (broader membership, more aggressive on electoral politics), industry trade associations (sector-specific), and the American Legislative Exchange Council ([[ALEC]], focused on state-level legislation). The BRT is not formally connected to the [[Koch Network]] or [[Heritage Foundation]], but all three operate in the same patron-class ecosystem and on overlapping policy objectives.

## Controversies / Investigations

- The BRT's 2019 stakeholder capitalism statement was criticized by shareholder-primacy advocates (e.g., the Business Roundtable's own prior 1997 position) and by progressives who argued it was purely rhetorical with no enforcement mechanism.
- The BRT's role in blocking labor law reform in 1978 is documented by scholars as a pivotal defeat for the American labor movement.
- The BRT has never been the subject of a formal government investigation.

## Open Questions

- How tightly was the BRT's founding coordinated with the Lewis Powell Memo's circulation? The timing (1971 memo, 1972 founding) is suggestive but causal documentation is incomplete.
- To what extent do BRT policy positions coordinate with the Koch Network versus operate independently?
