## Summary

The Thomas Jefferson Center for Studies in Political Economy and Social Philosophy was the founding institutional home of what became the Virginia School of political economy — the intellectual tradition that produced public choice theory and, ultimately, a body of constitutional economics that Nancy MacLean argues (controversially) underpins the modern right's [[Constitutional entrenchment strategy]]. Established in 1957 by James M. Buchanan and Warren Nutter at the University of Virginia, the Center operated for roughly a decade before political and administrative tensions led to Buchanan's departure. Its dissolution and Buchanan's move to Virginia Tech in 1969 directly occasioned the founding of the [[Center for Study of Public Choice]] — the institutional vehicle that eventually migrated to George Mason University in 1983.

## Identity

Buchanan arrived at UVA in 1956 as a professor and department chair; Nutter joined as associate professor in 1957. Together they shared what they described as dissatisfaction with the increasingly formal and technical direction of academic economics, and a desire to return to classical political economy — David Hume, Adam Smith, and the tradition of analyzing social order through the lens of individual liberty. The Center was established in early 1957 with support from the UVA administration as an interdisciplinary home for scholars working in that tradition.

The full name — "for Studies in Political Economy and Social Philosophy" — signals the Center's ambition to integrate economics with political philosophy, foreshadowing Buchanan's later constitutional political economy work that would win him the Nobel Prize in 1986.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **James M. Buchanan**: recruited to UVA 1956; became intellectual engine of the Center; departure from UVA 1968 precipitated by administrative conflicts over the Center's direction
- **G. Warren Nutter**: co-founder; shared Buchanan's classical liberal orientation; later became Assistant Secretary of Defense for International Security Affairs (Nixon administration)
- **Ronald Coase**: associated with the UVA milieu; later won Nobel Prize in Economics 1991; brought transaction-cost analysis that complemented Buchanan's public choice work
- **Rutledge Vining**: UVA faculty member whose dissatisfaction with formalist economics paralleled Buchanan and Nutter's

## Activities

The Center functioned primarily as a scholarly community and discussion forum — hosting seminars, supporting graduate students, and producing working papers — rather than as a conventional grant-making or advocacy organization. Its intellectual output during this period included early development of the ideas that became *The Calculus of Consent* (Buchanan and Tullock, 1962), the foundational text of public choice theory.

The Center also made a point of recruiting visiting scholars with overlapping classical liberal orientations, building what was effectively the first node in a network that would expand through Virginia Tech, George Mason, and eventually the broader Koch-funded academic infrastructure.

## Controversies / Investigations

MacLean's *Democracy in Chains* argues that the Ford Foundation's refusal to fund the Thomas Jefferson Center, and the UVA administration's eventual withdrawal of support, reflect establishment resistance to Buchanan's ideological program. Critics of MacLean counter that the tensions were about academic standards and that her narrative overstates ideological conspiracy. The documented fact of the Center's closure and Buchanan's departure are uncontested; the precise causes and their ideological significance remain historiographically disputed. **Evidence tier**: institutional facts Tier-1; MacLean's causal interpretation Tier-3.
