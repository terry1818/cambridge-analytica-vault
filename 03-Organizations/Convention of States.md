# Convention of States

## Summary

Convention of States — formally **Convention of States Action**, a project of the nonprofit Citizens for Self-Governance — is a national campaign founded in 2013 to invoke Article V of the U.S. Constitution. Article V permits two-thirds of state legislatures (34 states) to compel a "convention for proposing amendments" without the consent of Congress or the President; any amendments produced would then require ratification by 38 states. Convention of States seeks to call such a convention restricted to three subjects: imposing fiscal restraints on the federal government, limiting its "power and jurisdiction," and imposing term limits on federal officials and members of Congress.

The organization is led by **[[Mark Meckler]]**, a former Tea Party Patriots co-founder, and is the most organizationally advanced of the modern Article V movements. It claims roughly five million supporters, and by the mid-2020s 19 states had passed its convention resolution (the count varies by year as resolutions are passed and rescinded). Convention of States matters to this vault because it is the most concrete present-day vehicle of [[constitutional capture]]: a successful convention would lock fiscal and jurisdictional limits into the constitutional text itself, beyond the reach of any future Congress, president, or electoral majority. Its funding ties — documented reporting links it to [[Donors Trust]], the Mercer family, the [[Koch Network]], and the [[American Legislative Exchange Council]] (ALEC) — place it squarely in the patron-wing ecosystem. Critics including Common Cause warn that a convention's scope cannot be reliably limited once convened (the "runaway convention" risk), an interpretive dispute the movement rejects.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **[[Mark Meckler]]** — President of Convention of States Action; co-founder; former co-founder of Tea Party Patriots; constitutional activist.
- **Eric O'Keefe** — co-founder of parent organization Citizens for Self-Governance; long-time conservative donor-network operative.
- **Michael Farris** — early COS leader and chief counsel; constitutional lawyer and founder of the Home School Legal Defense Association; later head of Alliance Defending Freedom.

## Funding & money flows

- **[[Donors Trust]]** — donor-advised fund (the so-called "dark-money ATM" of the right) identified as a significant contributor.
- **Mercer family** — reporting documents at least roughly $500,000 from Mercer-linked sources (PR Watch / Bill Moyers).
- **[[Koch Network]]** — reporting indicates the parent organization received well over $10 million from Koch-linked groups and allied conservative donors across the 2010s.
- **[[American Legislative Exchange Council]]** — ALEC has promoted Article V at its conferences, published a "how-to" handbook, and circulated model resolution language; Common Cause has called ALEC the most influential institutional promoter of an Article V convention.

(Funder figures are drawn from investigative reporting and tax filings; the movement disputes characterizations of itself as donor-driven, emphasizing small-dollar grassroots support.)

## Activities

Convention of States runs a state-by-state legislative campaign to pass identically worded resolutions calling for an amendments convention on its three subjects. It maintains volunteer "district captains," lobbies state legislators, runs simulated conventions to model procedure, and produces media and educational content. It is a 501(c)(4) advocacy organization paired with educational and legal affiliates. Its strategic premise is that federal overreach cannot be corrected from within Washington, so the states must amend the Constitution directly.

## Timeline

- `2012` — Mark Meckler and Eric O'Keefe found [[Citizens for Self-Governance]]
- `2013` — Convention of States Project launched as a CSG initiative
- `2014` — Georgia, Alaska, and Florida become early states to pass the COS resolution
- `2015–2017` — ALEC promotes Article V workshops; resolution spreads to additional states; budget grows sharply
- `2017` — PR Watch / Bill Moyers reporting documents Koch- and Mercer-linked funding
- `2021` — Mark Meckler briefly serves as interim CEO of Parler; HuffPost profile frames COS as "dangerously close" to success
- `2022–2025` — Resolution count reaches ~19 states; some states rescind earlier calls; movement continues lobbying toward the 34-state threshold
- `2026` — Campaign ongoing; no convention yet triggered

## Successor / related entities

- **[[Citizens for Self-Governance]]** — parent organization.
- **[[American Legislative Exchange Council]]** — institutional ally promoting Article V model legislation.
- **Balanced Budget Amendment Task Force** — separate but overlapping Article V effort focused narrowly on a balanced-budget amendment.
- **[[Constitution Party]]** — ideologically adjacent project (Howard Phillips's party) committed to constitutional restoration, though organizationally distinct.

## Controversies / investigations

- **"Runaway convention" risk** — Common Cause, the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, and constitutional scholars argue an Article V convention's agenda cannot be legally constrained once convened, raising the prospect of amendments far beyond the stated three subjects. Convention of States rejects this, citing state-level enabling laws and delegate instructions. `#claim/disputed`
- **Donor transparency** — investigative outlets (PR Watch, Bill Moyers, National Memo) characterize COS as a billionaire-backed project; the movement insists it is grassroots-funded.
- **Underlying policy aims** — critics argue the fiscal-restraint framing masks hostility to Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, and federal regulation; supporters frame it as restoring constitutional limits.
