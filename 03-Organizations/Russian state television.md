# Russian State Television — The Channel One / Rossiya / NTV Apparatus

## Summary

Russian state television refers collectively to the three dominant broadcast networks that together reach effectively the entire adult population of Russia: Channel One (Pervyy Kanal), Rossiya 1 and Rossiya 24 (operated by the state holding VGTRK), and NTV (controlled by Gazprom-Media, a subsidiary of state energy giant Gazprom). All three were brought under effective Kremlin editorial control between 2000 and 2002, shortly after [[Vladimir Putin]] assumed the presidency. Peter Pomerantsev's *Nothing Is True and Everything Is Possible* — written from his experience as a British producer working inside Russian commercial television — provides the most vivid account of how the system operates: not through crude Soviet-style censorship but through a more sophisticated management of reality in which truthfulness is optional and emotional manipulation is the primary currency.

## Identity

Post-Soviet Russian television began with a brief period of genuine pluralism (roughly 1991–2000). By 2002, all three major networks had been brought under state or state-aligned control through a combination of regulatory pressure, criminal prosecution of owners, and forced asset sales. NTV — founded in 1993 with financing from Vladimir Gusinsky as an independent network — was the most dramatic case: within days of Putin's inauguration in May 2000, raids began at Gusinsky's Media-Most holding; he was arrested; and within months NTV was transferred to Gazprom-Media under duress. Channel One (a successor to Soviet-era Ostankino) was already majority state-owned. Rossiya (VGTRK) was always state-owned. By 2003 the transformation was complete: all major broadcast channels were editorially responsive to the Kremlin.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Konstantin Ernst**: Director-General of Channel One since 1999; widely regarded as one of Russia's most capable television executives; credited with creating the sophisticated entertainment and news formats that made Kremlin propaganda palatable.
- **Oleg Dobrodeyev**: Director of VGTRK (Rossiya holding); a veteran television executive who ran NTV before its transfer and subsequently moved to the state side.
- **Dmitry Kiselev**: Host of the flagship political talk show *Vesti Nedeli* (News of the Week) on Rossiya 1; became internationally known for his extreme pro-war commentary after 2022, including threats of nuclear weapons use; head of state news agency Rossiya Segodnya.
- **Margarita Simonyan**: Editor-in-chief of RT (Russia Today) and Rossiya Segodnya; while RT is technically separate, it is part of the same state-media apparatus with shared editorial lines.

## Funding & Money Flows

Channel One receives direct state budget allocations and advertising revenue. VGTRK is fully state-funded. NTV's parent Gazprom-Media is commercially structured but state-controlled through Gazprom's majority state ownership. The operational budgets are not publicly disaggregated, but the system is understood to be generously resourced compared to European public broadcasters of comparable reach.

## Activities

- **Political framing**: During elections, all three networks provide saturation favorable coverage of Kremlin-backed candidates and minimal or hostile coverage of opposition figures.
- **Reality manufacture**: Pomerantsev documents the production of political "reality shows" and constructed events — including staged pro-Putin street demonstrations — that blurred the line between authentic politics and television production.
- **Foreign disinformation**: Post-2014, the apparatus pivoted to systematic disinformation targeting Ukrainian and Western audiences, producing fabricated atrocity claims, fake witness interviews, and conspiracy narratives. The MH17 shootdown coverage in 2014 is a documented case study.
- **War propaganda (2022–present)**: All three channels present the Ukraine war uniformly as a "special military operation" against Ukrainian "Nazis"; coverage of battlefield setbacks is suppressed; atrocities are denied or blamed on Ukraine.
- **Soft-power entertainment**: The channels produce high-quality entertainment programming — dramas, game shows, reality television — that embeds political messaging within popular culture rather than relying solely on news.

## Controversies / Investigations

The 2014 MH17 disinformation campaign — including fabricated radar images, false witnesses, and conspiracy theories blaming Ukraine — has been documented by open-source investigators (Bellingcat) and Dutch prosecutors. The European Union sanctioned Rossiya 1, Russia-24, and NTV Plus in 2022, and RT France's broadcast licence was revoked. Dmitry Kiselev was personally sanctioned by the EU, UK, US, Canada, and Australia.
