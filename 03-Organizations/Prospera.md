## Summary

Próspera is a semi-autonomous private charter city established on the island of Roatán, Honduras, under the ZEDE (Zona de Empleo y Desarrollo Económico) legal framework. Founded in 2017 by Venezuelan-born entrepreneur Erick Brimen and incorporated in Delaware, it operates under a "governance as a service" model in which laws function as products and residents sign an "Agreement of Coexistence" upon entry. [[Peter Thiel]] and [[Marc Andreessen]] are among its early investors. It is the most-cited contemporary example of the libertarian-right theory of [[Plutocratic secession]] — exiting democratic jurisdiction rather than reforming it.

## Identity

Próspera presents itself as a low-tax, low-regulation business and residential enclave with its own civil and commercial codes. Residents and businesses can select applicable regulations from a menu of approved foreign jurisdictions, propose custom regulations subject to ZEDE board approval, or operate under common law. Criminal law remains Honduran. The governance model was designed by Brimen with input from the [[Tech Right]] investor network.

Próspera is physically located on private land on Roatán. Population has remained small (in the hundreds) since its founding; the project's significance has been primarily ideological and as a legal test case rather than as a working city.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **Erick Brimen** — founder and CEO; Venezuelan-born entrepreneur; primary public face.
- **Mark Lutter** — economist; founding executive director of the Charter Cities Institute, closely affiliated with the project in its early years.
- Investors include [[Peter Thiel]], [[Marc Andreessen]], and figures connected to [[Pronomos Capital]].

## Funding & money flows

Backed by US venture capital and libertarian-network donors. Reported early investment includes tens of millions of dollars from Silicon Valley sources. The legal dispute with Honduras has revealed the scale of claimed investor exposure: Próspera's ICSID arbitration claims damages of approximately $10.7–11 billion under the US–Honduras bilateral investment treaty.

## Activities

- Established physical infrastructure (business park, residences, co-working facilities) on Roatán.
- Operated its own arbitration system and civil code.
- Attracted a small number of businesses and remote workers.
- Filed ICSID (International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes) arbitration against Honduras after the 2022 ZEDE repeal.

## Controversies / investigations

- Opponents, including Honduran civil society and the Castro government, characterized ZEDEs broadly as sovereignty-stripping foreign enclaves with echoes of colonial concessions.
- The Latin America Working Group documented allegations that the ZEDE regime enabled exploitation and potential organized crime sanctuary.
- The $10.7 billion ICSID claim is widely cited as an example of investor-state dispute mechanisms being weaponized against democratic mandates.

## Open questions

- Will the ICSID arbitration succeed, and if so what damages will be awarded?
- Does the 50-year Legal Stability Agreement grandfathering clause survive constitutional repeal?
- What precedent does this set for other ZEDE/charter-city projects globally?
