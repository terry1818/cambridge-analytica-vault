## Summary

The Know-Nothing Party (formally the American Party) was the United States' first significant nativist mass-movement party, growing from a secret society in 1849 to a major electoral force by 1854–1856 before collapsing over slavery by 1860. At its peak it elected 8 governors, more than 100 congressmen, and held legislative majorities across the Northeast. Its platform fused anti-Catholic conspiracy theory, anti-immigration restriction (specifically targeting Irish and German Catholic immigrants), a 21-year residency requirement for citizenship, and Protestant cultural nationalism. The party is the canonical nineteenth-century precedent in Steven Hahn's [[Source - Hahn 2024 - Illiberal America]] for the recurring illiberal strand in American politics. It connects to the vault's comparative framework for nativist populism and directly anticipates the restrictionist arguments that produced the [[Immigration Act of 1924]].

## Identity

The Know-Nothings organized around the conviction that an alleged "Romanist" conspiracy — orchestrated by the Pope, Jesuit agents, and Catholic immigrants — was subverting American Protestant republican institutions. Their core beliefs:

- Catholic immigrants (especially Irish and German) posed a civilizational threat to Protestant, Anglo-Saxon self-governance.
- Native-born Protestant Americans needed a political vehicle to defend "traditional" cultural and religious values against a foreign, hierarchical Church.
- Naturalization should be delayed to 21 years; foreign-born persons should be excluded from public office.
- Government should be non-partisan, technocratic, and "American" — a hostility to political machines and immigrant-bloc voting.

The secrecy component — members instructed to say "I know nothing" when asked about the organization — gave the movement its popular name and reflected its origins as the Order of the Star-Spangled Banner (founded 1849).

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Nathaniel P. Banks** — Speaker of the House of Representatives, 1856 (initially elected with Know-Nothing support).
- **Millard Fillmore** — 1856 presidential candidate on the American Party ticket; former Whig president; won 21.6% of the popular vote and one state (Maryland).
- **Henry Wilson** — Massachusetts Know-Nothing senator who later became Ulysses Grant's Vice President after migrating to the Republican Party.
- Most Know-Nothing leaders migrated to the Republican Party by 1856–1858, primarily on the anti-slavery issue.

## Activities

- Organized through a network of local lodges descended from the Order of the Star-Spangled Banner; semi-secret organizational structure.
- Targeted Catholic immigration from Ireland (famine emigration, 1845–1855) and Germany; approximately 2.9 million immigrants arrived 1845–1854, more than the seven previous decades combined.
- Pursued state-level legislation restricting immigrant voting rights, mandating Protestant Bible reading in public schools, and limiting Catholic institutional property.
- Associated with anti-Catholic riots and violence in several cities (Louisville "Bloody Monday," 1855; Baltimore electoral violence).
- Rapidly won state-level offices in 1854–1855: majorities in Massachusetts, Pennsylvania, and elsewhere.

## Successor / Related Entities

- **Republican Party** (absorbed most northern Know-Nothings by 1856–1858, primarily on anti-slavery grounds; the Whig and Know-Nothing coalitions formed the Republican base).
- **Immigration restriction movement** (late 19th–early 20th century): direct ideological descent; the restrictionist arguments of the 1890s–1920s echo Know-Nothing nativism with pseudo-scientific eugenics added.
- **[[Immigration Act of 1924]]**: the legislative culmination of the tradition Know-Nothings pioneered.
- **George Wallace's American Independent Party**: later iteration of a third-party nativist political vehicle.

## Controversies / Investigations

- Associated with electoral violence and anti-Catholic mob activity in several cities; historians dispute the degree to which party leadership endorsed or merely tolerated street violence.
- Scholars debate whether Know-Nothing nativism was primarily religious (anti-Catholicism) or ethnic (anti-Irish/German); most conclude both elements were inextricable.

## Open Questions

- How directly does the Know-Nothing ideological template map onto subsequent movements? Hahn argues for structural continuity; other historians emphasize discontinuities.
- The party's rapid collapse over slavery suggests that nativism alone could not sustain a national coalition — a lesson with implications for analyzing contemporary nativist movements.
