## Summary

The Committee on Public Information (CPI), popularly known as the Creel Committee after its chairman [[George Creel]], was established by President Woodrow Wilson on April 13, 1917 — six days after the United States declared war on Germany — through Executive Order 2594. It was the first large-scale, centrally coordinated propaganda agency in US history, charged with mobilizing public support for the war effort using every available communication medium: newspapers, magazines, motion pictures, posters, pamphlets, and public speakers.

Tim Wu's *The Attention Merchants* (2016) treats the CPI as a foundational historical case study demonstrating that mass media attention, once commercially cultivated, can be captured by the state for political and military mobilization. The CPI pioneered many techniques — including the targeted deployment of "influencers" (the Four Minute Men), the manufacture of atrocity narratives, and the orchestration of news coverage — that later became standard tools of both commercial advertising and information warfare.

The CPI also launched the career of [[Edward Bernays]], who worked in its foreign publicity division and explicitly credited his CPI experience as the foundation of his theory of "engineering consent."

## Identity

The CPI operated across multiple divisions:
- **News Division**: Placed government-generated content in an estimated 20,000 newspaper columns per week.
- **Film Division**: Produced and distributed propaganda films.
- **Foreign Section**: Distributed material to allied and neutral countries; employed [[Edward Bernays]] among others.
- **Four Minute Men**: Recruited approximately 75,000 volunteer speakers who delivered standardized short speeches at movie theaters, churches, and public events — a model for "grassroots" messaging that was in fact centrally scripted.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

[[George Creel]] — Chairman; former investigative journalist and progressive muckraker from Kansas City and Denver; close ally of Woodrow Wilson. Appointed to CPI within a week of the war declaration.

[[Edward Bernays]] — Worked in the Latin America section of the Foreign Press Bureau; later applied CPI methods directly to commercial public relations and political consulting.

## Activities

- Produced more war posters than all other belligerent nations combined over the course of the war.
- Estimated to have placed material in 20,000 newspaper columns weekly.
- Created and distributed the now-iconic imagery of American WWI participation (including James Montgomery Flagg's "Uncle Sam Wants YOU," though this was actually commissioned by the US Army, not directly the CPI — the CPI distributed similar imagery at scale).
- Organized the Four Minute Men speaker network — 75,000 volunteers delivering 4-minute standardized pro-war speeches at public gatherings.
- Conducted foreign propaganda operations to influence neutral and allied publics.

## Timeline

- **1917-04-13** — Established by Woodrow Wilson via Executive Order 2594.
- **1917** — Four Minute Men network established and deployed to movie theaters nationwide.
- **1917–1918** — Massive poster and pamphlet production; foreign press bureau active.
- **1918-11-11** — Armistice; war ends.
- **1919-08** — CPI officially dissolved by Wilson.

## Successor / Related Entities

- The CPI's methods were directly absorbed into commercial advertising and public relations through [[Edward Bernays]] and others who moved from CPI into the private sector.
- The [[Office of War Information]] (WWII) was the closest institutional successor.
- [[Claude Hopkins]]' contemporaneous work on scientific advertising shares the CPI's assumption that mass attention can be systematically captured and redirected.

## Controversies / Investigations

- Post-war backlash: Many Americans concluded the CPI had "oversold" the war and had suppressed legitimate dissent through the climate it created. This backlash contributed to post-war isolationism.
- Civil liberties: The CPI operated alongside the Espionage Act (1917) and Sedition Act (1918), creating an environment in which criticism of the war was criminalized; the CPI's propaganda contributed to this climate of suppression, though the CPI itself was not a law-enforcement agency.
- The Four Minute Men scripts demonized German-Americans and immigrants, contributing to ethnic hostility and mob violence during the war.

## Open Questions

- What specific techniques developed by the CPI were explicitly transferred by Bernays into commercial and political practice?
- To what degree did CPI foreign operations establish the template for later US Cold War information operations?
