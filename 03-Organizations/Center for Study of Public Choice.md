## Summary

The Center for Study of Public Choice (CSPC) is the institutional home of the Virginia School of political economy and the academic base from which [[James M Buchanan]] developed and disseminated public choice theory — the application of economic methodology to political decision-making. Founded at Virginia Tech in 1969 as successor to Buchanan's earlier [[Thomas Jefferson Center]] at the University of Virginia, the Center moved with Buchanan to George Mason University in 1983, where it became one of the most influential nodes in the Koch-funded academic infrastructure. Nancy MacLean's *Democracy in Chains* argues — controversially, with substantial scholarly pushback — that the Center's work provided the intellectual scaffolding for the constitutional entrenchment strategy pursued by the Koch network, including the [[Convention of States Action]] campaign.

## Identity

The Center was established in 1969 at Virginia Polytechnic Institute (Virginia Tech) by Buchanan, Gordon Tullock, and Charles J. Goetz, following Buchanan's departure from the University of Virginia under pressure related to his center's perceived ideological orientation. Buchanan served as its director from founding until retirement in 2007. In 1983 the entire Center — seven faculty members — relocated to George Mason University to be closer to Washington, D.C., and to an institution more receptive to its research program.

The CSPC's alumni network is substantial: as of Buchanan's death in 2013, it had produced 148 doctoral students from George Mason, 113 from Virginia Tech, and 109 from UVA — a pipeline that seeded economics departments and policy think tanks across the conservative intellectual ecosystem.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **James M. Buchanan**: Nobel Prize in Economics 1986; directed the Center from founding until 2007; his ideas form the core intellectual product
- **Gordon Tullock**: co-founder; prolific contributor to public choice theory; co-authored *The Calculus of Consent* (1962) with Buchanan
- **Tyler Cowen** and other George Mason faculty: associated with the broader GMU free-market cluster including the [[Mercatus Center]]

## Funding & Money Flows

The Center is embedded within George Mason University, which has received substantial Koch family contributions. The Koch brothers gave GMU more than **$30 million** across various programs; while CSPC's specific share is not publicly itemized, it exists within the same institutional complex as the [[Mercatus Center]] and the Institute for Humane Studies, both of which are primary Koch funding targets. MacLean documents Koch interest in Buchanan's work as early as the late 1970s and describes a 1997 meeting between Charles Koch and Buchanan to discuss strategy.

## Activities

The Center's primary output is academic research and doctoral training in public choice economics. Core intellectual contributions include:
- **Public choice theory**: applying rational-choice economics to political actors (voters, legislators, bureaucrats), yielding conclusions skeptical of government expansion
- **Constitutional political economy**: Buchanan's later focus on designing constitutional rules that constrain majoritarian democracy
- **Fiscal federalism**: analysis of multi-level government that informed arguments for devolving power to states

These ideas provided theoretical grounding for policy agendas including balanced-budget amendments, tax and expenditure limitations, and the scholarly case for supermajority requirements — the formal underpinning of what this vault terms the [[Constitutional entrenchment strategy]].

## Controversies / Investigations

MacLean's *Democracy in Chains* (2017) is the most prominent critical account, arguing the Center's work was instrumentalized by the Koch network to provide intellectual cover for anti-democratic constitutional entrenchment. This interpretation has been substantially contested: Henry Farrell and Steven Teles called it a "conspiracy theory in the guise of intellectual history," and multiple economists have documented factual errors in MacLean's treatment of Buchanan's texts. **Vault evidence tier**: MacLean's interpretive thesis is Tier-3 (contested); the documented facts of Koch funding and the intellectual influence of CSPC work on Koch-network policy positions are Tier-1/2.
