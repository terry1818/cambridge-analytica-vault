# TSA

## Summary

The Transportation Security Administration is the [[DHS|Department of Homeland Security]] component responsible for securing U.S. civil transportation, primarily commercial aviation. Created by the Aviation and Transportation Security Act weeks after the September 11 attacks, the TSA federalized airport screening. For this vault the TSA matters because it is where surveillance technology becomes a routine, near-mandatory condition of domestic mobility for ordinary Americans. Its facial-recognition program is expanding aggressively across U.S. airports, its watchlist-driven "Quiet Skies" program covertly tracks travelers who are not under criminal investigation, and the practical difficulty of declining face scans makes "consent" largely nominal. The TSA thus illustrates how the post-9/11 [[national security state]] normalizes biometric monitoring of the general public — not at the border, but in the everyday act of boarding a flight.

- **Founded:** November 19, 2001, by the Aviation and Transportation Security Act; originally placed in the Department of Transportation, moved to the new [[DHS]] in March 2003.
- **Parent / owner:** [[DHS|U.S. Department of Homeland Security]].
- **Registered jurisdiction:** United States; headquartered in Springfield, Virginia.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **John Magaw** — first TSA Administrator, 2002.
- The TSA is led by an Administrator who reports to the Secretary of Homeland Security.

## Funding & money flows

The TSA receives roughly $10 billion in annual appropriations, funded partly by the September 11 Security Fee on airline tickets. Vault-relevant spending includes procurement of Credential Authentication Technology (CAT-2) units — the camera-equipped ID scanners that perform facial matching at checkpoints — and the broader biometric-identity infrastructure being deployed across airports.

## Activities

**Airport facial recognition.** The TSA operates a facial-recognition program in which checkpoint cameras capture a traveler's live image and match it against the photo on their identity document. Originally piloted at about 15 airports — including Atlanta, Chicago O'Hare, Dallas-Fort Worth, Los Angeles, and New York JFK — the program is being expanded to roughly 65 airports by spring 2026, a sharp escalation. The TSA states travelers may opt out and request manual screening without penalty, but reporting found airport staff offered the opt-out to only about 1 percent of travelers, and some who declined reported friction — making the "voluntary" framing weak in practice.

**Quiet Skies.** The TSA's "Quiet Skies" program subjects selected travelers — identified through watchlist and pattern criteria, not criminal predicate — to enhanced screening and covert in-flight surveillance by Federal Air Marshals, who log behavioral details such as whether a passenger sweats, fidgets, or uses a phone. The program has drawn criticism for tracking people who are not suspected of any crime and for its potential use against political figures and travelers, raising democratic-backsliding concerns about a watchlist apparatus operating without transparency or meaningful redress.

**Watchlist screening.** The TSA screens all passengers against the federal terrorist watchlist (the No Fly List and Selectee List) via Secure Flight, a system long criticized for opaque listing criteria and inadequate redress for misidentified travelers.

- [[ICE]] — sister DHS component; broader immigration-enforcement surveillance.
- [[FBI]] — maintains the Terrorist Screening Database underlying TSA watchlist screening.

## Controversies / investigations

- **Facial-recognition expansion** — civil-liberties objection that checkpoint face-matching builds biometric infrastructure with weak opt-out; DHS IG audit opened January 2025; DHS's own analysis found lower face-match success rates for self-identified Black travelers.
- **Quiet Skies** — criticized for covertly surveilling travelers not under criminal investigation, including the logging of innocuous behavior.
- **Watchlist redress** — long-running due-process objections to opaque No Fly List criteria.
