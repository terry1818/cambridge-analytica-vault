## Company role in the vault

[[Palantir Technologies]] is the data-integration and analytics company founded in 2003 by [[Peter Thiel]], Alex Karp, Stephen Cohen, Joe Lonsdale, and Nathan Gettings, with early funding from In-Q-Tel (the CIA-affiliated venture fund). Its two product lines (Palantir Gotham, originally for intelligence and defence customers; Palantir Foundry, for commercial and civilian-government data integration) are the operational vehicle through which Thiel-aligned data architecture has moved into US federal and state government infrastructure.

The vault treats Palantir as the load-bearing tech-right infrastructure node for the 2024-2026 phase. Where [[Cambridge Analytica]] in 2016 was an outsider operation injected into a campaign, Palantir in 2025 is sitting inside HHS, ICE, DoD, IRS, and (via DOGE-adjacent data work) the broader federal data backbone. The capabilities are larger; the regulatory friction is smaller; the public visibility is also smaller.

## Founding context and the In-Q-Tel anchor

Palantir was founded in 2003 by [[Peter Thiel]] (lead funder), Alex Karp (CEO from inception), Stephen Cohen, Joe Lonsdale, and Nathan Gettings. The founding investment included In-Q-Tel, the CIA-affiliated venture-capital arm whose participation signalled both the company's intended customer base (US intelligence and defence) and the kind of methodology and technology positioning it would adopt (counter-terrorism analytics post-9/11).

The In-Q-Tel anchor is the formative factor that distinguishes Palantir from other enterprise-data-integration competitors. Most enterprise-software vendors built first for commercial customers and then attempted to add government capability. Palantir built first for the intelligence community and then attempted to extend into commercial markets. The technical architecture, the access-control model, the audit-logging defaults, and the deployment style all reflect the intelligence-community origin.

## Product line distinctions: Gotham vs Foundry

[[Palantir Gotham]] is the original product, designed for intelligence-and-defence customers. Its core capability is fusing data across multiple structured and unstructured sources (HUMINT reports, SIGINT intercepts, financial records, geospatial data, biometrics) into a unified analytical environment that supports investigation, target-development, and operational planning. Gotham deployments have been documented across CIA, NSA, FBI, DoD components, and foreign intelligence services with which Palantir has US-government-approved relationships.

[[Palantir Foundry]] is the later commercial-and-civilian-government product, designed for enterprise data integration in less-sensitive operational environments. Foundry customers include Fortune 100 commercial firms (BP, Airbus, Merck), federal civilian agencies (HHS, IRS, SSA, the various Treasury components), and increasingly state-level government customers.

The two product lines share architectural foundations but have diverged operationally. The 2024-2026 expansion of Palantir's federal civilian footprint has been principally on the Foundry side, with the HHS master-database integration (documented in [[New Republic 2025 Trump Palantir Database]]) and the ICE enforcement-targeting integration (documented in [[ACLU Palantir Deportation]]) as the most consequential current deployments.

## The Karp-vs-Thiel dynamic

A specific feature of Palantir that distinguishes it from other Thiel-portfolio companies is the Karp-Thiel public-political divergence. Karp identifies publicly as a progressive Democrat (he grew up in a progressive Jewish household, holds a PhD in Neoclassical Social Theory from Frankfurt, is publicly critical of various MAGA positions). Thiel is the principal Trump-aligned funder. The two have maintained a working relationship for 20+ years across this political divergence.

The vault's reading of the Karp-Thiel dynamic: the political divergence is real but operationally bounded. Karp's progressivism is principally about social-cultural issues; Karp and Thiel agree on the central operational question of whether the US national-security state should aggressively integrate AI and data infrastructure (both yes). The divergence does not affect Palantir's actual operational decisions in ways the public record can identify.

## 2024-2026 federal expansion

The Trump 2.0 federal data-integration expansion has positioned Palantir as the principal infrastructure beneficiary. Documented or strongly indicated current contracts include:

- HHS master-database integration (consolidating CDC, NIH, CMS, FDA, and HRSA data systems into a unified analytical layer)
- IRS data-integration support (post-[[DOGE]] access programme; documented in 2025 reporting)
- ICE Investigative Case Management successor systems
- DoD Project Maven 2.0 / successor programmes for AI-driven targeting
- Army data-integration contracts across the Army Vantage and adjacent platforms

The 2025 expansion has been documented in [[Source Rolling Out 2025-11-24 Vance Palantir]] and adjacent reporting; the underlying contracting picture is partially obscured by the use of pass-through prime contractors and by the limits of public contract-award databases.
