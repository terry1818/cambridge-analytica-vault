## Summary

Officers' Christian Fellowship (OCF) is an evangelical parachurch organization of approximately 17,000 US military officers, family members, and retirees that operates at installations throughout the US military. Its stated purpose is to "glorify God by uniting Christian officers for biblical fellowship and outreach" and to build a "spiritually transformed military, with Christlike officers as a leavening influence." Jeff Sharlet's *C Street* (2010) treats OCF as part of the broader infrastructure linking elite evangelical networks to military leadership, examining how the organization cultivates an officer corps shaped by a specifically militant, hierarchical reading of Christian identity.

## Identity

OCF operates on the premise that military officers occupy a position of providential responsibility. The organization's framing fuses national defense with Christian duty, presenting the military profession as a calling ordained by God. This theological framing — sometimes referred to as the "warrior faith" — creates an organizational culture in which command authority and religious authority are mutually reinforcing. The group distributes Bible study materials, hosts retreats, and operates small-group programs at military academies and installations.

OCF's ambitions are explicitly evangelistic toward the broader military. Its literature describes officers as a "leavening influence" — a phrase drawn from Matthew 13 — implying that Christianizing the officer corps will organically Christianize the force below it. This framing is relevant to the broader theocratic-wing project of embedding Christian nationalist sensibility within institutions of state power.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

OCF was founded in 1943 by US Army Lieutenant Colonel Hayes A. Kroner, who had encountered the British Officers' Christian Union while aboard a ship bound for Palestine in 1939 and returned resolved to establish an American counterpart. Kroner led a small Washington, DC Bible study before formalizing the organization in 1943. Kroner served as OCF's first president. In 1972, under executive director Paul Pettijohn, the organization was renamed from Officers' Christian Union to Officers' Christian Fellowship. By 1947, membership had spread to West Point and Annapolis; by the 2020s OCF claimed over 17,000 members.

## Funding & Money Flows

OCF is a registered 501(c)(3) nonprofit (EIN 84-1415401) accredited by the Evangelical Council for Financial Accountability (ECFA). It derives income from individual donations and member support. Specific donor networks are not publicly disclosed. No documented ties to major dark-money infrastructure have been identified in available sources.

## Activities

- Small-group Bible studies and fellowship at military installations worldwide
- Annual retreats and leadership development programs
- Campus outreach at US service academies (West Point, Annapolis, Air Force Academy, Coast Guard Academy)
- Publication and distribution of devotional and discipleship materials
- "Leavening" mission: embedding Christian officers as influencers within military culture

## Controversies / Investigations

- Critics, including Military Religious Freedom Foundation (MRFF), argue that OCF's presence at military academies and installations constitutes preferential treatment for evangelical Christianity, pressures non-Christian service members, and blurs the constitutional line between military authority and religious affiliation.
- Sharlet documents OCF as part of the elite evangelical network that operates below public scrutiny, cultivating a specifically deferential, hierarchical political theology within institutions that are constitutionally prohibited from establishing religion.
