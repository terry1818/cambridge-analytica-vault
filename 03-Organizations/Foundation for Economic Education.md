# Foundation for Economic Education

## Summary

The Foundation for Economic Education (FEE) is widely considered the oldest free-market think tank in the United States. Founded on March 7, 1946 by [[Leonard Read]] and Henry Hazlitt with backing from major corporations and the [[William Volker Fund]], FEE was established explicitly to challenge and roll back the New Deal regulatory state. It functioned as the institutional nursery for what would become the modern libertarian and conservative intellectual movements, providing ideological infrastructure at a moment when mainstream academic and political opinion strongly favored interventionism. FEE's founding model — corporations funding ideological education at arm's length through a nonprofit — became a template replicated by every subsequent generation of patron-class infrastructure, including the [[Koch Network]] and the [[Heritage Foundation]].

## Identity

FEE was conceived as a counter-establishment operation. Its founding trustees wrote that "the job of economic education must be undertaken now while those who appreciate the value of liberty are still in a position to support it," framing the effort in urgent, defensive terms. The organization focused on producing and distributing free-market literature, hosting seminars, and training a cadre of advocates who would carry laissez-faire ideas into academia, journalism, and policy. Its ideological lineage ran through the Austrian School of economics, particularly [[Ludwig von Mises]] and Friedrich Hayek. FEE's founding directly inspired Hayek to establish the [[Mont Pelerin Society]] in 1947.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Leonard Read** (founder, president 1946–1983): Read served as president from founding until his death. A former Chamber of Commerce executive, he was the organizational genius who assembled the coalition of corporate funders and intellectual talent.
- **Henry Hazlitt**: Journalist and economist, co-founder, author of *Economics in One Lesson* (1946), which FEE distributed widely.
- **Ludwig von Mises**: Served as a FEE staff member and regular seminar participant; his presence gave FEE direct Austrian School credibility.

## Funding & Money Flows

Corporate backing was substantial from the outset. FEE was set up with support from approximately twenty of the largest US corporations, including General Motors, Chrysler, Southern California Edison, and DuPont. By the end of 1949, 46 corporations had collectively contributed approximately $1 million (roughly $13 million in 2024 dollars). The William Volker Fund provided early critical support. This model of large corporations funding ideological nonprofits at arm's length — creating deniable ideological infrastructure — became the signature funding mechanism of what later scholars called the patron-class network.

## Activities

FEE's primary activities were educational and propagandistic in the classical sense: producing books, pamphlets, and periodicals (its journal *The Freeman* launched in 1950); hosting residential seminars for businessmen, students, and journalists; and maintaining a library of pro-market literature for broad distribution. It served as a talent incubator: figures who passed through FEE's orbit went on to found or staff institutions including the Cato Institute and the Intercollegiate Studies Institute.

## Successor / Related Entities

FEE is the direct ancestor of the postwar pro-business think tank ecosystem. Its model was replicated by the [[Heritage Foundation]] (1973), [[Cato Institute]] (1977), and hundreds of state-level policy organizations. The [[Koch Network]] consciously built on FEE's educational infrastructure model with [[Americans for Prosperity]] and related entities. [[Antony Fisher]], who visited Read in 1945 before founding the Institute of Economic Affairs in London, carried the FEE model to the UK and later helped establish the Atlas Network of international free-market think tanks.

## Controversies / Investigations

FEE's funding from major corporations for ideological purposes was noted critically by journalists and labor advocates during the 1940s and 1950s. Critics argued FEE was producing business-funded propaganda dressed as education. The organization has never been the subject of a formal government investigation.

## Open Questions

- To what extent did the corporate donors exert editorial control over FEE's output, versus providing arms-length funding?
- How directly did FEE's mid-century seminar alumni feed into the Heritage Foundation and Cato funding networks of the 1970s?
