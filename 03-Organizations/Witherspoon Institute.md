## Summary

The Witherspoon Institute is a social conservative research institute based in Princeton, New Jersey, founded in 2003 by Princeton professor [[Robert P. George]], Luis Tellez, and colleagues associated with the James Madison Program in American Ideals and Institutions. Named after John Witherspoon — president of the College of New Jersey (later Princeton) and the only active clergyman to sign the Declaration of Independence — the institute applies natural-law philosophy to contemporary questions in bioethics, constitutional law, political theory, and sexual ethics.

The institute is financially independent of Princeton University, operates as a 501(c)(3) nonprofit, and publishes *Public Discourse: Ethics, Law, and the Common Good*, an online journal that serves as a primary vehicle for natural-law arguments against abortion, same-sex marriage, assisted suicide, and embryo-destructive research. In the architecture of the Catholic neoconservative / theocon intellectual network, Witherspoon functions as the semi-academic production hub that translates George's scholarly natural-law framework into accessible policy arguments.

## Identity

The Witherspoon Institute occupies a distinctive position: it is adjacent to Princeton University but not part of it, allowing it to pursue an explicitly value-laden research agenda while borrowing prestige from the university's proximity. Its seminars focus on natural-law philosophy and train younger scholars in its application to contemporary political questions — functioning partly as a talent pipeline for the conservative legal and policy movement.

The institute's name invokes a founder who was both a Presbyterian minister and a Founding Father, positioning the organization within a tradition that views religion and republican government as complementary rather than separate. This framing directly addresses the [[The naked public square]] problem identified by [[Richard John Neuhaus]]: the institute's work provides the intellectual resources to reintroduce religiously grounded natural law into public debate under the rubric of universal reason.

## Leadership & key personnel

- **[[Robert P. George]]** — co-founder; Herbert W. Vaughan Senior Fellow; primary intellectual figure.

- Ryan T. Anderson — former fellow; later president of the [[Ethics and Public Policy Center]]; author of *When Harry Became Sally* (2018).

## Activities

- Hosts graduate seminars on natural law, political philosophy, and bioethics.
- Publishes *Public Discourse* — a daily online journal featuring articles by fellows, scholars, and policy advocates on abortion, marriage, religious liberty, bioethics, and constitutional theory.
- Co-sponsors conferences bringing together scholars from multiple disciplines to apply natural-law reasoning to legal and policy questions.
- Has produced scholarship that was cited in amicus briefs in major Supreme Court cases on marriage and religious liberty.
- The institute's *What Is Marriage?* project (co-authored by George, Sherif Girgis, and Ryan T. Anderson) became the most cited academic argument against *Obergefell v. Hodges* before and after the 2015 ruling.

## Successor / related entities

- [[American Principles Project]] — the advocacy-oriented 501(c)(4) counterpart to Witherspoon's research function; also co-founded by George.
- [[Ethics and Public Policy Center]] — overlapping fellow networks; Ryan T. Anderson moved from Witherspoon fellow to EPPC president.
- [[First Things magazine]] — publishes work by Witherspoon-affiliated scholars.

## Controversies / investigations

- Critics have argued that *Public Discourse* functions as a partisan advocacy outlet rather than a genuine research publication, citing consistent alignment with Republican policy positions.
- The institute's proximity to Princeton has been cited as an example of conservative institution-building within elite universities — a strategy documented by critics of the movement.

## Open questions
