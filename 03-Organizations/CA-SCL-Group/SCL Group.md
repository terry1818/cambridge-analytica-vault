## Corporate structure and ownership chain

[[SCL Group]] (Strategic Communication Laboratories) was incorporated in 1993 by Nigel Oakes. The corporate structure that the [[ICO Investigation]] and the various national regulatory inquiries reconstructed includes a parent SCL Group Limited (UK-domiciled, Companies House registered), a working subsidiary SCL Elections Limited (the political-consulting arm under Nix from 2006 onward), SCL Defence (the original defence-and-government-behavioural-change arm under Oakes), and various international subsidiaries including SCL US Holdings (Delaware-domiciled, became the corporate parent of [[Cambridge Analytica]] LLC in 2013-2014).

The ownership chain at the time of the 2018 collapse included Oakes-family interests (residual through SCL Group Limited), the Mercer family interests (through SCL US Holdings via the 2013-2016 capitalisation), and a number of smaller minority interests that the Companies House filings document. Post-bankruptcy disposition of the various subsidiaries is the [[Emerdata]] / [[Auspex International]] / [[Frontier Services Group]]-adjacent successor map that the vault tracks separately.

[[Source Companies House CA Records]] is the canonical primary source for the corporate-structure reconstruction.

## Methodological prehistory

SCL's working methodology has roots in two distinct lineages. The first is the British military-adjacent psychological-operations tradition: Nigel Oakes had been a sound-engineering-and-broadcasting professional before founding SCL, and his early collaborators included figures with backgrounds in the UK MoD's Joint Force Psychological Operations work and the Behavioural Dynamics Institute. The "behavioural change communications" framing that SCL used in its early defence contracts was a softened-and-repackaged version of the same toolkit.

The second lineage is consumer marketing and advertising: SCL's pitch decks borrowed heavily from the Saatchi & Saatchi-style brand-positioning tradition that British advertising had developed in the 1970s-80s. The cross-pollination of psyops-adjacent methodology with consumer-marketing presentation style is what distinguishes SCL from the earlier (purely military) and the contemporary (purely commercial) competitors.

## International elections caseload

The pre-CA SCL Elections caseload through 2006-2013 is partially reconstructed from Nix's own subsequent characterisations, from Wylie and Kaiser testimony, and from the [[HindsightFiles Release 2020]] document leak. Documented or strongly indicated deployments include: [[India SCL elections 2003-2012]] (multiple state-level contracts), [[Nigeria 2007 2015 Elections]] (multiple cycles), [[Trinidad Tobago elections]] (a documented behavioural-change campaign that became a CA marketing case study), [[Kenya 2013 2017 Elections]] (Kenyatta campaigns), [[Malta 2013 Joseph Muscat]], [[Australia CA office 2015]], [[Argentina Election Work]], and a long tail of less-documented engagements across the Caribbean, Africa, and parts of Southeast Asia.

The caseload matters because it documents that the methodology was operationally deployed at scale before the 2016 cycle made the firm globally famous. CA's 2016 deployments were not novel; they were the largest-scale application of an already-proven toolkit.

## The CA spin-out and the Mercer capitalisation

The 2013-2014 formation of [[Cambridge Analytica]] as the SCL US vehicle was driven by three convergent factors: (a) the US political-consulting market was potentially much larger than SCL's existing international caseload combined, (b) the Mercer family was willing to capitalise a US-focused entity at a scale SCL Group as a whole had never had access to, and (c) the [[Steve Bannon]] / [[Breitbart News]] strategic infrastructure could provide the political-narrative direction that SCL's existing methodology lacked.

The deliberate brand separation (CA was marketed as a US firm with Cambridge-academic credibility; SCL was kept in the background as the corporate parent) was an operational choice that worked through the 2014-2016 period and then became a regulatory complication after 2018, when the corporate-structure question became material to who was liable for what.

## Regulatory disposition

SCL Group Limited and SCL Elections Limited filed for administration in May 2018 alongside the CA bankruptcy. The [[ICO Investigation]] issued enforcement notices against multiple SCL entities and against [[AggregateIQ]] (which the regulatory record treats as functionally SCL Canada). The [[DCMS Disinformation Inquiry]] hearings produced sworn testimony from SCL and CA leadership across multiple sessions.

The successor-entity question is the regulatory thread that did not get fully closed in 2018. The [[Emerdata]] formation in late 2017 (before the bankruptcy) appears in retrospect to have been a deliberate vehicle to preserve methodology, IP, and personnel through the corporate-collapse window. [[Emerdata FSG Prince connection]] documents the partially-reconstructed ownership and personnel overlap.

## Why the vault treats SCL Group as a top connector

SCL Group is the corporate root from which the entire CA-era operator network branches. Without SCL: no methodology base for CA to inherit; no international elections-consulting infrastructure; no [[AggregateIQ]] (which was SCL Canada in functional terms); no Emerdata / Auspex successor chain. The CA story is in important respects an SCL story with a different US-facing brand.
