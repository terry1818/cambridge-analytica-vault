# X-Mode Social / Outlogic

## Summary

X-Mode Social was a location-data brokerage founded in 2013 by Joshua Anton in Reston, Virginia. The company evolved from an app-development startup — best known for eccentric consumer apps including "Drunk Mode" — into a data broker that paid mobile-app developers to embed its SDK, harvesting precise GPS coordinates from tens of millions of devices worldwide. By the time Vice/Motherboard exposed its government ties in 2020, X-Mode was collecting data from over 400 apps and tracking approximately 25 million U.S. devices and 40 million internationally. Its clients included U.S. military intelligence contractors, making it a documented link between the commercial ad-data ecosystem and national security surveillance. In August 2021, the company was acquired by Digital Envoy and rebranded as Outlogic. In January 2024, the FTC announced its first-ever enforcement action specifically targeting a geolocation data broker, ordering X-Mode/Outlogic to stop selling sensitive location data and to delete previously collected records. The final order was issued in April 2024. The case established that collecting and selling location data revealing visits to sensitive sites — religious facilities, medical clinics, reproductive health providers, domestic abuse shelters — without genuine informed consent is an unfair practice under Section 5 of the FTC Act.

## Identity

Founded as a general app-development company in 2013, X-Mode pivoted in 2017 to a pure location-data business model. The company's SDK collected location data more accurately than competing SDKs of the era (using battery-efficient methods), and X-Mode paid app developers a revenue share for integrating the SDK. This "supply side" aggregation model meant that millions of users of unrelated apps — prayer apps, utility apps, games — were unwittingly feeding location pings to X-Mode without meaningful notice or consent. In 2021, following Apple and Google banning apps with the X-Mode SDK from their stores after the Vice reporting, the company was sold and rebranded Outlogic.

## Funding & Money Flows

X-Mode raised venture funding (total amount not confirmed in reviewed sources). Revenue came from two streams: (1) licensing location data to commercial clients in real estate, finance, and retail; (2) selling to government contractors — documented clients included defense contractors passing data to U.S. military intelligence. Exact contract values with government-adjacent buyers are not publicly available. After the FTC action, the consent order required not only a prohibition on sensitive-data sales but also deletion of previously collected data in certain categories.

## Activities

**SDK-based data collection:** X-Mode's core mechanism was a software development kit embedded in third-party apps. Developers received revenue share; X-Mode received a continuous stream of precise location pings linked to device advertising identifiers (which can be cross-referenced with identity). The company publicly stated it tracked 25 million active users in the U.S. and 40 million worldwide through 400+ apps.

**Government and military sales:** Senator Ron Wyden's office, working from reporting by Vice/Motherboard, established that X-Mode sold location data through defense contractors to U.S. military and intelligence clients. Motherboard first reported in November 2020 that Muslim Pro — a prayer-time and Quran app with approximately 98 million downloads — was sending granular location data to X-Mode, which in turn supplied it to military intelligence contractors. This was particularly significant because Muslim Pro's user base is globally distributed and skews toward Muslim-majority countries — effectively making the app an inadvertent SIGINT-adjacent collection platform.

**Commercial data sales:** X-Mode also sold location data to hundreds of non-government clients across industries, aggregated into audience profiles for ad targeting and market research.

**App-store bans:** In November 2020, Apple and Google required developers to remove the X-Mode SDK from their apps or face removal from the respective stores, citing the company's data practices. X-Mode claimed this was an overreaction; developers scrambled to comply.

**FTC-regulated sensitive locations:** The FTC's complaint documented that X-Mode/Outlogic sold data that revealed device visits to reproductive health clinics, domestic abuse shelters, places of worship, and addiction treatment centers — categories the FTC treated as warranting heightened protection.

## Controversies / Investigations

**FTC Enforcement Action (2024):** The FTC charged X-Mode/Outlogic with (1) selling precise location data without obtaining verifiable consumer consent for commercial or government surveillance uses; (2) collecting and selling data revealing visits to sensitive locations including religious sites, medical facilities, and shelters. The January 2024 proposed order and April 2024 final order constituted the first FTC settlement with a standalone geolocation data broker, setting a regulatory benchmark. The order requires data deletion and prohibits future sales of sensitive-location data.

**Muslim Pro / Military Contractor Exposure (2020):** The Vice/Motherboard investigation that disclosed X-Mode's military ties generated substantial congressional attention. The combination of a religious-app user base, precise location data, and sales to military intelligence contractors prompted Senator Wyden to send letters to X-Mode and to DoD.

**App-Store Bans (2020):** Apple and Google banning the SDK — and requiring removal from existing apps — was an extraordinary step, signaling that the SDK-embedding model X-Mode pioneered was recognized as a systemic privacy threat by platform gatekeepers.

**Informed Consent Failure:** The FTC's complaint established the legal theory that consent buried in terms-of-service — without meaningful, specific notice that data would be sold to government surveillance clients — does not constitute lawful consent under the FTC Act. This theory has broad implications for the bidstream data industry.
