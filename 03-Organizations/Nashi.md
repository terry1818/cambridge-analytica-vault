# Nashi — The Kremlin-Backed Pro-Putin Youth Movement

## Summary

Nashi ("Ours" in Russian) was a state-sponsored Russian youth movement formally founded on 15 April 2005, widely understood to have been conceived by presidential administration deputy chief [[Vladislav Surkov]] and channeled through youth organizer Vasily Yakemenko. It described itself publicly as "democratic, anti-fascist, and anti-oligarchy" but functioned in practice as a government-organized non-governmental organization (GONGO) designed to mobilize pro-Kremlin youth as a street force, prevent scenarios analogous to Ukraine's 2004 Orange Revolution, and provide a career conveyor belt into the bureaucratic elite. Pomerantsev's *Nothing Is True and Everything Is Possible* describes the movement as emblematic of the Kremlin's approach to manufacturing political reality: genuine enthusiasm manufactured through state resources, deployed to perform loyalty.

## Identity

Nashi emerged from an earlier Kremlin-linked youth group, "Walking Together" (Idushchiye Vmeste), led by Vasily Yakemenko — who became the key link between the nascent movement and Surkov in the presidential administration. The Orange Revolution in Ukraine (2004) was a direct triggering event: Kremlin officials feared that Western-backed NGOs and youth groups could replicate similar street mobilization in Russia ahead of the 2007–2008 electoral cycle. Nashi was designed to flood the zone — providing a loyalist counter-mass to any opposition street presence and signaling to potential opposition youth that the regime had its own youth infrastructure. The Bureau of Investigative Journalism described it as "Putin's private army." Moisés Naím used it as a canonical example of a GONGO.

## Leadership & Key Personnel

- **Vasily Yakemenko**: Founding commissioner; led "Walking Together" predecessor; served as primary organizer through early years.
- **[[Vladislav Surkov]]**: Deputy head of the presidential administration; credited by multiple sources as the conceptual architect and Kremlin sponsor. Surkov's philosophy of "managed democracy" viewed youth mobilization as a managed political performance rather than genuine civic engagement.
- **Nikita Borovikov**: Served as commissioner in a later phase.

## Funding & Money Flows

Nashi was financed through a combination of direct state subsidies, oligarchic donations coordinated through the Kremlin, and commercial partnerships with state-aligned businesses. The movement ran large summer camps at Lake Seliger, where attendance and participation were reportedly tied to career advancement in state-connected industries. The precise funding flows were never fully disclosed.

## Activities

- **Lake Seliger camps**: Annual gatherings of tens of thousands of young Russians, combining political instruction, nationalist programming, and social networking. Attendees included future government officials.
- **Street mobilization**: Nashi organized counter-demonstrations against opposition protests, including sieges of the British and Estonian embassies and demonstrations against opposition figures.
- **Anti-opposition operations**: Members were involved in harassment of opposition journalists and bloggers; the movement's online wing was accused of coordinated harassment campaigns pre-dating formal troll farm operations.
- **Ambassador harassment**: Nashi organized weeks-long protests outside the British Embassy in 2007 and harassed Estonian diplomats during the Bronze Soldier dispute.
- **Career pipeline**: The movement explicitly offered participants access to internships, jobs, and political positions within United Russia and the state apparatus.

## Controversies / Investigations

The movement was linked to coordinated online harassment of journalists — most notably Novaya Gazeta reporters and bloggers critical of Putin — in what appeared to be an early iteration of the organized online pressure campaigns later associated with the [[Internet Research Agency]]. Foreign governments lodged formal protests over ambassador harassment. The movement was widely condemned by Russian civil society and Western governments as a state-manufactured pseudo-civil-society organization.
