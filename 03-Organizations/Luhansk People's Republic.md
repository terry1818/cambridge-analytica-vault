# Luhansk People's Republic

## Summary

The Luhansk People's Republic (LPR) is a Russian-backed proxy entity proclaimed in the Luhansk region of eastern Ukraine in April 2014, in parallel with the [[Donetsk People's Republic]] in the neighboring region. It belongs in this vault as the second half of the Donbas proxy case — a near-identical demonstration of how the Kremlin used manufactured "breakaway republics" to wage a deniable war and then to manufacture a pretext for open invasion. After Ukraine's 2014 Revolution of Dignity and the Russian annexation of Crimea, pro-Russian armed groups seized government buildings across the Donbas; separatists in Luhansk proclaimed the LPR, and on 12 May 2014 both the LPR and the DPR declared themselves "independent states" following staged referendums.

Like its Donetsk counterpart, the LPR was a Russian proxy: although Ukrainian citizens such as Valery Bolotov, Igor Plotnitsky, and Leonid Pasechnik held leadership positions, the entity was supplied with weapons and personnel and directed by Russia, which publicly denied involvement. It received no recognition from any UN member state before 2022. The LPR's value to the Kremlin was the same as the DPR's — deniability and a controllable point of leverage over Ukraine. The two republics were treated as a single political object in Russian messaging, and on 21 February 2022 Russia formally recognized both as sovereign states, using their claimed plight as a stated justification for the full-scale invasion launched three days later. In September 2022, after sham referendums, Russia proclaimed the LPR annexed and began integrating its paramilitary forces into the Russian armed forces.

## Identity

- **Legal name(s):** Luhansk People's Republic (Luganskaya Narodnaya Respublika).
- **Other names / fronts:** LPR, LNR.
- **Founded:** Proclaimed in late April 2014; declared "independence" jointly with the DPR on 12 May 2014.
- **Dissolved / restructured:** Functionally absorbed into the Russian Federation following the September 2022 annexation claim.
- **Parent / owner:** The Russian Federation, in de facto control throughout.
- **Registered jurisdiction:** Internationally recognized as part of Ukraine; no UN-member recognition before 2022.

## Leadership & key personnel

- Valery Bolotov — an early LPR leader during the 2014 takeover.
- Igor Plotnitsky — LPR leader from 2014 until his ouster in 2017.
- Leonid Pasechnik — separatist leader from 2017, who oversaw the entity through the 2022 annexation.

## Funding & money flows

The LPR was wholly dependent on Russia for military supplies, personnel, fuel, and budgetary support. It had no viable independent economy and functioned as a Russian-subsidized occupation administration in the smaller of the two Donbas regions.

## Activities

The LPR's activities mirrored the DPR's: fielding "People's Militia" forces alongside Russian units in the war in Donbas (2014–2022); administering occupied territory; and sustaining the information narrative of an indigenous separatist republic. As with the DPR, the LPR's claimed statehood was itself the central fiction of a long-running information operation.

## Successor / related entities

- [[Donetsk People's Republic]] — the parallel Russian-backed proxy in the neighboring Donetsk region, founded in the same period and following the same trajectory; the two were consistently treated as a pair.
- The Russian Federation — into which the LPR's forces and administration were absorbed after 2022.

## Controversies / investigations

- The 2014 and 2022 "referendums" on independence and on joining Russia were condemned internationally as illegitimate and fraudulent. `#claim/disputed` — Russia frames them as self-determination; the international community rejects them.
- The LPR, like the DPR, has been the subject of human-rights reporting on detentions, abuses, and the suppression of dissent in occupied territory.
