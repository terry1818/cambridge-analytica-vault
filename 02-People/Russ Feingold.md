# Russ Feingold

## Summary

Russell Dana Feingold is an American politician, lawyer, and author. He served as a U.S. Senator from Wisconsin from 1993 to 2011, and was President of the [[American Constitution Society]] (ACS) from March 2020 until early 2025. In 2022, he co-authored *The Constitution in Jeopardy: An Unprecedented Effort to Rewrite Our Fundamental Law and What We Can Do About It* (PublicAffairs) with legal scholar [[Peter Prindiville]] — the book this vault cites as [[Source - Feingold 2022 - The Constitution in Jeopardy]]. Feingold is the most prominent political figure associated with the opposition to the right-wing [[Article V Convention]] campaign, and his book is the most widely cited progressive analysis of how the [[Convention of States Action]], [[Balanced Budget Amendment Task Force]], and allied organizations are attempting to use Article V's convention mechanism to structurally rewrite the U.S. Constitution.

## Key Facts

- **Born**: March 2, 1953, Janesville, Wisconsin.
- **Education**: B.A. University of Wisconsin-Madison; B.A. and M.A. University of Oxford (Rhodes Scholar); J.D. Harvard Law School (all with honors).
- **Senate career**: Wisconsin State Senator, 27th District (1983–1993); U.S. Senator (1993–2011, three terms). Defeated in 2010 by Ron Johnson (Tea Party wave). Lost a 2016 rematch to Johnson.
- **McCain-Feingold Act**: The Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act of 2002 — co-sponsored with Sen. John McCain — was the major campaign finance reform legislation of the era, banning "soft money" from national party committees and restricting corporate/union-funded "issue ads" near elections. Partially invalidated by *Citizens United v. FEC* (2010). Feingold and McCain received the 1999 John F. Kennedy Profile in Courage Award.
- **PATRIOT Act**: Feingold was the *only* U.S. senator to vote against the USA PATRIOT Act in 2001, on civil liberties grounds.
- **ACS presidency**: Appointed March 2020; announced step-down in late 2024/early 2025.
- **Article V work**: *The Constitution in Jeopardy* (2022) is the primary output. The book was produced with [[Peter Prindiville]] providing constitutional legal analysis. Feingold provided the political framing, the institutional platform of ACS, and his credibility as a former senator with deep constitutional expertise.

## Timeline

- **1983**: Elected to Wisconsin State Senate.
- **1993**: Elected to U.S. Senate.
- **1999**: McCain-Feingold profile in courage award.
- **2001**: Only senator to vote against PATRIOT Act.
- **2002**: Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act (McCain-Feingold) signed into law.
- **2010**: Defeated for re-election by Ron Johnson.
- **2011**: Returns to private life; takes various policy and legal roles.
- **2014–2016**: U.S. Special Envoy for the Great Lakes Region of Africa and the DRC (Obama administration).
- **2016**: Loses Wisconsin Senate rematch to Ron Johnson.
- **March 2020**: Appointed President of the [[American Constitution Society]].
- **2022**: *The Constitution in Jeopardy* published; becomes a key resource for Article V opposition advocacy.
- **Early 2025**: Announces step-down from ACS presidency.

## Relationships

- **[[Peter Prindiville]]**: Co-author of *The Constitution in Jeopardy*; Feingold provided political platform and framing, Prindiville the detailed constitutional analysis.
- **[[American Constitution Society]]**: Institutional home for his Article V opposition work.
- **[[Stanford Constitutional Law Center]]**: Feingold has been associated with Stanford Law School during and after his ACS tenure (Stanford Law hosted ACS events and covered his appointment).
- **John McCain**: Co-sponsor of BCRA 2002; collaborative relationship across the aisle.
- **Opposition: [[Convention of States Action]], [[Balanced Budget Amendment Task Force]], [[Mark Meckler]]**: The primary targets of his Article V opposition advocacy.

## Public Statements

Feingold has consistently framed the Article V convention campaign as "the most dangerous threat to our democracy you've never heard of." He has emphasized that a convention, once convened, could propose amendments to anything in the Constitution, including the Bill of Rights, the First Amendment's press protections, and the equal protection clause — none of which would be legally protected from a convention's attention.
