## Summary

Newton "Newt" Gingrich served as a Republican congressman from Georgia from 1979 to 1999 and as Speaker of the House from January 1995 to January 1999. He is the central case study in Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt's *How Democracies Die* for the proposition that democratic backsliding in the United States did not begin with Donald Trump — it began with the deliberate dismantling of informal congressional norms that had previously made bipartisan governance possible.

**Why the vault holds this figure as a comparative case.** Levitsky & Ziblatt argue that healthy democracies rest on two informal pillars — mutual toleration (accepting the legitimacy of opponents) and institutional forbearance (restraint in using legally available powers to the maximum). Gingrich is their canonical example of a politician who *consciously and strategically* attacked both. His GOPAC memos taught Republican candidates to describe Democrats with words like "corrupt," "sick," "traitors," and "anti-family." He weaponized ethics investigations as a political tool, used government shutdown as routine leverage, and pioneered the model of treating the opposition party as an enemy to be destroyed rather than a legitimate adversary. The vault uses Gingrich as the origin node for the norm-erosion pathway that eventually enables more extreme [[Hypothesis - Three-wing coalition]] behavior — distinguishing the gradual hollowing of norms from the acute crisis stage.

## Key facts

- Elected to Congress in 1978 after two failed bids; by the early 1980s had a deliberate long-game strategy to flip the House.
- Founded GOPAC in 1986 and used it to train an entire generation of Republican candidates in confrontational messaging.
- Filed over 80 ethics complaints against Democratic Speaker Jim Wright (1987–89), eventually forcing his resignation — a then-unprecedented use of ethics process as political weapon.
- Led the Republican Revolution of 1994, winning 54 House seats and ending 40 years of Democratic House control.
- As Speaker, engineered two government shutdowns (1995–96) over budget disputes with President Clinton — a hardball tactic previously considered off-limits.
- Was himself found guilty of an ethics violation in 1997 (providing false information in an ethics investigation) and fined $300,000.
- Resigned the speakership and his House seat in 1998 following Republican midterm losses and revelations of an extramarital affair while pursuing Clinton's impeachment.
- Ran for president in 2012, winning the South Carolina primary before losing to Mitt Romney.
- Remained an active Trump ally and media commentator; authored books promoting Trumpist politics through at least 2024.

## Public statements

- GOPAC memo "Language: A Key Mechanism of Control" (1990): distributed list of "optimistic positive governing words" for Republicans and "contrasting words" for opponents including: "decay," "sick," "pathetic," "corrupt," "traitors." (Tier-1: document is publicly archived)
- "I think one of the great problems we have in the Republican Party is that we don't encourage you to be nasty." — speech to College Republicans, 1978. (Tier-2: widely reported, original recording not universally verified)
