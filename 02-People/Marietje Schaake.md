## Summary

Marietje Schaake served as a Member of the European Parliament from 2009 to 2019, representing the Netherlands for Democrats 66 within the ALDE (Alliance of Liberals and Democrats for Europe) group. During her decade in parliament she was one of the most prominent voices for digital rights, platform accountability, and technology export controls. After leaving parliament she moved to Stanford University, where she holds posts at the Cyber Policy Center and the Institute for Human-Centered AI. In September 2024, she published *The Tech Coup: How to Save Democracy from Silicon Valley* (Princeton University Press), which argues that technology companies have seized functions that rightly belong to democratic states and proposes a framework of publicly governed digital infrastructure — the [[The public stack]] — as a corrective.

## Key facts

- Born 28 October 1978; raised in Leiden, Netherlands; studied at Haags Montessori Lyceum; BA from Wittenberg University (Ohio); sociology/new media from University of Amsterdam.
- Elected to European Parliament in 2009 at age 30; re-elected 2014; served two full terms.
- The Wall Street Journal called her "Europe's most wired politician."
- In parliament: vice-president of delegation for EU–US relations; worked on Digital Single Market, GDPR implementation, tech export controls to authoritarian regimes, and the forerunner legislation to the [[EU Digital Services Act]].
- Post-MEP: International Policy Director, Stanford Cyber Policy Center; International Policy Fellow, Stanford HAI.
- *The Tech Coup* (Princeton UP, 2024): argues Big Tech has conducted a de facto "coup" by occupying governance functions; proposes [[The public stack]] as a democratically accountable alternative.
- Has been an outspoken critic of Silicon Valley's alignment with the Trump 2.0 administration.

## Public statements

Schaake has argued that the concentration of power in private hands over critical digital infrastructure — AI systems, cloud computing, social media moderation — represents a fundamental threat to democratic self-governance. She has called for mandatory algorithmic audits, export controls on surveillance technology sold to authoritarian regimes, and the construction of a public digital stack as a counterweight to corporate monopoly.
