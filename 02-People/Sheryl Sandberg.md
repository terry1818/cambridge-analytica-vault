## Summary

Sheryl Sandberg was the operational architect of [[Facebook]]'s business model, serving as COO from 2008 to 2022. She was recruited by [[Mark Zuckerberg]] from Google, where she had built and scaled the company's advertising sales operation. At Facebook, she translated Zuckerberg's platform vision into a massive behavioral-advertising revenue engine — the same surveillance-capitalism infrastructure documented in Zuboff's analysis of the sector. During her tenure, Facebook grew from approximately 150 million users and minimal revenue to a company generating over $100 billion in annual advertising revenue. She simultaneously maintained Facebook's public relationships — with advertisers, politicians, regulators, and civil society — and cultivated an image as a progressive feminist leader through her 2013 bestseller *Lean In* and the associated LeanIn.Org organization. The gap between this public image and Facebook's documented internal behavior — particularly in the years of the [[Cambridge Analytica]] scandal, Russian election interference, and the [[Definers Public Affairs]] episode — became the central tension of Frenkel & Kang's account of her role.

## Key Facts

- Born August 28, 1969, Washington, D.C.; raised in North Miami Beach, Florida
- Harvard AB in Economics (1991, summa cum laude); Harvard MBA (1995, highest distinction)
- Chief of Staff to Treasury Secretary Lawrence Summers 1996–2001; focused on developing-world debt relief
- Joined Google in 2001; grew Google's advertising and publishing sales team from 4 to 4,000 employees
- Joined Facebook as COO March 2008; credited with building the advertising monetization that turned Facebook profitable
- Her book *Lean In: Women, Work, and the Will to Lead* (2013) became a bestselling manifesto for women's advancement in corporate environments; simultaneously made her the most prominent female executive in Silicon Valley
- Served as the primary public face of Facebook before Congress and in media during the [[Cambridge Analytica]] scandal (2018) and Russian election interference investigations
- Frenkel & Kang document that she played a central role in directing [[Definers Public Affairs]]' work against Facebook critics, including the Soros opposition-research campaign
- Sandberg and Zuckerberg both publicly denied knowing Definers was working on Soros; outgoing communications head Elliot Schrage took formal responsibility
- Disclosed in June 2022 that she was departing Facebook; attributed departure to desire for philanthropic focus; departure occurred amid reporting about her pressure on a British tabloid to suppress stories about then-boyfriend Activision CEO Bobby Kotick
- Left Facebook board of directors in 2022; subsequent public profile significantly reduced

## Relationships

- **[[Mark Zuckerberg]]** — 14-year working partnership; she provided operational and political credibility that Zuckerberg lacked; their public denials of Definers knowledge were aligned
- **[[Joel Kaplan]]** — Operated in parallel tracks; Sandberg managed external relations and advertiser relationships; Kaplan managed political and regulatory relationships
- **[[Alex Stamos]]** — Stamos's push for greater Russian-interference disclosure met resistance attributed in part to Sandberg's communications and advertiser-relations concerns
- **[[Definers Public Affairs]]** — Frenkel & Kang document Sandberg's central role in directing Definers' opposition research against Facebook critics
- **[[Nick Clegg]]** — Clegg was brought in partly to manage the European regulatory relationships that had grown beyond Sandberg's bandwidth

## Public Statements

Sandberg gave extensive Congressional testimony in September 2018 on Russian election interference and Facebook's response. She publicly denied knowledge of Definers' Soros work. In her June 2022 departure announcement, she cited desire to focus on philanthropy and Roe v. Wade as motivating factors.
