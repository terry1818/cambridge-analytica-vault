# Lou Engle

## Summary

Lou Engle is an American Charismatic Christian apostle within the [[New Apostolic Reformation]] and the founder of [[TheCall (prayer movement)]], a mass prayer-rally organization that drew hundreds of thousands of participants at its peak. Operating from a theology of "militant intercession" — the idea that sustained, physically intense prayer and fasting can shift spiritual and political realities — Engle built one of the most effective NAR mass-mobilization platforms of the 2000s and 2010s. He is politically aligned with the hard Christian right, having organized events supporting anti-abortion ballot measures, anti-LGBTQ legislation, and Republican political candidates including Rick Perry (2011) and Sarah Palin (2008). He attracted Southern Poverty Law Center designation as an extremist figure following his support for Uganda's anti-homosexuality bill (2010). In 2018 he formally dissolved TheCall and rebranded as Lou Engle Ministries.

## Key Facts

- Born October 9, 1952; married to Therese; seven children.
- Senior leader at the International House of Prayer (IHOP), Kansas City — the NAR's flagship prayer institution co-founded by Mike Bickle.
- Described by journalist Joe Conason as a "radical theocrat."
- An original member of C. Peter Wagner's inner circle of apostles and prophets, alongside [[Dutch Sheets]], [[Cindy Jacobs]], [[Lance Wallnau]], Chuck Pierce, and Ché Ahn.
- Featured in the 2006 documentary *Jesus Camp*, which brought his youth-mobilization methods to broad public attention.
- Known for his distinctive physical manner during prayer: a gravelly voice and vigorous rocking while speaking.
- Published *Possessing the Gates of the Enemy* and *Nazarite DNA* as core theological texts.
- Briefly a Washington DC roommate of then-Senator Sam Brownback; Brownback later spoke at TheCall Nashville; their association became a campaign issue in Brownback's 2010 Kansas gubernatorial race.
- In smaller settings, Engle's rhetoric "ventured into bloodlust," per SPLC; partnered with the militant group Joel's Army and employed heavy battle rhetoric and end-times theology.
