# Ludwig von Mises

## Summary

Ludwig von Mises (1881–1973) was an Austrian-born economist and classical-liberal philosopher whose work became one of the principal intellectual sources of twentieth-century American libertarianism. Educated at the University of Vienna under Carl Menger's influence and Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk's lectures, Mises argued that rational economic calculation is impossible under socialism because the absence of market prices for capital goods deprives planners of the information needed to allocate resources — the "socialist calculation problem." He was a professor at the University of Vienna (1913–1934), mentored [[Friedrich Hayek]], and, as a Jewish liberal, fled the Nazi advance: first to Geneva in 1934, then to the United States in 1940.

Mises matters to this vault as an upstream node in the patron-wing intellectual lineage. From a visiting position at New York University (1945–1969) — a post funded substantially by sympathetic businessmen rather than the university — Mises ran a private seminar that trained a generation of American libertarian leaders, including [[Murray Rothbard]], Israel Kirzner, Hans Sennholz, George Reisman, Ralph Raico, and Leonard Liggio. His treatise *Human Action* (1949) set out "praxeology," a deductive, a priori method that treats free-market conclusions as logically necessary rather than empirically contingent. That epistemology — economics as proof rather than as testable claim — feeds directly into the conviction, central to [[constitutional economics]] and the [[constitutional capture]] project, that markets are not merely efficient but that democratic interference with them is categorically illegitimate. The [[Mises Institute]], founded in 1982 in his name, carries the most radical strand of this tradition.

## Key facts

- **Role(s):** Economist; classical-liberal theorist; central figure of the Austrian School.
- **Affiliations:** University of Vienna; Graduate Institute of International Studies, Geneva; [[New York University]]; founding member of the [[Mont Pelerin Society]] (1947).
- **Active period:** ~1906–1969.
- **Known for:** The socialist-calculation argument (1920); *Liberalism* (1927); *Human Action* (1949); the NYU seminar that seeded postwar American libertarianism.

## Relationships

- **[[Friedrich Hayek]]** — Mises's most prominent protégé in Vienna; together they anchored the Austrian School and co-founded the Mont Pelerin Society.
- **[[Murray Rothbard]]** — NYU-seminar student who radicalized Mises's ideas into anarcho-capitalism; co-founder of the Mises Institute and the Cato Institute.
- **Carl Menger / Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk** — the first-generation Austrian economists whose work Mises built upon.
- **[[Mont Pelerin Society]]** — the international network through which Mises's classical liberalism linked to the postwar free-market movement and, later, [[Koch Network]] funding.
- **[[Mises Institute]]** — the Alabama think tank carrying his name and the most uncompromising branch of his tradition.

## Public statements

- "Economic Calculation in the Socialist Commonwealth" (1920): argued that without market prices, socialist planning cannot rationally allocate capital.
- *Human Action* (1949): "Economics is a theoretical science and as such abstains from any judgment of value" — yet the praxeological method renders laissez-faire conclusions deductively certain.
