## Summary

Ross Perot was a Texas billionaire and technology entrepreneur whose two presidential campaigns exposed the breadth of voter alienation from mainstream politics in the early 1990s. His 1992 independent campaign — conducted partly through paid 30-minute television infomercials — drew 18.9% of the popular vote, the strongest third-party showing since 1912. He founded the Reform Party in 1995 as an institutional vehicle, but the party was subsequently captured by [[Pat Buchanan]] in 2000, marking the point at which economic-populist discontent was channelled toward explicit cultural nationalism. John Ganz's *When the Clock Broke* treats Perot as evidence that the post-Cold War consensus was already crumbling before Clinton's "triangulation" strategy could stabilise it.

## Key facts

- Born 27 June 1930, Texarkana, Texas; died 9 July 2019.
- US Naval Academy graduate; worked as IBM salesman before founding Electronic Data Systems (EDS) in 1962. Sold EDS to General Motors in 1984 for approximately $2.5 billion.
- Founded Perot Systems in 1988; sold to Dell in 2009 for $3.9 billion.
- 1992: ran as independent; received 19,741,065 votes — 18.9% of the popular vote — without winning any electoral votes. Debate appearances against Bush and Clinton are credited with legitimising the campaign.
- Withdrew from the race in July 1992 citing alleged Republican dirty-tricks plots, then re-entered October 1992 — a move that damaged credibility but did not prevent a strong finish.
- 1995: founded the Reform Party with a focus on fiscal conservatism, balanced budgets, campaign finance reform, and trade protectionism — an agenda that blended left and right populism.
- 1996: Reform Party nominee; received 8,085,402 votes (8.0%), excluded from presidential debates after the bipartisan Commission on Presidential Debates set a 15% polling threshold.
- Supported NAFTA opposition, a signature issue that overlapped with Buchanan's protectionism (though Perot's framing was economic rather than nativist — his famous "giant sucking sound" warning about NAFTA jobs going to Mexico).
- The Reform Party was subsequently torn apart: [[Pat Buchanan]] won its 2000 nomination, and Jesse Ventura's wing broke away; the party never recovered.

## Relationships

- [[Pat Buchanan]] — Buchanan took over Perot's Reform Party vehicle in 2000, redirecting its populist energy toward explicit cultural nationalism.
- [[Middle American Radicals]] — Perot's coalition overlapped analytically with the MARs category: economically anxious middle-income voters alienated from both parties.
- [[Paleoconservatism]] — Perot was not a paleocon ideologically, but his campaigns demonstrated the electoral reservoir that paleocons theorised.
