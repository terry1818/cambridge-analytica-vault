## Summary

Slobodan Milošević is the canonical comparative case for authoritarian democratic breakdown through ethnic-nationalist mobilization. Beginning as a Communist Party apparatchik, he pivoted to Serbian ethnic nationalism in 1987 when he discovered its electoral power in Kosovo, and subsequently used state media control, demographic grievance, and institutional capture to consolidate authoritarian rule across the disintegrating Federal Republic of Yugoslavia. His career illustrates the mechanism Barbara Walter documents in [[Source - Walter 2022 - How Civil Wars Start]]: an [[Anocracy]] midway between democracy and autocracy, where leaders facing electoral competition weaponize ethnic identity to outbid rivals — producing civil war as a predictable output. He was the first sitting head of state to be indicted by an international war crimes tribunal (ICTY, 1999).

## Key Facts

- Born in Požarevac, Yugoslavia, 1941; joined the Communist Party at age 18; rose through party and banking structures to become head of a major Belgrade bank.
- The 1987 pivot is decisive: visiting Kosovo to calm ethnic Serb protesters, he told the crowd "No one should dare to beat you" — a moment captured on television that made him a nationalist hero overnight.
- Became president of the Serbian Republic in 1989; used the position to revoke the constitutional autonomy of Kosovo and Vojvodina, triggering secessionist movements in Slovenia, Croatia, and Macedonia.
- Gained formal federal presidency in 1997, following constitutional limits in Serbia. Held power continuously 1989–2000.
- Weaponized state broadcaster RTS (Radio Television of Serbia) systematically, turning it into a vehicle for ethnic nationalist propaganda; this media-capture strategy is documented as a precursor to the [[Firehose of falsehood]] technique.
- Supported and coordinated Serbian paramilitary forces during the [[Yugoslav Wars]], including operations that resulted in the Srebrenica massacre (1995), categorized as genocide by the ICTY and ICJ.
- Lost the September 2000 presidential election to Vojislav Koštunica; attempted to dispute results; mass popular uprising (the "Bulldozer Revolution," October 5, 2000) forced him from power.
- Extradited to The Hague in 2001; died of heart failure in his ICTY detention cell, March 11, 2006, before verdict was reached in his trial.

## Open Questions

- ICTY prosecutors argued at trial that evidence established his command responsibility for genocide; the chamber did not deliver a final verdict. Counterfactual guilt remains a matter of scholarly debate, not established legal fact.
- To what extent did Western governments' initial accommodation of Milošević in the Dayton process (1995) enable subsequent atrocities?
