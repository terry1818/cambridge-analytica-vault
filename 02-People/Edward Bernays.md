## Summary

Edward Bernays is universally recognized as the founding architect of modern public relations and a principal theorist of mass persuasion. The nephew of Sigmund Freud (his mother was Freud's sister; his father was the brother of Freud's wife Martha), Bernays systematically applied psychoanalytic theory to public opinion management. His books *Crystallizing Public Opinion* (1923) and *Propaganda* (1928) defined the field and remain foundational texts. His concept of the "engineering of consent" — the deliberate manipulation of mass opinion through psychological technique — is the intellectual ancestor of every modern influence operation, including [[psychographic microtargeting]] and the methods documented in [[Cambridge Analytica]]'s work. Renée DiResta opens *Invisible Rulers* by invoking Bernays as the origin point for understanding the propaganda continuum that runs to the present.

## Key facts

- Born November 22, 1891 in Vienna; emigrated with family to New York as an infant. Died March 9, 1995 at age 103.
- Worked for the US Committee on Public Information (Creel Commission) during World War I — the first large-scale government propaganda apparatus in American history. This experience convinced him that mass persuasion techniques effective in wartime could be applied commercially and politically in peacetime.
- Arranged the US translation and publication of Freud's works; applied Freud's theories of the unconscious to advertising and public persuasion campaigns throughout his career.
- *Crystallizing Public Opinion* (1923): first systematic theory of public relations as a profession.
- *Propaganda* (1928): argued that the conscious manipulation of public opinion was a necessary feature of democratic society — that "invisible governors" organize public consent on behalf of elites.
- Coined the phrase **"engineering of consent"** (also the title of a 1947 essay and 1955 book) — the deliberate shaping of public attitudes through systematic psychological technique.
- Notable campaigns:
  - *Torches of Freedom* (1929): rebranded cigarette smoking for women as a feminist act on behalf of American Tobacco; dramatically expanded the female smoking market.
  - *United Fruit Company* (1950s): PR campaign that helped construct the political justification for US support of the 1954 CIA-orchestrated overthrow of Guatemalan president Jacobo Árbenz — one of the earliest documented intersections of PR, corporate interest, and covert political action.
- His techniques were later adopted by the Nazi propaganda ministry; Joseph Goebbels is reported (by Bernays's own account) to have had Bernays's books on his shelves.
- DiResta and other modern disinformation scholars cite Bernays to establish the historical continuity of influence operation logic: the tools change (pamphlets → broadcast → social media → AI), the underlying theory of manufacturing consent does not.
