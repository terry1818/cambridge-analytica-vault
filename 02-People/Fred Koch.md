## Summary

Fred Chase Koch (23 September 1900 – 17 November 1967) was a chemical engineer, oil entrepreneur, and anti-communist activist whose business and ideological legacy directly produced both [[Koch Industries]] and the Koch family's decades-long campaign to reshape American politics. He developed a more efficient thermal cracking process for refining crude oil in 1927, which formed the foundation of the family fortune. His time working in Stalin's Soviet Union in the late 1920s—building oil refineries under contract—left him with a visceral hatred of communism and government control. He became a co-founding member of the [[John Birch Society]] in 1958 and remained active in it until his death. Christopher Leonard's *Kochland* documents how Fred's anti-statist ideology was transmitted directly to Charles Koch, shaping the political program the Koch network would pursue for the next half-century.

## Key Facts

- Earned a degree in chemical engineering from MIT (1922).
- In 1927, developed an improved thermal cracking process for oil refining that enabled smaller refiners to compete with Standard Oil; major oil companies used their patent leverage to exclude him from the US market, forcing early business development abroad and in the Soviet Union.
- 1928–1929: Traveled to the Soviet Union to build oil refineries under contract; came away with deep hostility to the Soviet system.
- 1940: Partnered to form the Wood River Oil and Refining Company, the direct precursor to Koch Industries.
- 1958: Co-founded the [[John Birch Society]] alongside Robert Welch; served on its National Council.
- Published a 39-page anti-communist pamphlet, *A Business Man Looks at Communism*, warning of communist infiltration of American institutions.
- His will left the business to his four sons; Charles Koch took operational control after Fred's death in 1967.
- Evidence tier on founding roles: **Tier 1** (documented in corporate records, JBS records, and Leonard *Kochland*).

## Relationships

- **[[Charles Koch]]** — eldest son; inherited anti-statist ideology and operational control of the business; transformed Fred's political instincts into a systematic movement-building program.
- **David Koch** — younger son; became the public face of Koch political advocacy.
- **[[John Birch Society]]** — co-founder; the JBS's anti-communist, anti-government framing was the template for Fred's political worldview.
- **Robert Welch** — fellow JBS founder.
- **[[Koch Industries]]** — the company Fred built became the financial engine of the family's political influence.
- **[[Koch Network]]** — the donor-seminar and think-tank infrastructure Charles built directly extended Fred's anti-government ideology into systematic policy operations.

## Public Statements

From *A Business Man Looks at Communism* (c. 1960): Fred warned that communism was infiltrating American institutions including schools, churches, and media — a framing that anticipated later right-wing culture-war themes. The pamphlet is documented in Leonard's *Kochland* and JBS archives.

## Open Questions
