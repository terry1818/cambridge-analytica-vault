# Eitan Hersh

## Summary

American political scientist; Professor of Political Science at **Tufts University** (previously Yale). Author of **[[Source Hersh Hacking the Electorate 2015|Hacking the Electorate: How Campaigns Perceive Voters]]** (Cambridge University Press, 2015) — a pre-CA-scandal scholarly account of how US campaigns actually use voter data, based on extensive field research with campaign operatives. Hersh's empirical work makes him the **most cited academic skeptic** of post-2018 "CA decided the 2016 election" narratives. He has consistently argued that:

- Real-world campaign data operations are **far less precise** than the marketing claims suggest
- Psychographic targeting in the [[OCEAN Model]] sense has **weak empirical support** at the persuasion-scale (vs. the segmentation-scale)
- The 2016 outcome was **overdetermined** by factors well beyond any single targeting vendor's contribution

## Key works

- **Hacking the Electorate** (Cambridge University Press, 2015) — *the* baseline work. Pre-CA scandal.
- **Politics is for Power** (Scribner, 2020) — argues that small-d democratic participation matters more than "hobbyist" political engagement; tangentially relevant to influence-ops framing
- Multiple peer-reviewed papers on voter behavior, campaign data, and electoral analytics

## Notable analytical positions

- Targeted advertising in elections has **smaller effect sizes** than vendor claims suggest
- Most campaign data operations target turnout and persuasion among **already-likely voters of one's own party**, not flipping persuadables at scale
- Psychographic models add **marginal lift** over standard demographic + voting-history models — not a categorical advance
- CA's actual contribution to Trump 2016 is **operationally distinct** from CA's marketing claims about that contribution
