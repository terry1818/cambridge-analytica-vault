# Brendan Nyhan

## Summary

American political scientist; **Professor of Government at Dartmouth College** (previously University of Michigan and Ford School of Public Policy). One of the **leading empirical researchers** on misinformation effects, fact-checking, and the operational impact of online political communications. Nyhan is, alongside [[Eitan Hersh]], the **central academic voice** in the [[Lens - Disputed Claims|disputed-claims]] thread on the actual electoral impact of [[Cambridge Analytica]]-style microtargeting. His empirical work consistently finds that:

- **Misinformation exposure is concentrated** in narrow heavy-consumer audiences rather than broadly diffused
- **Microtargeting effects are bounded** by base rates and message-channel saturation
- **Fact-checking has measurable but small persuasive effects** — the original "backfire effect" finding (where corrections sometimes increase rather than decrease misperceptions) has been **substantially revised downward** in later replications, of which Nyhan was an author
- **CA's specific electoral impact** is empirically separable from broader claims about platform-era political communication

Nyhan's career has involved a **distinctive intellectual honesty pattern**: he and co-authors have **explicitly revised down their own earlier findings** when subsequent replications found smaller effects — modeling the academic-correction behavior that distinguishes empirical research from advocacy.

## Significance for the vault

Nyhan plays a complementary role to [[Eitan Hersh]] in the vault's empirical-skepticism layer:

- **Hersh** focuses on **how campaigns actually use data** (operational realism in voter targeting)
- **Nyhan** focuses on **how messages actually affect audiences** (operational realism in persuasion outcomes)

Together they form the **academic dual-skeptic foundation** for [[Lens - Disputed Claims]] and the [[Methodology - Verifying a CA claim]] verification methodology. Their work is what allows the vault to **distinguish maximalist post-2018 narratives from empirically grounded analysis** — a distinction critical to treating this as research rather than advocacy.

## Key works (selected)

- **"When Corrections Fail"** (Nyhan & Reifler, 2010) — original "backfire effect" finding
- **Subsequent replication work** (Wood & Porter 2019; Nyhan et al. 2020-2022) — revised the backfire-effect framework substantially
- **Multiple peer-reviewed papers** on fact-checking effects across US political contexts
- **2017-2024 research** on misinformation exposure patterns — finding **heavy concentration in narrow audiences** rather than mass diffusion
- **Op-ed and public-facing work** at Upshot (NYT), MonkeyCage, and elsewhere — translating empirical findings for public-discourse contexts

## Notable analytical positions

- **Misinformation consumption is concentrated** — a small high-consumption audience accounts for most exposure
- **Fact-checking has small but real effects** on belief, somewhat less on behavior
- **Microtargeting persuasion effects** are **bounded by message-saturation and base rates** in field-deployment contexts
- **CA's effects on 2016 outcome** are **empirically separable** from broader narrative — and on the available evidence, **bounded by what's plausible given microtargeting effect sizes more generally**
- **Methodological discipline:** the framing of "what's the counterfactual?" is central — Nyhan's work consistently asks what difference a specific intervention made *relative to what would have happened otherwise*
