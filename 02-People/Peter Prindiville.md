# Peter Prindiville

## Summary

Peter Prindiville is an American legal scholar and a Non-Resident Fellow at the [[Stanford Constitutional Law Center]] at Stanford Law School. He is best known as the co-author, with former U.S. Senator [[Russ Feingold]], of *The Constitution in Jeopardy: An Unprecedented Effort to Rewrite Our Fundamental Law and What We Can Do About It* (PublicAffairs, 2022) — the book this vault cites as [[Source - Feingold 2022 - The Constitution in Jeopardy]]. Prindiville served as the principal legal researcher on the book, providing the detailed constitutional analysis underlying Feingold's more public political narrative. His work is the primary substantive source for the vault's coverage of the [[Article V aggregation problem]], the [[Article V delegate-selection vacuum]], and the legal significance of [[Hawke v Smith 1920]] and [[Dillon v Gloss 1921]] in the convention debate.

## Key Facts

- Non-Resident Fellow at the [[Stanford Constitutional Law Center]].
- Co-authored *The Constitution in Jeopardy* with [[Russ Feingold]] in 2022, published by PublicAffairs (Hachette Book Group).
- The book is the most comprehensive recent legal treatment of risks inherent in the Article V convention mechanism, with particular focus on: the [[Runaway Convention]] risk; the [[Article V aggregation problem]]; the [[Article V delegate-selection vacuum]]; the SCOTUS case law (*Hawke v. Smith*, *Dillon v. Gloss*, *Coleman v. Miller*); and the specific campaigns of [[Convention of States Action]] and the [[Balanced Budget Amendment Task Force]].

- Described in the book's framing as bringing the legal/constitutional expertise while Feingold brought the political experience and platform.

## Public Statements

Prindiville has stated publicly (in ABA podcast appearances, 2022) that his central concern is who would actually hold the pen at a constitutional convention: "In law, the most powerful person is the one holding the pen." He has emphasized that Article V provides no guidance on delegate selection, and that theories advocating for convention advocates to control that selection have "rushed in" to fill the constitutional vacuum. This framing is the basis of the [[Article V delegate-selection vacuum]] concept-node.
