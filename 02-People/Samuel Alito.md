# Samuel Alito

## Summary

Samuel Anthony Alito Jr. (born 1950) is an Associate Justice of the U.S. Supreme Court, appointed by President George W. Bush in 2006 to succeed Justice Sandra Day O'Connor. A former federal prosecutor and Third Circuit appellate judge, Alito was confirmed on a near-party-line 58–42 vote and quickly became one of the Court's most reliably conservative members. He is best known for writing the 5–4 (effective) majority opinion in *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization* (2022), which overturned *Roe v. Wade* and *Planned Parenthood v. Casey*, ending the constitutional right to abortion — the single most consequential rollback of a recognized constitutional right in modern U.S. history.

Alito matters to this vault as a central figure in the "constitutional capture" project: the long, donor-financed effort, channeled through the [[Federalist Society]] judicial pipeline, to remake American constitutional law. *Dobbs* was the project's signal victory. Alito's jurisprudence in religious-liberty, gun-rights, and administrative-law cases has consistently advanced the network's agenda. Like fellow Justice [[Clarence Thomas]], Alito became the subject of investigative reporting — ProPublica documented in 2023 that Alito accepted an undisclosed luxury Alaska fishing trip arranged in part by hedge-fund billionaire Paul Singer, whose interests later came before the Court. Alito disputed that disclosure was required and declined to recuse. Separate reporting in 2024 about provocative flags flown at Alito's residences raised further questions about the appearance of partisanship. The vault treats *Dobbs* and the disclosure facts as documented, while noting that questions of influence and intent remain interpretive.
