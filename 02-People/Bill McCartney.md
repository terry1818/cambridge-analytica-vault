## Summary

Bill McCartney spent thirteen seasons as head football coach at the University of Colorado, winning three consecutive Big Eight Conference titles and the 1990 AP national championship. In 1990, while still serving as head coach, he founded [[Promise Keepers]], an evangelical men's organization whose stadium-filling events became one of the most significant mass religious mobilizations in late 20th century American Christianity.

Promise Keepers was organized around a set of seven promises that male participants pledged to keep — emphasizing spiritual leadership of their families, sexual purity, racial reconciliation, and commitment to the local church. The movement drew hundreds of thousands of men to sports venues in the 1990s, culminating in the "Stand in the Gap" gathering in Washington, D.C., in October 1997, which drew what organizers described as over one million attendees (widely reported as 700,000+).

Kristin Du Mez's [[Source - Du Mez 2020 - Jesus and John Wayne]] analyzes Promise Keepers as a pivotal institution in the consolidation of militarized evangelical masculinity as a cultural and political force — one that channeled evangelical men into a framework of hierarchical authority, masculine spiritual leadership over women and families, and political engagement on culturally conservative terms. McCartney's vision of Christian men reclaiming cultural dominance through personal conversion and organized mass action is a direct antecedent to the more overtly political masculine Christianity of the 2010s–2020s.

McCartney publicly opposed Colorado's Amendment 2 gay rights protections in 1992, referring to homosexuality as "an abomination against almighty God" at public events held in CU facilities — a statement that drew institutional controversy. He retired from coaching in 1994 to lead Promise Keepers full-time.

## Key Facts

- Born August 22, 1940, Riverview, Michigan; died January 10, 2025
- B.A., University of Missouri (football scholarship)
- Head coach, University of Colorado, 1982–1994; record 93–55–5
- Won three consecutive Big Eight titles (1989–1991); 1990 AP national champion
- Founded Promise Keepers, 1990, while still coaching at CU
- Publicly opposed Colorado Amendment 2 (1992), calling homosexuality "an abomination"
- Retired from coaching 1994 to lead Promise Keepers full-time
- "Stand in the Gap" Washington, D.C. gathering, October 1997: 700,000+ men
- Promise Keepers' racial reconciliation emphasis drew resistance from ~40% of attendees (1996 surveys)
- Inducted into College Football Hall of Fame (coach), 2013
- Promise Keepers experienced financial difficulties and downsizing in the late 1990s–2000s
