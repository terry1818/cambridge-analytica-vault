## Summary

Lyman Stewart represents the earliest and most consequential fusion in American history between the oil industry and conservative Protestant religious institution-building. His co-founding of Union Oil Company of California gave him the capital to finance, at massive scale, the ideological project of defending orthodox Protestantism against modernism, higher biblical criticism, and Darwinism. The twelve-volume essay series he bankrolled — [[The Fundamentals]] (1910–1915) — distributed over three million volumes free of charge to clergy and religious educators across the English-speaking world, effectively naming and constituting the fundamentalist movement. Stewart is thus the upstream ancestor of the corporate-Christian alliance that [[J. Howard Pew]] and [[James Fifield]] later formalized in the 1930s–50s.

## Key facts

- Co-founded Hardison and Stewart Oil Company (later Union Oil Company of California, eventually Unocal) in the late 19th century; built it into one of California's major petroleum enterprises.
- Beginning in 1909, conceived and funded the publication of *The Fundamentals: A Testimony to the Truth*, a twelve-volume series of ninety essays defending orthodox Protestant doctrine. He and his brother Milton Stewart each contributed $150,000 to the project (approximately $8–9 million in 2025 dollars combined).
- The essays were distributed entirely free of charge to every English-speaking Protestant pastor, theology student, missionary, and YMCA/YWCA secretary whose address could be obtained — an early example of mass ideological distribution funded by private capital.
- In 1908, with preacher Thomas Horton, founded the Bible Institute of Los Angeles (Biola), which became a flagship institution of conservative evangelical education.
- Stewart's ideological motivation was specifically defensive: he sought to counter the encroachments of theological liberalism and higher biblical criticism within mainline Protestant denominations.
- Theologically a Presbyterian; politically a capitalist who believed Christian orthodoxy and free enterprise were mutually reinforcing.

## Relationships

- **[[The Fundamentals]]** — the primary intellectual product of his patronage; gave the movement its name and doctrinal framework.
- **A.C. Dixon** — the editor commissioned to execute The Fundamentals project.
- **Biola University (Bible Institute of Los Angeles)** — the educational institution Stewart founded.
- **[[National Association of Evangelicals]]** — indirectly, as the NAE (founded 1942) was an heir to the fundamentalist-turned-evangelical tradition The Fundamentals helped create.
- **[[J. Howard Pew]]** — the next generation of the oil-Christian patron model; Pew explicitly built on the institutional tradition Stewart established.
- **[[H.L. Hunt]]** — a later Texas oil-industry patron of the religious right, operating in the same structural role though with different theological and political emphases.

## Open questions

- Whether Stewart coordinated with other major industrial interests in funding The Fundamentals, or acted independently, is not firmly established.
- The relationship between Stewart's Biola and later institutions in the evangelical education network (Fuller Seminary, etc.) deserves further mapping.
