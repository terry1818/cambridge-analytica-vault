## Summary

Jonathan Rauch is a senior fellow at the Brookings Institution and longtime contributing writer at *The Atlantic*, best known for *The Constitution of Knowledge: A Defense of Truth* (2021). The book argues that liberal democracy rests on an underappreciated epistemic infrastructure — a social system of rules, institutions, and norms for turning disagreement into shared reality — which he calls the "Constitution of Knowledge." Rauch's core warning is that this infrastructure is under coordinated attack from two directions simultaneously: disinformation and troll culture (weaponized lies) and ideological conformism and cancel culture (self-censorship). Both, he argues, corrode the epistemic conditions democracy requires. The vault's information-warfare and disinformation clusters draw directly on this framework when analyzing [[Coordinated Inauthentic Behavior]], [[Firehose of falsehood]], and [[Bespoke realities]].

## Key Facts

- Senior fellow at the Brookings Institution; his work sits at the intersection of political philosophy, free speech, and the sociology of knowledge.
- Long-time contributing writer at *The Atlantic*; earlier career at *National Journal*.
- *The Constitution of Knowledge* (2021) builds on his earlier *Kindly Inquisitors: The New Attacks on Free Thought* (1993), extending its liberal epistemological framework to the social-media era.
- Argues that the "two insurgencies" — viral disinformation and forced conformity — use parallel techniques to destroy shared epistemic ground, even though they are usually treated as opposite pathologies.
- Defines the "Constitution of Knowledge" as the informal norms and institutions (peer review, journalism, courts, political debate) that adjudicate factual claims and build provisional consensus — the epistemic analog of political constitutionalism.
- As a gay rights advocate and self-described member of a sexual minority, Rauch notes the historical irony that some progressive activists now deploy socially coercive tactics once used against LGBTQ people.
- Jonathan Haidt described *The Constitution of Knowledge* as "among the three or so most important books I have read in the last five years."
