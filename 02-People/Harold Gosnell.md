## Summary

Harold Foote Gosnell was an American political scientist at the University of Chicago who, in 1924, ran what is now recognized as the first randomized field experiment on voter mobilization in political science. He randomly assigned Chicago households to receive get-out-the-vote mailers — including materials depicting non-voters as unpatriotic "slackers" — and measured turnout effects using post-election voter rolls. His 1927 book *Getting Out the Vote: An Experiment in the Stimulation of Voting* documented the methodology and results. Gosnell's work was largely forgotten for decades until the revival of experimental political science associated with [[Gerber and Green field experiments]] in the late 1990s. [[Source - Issenberg 2012 - The Victory Lab]] uses Gosnell's 1924 study as the historical starting point for the lineage of experimental GOTV research.

## Key Facts

- **1924 Chicago experiment**: Gosnell designed a randomized experiment set around the November 1924 general election. Households were randomly assigned either to receive a GOTV mailing or to a control group; turnout was measured from public voter-roll records. This design mirrors modern RCT logic.
- **Materials used**: The mailers included political cartoons depicting non-voters as unpatriotic. This constitutes an early example of identity-based and social-norm messaging — the same mechanism later examined by [[Todd Rogers]] and collaborators.
- **Co-authored foundational text**: With Charles Merriam, he co-authored *Non-Voting, Causes and Methods of Control* (1924), which framed non-participation as a civic and scientific problem.
- **Scholarly obscurity**: Gosnell's experimental approach did not spawn an immediate tradition; quantitative political science drifted toward surveys and statistical modeling. The experimental tradition was essentially reinvented when Gerber and Green ran their 1998 New Haven study without full awareness of Gosnell's precedent.
- **Legacy**: Widely cited since 2000 as the "first" field experimenter in political science. The J-PAL (Poverty Action Lab) and EGAP (Evidence in Governance and Politics) literatures both acknowledge him as a progenitor.
