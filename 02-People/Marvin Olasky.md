## Summary

Marvin Olasky is the central intellectual figure behind "compassionate conservatism," the domestic policy framework George W. Bush made a signature of his 2000 presidential campaign and presidency. Olasky coined the term and developed its intellectual basis primarily through his 1992 book *The Tragedy of American Compassion*, which argued that secular government welfare programs had displaced the faith-based, personal-relationship approach to poverty that he saw as historically more effective. That argument became the intellectual foundation for Bush's faith-based and community initiatives.

As editor of WORLD magazine from 1994 to 2021, Olasky shaped one of the most widely read evangelical Christian news publications in the United States, combining doctrinal conservatism with actual news reporting on international crises and policy debates. Anne Nelson's [[Source Anne Nelson Shadow Network 2019]] documents Olasky and WORLD within the broader Council for National Policy network as a key node in the evangelical media infrastructure.

Olasky's personal biography — from Judaism through atheism and communism to evangelical Christianity and political conservatism — became itself a narrative central to his public presentation.

## Key Facts

- Born 1950; raised in a Jewish family in Boston area
- Graduated from Yale University (A.B., 1971); Ph.D. in American Culture, University of Michigan, 1976
- Early career: reporter at Boston Globe; brief membership in Communist Party USA (which he later publicly renounced)
- Converted to evangelical Christianity in the early 1970s
- Joined WORLD magazine 1990; became editor 1994; editor-in-chief 2001
- *The Tragedy of American Compassion* (1992) — argued private charity and faith-based organizations were superior to government welfare; foundational text for "compassionate conservatism"
- Coined "compassionate conservatism" as a term; the phrase was adopted by George W. Bush's 2000 campaign and became White House policy
- Long academic career: University of Texas at Austin (1983–2004), The King's College, Patrick Henry College, World Journalism Institute
- Departed WORLD magazine in 2021 amid reported disagreements with WORLD's board
- Connected to CNP network per Anne Nelson's research

## Public Statements
