## Summary

Alexander Lukashenko has ruled Belarus since winning its first presidential election in 1994. He rapidly consolidated power, expanding presidential authority through a 1996 constitutional referendum and subordinating parliament, courts, and the security apparatus — still named the KGB — to his personal rule. Western analysts and Lukashenko himself have acknowledged the system's authoritarian character. He is a core case in Anne Applebaum's *Autocracy, Inc.* (2024): a regime that survives not through ideology or popular legitimacy but through mutual support networks connecting him to Russia, and to a lesser extent to China and Iran.

**Vault relevance — comparative case:** Lukashenko illustrates Applebaum's thesis that contemporary autocracies survive through transnational support — economic bailouts, security assistance, propaganda supply, and diplomatic cover — rather than through internal strength alone. His Belarus is the clearest European example of a state that would likely not survive without external autocratic sponsorship.

## Key facts

- Came to power as an anti-corruption outsider from collective-farm management; initially lacked deep party backing.
- 1996 referendum expanded presidential term limits, granted decree powers, and allowed appointment of one-third of the upper legislative chamber.
- Economy remained heavily state-directed and dependent on Russian energy subsidies throughout his rule.
- Security services retain the Soviet KGB name and function; political prisoners have been held throughout his tenure.
- Self-described as "authoritarian" in a 2022 interview with France 24.
- The August 2020 presidential election, in which he claimed ~80% of the vote, was rejected as fraudulent by the EU, US, and most international observers; the genuine tally was not officially released.
- Post-2020 crackdown: over 27,000 detained, hundreds tortured, multiple deaths; Sviatlana Tsikhanouskaya emerged as opposition leader in exile.
- Russia replaced departing Belarusian state journalists who refused to broadcast false election results with Russian broadcasters.
- Belarus allowed Russian forces to stage on its territory for the February 2022 invasion of Ukraine.
- January 2025: declared winner of another presidential election; opposition in exile called it fraudulent.

## Relationships

- [[Vladimir Putin]] — primary external patron; security, economic, and diplomatic lifeline post-2020
- [[Sviatlana Tsikhanouskaya]] — principal opposition figure, in exile since 2020
- Belarusian KGB — domestic enforcement arm; retains Soviet branding

## Public statements

- "I'm authoritarian. Yes. Just as the Belarusian state is authoritarian." — *France 24*, July 2022 (widely reported).
- Has repeatedly framed protests as Western-orchestrated destabilization operations.

## Open questions

- Succession: no clear heir designated; regime stability post-Lukashenko is a significant unknown.
- Degree of formal vs. informal subordination to Moscow — some analysts argue Belarus has become effectively a Russian protectorate since 2020.
- Whether any element of genuine domestic support base survives among rural and state-employed populations.
