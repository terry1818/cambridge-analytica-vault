# Radovan Karadzic

## Summary

Radovan Karadžić (born 1945) was a Bosnian Serb politician — originally a psychiatrist and poet — who led the self-proclaimed [[Republika Srpska]] during the Bosnian War (1992–1995) and was subsequently convicted of genocide and crimes against humanity by the [[International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia]] (ICTY). He appears in this vault as a comparative case in two ways. First, his career demonstrates how sustained ethnonationalist propaganda — the construction of an existential threat narrative around an ethnic and religious "other" — provides the ideological infrastructure for mass atrocity. Second, his eventual capture, trial, and conviction is one of the rare instances where an international legal process formally assigned individual command responsibility for that atrocity, making him a reference point for what accountability can (and cannot) look like.

Karadžić co-founded the Serbian Democratic Party (SDS) and, as Bosnia and Herzegovina moved toward independence from Yugoslavia, led the Bosnian Serb political project to carve out an ethnically Serb territory by force. As president of Republika Srpska and supreme commander of its army, he bore political and command responsibility for a campaign of "ethnic cleansing" against Bosniak (Bosnian Muslim) and Croat civilians, the years-long siege of Sarajevo, and the July 1995 massacre at Srebrenica, where Bosnian Serb forces killed more than 7,000–8,000 Bosniak men and boys at a UN-designated "safe area." Indicted in 1995, he evaded arrest for 13 years, living in Belgrade in disguise under the alias "Dragan Dabić." Captured in 2008, he was convicted in 2016 and ultimately sentenced to life imprisonment.

## Key facts

- **Role(s):** Psychiatrist and poet by training; SDS co-founder and leader; wartime president of Republika Srpska; supreme commander of its army.

## Relationships

- **Reports to / worked under:** Politically aligned with Slobodan Milošević's Serbia, which materially and politically backed the Bosnian Serb war effort.
- **Subordinates / hired:** Ratko Mladić — commander of the Army of Republika Srpska, separately convicted by the ICTY of genocide; the political-military pairing of Karadžić and Mladić directed the Srebrenica operation.
- **Opponents / accusers:** The [[International Criminal Tribunal for the former Yugoslavia]], which indicted, tried, and convicted him; the Bosniak survivors and victims' families who testified against him.

## Public statements

- In the months before the war, Karadžić publicly warned the Bosnian parliament that Bosnian independence could lead Muslims to "disappear" — rhetoric the ICTY and historians cite as part of the documented propaganda groundwork for the violence that followed.
- During his trial he denied responsibility and presented himself as a peacemaker — a self-characterization the tribunal rejected.

## Open questions

- **Propaganda-to-atrocity mechanism:** The Bosnian case is heavily studied for how state and party media, and leadership rhetoric, manufactured ethnic fear before the killing. The vault uses it as evidence that dehumanizing information campaigns are a precondition, not an aftereffect, of mass atrocity — though the precise causal weight of propaganda versus other factors is an academic debate.
- **The limits of international justice:** Karadžić's 13 years as a fugitive, and the decades-long, expensive ICTY process, raise the question of how effective and timely international accountability mechanisms actually are — relevant to the vault's broader interest in whether democratic and legal institutions can constrain authoritarian violence.
