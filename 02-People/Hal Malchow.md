## Summary

Hal Malchow (November 4, 1951 – March 21, 2024) was an American political consultant and securities lawyer widely credited as the Democratic Party's foremost pioneer in applying commercial consumer-database segmentation to electoral targeting. From the mid-1990s onward he helped transform political direct mail from blunt demographic messaging into individualized, data-driven contact, work that [[Sasha Issenberg]] documents extensively in [[Source - Issenberg 2012 - The Victory Lab]]. Malchow died by assisted suicide in Zürich at age 72.

## Key Facts

- In **1995** he used what is regarded as the first consumer-database microtargeting operation in a US federal campaign to help Ron Wyden win a special Senate election in Oregon — identifying "soft" Republican women who might cross over on specific policy issues.
- He founded **MSHC Partners** (1989) with Rich Schlackman, Trish Hoppey, and Jim Crounse. The firm handled direct-mail for more than 30 Senate campaigns and 20 gubernatorial campaigns, as well as every Democratic presidential nominee from 1988 through 2004.
- He wrote **"The New Political Targeting"** (2003), which became a practitioner reference explaining how micro-segmentation worked in elections — one of very few published how-to texts available before Cambridge Analytica's methods became public.
- He co-founded the **[[Analyst Institute]]** in 2007 alongside [[Mike Podhorzer]] (AFL-CIO) as a Democratic-aligned clearinghouse to share and validate randomized GOTV research.
- He pioneered the use of **control-group experiments** to measure and improve campaign mail tactics — a methodological move that preceded but paralleled the academic [[Gerber and Green field experiments]] tradition.

No major whistleblower or adversarial disclosures on record. Malchow gave numerous practitioner interviews late in career. His 2003 book is the primary published statement of his methodology.
