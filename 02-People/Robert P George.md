## Summary

Robert Peter George is the McCormick Professor of Jurisprudence at Princeton University and the most influential Catholic natural-law philosopher in American public life. Since the 1990s he has served as the primary intellectual architect of a cross-denominational conservative coalition on abortion, same-sex marriage, and religious liberty — bridging Catholic, evangelical Protestant, and Eastern Orthodox traditions in ways that shaped litigation strategy, legislative advocacy, and judicial appointments across two decades.

Damon Linker's 2006 book *The Theocons* identified George as the scholarly linchpin of a Catholic neoconservative network that sought to move natural-law moral reasoning from academic philosophy into enforceable public policy. George's position differs from [[Catholic Integralism]] — he grounds his arguments in secular natural-law reasoning accessible to all citizens — but the practical agenda overlaps substantially with integralist policy goals.

## Key facts

- Born 1955 in Morgantown, West Virginia; grandson of immigrant coal miners.
- BA from Swarthmore College; JD from Harvard Law School; DPhil from New College, Oxford (1986), supervised by John Finnis and Joseph Raz.
- Joined Princeton faculty 1985; tenured; holds the McCormick chair (sixth holder of a chair established 1779).
- Founded Princeton's James Madison Program in American Ideals and Institutions in 2000 — a vehicle for bringing conservative and natural-law scholars to campus.
- Co-founded [[Witherspoon Institute]] in 2003 with Luis Tellez; co-founded [[American Principles Project]] in 2009 with Jeff Bell and Francis Cannon.
- Principal author of the 2009 Manhattan Declaration, signed by more than 500,000 people, asserting civil disobedience rights on abortion, marriage, and religious liberty.
- Served on the President's Council on Bioethics (2002–2009) under George W. Bush.
- Awarded the Presidential Citizens Medal by President George W. Bush, December 2008.
- Holds twenty-three honorary degrees.
- Described by the *New York Times* (2009) as "the country's most influential conservative Christian thinker."
- Close colleague and collaborator with [[Adrian Vermeule]] and [[Patrick Deneen]] at Princeton, though George himself does not endorse integralism.

## Relationships

- **[[George Weigel]]** — fellow Catholic neoconservative intellectual; overlap at [[Ethics and Public Policy Center]].
- **[[Michael Novak]]** — generation above George; shared AEI/EPPC orbit.

- **[[Patrick Deneen]]** — Princeton colleague; Deneen has moved toward post-liberal integralism that George resists.

## Public statements

- George has argued that the exclusion of natural-law reasoning from constitutional adjudication represents "liberal ideology" masquerading as neutrality.
- Co-authored *What Is Marriage? Man and Woman: A Defense* (2012) with Sherif Girgis and Ryan T. Anderson — the most cited academic argument against same-sex marriage.
- On the Manhattan Declaration: "We will not comply with any edict that compels us to participate in or facilitate abortion… or to treat as marriages… unions of persons of the same sex."

## Open questions

- To what extent did George's natural-law framework provide intellectual cover for more explicitly theocratic arguments that followed? Relationship to post-liberal/integralist turn among former students requires further mapping.
- George has positioned himself as a defender of liberal democratic proceduralism while opposing liberal policy outcomes — the tension between these commitments is contested in recent scholarship.
