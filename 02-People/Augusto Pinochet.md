## Summary

Augusto Pinochet was the Chilean army general who led the military coup on September 11, 1973, that overthrew the elected government of Salvador Allende. He ruled Chile until 1990, overseeing one of the Western Hemisphere's most systematic human rights atrocities: approximately 3,000 killed or forcibly disappeared, 27,000 tortured, and 200,000 exiled. His regime simultaneously implemented radical free-market economic reforms. He is arrested in London in 1998 on a Spanish extradition warrant but returned to Chile in 2000 on health grounds and died in 2006 without facing full criminal accountability.

**Why the vault holds this figure as a comparative case.** Ben-Ghiat's *Strongmen* uses Pinochet as a case in the post-coup consolidation phase — specifically the use of *institutional architecture* to extend authoritarian control beyond the regime's survival. The 1980 Chilean constitution, drafted under Pinochet's direction via a rigged plebiscite, was explicitly designed to be difficult to amend and to constrain future democratic governments: it required supermajority thresholds, created designated senators, entrenched private pension and health systems, and protected the military from civilian oversight. Elements of this constitution survived until 2005 partial reforms and were only fully addressed in the 2022 constitutional convention process (which ultimately failed at referendum). The vault uses Pinochet as the primary "constitutional lock-in" model — the clearest historical example of an authoritarian regime embedding structural constraints on democracy into the constitutional order itself. This directly informs the vault's analysis of [[Project 2025]] and the [[Hypothesis - Three-wing coalition]]'s long-term institutional agenda: the question of whether the current US project aims at Pinochet-style lock-in rather than merely transient power.

## Key facts

- Career military officer; appointed Commander-in-Chief by Allende himself just weeks before the coup, a fact that deepened the sense of betrayal.
- The DINA (secret police), created 1974, operated torture centers (Villa Grimaldi being the most notorious) and conducted international assassinations through *Operation Condor* — a coordinated effort with other South American military regimes.
- Operation Condor linked Chilean, Argentine, Brazilian, Uruguayan, Paraguayan, and Bolivian intelligence services in tracking and killing political opponents abroad; the US provided intelligence support.
- The *Chicago Boys* — Chilean economists trained at the University of Chicago under Milton Friedman — implemented a radical free-market program: privatizing state enterprises (including the pension system), cutting public spending, and deregulating financial markets. This was explicitly showcased internationally as a "model."
- The 1980 constitution was approved in a plebiscite conducted without electoral rolls or free media — its legitimacy was contested from the start.
- Pinochet lost a 1988 plebiscite on extending his rule (the "No" campaign won 55.99%), triggering a managed transition: he remained Army Commander until 1998 and became a senator for life under the constitution he had written.
- Arrested in London in October 1998 on a warrant from Spanish judge Baltasar Garzón; held for 503 days; returned to Chile in March 2000 on health grounds.
- Died December 10, 2006, under house arrest, facing hundreds of criminal charges but never convicted.
