# David Koch

## Summary

David Hamilton Koch (1940–2019) was an American chemical engineer, industrialist, philanthropist, and political financier — the younger and more publicly visible of the two "Koch brothers" who built one of the most consequential donor operations in modern American politics. With his elder brother [[Charles Koch]], he co-owned [[Koch Industries]], the second-largest privately held company in the United States. While Charles ran the company day-to-day from Wichita, David was based in New York, headed the subsidiary Koch Engineering (later Koch Chemical Technology Group), and served as the network's most prominent public face, philanthropist, and — in 1980 — its candidate.

David Koch's place in this vault is as a co-architect of the [[Koch Network]]: the multi-decade, multi-billion-dollar apparatus of foundations, think tanks, advocacy groups, academic centers, and donor seminars that financed the libertarian and free-market right. The network's flagship advocacy vehicle, [[Americans for Prosperity]], was built with Koch money; its predecessor, Citizens for a Sound Economy, was founded with David Koch's backing in 1984. Through these vehicles the Kochs funded opposition to taxation, environmental and financial regulation, organized labor, climate policy, and the Affordable Care Act, and helped seed the Tea Party movement. The network is a principal funding stream for the "constitutional capture" project the vault tracks — the long effort to insulate market and constitutional structures from democratic majorities.

## Key facts

- **Role(s):** Industrialist; co-owner and Executive VP of [[Koch Industries]]; political donor and network co-founder; 1980 Libertarian Party VP nominee; philanthropist.
- **Affiliations:** [[Koch Industries]], [[Americans for Prosperity]], Citizens for a Sound Economy, [[Cato Institute]] (co-founder/funder), Reason Foundation, [[Koch Network]].
- **Active period:** ~1970–2018 (business); 1980–2018 (political).
- **Known for:** Co-building the Koch donor network; the 1980 Clark–Koch Libertarian ticket; funding Americans for Prosperity.
- David Koch was an MIT-trained chemical engineer (B.S. 1962, M.S. 1963).
- He joined [[Koch Industries]] in 1970 and became co-owner with Charles in 1983 after a bitter buyout of brothers Frederick and William Koch.
- Diagnosed with prostate cancer in 1992; he became a major funder of cancer research, donating an estimated $1.3 billion to philanthropic causes overall (medical research, the arts, the David H. Koch Theater at Lincoln Center, NewYork–Presbyterian Hospital).
- He retired from [[Koch Industries]] and from network political activity in mid-2018 due to declining health.

## Relationships

- **[[Charles Koch]]** — elder brother, business co-owner, and co-founder of the [[Koch Network]]; the dominant partner in network strategy.
- **Fred C. Koch** — father; founder of the company that became [[Koch Industries]] and a founding member of the [[John Birch Society]].
- **Frederick R. Koch and William "Bill" Koch** — brothers bought out in 1983 after litigation over the family company.
- **Ed Clark** — 1980 Libertarian presidential nominee; David Koch was his running mate.
- **[[Cato Institute]]** — libertarian think tank David Koch helped fund and direct.
- **[[Americans for Prosperity]]** — advocacy group David chaired the foundation arm of; central network vehicle.
- **[[Mont Pelerin Society]] / [[Institute for Humane Studies]] / [[Mercatus Center]]** — parts of the broader academic-funding infrastructure the Kochs sustained.

## Public statements

- The 1980 Clark–Koch platform called for abolishing Social Security, the Federal Reserve, the SEC, EPA, FTC, OSHA, FBI, CIA, the Department of Energy, corporate income tax, minimum-wage laws, and farm and business subsidies — an explicit catalogue of the libertarian program later pursued incrementally through the network.
- David Koch repeatedly described himself as a "social liberal" (supporting abortion rights and gay rights) and a fiscal conservative, distinguishing his personal libertarianism from the religious right — a tension within the broader coalition the vault tracks.

## Open questions

- How should David Koch's individual influence be weighted against Charles Koch's, given that Charles drove network strategy? Reporting treats the network as a joint project but operationally Charles-led.
- The network's deliberate use of donor anonymity ("dark money") makes the full scale of David Koch's political spending impossible to document precisely; published totals are estimates.
- Contested interpretation: whether the network's aim is ordinary advocacy or, as critics (e.g., Jane Mayer, Nancy MacLean) argue, a deliberate project to entrench minority economic power against democratic majorities. The vault treats the funding facts as documented and the "intent" framing as contested interpretation.
