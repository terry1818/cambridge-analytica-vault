# Anne Applebaum

## Summary

Anne Applebaum (born 1964) is an American-Polish historian and journalist who has spent her career documenting Soviet and post-Soviet authoritarianism and, more recently, the erosion of liberal democracy across the West. She won the 2004 Pulitzer Prize for General Nonfiction for "Gulag: A History" (2003), and her later "Iron Curtain: The Crushing of Eastern Europe, 1944–1956" (2012) and "Red Famine" (2017) extended that work. A staff writer at The Atlantic and a senior fellow at the Johns Hopkins SNF Agora Institute, she was among the earliest Western commentators to warn about anti-democratic drift inside democracies, not merely outside them.

Applebaum matters to this vault as a principal supplier of its comparative-authoritarianism framework. Her 2020 book "Twilight of Democracy: The Seductive Lure of Authoritarianism" examines why intellectuals, writers, and political operatives — what she calls the "clercs," borrowing from Julien Benda — defect from democratic norms to serve authoritarian-leaning movements. Using case studies in Poland, Hungary, the United Kingdom, and the United States, she argues that authoritarianism advances not through coups but through the collaboration of well-placed elites who supply legitimacy, propaganda, and bureaucratic competence. Her 2024 book "Autocracy, Inc." develops the related argument that today's autocracies operate as a transnational network of mutual financial, propaganda, and surveillance support. These frames are central to the vault's reading of [[democratic backsliding]] as a long arc connecting U.S. and European cases.

## Relationships

- **Intellectual interlocutors:** Draws on Julien Benda's concept of the "clerc"; engages comparative scholars of [[democratic backsliding]] such as the authors of "How Democracies Die" (Levitsky and Ziblatt).
- **Subjects of critique:** Documents the trajectories of Polish (Law and Justice), Hungarian (Orbán/Fidesz), and U.S. figures who moved from democratic conservatism toward authoritarian populism.
- **Vault role:** Her work pairs with the vault's other comparative sources to frame U.S. trends — [[Christian nationalism]], [[constitutional capture]], election denial — within a transnational pattern.

## Public statements
