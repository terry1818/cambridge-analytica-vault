## Summary

J. Howard Pew was the most consequential corporate patron of the mid-20th-century fusion between evangelical Christianity and free-market conservatism. As president of Sun Oil Company (later Sunoco), he channeled substantial personal and corporate wealth into a coordinated set of institutions designed to align Protestant clergy and lay leadership with laissez-faire economics and against New Deal liberalism. His patronage of [[Spiritual Mobilization]], his co-founding of the Christian Freedom Foundation (1950), his role in launching [[Christianity Today]] (1956), and his financial support for the [[National Association of Evangelicals]] represent the clearest historical case of a single industrial patron directly constructing the institutional architecture of religious-right politics.

## Key facts

- With his brother Joseph N. Pew Jr., took over management of Sun Oil Company in 1912; built it into one of the major American petroleum companies.
- A lifelong Presbyterian and committed opponent of the Social Gospel and New Deal labor regulation, Pew saw religious institutions as the correct vehicle for opposing government intervention in the economy.
- Co-founded the Christian Freedom Foundation (CFF) in 1950 with Howard Kershner to promote "social and economic conservatism" among clergy — a direct precursor to later religious-right organizations.
- Provided early and sustained financial backing to [[James Fifield]]'s [[Spiritual Mobilization]] and to the [[Foundation for Economic Education]], creating a unified institutional cluster that treated free-market economics as Christian doctrine.
- Worked with Billy Graham and Harold Ockenga to found [[Christianity Today]] in 1956; served on its board as an early director and major donor until his death in 1971. Scholars note Pew attempted to steer the magazine toward explicit anti-union and pro-business editorial content, creating tension with editor Carl F.H. Henry.
- Supported the [[National Association of Evangelicals]], providing what historians describe as "a direct connection at the highest levels between [the Foundation for Economic Education] and evangelicalism."
- The Pew Charitable Trusts — now a major mainstream philanthropy — were established in his and his family's name, though they have evolved far beyond his original conservative-religious mission.

## Relationships

- **[[James Fifield]]** — the most prominent recipient of Pew's religious-political patronage; Pew funded Spiritual Mobilization and shared its anti-New Deal theology.
- **Billy Graham** — Graham and Pew collaborated on Christianity Today's founding; their relationship was sometimes strained by Pew's desire for harder-edged economic conservatism.
- **[[National Association of Evangelicals]]** — Pew was an early patron; his funding helped establish the NAE as a credible institutional voice.
- **[[Christianity Today]]** — Pew was a founding board director and major donor; his influence shaped the magazine's tilt toward economic conservatism in its early years.
- **[[Foundation for Economic Education]]** — donor; part of the overlapping cluster of libertarian-Christian institutions Pew supported.
- **[[Spiritual Mobilization]]** — patron.

## Open questions

- The precise dollar amounts Pew directed to Spiritual Mobilization versus other religious-libertarian organizations have not been fully disclosed in public scholarship.
- The degree to which Pew coordinated his giving with other oil-industry patrons (e.g., H.L. Hunt, Lyman Stewart's successors) in a strategic funding network has not been established.
