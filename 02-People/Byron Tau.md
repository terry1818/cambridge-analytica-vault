## Summary

Byron Tau is an American investigative journalist specializing in national security, surveillance, law, and courts. He is best known for his extensive reporting on the US government's covert use of commercial data markets — particularly location data purchased from data brokers — and for translating those findings into the 2024 book *Means of Control: How the Hidden Alliance of Tech and Government Is Creating a New American Surveillance State* (Random House). His reporting career spans positions at the Wall Street Journal, the Associated Press, and the nonprofit newsroom NOTUS.

## Key facts

- His book *Means of Control* (2024) documents how, in the years after 9/11, the US intelligence and law enforcement community began purchasing commercially available data — location pings, device identifiers, consumer profiles — to build surveillance capacity without obtaining court warrants.
- Tau documented that data brokers and defense contractors developed hybrid corporate identities: "defense contractors have marketing subsidiaries and marketing companies have defense contractor subsidiaries."
- He revealed government agencies purchasing data from obscure consumer apps (including location apps such as Drunk Mode) without users' knowledge.
- His reporting preceded and informed congressional and judicial scrutiny of warrantless government data purchases.
- Tau's work fits the broader pattern of [[commercial surveillance]] intersecting with [[national security state]] expansion — thematically adjacent to the [[Cambridge Analytica]] data-harvesting ecosystem.
- He has been cited in discussions of the legal gaps that allow bulk commercial data acquisition to sidestep Fourth Amendment protections.
