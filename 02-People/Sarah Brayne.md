## Summary

Sarah Brayne is an American sociologist whose multi-year ethnographic study inside the Los Angeles Police Department produced the most detailed empirical account of how law enforcement agencies adopt big-data analytics. Her 2020 book *Predict and Surveil: Data, Discretion, and the Future of Policing* (Oxford University Press) documented how programs such as [[Operation LASER]] and the [[PredPol]] software reshaped police practice and institutional decision-making. Brayne's work is foundational in the sociology of surveillance and is widely cited in debates over algorithmic governance and civil liberties.

## Key facts

- Holds an appointment in the Department of Sociology at the University of Texas at Austin; previously at Stanford.
- Conducted several years of participant observation within the LAPD, complemented by interviews with sworn officers and civilian data analysts — an access level rarely granted to academic researchers.
- Originated the concept of **[[System avoidance]]**: individuals with criminal justice contact strategically withdraw from record-keeping institutions (medical, financial, educational) to reduce re-exposure to surveillance.
- Identified the **[[Secondary surveillance network]]**: data-driven policing extends reach beyond primary suspects to anyone linked to them in departmental databases.
- Published a landmark 2014 article in the *American Sociological Review* — "Surveillance and System Avoidance" — before completing the book-length study.
- Published a 2017 article "Big Data Surveillance: The Case of Policing" in *American Sociological Review*.

## Public statements

Brayne has testified and spoken publicly on the risks of automated surveillance expanding institutional reach over populations already subject to criminal justice contact. She has emphasized that algorithmic tools do not eliminate discretion — they relocate and obscure it.
