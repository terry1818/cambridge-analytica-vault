## Summary

Salvador Allende was a physician and socialist politician who ran for the presidency four times before winning in 1970 with a plurality (36.2% of the vote) as the candidate of the Popular Unity coalition. His government pursued nationalization of copper mines, land reform, and social programs. He was overthrown on September 11, 1973, in a military coup backed by the CIA and supported by Chilean business and conservative elites; he died in the La Moneda presidential palace during the assault, with the official finding being suicide, though the circumstances were disputed for decades.

**Why the vault holds this figure as a comparative case.** Levitsky & Ziblatt in *Tyranny of the Minority* use the Allende case in a complex way: not simply as a victim of authoritarianism, but as a case study in how democracies collapse when *all major actors* — government and opposition — begin treating elections as zero-sum existential conflicts. The Chilean right (with US backing) refused to accept the legitimacy of a fairly elected socialist government, using every institutional and extra-institutional tool available — economic sabotage, congressional obstruction, and ultimately coup support. But Allende's coalition also pushed policy beyond what its electoral mandate could sustain, contributing to polarization and elite defection. The vault uses the Allende case to examine the *conditions under which elites defect from democratic norms* — a question directly applicable to the [[Conservative complicity thesis]] and the post-2024 US environment. It also provides the context node for [[Augusto Pinochet]], who represents the aftermath.

## Key facts

- Trained as a physician; co-founded the Socialist Party of Chile in 1933; elected to Congress in 1937.
- Ran for president in 1952, 1958, and 1964 before winning in 1970.
- The 1970 victory was by plurality (three-way race); the Chilean Congress confirmed him as president despite US pressure on legislators to block his confirmation — a constitutional process that itself became a site of norm contestation.
- Nationalized the copper industry (with broad congressional support initially), pursued agrarian reform, and implemented social programs including free milk for children.
- Faced sustained economic warfare: the Nixon administration cut off US credit, US firms reduced copper purchases, and internal business sectors engaged in strikes and capital flight.
- The CIA's "Track II" — an explicit attempt to prevent his inauguration via a coup — was ordered by Nixon and Kissinger in 1970 but failed.
- By 1973, hyperinflation, shortages, and political violence from both left and right had produced a crisis of governability.
- The September 11, 1973 coup was led by General Pinochet; the air force bombed the La Moneda Palace. Allende's death during the assault was ruled suicide by a 2011 forensic investigation, though political debate continued.
- His legacy remains central to Chilean politics; the 2022 constitutional convention process revisited many of the 1973 fault lines.

## Open questions

- To what extent did the Allende government's policy overreach (governing beyond its electoral plurality) contribute to the conditions for the coup, versus the coup being entirely driven by US-backed elite refusal to accept democratic outcomes?
- L&Z's use of Allende is somewhat contested: some scholars argue it implicitly assigns co-responsibility to the democratic victim, which risks misattributing causation.
