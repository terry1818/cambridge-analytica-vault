## Summary

Samuel T. Francis was the most consequential intellectual of the American paleoconservative movement in the 1980s and 1990s. Drawing on James Burnham's elite theory and his own sociological analysis of the white working and middle class, Francis developed the concept of [[Middle American Radicals]] (MARs) — a constituency he argued could form the base of a revolutionary right-wing political movement against the "managerial elite." He served as [[Pat Buchanan]]'s chief intellectual and ghost-writer, and his work is now widely cited as the theoretical precursor to Trumpism. He was fired from the *Washington Times* in 1995 after making overtly racist remarks at an American Renaissance conference, and spent the rest of his career at the explicitly white-nationalist margins. John Ganz's *When the Clock Broke* treats Francis as the theorist who most clearly articulated what the 1990s upheaval was actually about.

## Key facts

- Born 29 April 1947, Chattanooga, Tennessee; died 15 February 2005, Cheverly, Maryland (complications from aortic aneurysm surgery).
- BA, Johns Hopkins (1969); MA (1971) and PhD (1979), University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. Dissertation on the Bolshevik Revolution.
- Policy analyst at the Heritage Foundation in the late 1970s; then Senate aide to Senator John P. East (R-NC).
- Joined the *Washington Times* in 1986 as an editorial writer and columnist; won the Distinguished Writing Award from the American Society of Newspaper Editors (1989).
- Fired by the *Washington Times* in 1995 following public outcry over racist remarks at an American Renaissance conference (1994) in which he argued that white civilisation was not compatible with racial integration.
- Continued writing for *Chronicles* magazine and various paleoconservative publications; became a contributor to white-nationalist outlets.
- Key intellectual borrowings: James Burnham's *Managerial Revolution* (1941) provided the elite-theory framework; Francis adapted it to argue that both parties were captured by a liberal managerial class against which the MARs could revolt.
- His 1996 *Chronicles* essay "From Household to Nation" explicitly outlined the political strategy of mobilising white working-class resentment through cultural and economic nationalism — a programme Trump operatives later cited.
- Not to be confused with the economist James M. Buchanan (see [[James M Buchanan]]).

## Relationships

- [[Pat Buchanan]] — primary political patron; Francis shaped Buchanan's intellectual positioning.
- [[Middle American Radicals]] — Francis's core analytical concept; adopted from Donald Warren and radicalised.
- [[Paleoconservatism]] — Francis was the movement's most systematic theorist alongside Paul Gottfried.
- [[Steve Bannon]] — Bannon and the Breitbart circle drew heavily on Francis's framework without always naming him.
- American Renaissance / white-nationalist networks — Francis moved into these explicitly after 1995.

## Public statements

- "The civilization that we as whites created in Europe and America could not have developed apart from the racial endowments of the peoples who created it." — American Renaissance conference, 1994 (remarks that led to his firing).
- "Middle Americans … are radical in the sense that they perceive, however dimly, that their livelihoods, values, and way of life are under attack." — *Chronicles*, 1990s.

## Open questions

- The degree to which Bannon-era strategists consciously operationalised Francis's MARs framework vs. independently arriving at similar conclusions.
