# John Roberts

## Summary

John G. Roberts Jr. is the 17th Chief Justice of the United States, appointed by President George W. Bush in 2005. He is the central judicial figure in the decades-long effort to limit federal oversight of state voting laws and redistricting. His paper trail begins in the Reagan administration, where as an associate counsel he wrote more than 25 memos opposing the 1982 strengthening of the [[Voting Rights Act of 1965]], arguing that requiring minority representation "went too far." As Chief Justice, Roberts authored the majority opinions in *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) — which gutted Section 5 preclearance — and *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019) — which closed federal courts to partisan gerrymandering claims. He joined the *Brnovich* (2021) majority narrowing Section 2. In 2023, he wrote the majority opinion in *Allen v. Milligan* preserving Section 2 redistricting claims, in a 5–4 decision joining the four liberal justices — marking a partial and contested limit on the erosion he had previously overseen.

## Key Facts

- Born January 27, 1955, in Buffalo, New York; grew up in northwest Indiana.
- Harvard College undergraduate, Harvard Law School J.D. (1979). Graduated *magna cum laude*.
- Clerked for Judge Henry Friendly (2d Cir.) and then for Justice William Rehnquist (SCOTUS).
- Served as Associate Counsel to President Reagan 1982–1986. During this period wrote extensive memos opposing VRA reauthorization and arguing against expansion of Section 2.
- Served as Principal Deputy Solicitor General under President George H.W. Bush, arguing cases before the Supreme Court; eventually argued 39 cases before the Court, winning 25.
- In private practice at Hogan & Hartson (now Hogan Lovells) between government stints.
- Appointed to the D.C. Circuit in 2003; named Chief Justice in 2005 after William Rehnquist's death.
- In *Northwest Austin Municipal Utility District v. Holder* (2009), wrote for the Court raising constitutional concerns about VRA preclearance but declining to decide; widely read as a warning to Congress.
- In *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013), wrote for 5–4 majority striking Section 4(b) coverage formula.
- In *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019), wrote for 5–4 majority declaring partisan gerrymandering non-justiciable.
- In *Brnovich v. DNC* (2021), joined 6–3 majority narrowing Section 2.
- In *Allen v. Milligan* (2023), wrote for 5–4 majority upholding Section 2 redistricting claims, joining liberal bloc.
- In *Moore v. Harper* (2023), wrote for majority rejecting the Independent State Legislature theory, limiting state legislature power over federal elections.
- Roberts is also the author of *Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission* (2010) — joined by the majority — allowing unlimited corporate campaign spending.

## Relationships

- **[[Leonard Leo]]:** Roberts's appointment followed the Federalist Society infrastructure Leo helped build; Roberts is not a formal Federalist Society member but emerged from its network.
- **[[Thomas Hofeller]]:** Hofeller's North Carolina maps were directly at issue in *Rucho*; Roberts insulated them from federal challenge.
- **[[REDMAP]]:** Roberts's *Shelby County* and *Rucho* rulings combined to create a legal environment in which REDMAP-era maps were maximally protected from federal judicial review.
- **Rehnquist Court / Warren Burger Court legacy:** Roberts clerked for Rehnquist and has built a jurisprudence that systematically constrains the Warren Court's voting rights expansions.

## Public Statements

Roberts's judicial philosophy on voting rights is most fully expressed in his opinions. His personal summary in *Shelby County* — that the VRA coverage formula was "based on 40-year-old data" and represented an outdated assumption — is the core statement of his position. His statement in *Parents Involved* (2007) — "the way to stop discrimination on the basis of race is to stop discriminating on the basis of race" — captures his broader colorblind constitutionalism.
