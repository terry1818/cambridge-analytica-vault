# Kristin Du Mez

## Summary

Kristin Kobes Du Mez (born 1975) is an American historian of gender, religion, and politics and, until her 2024 retirement from full-time teaching, a professor of history and gender studies at Calvin University in Grand Rapids, Michigan. She holds a B.A. in history and German from Dordt College and a Ph.D. in American history from the University of Notre Dame, where she later held a research fellowship at the Center for Philosophy of Religion. She is also affiliated with the Public Religion Research Institute (PRRI).

Du Mez matters to this vault as one of the most influential scholarly interpreters of the modern religious right. Her 2020 book *Jesus and John Wayne: How White Evangelicals Corrupted a Faith and Fractured a Nation* (Liveright/Norton) became a *New York Times* bestseller and a touchstone for understanding why roughly 80 percent of white evangelical voters supported [[Donald Trump]]. Her central argument — that this support was not a betrayal of evangelical values but their logical fulfillment — provides an interpretive frame the vault uses when tracing the [[Christian nationalism]] thread. Where the vault distinguishes documented fact from interpretation, Du Mez's thesis sits squarely on the interpretive side: it is a widely respected, evidence-grounded historical argument, contested by some conservative scholars, not an established consensus.

## Relationships

- **[[Calvin University]]** — her academic home for two decades; a Christian Reformed institution, a notable context for a critical historian of evangelicalism.
- **University of Notre Dame** — granted her Ph.D.; later host of a research fellowship.
- **Public Religion Research Institute (PRRI)** — research affiliation; PRRI survey data on Christian nationalism complements her historical work.
- **[[Philip Gorski]]** and **[[Samuel Perry]]** — fellow scholars of Christian nationalism; their *The Flag and the Cross* is frequently cited alongside *Jesus and John Wayne*.
- **Conservative evangelical critics** — figures who dispute her thesis as overstated or polemical; the disagreement is itself documented in evangelical media.

## Public statements

- *Jesus and John Wayne* (2020): argues white evangelicals traded the "Jesus of the Gospels for an idol of rugged masculinity and Christian nationalism" — the book's central claim, framing Trump support as cultural fulfillment rather than betrayal.
- In interviews she has emphasized that militant masculinity, not theology narrowly defined, is the through-line connecting decades of evangelical popular culture to contemporary politics.
