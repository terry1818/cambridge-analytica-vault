## Summary

Carl Schmitt was one of the most consequential — and most controversial — legal and political theorists of the twentieth century. Writing during the Weimar Republic, he produced a series of works that challenged liberal constitutional democracy at its foundations: *Political Theology* (1922) argued that all modern political concepts are secularized theological concepts, and that "sovereign is he who decides on the exception"; *The Concept of the Political* (1932) defined the political as the friend/enemy distinction; and *The Crisis of Parliamentary Democracy* (1923) argued that liberal parliamentarism and genuine democracy are incompatible. He joined the Nazi Party in May 1933, provided legal argumentation supporting the Enabling Act (which allowed Hitler to rule without Reichstag consent), and was named to the Prussian State Council. He was expelled from the SS inner circle by 1936 after attacks by Himmler's circle on his Catholic background, but was never prosecuted post-war.

**Vault relevance — intellectual-influence case:** Schmitt is cited in *The Anatomy of Fascism* (Paxton, 2004) and in broader scholarship on fascism and authoritarianism as a key intellectual figure who provided legal-theoretical resources for authoritarian rule. His concepts of the state of exception and constitutional suspension have had documented revival in multiple authoritarian and post-liberal contexts, including post-9/11 US emergency law debates, Hungarian constitutionalism under Orbán, and explicitly by figures in the US "common good constitutionalism" movement (Adrian Vermeule draws explicitly on Schmitt). The vault holds Schmitt as a reference node because his conceptual vocabulary recurs in the legal theory of movement figures tracked elsewhere in the vault.

**Evidence-tier note:** Schmitt's Weimar writings and Nazi-era activities are well-documented historical record (Tier 1). His *influence* on specific contemporary figures ranges from documented (Vermeule explicitly cites him) to interpretive (broader claims about Schmittian influence on Trump-era emergency powers). These should be distinguished.

## Key facts

- Born 1888 in Plettenberg, Westphalia; studied law; became a leading Weimar-era legal theorist.
- *Political Theology* (1922): introduced "state of exception" — the emergency that suspends normal legal order reveals where true sovereignty lies.
- *The Crisis of Parliamentary Democracy* (1923): argued liberalism and democracy are fundamentally different; parliaments are liberal institutions, not democratic ones; democracy can be expressed through a leader without a legislature.
- *The Concept of the Political* (1932): all genuinely political relationships reduce to the friend/enemy distinction; politics cannot be dissolved into economics or ethics.
- *Legality and Legitimacy* (1932): argued the Weimar constitutional order was in a self-destructive crisis, opening the door to Hindenburg's use of emergency Article 48.
- Joined Nazi Party May 1, 1933; provided legal justification for the Enabling Act.
- Appointed to Prussian State Council and became an important public intellectual of the early Nazi regime; wrote prolifically in support of Nazi governance 1933–1936.
- 1934: publicly justified the Night of the Long Knives (Röhm purge) as a legitimate act of sovereign decision.
- 1936: attacked by SS publication *Das schwarze Korps* as insufficiently committed; removed from leading positions; retreated to academic work.
- Post-war: not prosecuted at Nuremberg; held briefly and refused to answer Allied questionnaires; remained intellectually productive until his death.
- Died 1985; his work has never fallen out of scholarly circulation; both left (Giorgio Agamben, Chantal Mouffe) and right (Patrick Deneen, Adrian Vermeule) have drawn on his concepts.
