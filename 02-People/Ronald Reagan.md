# Ronald Reagan

## Summary

Ronald Reagan (1911–2004) was the 40th President of the United States and the political figure around whom the modern American conservative coalition crystallized. He matters to this vault not for any single policy but as the hinge of a realignment. The 1980 election brought together three forces that had been building separately for decades — the newly mobilized religious right organized through groups like the [[Moral Majority]], the libertarian and free-market deregulatory movement, and the donor-funded institutional infrastructure of think tanks and advocacy groups — and welded them into a single durable governing coalition under the Republican Party. The political machinery that the rest of this vault tracks (the donor networks, the data operations, the Christian-nationalist movement, the project to reshape the judiciary and the structure of government) operates within the coalition that Reagan's victory consolidated.

The realignment was not invented in 1980. It was the convergence of forces — civil rights backlash, cultural conflict over abortion and feminism and school prayer, religious mobilization, anti-tax and anti-regulation organizing — that had been developing since at least the 1960s and 1970s. But Reagan's election was the moment those forces became *governing* forces. The Moral Majority, founded in 1979 by Jerry Falwell Sr., ran registration drives and turned out evangelical voters; roughly 61% of evangelicals voted for Reagan in 1980. The vault treats Reagan as the figure who made movement conservatism electable and respectable, opening the institutional space later occupied by the actors central to its core story.

## Key facts

- **Role(s):** Hollywood actor and SAG president; Governor of California; two-term U.S. President.
- **Affiliations:** Republican Party; allied with the [[Moral Majority]] and the broader emerging religious right and free-market donor movement.
- **Active period:** 1964 (national political debut) – 1989.
- **Known for:** The 1980 realignment; supply-side economics and deregulation; the evangelical-Republican alliance; appointing a generation of conservative federal judges.

## Relationships

- **Co-conspirators / collaborators:** Jerry Falwell Sr. and the [[Moral Majority]] — the organized vehicle that mobilized evangelical voters into the Reagan coalition. Reagan's embrace of the religious right ("I know you can't endorse me, but I endorse you") sealed the alliance.
- **Subordinates / hired:** A cohort of movement conservatives staffed his administration and judiciary; the [[Federalist Society]], founded in 1982, grew within the legal space his administration opened.
- **Opponents / accusers:** Jimmy Carter — the incumbent Reagan defeated; notably an evangelical himself, but the religious-right organizing of 1979–1980 broke decisively toward Reagan.

## Public statements

- "A Time for Choosing" (1964): the speech that introduced Reagan as a national conservative voice, framing the choice as between freedom and an overreaching government.
- 1980, to the Religious Roundtable in Dallas: addressing thousands of evangelical activists, Reagan publicly aligned himself with the religious right's agenda — a defining moment in the coalition's formation.
- First inaugural address (1981): "Government is not the solution to our problem; government is the problem" — the compressed thesis of the deregulatory turn.

## Open questions

- **How much was Reagan, how much was the movement:** Scholars debate whether Reagan drove the realignment or rode forces already in motion. The vault's position is that he was the catalyst and the figurehead, not the originator — the religious-right and donor infrastructure pre-dated and outlasted him.
- **Continuity to the present:** The degree to which the coalition Reagan built is continuous with the contemporary movement the vault tracks — versus having mutated into something materially different — is a recurring analytic question across the vault's threads.
