## Summary

Kris Kobach is one of the most consequential architects of modern voter-suppression infrastructure in the United States. As Kansas Secretary of State from 2011 to 2019 he championed documentary proof-of-citizenship (DPOC) requirements for voter registration, expanded the [[Interstate Crosscheck]] multi-state voter-roll matching program to a peak of 29 participating states, and authored or co-authored restrictive immigration ordinances for municipalities across the country. After losing the 2018 Kansas gubernatorial race, he was elected Kansas Attorney General in 2022. In 2017 President Trump appointed him co-chair of the Presidential Advisory Commission on Election Integrity, which disbanded in January 2018 without producing a report after multiple states refused to share voter data.

## Key facts

- Elected Kansas Secretary of State in 2010; re-elected 2014.
- Championed Kansas's "SAFE Act" (2011), which required documentary proof of citizenship (e.g., passport or birth certificate) to register to vote — one of the strictest such laws in the country. A federal court struck it down as an unlawful restriction under the National Voter Registration Act (*Fish v. Kobach*, 10th Cir. 2018).
- Under Kobach, Kansas administered the Interstate Voter Registration Crosscheck Program, which grew from ~13 to 29 states. Kobach himself acknowledged the program generated "a significant number" of false positives.
- The Crosscheck list disproportionately flagged Black (overrepresented 45%), Hispanic (24%), and Asian-American (31%) voters relative to white voters (underrepresented 8%).
- In December 2019, a settlement of an ACLU Kansas lawsuit suspended Crosscheck indefinitely and imposed court oversight of Kansas's voter-roll management.
- Kobach served as general counsel to the [[Immigration Reform Law Institute]] and drafted ordinances for Hazleton, PA, Farmers Branch, TX, and other localities restricting services to undocumented residents, most of which were struck down.
- Lost the 2018 Kansas Republican gubernatorial primary by a razor-thin margin; lost a 2020 US Senate primary to Roger Marshall.
- Elected Kansas Attorney General in 2022; re-engaged with [[DOJ Voter Roll Lawsuits 24 States 2025]] litigation posture in second Trump term.
