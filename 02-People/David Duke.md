## Summary

David Duke is the most significant figure in the mainstreaming of white nationalism into American electoral politics before the twenty-first century. Having led the Knights of the Ku Klux Klan through the 1970s and branded himself as a "respectable" ethno-nationalist, he won a Louisiana state house seat as a Republican in 1989 and in 1991 forced a gubernatorial runoff in which he received 55% of the white vote, losing only because of massive Democratic and moderate-Republican turnout against him. John Ganz's *When the Clock Broke* treats Duke as evidence of the depth of racial resentment that the paleoconservative moment of the early 1990s surfaced.

## Key facts

- Born 1 July 1950. Enrolled at LSU in 1968, famously appeared on campus wearing a Nazi uniform.
- Joined the Ku Klux Klan in 1967; founded and led the Knights of the Ku Klux Klan (KKKK) 1974–1980, serving as "Grand Wizard."
- Resigned from the Klan in 1980; founded the National Association for the Advancement of White People (NAAWP) as a "respectable" vehicle.
- 1988: ran for president on the Populist Party ticket; received minimal vote share.
- 1989: won a Louisiana House of Representatives special election as a Republican — first Klan leader to win major elected office in the modern United States.
- 1990: ran for US Senate in Louisiana; received 43.5% of the vote.
- 1991: gubernatorial campaign reached the runoff; received 671,009 votes (38.6%) in the general election, losing to Democrat Edwin Edwards. Received an estimated 55% of the white vote — the result that crystallised national alarm.
- 1992: endorsed [[Pat Buchanan]] in the Republican presidential primary; Buchanan distanced himself but the endorsement underscored the overlap between paleoconservative and white-nationalist electorates.
- 1996: ran again for US Senate, receiving 141,489 votes (11.5%).
- 2000s: convicted of mail fraud and filing false tax returns; served 15 months in federal prison.
- 2016: ran for US Senate again after Donald Trump's election, receiving about 3% in the primary.

## Relationships

- [[Pat Buchanan]] — Duke's 1992 endorsement of Buchanan was a recurring embarrassment; the shared electoral base was analytically significant.
- [[Paleoconservatism]] — Duke operated at the explicit racial fringe of the same white-middle-class alienation that paleocons theorised more abstractly.
- [[Middle American Radicals]] — Duke's Louisiana results showed the numerical scale of the constituency [[Samuel Francis]] theorised.
