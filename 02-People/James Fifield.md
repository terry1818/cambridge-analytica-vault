## Summary

James W. Fifield Jr. was the central architect of what historians Kevin M. Kruse and others call [[Christian libertarianism]] — the ideological project of recasting laissez-faire capitalism as a divinely mandated principle of Christian freedom. As pastor of the wealthy First Congregational Church of Los Angeles and founder-president of [[Spiritual Mobilization]], Fifield spent the 1930s through 1950s building a clergy network underwritten by major corporations and oil-industry patrons. His work established the institutional template — clergy as ideological distribution nodes, corporate capital as the funding base — that subsequent religious-right organizations would replicate across generations.

## Key facts

- Led First Congregational Church of Los Angeles, one of the largest Congregational churches in the country, whose congregation was drawn heavily from the business and professional elite, earning him the sobriquet "Apostle to Millionaires."
- Founded [[Spiritual Mobilization]] in 1935, initially targeting Congregational, Presbyterian, and Episcopal clergy; by the late 1940s the organization claimed more than 17,000 minister-representatives nationally.
- Theological method: Fifield was doctrinally liberal but politically far-right. He systematically minimized New Testament passages on wealth and poverty, reframing material success as evidence of divine favor.
- Positioned the New Deal and the Social Gospel as Christian heresies equivalent to "pagan statism" and totalitarianism, providing theological cover for corporate opposition to the regulatory state.
- Published the monthly magazine *Faith and Freedom* (launched late 1940s), which featured libertarian economists including Ludwig von Mises, Henry Hazlitt, and Leonard Read of the [[Foundation for Economic Education]].
- Spiritual Mobilization's 1947 budget was approximately $270,000 (roughly $3 million in 2025 dollars), drawn from oil, manufacturing, and retail corporations whose executives also exerted social pressure on competitors and employees to donate.

## Relationships

- **[[J. Howard Pew]]** — Sun Oil president; among the corporate patrons who bankrolled Spiritual Mobilization and shared Fifield's fused Christian-libertarian worldview.
- **[[Spiritual Mobilization]]** — the organization Fifield founded and ran; the operational vehicle for his ideology.
- **Ludwig von Mises / Leonard Read / Henry Hazlitt** — libertarian intellectuals whose work Fifield platformed in *Faith and Freedom*, forming a clergy-economist alliance.
- **[[National Association of Evangelicals]]** — parallel umbrella evangelical body; SM worked in the same ideological space though it targeted mainline Protestant clergy more than NAE's evangelical base.
- **[[Biblical capitalism]]** — the theological concept Fifield most directly operationalized; cross-link for the broader vault concept.

## Public statements

- Fifield argued that Christianity was incompatible with the welfare state and that government intervention in the economy was a form of spiritual coercion, equating New Deal programs with collectivist tyranny.
- He reportedly told his wealthy congregants that the Bible's strictures on wealth applied only to individuals, not to the social order — a distinction that gave corporate donors theological permission to oppose redistribution.

## Open questions

- The extent to which Fifield coordinated directly with the [[National Association of Manufacturers]] (which is documented as a Spiritual Mobilization funder) versus receiving arms-length corporate donations warrants further primary-source investigation.
- Whether Fifield had direct contact with the Mont Pelerin Society or Friedrich Hayek has not been firmly established in available scholarship.
