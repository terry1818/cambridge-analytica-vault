## Summary

Virginia Eubanks is an American political scientist whose work investigates the intersection of technology, poverty, and social justice. Her 2018 book *Automating Inequality: How High-Tech Tools Profile, Police, and Punish the Poor* (St. Martin's Press / Picador) is one of the most influential empirical accounts of how data-driven systems harm low-income populations. Eubanks documented three case studies — the [[Indiana welfare automation]] privatization disaster, the [[Allegheny Family Screening Tool]] in child welfare, and a homeless-services placement algorithm in Los Angeles — to construct a unified critique she frames through the concept of the [[Digital poorhouse]].

## Key facts

- Associate Professor in the Department of Political Science at the University at Albany, SUNY.
- *Automating Inequality* won the Lillian Smith Book Award (2018) and was a finalist for the Aesop Prize; it was longlisted for the Carnegie Medal for Excellence in Nonfiction.
- Earlier book: *Digital Dead End: Fighting for Social Justice in the Information Age* (MIT Press, 2011) — examined technology access and poverty in Troy, New York.
- Eubanks draws on traditions of participatory action research, working alongside the communities she studies rather than studying them from a distance.
- Her central argument: automated systems applied to poor populations serve the same function as 19th-century poorhouses — they coerce, surveil, and punish — but at vastly greater scale and with less visibility.
- She has written for Harper's Magazine ("The Digital Poorhouse," January 2018) and testified before legislative bodies on algorithmic accountability.

## Public statements

Eubanks has consistently argued that the question is not whether algorithms are neutral but whose values they encode, and that systems designed to manage poverty inevitably replicate the moral judgments attached to poverty in American culture.
