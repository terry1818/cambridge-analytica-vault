## Summary

Jemar Tisby, PhD, is an American historian specializing in race, religion, and social movements in the United States. He is the New York Times bestselling author of *The Color of Compromise: The Truth About the American Church's Complicity in Racism* (Zondervan, 2019), which traces four centuries of white American church accommodation and complicity in racial hierarchy. He wrote the foreword for [[Source - Gorski Perry 2022 - The Flag and the Cross|Gorski and Perry's *The Flag and the Cross* (2022)]], bringing his historical perspective on racial Christianity to their analytical framework of white Christian nationalism.

Tisby's work is historically grounded and explicitly normative: he argues that white Christians have a moral reckoning to undertake and documents the history with which they must reckon. He is an important bridge figure between academic historical scholarship and public Christian discourse on race.

## Key facts

- Holds a BA from the University of Notre Dame; an MDiv from Reformed Theological Seminary; a PhD in History from the University of Mississippi (focusing on race, religion, and social movements).
- Founded The Witness, Inc., an organization dedicated to Black Christian uplift and counter-narrative to white evangelical dominance.
- Co-hosted the *Pass the Mic* podcast, one of the earlier major platforms for Black Reformed Protestant voices.
- Former PRRI senior fellow; his affiliation with [[PRRI]] connects him to [[Robert P. Jones]]'s survey-based research universe.
- Professor of History at Simmons College of Kentucky (HBCU, Louisville) 2022–2025; appointed Assistant Professor of History at Clemson University beginning January 2026.
- Writing has appeared in The New York Times, The Atlantic, and CNN; widely cited as an expert on race and the American church.
- Reformed Presbyterian background (PCA-adjacent) that has become increasingly strained given the denomination's race relations record — a biographical arc he has discussed publicly.

## Public statements

Tisby has argued that American Christianity's racial problem is not a matter of individual prejudice but structural complicity: "Racist ideas don't require racist people. The perpetuation of racism can occur through policies, laws, and cultural norms that advantage whiteness while disadvantaging non-white people — and most white churches have refused to challenge those structures." He has been publicly critical of Reformed evangelical institutions (seminaries, denominations) for historical and ongoing failure to address racism.
