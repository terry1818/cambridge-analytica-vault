## Summary

George Edward Creel (December 1, 1876 – October 2, 1953) was an American investigative journalist, progressive politician, and government official. He is best known for chairing the [[Committee on Public Information]] (CPI) — the US government's WWI propaganda agency — from its founding in April 1917 until its dissolution in August 1919. Tim Wu's *The Attention Merchants* treats Creel and the CPI as the origin point of a decisive shift in the relationship between the state, mass media, and public opinion in America: the moment when the techniques of commercial attention-harvesting were systematically applied to political mobilization at national scale.

Before his government role, Creel was a muckraking journalist in the progressive tradition, advocating for child labor reform, women's suffrage, and direct democracy from Kansas City newspapers. His appointment to the CPI reflected President Wilson's confidence in his ability to manage press relations and public opinion — skills Creel had developed across two decades of newspaper work.

## Key Facts

- Born in Blackburn, Lafayette County, Missouri; did not complete high school.
- Began newspaper career as reporter for the Kansas City World in 1898; briefly moved to New York, returned to Kansas City.
- Became sole editor and publisher of the Kansas City Independent in 1899; used the paper to advocate progressive causes.
- Later edited the Rocky Mountain News (Denver) and Denver Post.
- Appointed CPI chairman by President Wilson within a week of the US entering WWI in April 1917.
- The CPI produced more war posters than all other belligerent nations combined; placed estimated 20,000 newspaper columns of government-generated content weekly.
- Organized the Four Minute Men: 75,000 volunteer speakers delivering standardized pro-war speeches at public gatherings.
- [[Edward Bernays]] worked under the CPI in its foreign publicity division; Bernays later credited the CPI experience as foundational to his theory of public relations.
- After the war, Creel ran unsuccessfully for governor of California in 1934 in the Democratic primary (losing to Upton Sinclair, who then lost the general).
- Joined Collier's magazine as a feature writer after the CPI; retired late 1940s.
- FDR appointed Creel chairman of the National Advisory Board for the Works Progress Administration.
- Married to actress Blanche Bates from 1912 until her death in 1941.

## Relationships

- [[Woodrow Wilson]] — Political patron; Wilson appointed Creel and gave the CPI broad authority.
- [[Edward Bernays]] — Worked under Creel in the CPI's foreign press section; translated CPI methods into commercial public relations and political consulting.
- [[Claude Hopkins]] — Contemporaneous figure; Hopkins' scientific advertising methodology shares the assumption that mass audience attention can be systematically captured through emotional appeals and repetition.
- [[Committee on Public Information]] — The institution Creel created and led.

## Public Statements

- "How We Advertised America" (1920) — Creel's own account of CPI operations; title reflects his understanding that the CPI was essentially an advertising campaign for the war.
- Creel maintained throughout his life that the CPI's work had been honest persuasion, not propaganda — a claim that became increasingly contested as post-war scholarship documented the CPI's role in suppressing dissent.
