## Summary

Mark Driscoll founded Mars Hill Church in Seattle in 1996 with a deliberate strategy of reaching unchurched young men through a presentation of Christianity emphasizing aggressive masculinity, cultural engagement, and authoritative preaching. The church grew into one of the most widely discussed megachurches in the United States, eventually operating 13 locations across Washington, California, Oregon, and New Mexico, with tens of thousands of weekly attendees and a vast online and podcast reach.

Kristin Du Mez's [[Source - Du Mez 2020 - Jesus and John Wayne]] analyzes Driscoll as a defining figure in the construction and mass marketing of "militant masculinity" within evangelical culture — a framework that deliberately conflated Christian faith with aggressive, dominance-oriented manhood, framed passive or "soft" Christianity as the cause of social decay, and positioned women's submission to male authority as a core theological commitment. Driscoll's preaching style, his explicit and provocative statements about gender and sexuality, and Mars Hill's organizational culture were simultaneously influential and, to critics, emblematic of how authoritarian masculinity theology enabled institutional abuse.

In the summer of 2014, formal complaints from Mars Hill staff and congregants — alleging abusive behavior, bullying, financial opacity, and harsh member discipline — triggered an investigation by the Acts 29 Network, which Driscoll had co-founded. The Acts 29 board removed Mars Hill from membership and urged Driscoll to step down. A church investigation found Driscoll "quick-tempered, arrogant, and domineering." He resigned October 14, 2014; Mars Hill dissolved within three months. Since 2016, Driscoll has led The Trinity Church in Scottsdale, Arizona, and has continued to face calls from former Mars Hill elders to step down, with 40+ former elders publicly stating in 2021 that he is "presently unfit for serving the church in the office of pastor."

## Key Facts

- Born October 11, 1970, in Grand Rapids, Michigan
- B.A. in speech communications, Washington State University
- Founded Mars Hill Church, Seattle, 1996 — began as a small group in his living room
- Co-founded Acts 29 Network (church planting network)
- Mars Hill grew to 13 locations; one of the fastest-growing megachurches in the US
- Widely known for explicit, provocative statements on gender, sex, and sexuality from the pulpit
- Church investigation (2014) found him "quick-tempered, arrogant, and domineering"
- Acts 29 Network removed Mars Hill from membership and urged Driscoll's resignation (August 2014)
- Resigned from Mars Hill, October 14, 2014
- Mars Hill dissolved completely within three months of his resignation
- Founded The Trinity Church, Scottsdale, AZ, 2016
- 40+ former Mars Hill elders publicly called for him to step down from Trinity Church (2021), stating he is "presently unfit" for pastoral ministry
- Allegations: bullying, misogyny, financial opacity, harsh member discipline, plagiarism

## Relationships

- **Acts 29 Network** — Church planting organization Driscoll co-founded; removed him and Mars Hill in 2014
- **John Piper / Reformed complementarian network** — Theological allies in complementarianism (male authority over women); Driscoll operated in and pushed the boundaries of the Reformed evangelical world
- **[[Promise Keepers]] / Promise Keepers theology** — Driscoll's masculinity theology shares roots with and extends the Promise Keepers tradition
- **[[John Eldredge]]** — Fellow evangelical masculinity author; Du Mez traces both as part of the same cultural wave, with Driscoll's version more aggressive

## Public Statements

Driscoll made numerous documented inflammatory statements about gender, sexuality, and masculinity from the pulpit at Mars Hill. These are extensively documented in Christianity Today, the *Driscoll Controversy* website, and Du Mez's book. Attribution of specific quotes requires verification against primary recordings given the contested and evolving record.

## Open Questions

- The full financial record of Mars Hill Church, including how book revenue and speaking fees were handled, was not transparently resolved during the 2014 investigation
- The downstream influence of Mars Hill's church planting model through Acts 29 and similar networks on evangelical culture in the 2010s–2020s is significant and underexplored
- Driscoll's continued ministry at Trinity Church and his lack of formal accountability process raises ongoing questions about institutional mechanisms in non-denominational evangelical contexts
