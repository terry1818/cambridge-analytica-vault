## Summary

Ali Alexander (birth name Ali Abdul-Razaq Akbar) is an American far-right activist who rose from minor Republican operative to the primary organizer of the "Stop the Steal" movement following the 2020 election. He is documented in Donovan, Dreyfuss, and Friedberg's *Meme Wars* as a key node in the online-to-offline mobilization infrastructure that channeled far-right internet culture into organized street presence. His role in coordinating the January 6 rally, and his admitted scheming with Republican members of Congress, makes him a significant figure in the election-denial ecosystem.

## Key facts

- Born to a Christian African-American mother and a Muslim immigrant father from the UAE; raised in Fort Worth, Texas.
- Previously convicted of felony property theft and credit card abuse (2007–2008).
- Began in Republican politics around 2007 (John McCain campaign staffer).
- Long-standing ties to [[Roger Stone]], [[Alex Jones]], and other far-right network figures.
- Legally changed his name from Ali Akbar to Ali Alexander at some point during his activist career.
- Organized "Stop the Steal" rallies across battleground states after November 2020.
- Admitted in livestreams that he "schemed" with Representatives Andy Biggs, Paul Gosar, and Mo Brooks to maximize pressure on Congress on January 6.
- Banned from Twitter after the Capitol attack; the Stop the Steal account was also removed.
- Faced unverified but documented allegations of sexual misconduct involving minors.

## Relationships

- [[Roger Stone]] — mentor and network connection; Stone's tactics of deliberate provocation visible in Alexander's style.
- [[Alex Jones]] — collaborator in Stop the Steal amplification.
- [[Milo Yiannopoulos]] — documented social connection; allegations involve Yiannopoulos as a lure.
- Andy Biggs, Paul Gosar, Mo Brooks — GOP members of Congress Alexander claimed co-organized January 6 rally strategy.

## Public statements

- December 2020 livestream: "We four schemed up of putting maximum pressure on Congress while they were voting."
- Has described Stop the Steal as a constitutionally protected political movement.

## Open questions

- Extent of formal coordination with White House staff or Trump campaign in planning January 6 events remains disputed.
- Congressional investigations produced subpoenas; degree of cooperation and full testimony record not fully public as of this writing.
- Allegations of sexual misconduct have not resulted in criminal charges (as of 2026).
