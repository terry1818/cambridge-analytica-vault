## Summary

Michael Novak was the central theological architect of the Catholic neoconservative case for democratic capitalism — the argument that free-market economics, representative democracy, and Catholic moral tradition are not only compatible but mutually reinforcing. His 1982 book *The Spirit of Democratic Capitalism* became the foundational text for a generation of Catholic conservatives who sought to reconcile Church social teaching with American liberalism in its classical, market-oriented form.

Damon Linker's *The Theocons* (2006) placed Novak in the founding generation of Catholic neoconservatives centered on the journal *First Things* and institutions like AEI and the [[Ethics and Public Policy Center]]. Novak's project was distinct from later post-liberal and integralist thinkers: he sought integration with liberal capitalism rather than its rejection. His influence was felt most strongly from the 1980s through the 2000s.

## Key facts

- Born 1933 in Johnstown, Pennsylvania; died February 17, 2017 in Washington, D.C. (complications from colon cancer); age 83.
- Studied for the priesthood at the Stonehill College seminary; left before ordination; later married Karen Laub (1963).
- Began political life as a progressive Catholic aligned with Eugene McCarthy (1968 campaign); shifted rightward through the 1970s; identified as neoconservative by the 1980s.
- Joined AEI in 1978; retired 2010; held the George Frederick Jewett Chair in Religion, Philosophy, and Public Policy.
- Author of more than 40 books; most influential: *The Spirit of Democratic Capitalism* (1982), which argued capitalism has a "spiritual" dimension overlooked by both secular liberals and Catholic social critics.
- Served as US Ambassador to the UN Human Rights Commission under Reagan (1981–82, 1984).
- Received the 1994 Templeton Prize — at the time the largest monetary prize in the world, exceeding the Nobel Prize — for "exceptional contribution to affirming life's spiritual dimension."
- Close intellectual relationship with [[Richard John Neuhaus]]; both contributed to *First Things* and shaped Catholic neoconservative discourse through the 1990s.
- Memoir *Writing From Left to Right: My Journey From Liberal to Conservative* (2013) documented his political evolution.

## Relationships

- **[[Richard John Neuhaus]]** — closest intellectual ally; co-architects of Catholic neoconservatism; both contributed to *First Things*.
- **[[George Weigel]]** — fellow Catholic neoconservative at [[Ethics and Public Policy Center]]; shared orbit of AEI, EPPC, and *First Things*.
- **[[Robert P. George]]** — younger generation of the same network; George's natural-law work complemented Novak's political theology.
- **American Enterprise Institute** — primary institutional home 1978–2010.
- **[[Ethics and Public Policy Center]]** — affiliated; close to EPPC orbit.
- **[[First Things magazine]]** — regular contributor.

## Public statements

- "Democratic capitalism is not a system but a way of life. It asks us to trust one another, to cooperate in commerce... It is rooted in Jewish and Christian vision of God, sin, and the individual." (*The Spirit of Democratic Capitalism*, paraphrase)
- On capitalism and Catholic social teaching: argued that the Church's critique of capitalism in *Centesimus Annus* (1991) was being misread, and that John Paul II's encyclical actually endorsed market economies — a reading contested by other Catholic theologians and later addressed in scholarship on [[George Weigel]]'s similar interpretive moves.

## Open questions

- Novak's reading of *Centesimus Annus* as endorsing democratic capitalism has been contested by scholars who argue this was a selective interpretation. The degree to which his framework shaped subsequent Catholic accommodation of conservative economic policy warrants further examination.
- His early progressive phase and later full neoconservative alignment raises questions about the role of the Vietnam War and cultural disruption of the late 1960s as generative factors in Catholic neoconservatism broadly.
