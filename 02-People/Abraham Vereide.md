## Summary

Abraham Vereide was a Norwegian-born Methodist minister who emigrated to the United States in 1905 and built one of the most consequential — and deliberately obscure — religious political networks in American history. In April 1935, responding to what he described as a 1:30 a.m. divine vision, he gathered 19 Seattle businessmen and civic leaders for a prayer breakfast. The meeting was explicitly anti-New Deal in orientation: Vereide believed that elite conversion — reaching leaders rather than masses — was the correct strategy for Christianizing American public life. From that small gathering grew [[The Fellowship]], later known as The Family, an international organization that would connect senators, presidents, and foreign heads of state through a theology of power.

Jeff Sharlet's investigative reporting, documented in [[Source Sharlet 2008 The Family Book]], brought sustained public attention to Vereide's founding ideology: that the most powerful men are, by virtue of their power, closer to God's purposes, and that their conversion to a Christ-like leadership model would transform societies more effectively than any grassroots movement.

## Key Facts

- Born October 7, 1886, in Norway; emigrated 1905 after a neighbor gave him an unused ticket to Montana
- Worked as a Methodist minister and labor organizer before founding his prayer network
- Founded the first Seattle prayer breakfast in April 1935 with 19 business and civic leaders, explicitly as an alternative to FDR's New Deal politics
- By 1942 had established 60 breakfast groups in major US and Canadian cities
- Moved operations to Washington, D.C., in 1942, building a network among federal legislators and executive branch officials
- In 1953, with the Fellowship's backing, founded the Presidential Prayer Breakfast — later renamed the National Prayer Breakfast — which became an annual Washington institution attended by presidents
- Maintained deliberate institutional anonymity: the Fellowship avoided public profiles, press coverage, and formal membership lists
- His theological model emphasized personal relationships with powerful individuals over doctrinal conformity or public evangelism
- Died May 16, 1969; leadership passed to [[Doug Coe]], who ran The Fellowship for decades

## Relationships

- **[[Doug Coe]]** — Successor; ran The Fellowship from Vereide's death until Coe's own death in 2017. Continued and deepened Vereide's elite-access model.
- **[[The Fellowship]]** — The organization Vereide founded; also known as The Family and the Fellowship Foundation.
- **National Prayer Breakfast** — Annual event Vereide established in 1953; a primary mechanism for Fellowship influence on Capitol Hill.
- **C Street House** — [[C Street]] residence for Congressional members, a later manifestation of the Fellowship network Vereide built.

## Public Statements

No public statements on record — consistent with the organization's policy of deliberate obscurity. Vereide's writings survive in the Vereide Family Papers at the Archives of Wheaton College (Billy Graham Center Archives, Collection 459) and at Pacific Lutheran University Archives.

## Open Questions

- The exact ideological and financial networks that supported the 1935 Seattle founding remain underexplored
- The degree to which Vereide's anti-New Deal founding intent shaped long-term Fellowship strategy is not fully documented
- Connections between Vereide's Norwegian Methodist background and his organizational theology deserve further examination
