# Michael McConnell

## Summary

Michael W. McConnell (born 1955) is an American constitutional-law scholar, a professor at Stanford Law School where he directs the Constitutional Law Center, a senior fellow at the Hoover Institution, and a former judge of the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit (2002–2009). Widely regarded as one of the most cited and influential American constitutional scholars of his generation, McConnell is best known for a body of academic work — notably his 1990 Harvard Law Review article "The Origins and Historical Understanding of Free Exercise of Religion" — that reshaped how courts and scholars understand the First Amendment's protection of religion.

McConnell matters to this vault as a key figure in the *intellectual* infrastructure of the religious right's legal project. His scholarship argued, on originalist and historical grounds, for a robust constitutional accommodation of religious practice — including the position that the Free Exercise Clause may require exemptions from generally applicable laws. These arguments were advanced before the Supreme Court's 1990 *Employment Division v. Smith* decision narrowed free-exercise protection, and they helped supply the doctrinal vocabulary later used to push back toward expansive religious-liberty rulings. The vault treats McConnell as an example of the "constitutional capture" pattern's *legitimate-scholarship* end: unlike movement operatives, McConnell is a serious academic whose work is widely respected across the spectrum, and he has at times broken with conservative legal orthodoxy. But his ideas became load-bearing components of the litigation strategy through which the [[Federalist Society]] network and religious-liberty firms reshaped doctrine. Distinguishing genuine scholarly contribution from movement instrumentalization is exactly the kind of evidence-honesty problem this vault tracks.
