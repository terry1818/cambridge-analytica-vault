## Summary

Cathy O'Neil is an American mathematician and data scientist who transitioned from academic mathematics through finance to become one of the most prominent public critics of algorithmic decision-making. Her 2016 book *Weapons of Math Destruction: How Big Data Increases Inequality and Threatens Democracy* (Crown/Penguin Random House) provided a systematic framework for identifying when algorithms cause harm, long-listed for the National Book Award. O'Neil identified three defining features of a destructive algorithm: opacity (the model's workings are hidden), scale (it affects millions of people), and damage (it causes real harm, often to the already-disadvantaged). She later founded ORCAA, a firm that conducts algorithmic audits for organizations.

## Key facts

- PhD in mathematics from Harvard University (1999); prior undergraduate work at UC Berkeley.
- Held postdoctoral and faculty positions at MIT and Barnard College before leaving academia in 2007.
- Worked at D. E. Shaw & Co. as a quantitative analyst 2007–2011; left finance disillusioned by the 2008 financial crisis.
- Participated in the Occupy Wall Street movement's Alternative Banking Group, which prompted her turn toward algorithmic critique.
- *Weapons of Math Destruction* (2016): New York Times bestseller; longlisted for the National Book Award; won the MAA Euler Book Prize (2019).
- Founded O'Neil Risk Consulting & Algorithmic Auditing (ORCAA), a firm providing independent algorithmic audits.
- Third book: *The Shame Machine: Who Profits in the New Age of Humiliation* (Crown, 2022).
- Maintained the influential blog "mathbabe" from around 2011 onward.

## Public statements

O'Neil has argued that "a model is not reality; it's a simplification of reality," and that harmful algorithms launder human moral choices through the appearance of mathematical objectivity. She has consistently called for mandatory algorithmic auditing as a regulatory baseline.
