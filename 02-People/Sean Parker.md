## Summary

Sean Parker is a Silicon Valley entrepreneur best known as the co-founder of Napster and as Facebook's first president (2004–2005). In the context of this vault, he is significant for his November 2017 public remarks at an Axios event in Philadelphia, in which he described Facebook's engagement mechanics in terms strikingly consistent with the persuasive technology critique developed by [[B.J. Fogg]] and the concern about behavior manipulation raised by scholars like [[Siva Vaidhyanathan]]. His statements provide rare insider confirmation that Facebook's design was deliberate and understood by its founders as psychologically manipulative. Roger McNamee's *Zucked* treats Parker's 2017 candor as corroborating evidence for the platform's intentionally addictive architecture.

## Key facts

- Born 1979; taught himself to code as a teenager; hacked into corporate networks and was investigated by the FBI before age 16.
- Co-founded Napster with Shawn Fanning in 1999; Napster was shut down by court order in 2001.
- Became Facebook's first president in 2004 after introducing Mark Zuckerberg to Peter Thiel; departed 2005 after a personal legal incident.
- Early and significant investor in Spotify; served on its board.
- Founded the Parker Foundation, which focuses on life sciences, cancer immunotherapy, and civic engagement.
- November 2017, Axios event: stated that Facebook's design asked "How do we consume as much of your time and conscious attention as possible?" and described the "social validation feedback loop" as exploiting "a vulnerability in human psychology." Said he was a "conscientious objector" to social media.

## Relationships

- **Mark Zuckerberg**: Introduced Zuckerberg to Peter Thiel; shaped early Facebook corporate structure.
- **[[Facebook]]**: First president; departed before the company's major data-monetization build-out but was present during formative design decisions.
- **Peter Thiel**: Parker introduced Zuckerberg to Thiel, enabling the $500,000 angel investment that stabilized early Facebook.
- **[[B.J. Fogg]]**: The persuasive technology framework Fogg developed at Stanford is the academic precursor to what Parker described in lay terms; both describe the same design logic.

## Public statements

Key 2017 Axios remarks (widely reported, confirmed):
> "How do we consume as much of your time and conscious attention as possible? ... a little dopamine hit every once in a while because someone liked or commented on a photo or a post ... It's a social validation feedback loop ... You're exploiting a vulnerability in human psychology."

> "God only knows what it's doing to our children's brains."

## Open questions

- Whether Parker's departure from Facebook in 2005 meant he had no input into the design decisions he later criticized, or whether early architectural choices he helped make were determinative.
- The degree to which his 2017 remarks reflect genuine regret vs. strategic positioning in the post-2016 tech backlash.
