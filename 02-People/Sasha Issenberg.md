# Sasha Issenberg

## Summary

Sasha Issenberg is an American journalist and author best known for "The Victory Lab: The Secret Science of Winning Campaigns" (2012), the book that first explained to a general audience how randomized field experiments, behavioral psychology, and large-scale data mining were reshaping American electioneering. A former national political correspondent for The Boston Globe (covering the 2008 election) and later a columnist for Slate and Washington correspondent for Monocle, Issenberg also covered campaign technology for Bloomberg Politics. He has lectured on political science at UCLA.

Issenberg matters to this vault because "The Victory Lab" documents the *legitimate* scientific lineage of data-driven campaigning — the line of work running from [[Alan Gerber]] and [[Donald Green]]'s controlled field experiments on voter turnout through the analytics operations of the 2008 and 2012 Obama campaigns. This lineage is essential context for evaluating [[Cambridge Analytica]]'s later claims. CA marketed "psychographic" targeting as a revolutionary capability; understanding the actual evidentiary state of campaign science — what field experiments had and had not shown about persuasion versus mobilization — is what allows analysts to distinguish CA's genuine techniques from its sales hype. Issenberg's reporting frames campaigns as treating voters as "unwitting guinea pigs" in experiments, a phrase that captures both the scientific rigor and the ethical questions the vault tracks. His later book "The Engagement" (2021) is a history of the same-sex marriage debate.

## Relationships

- **Documented in his reporting:** [[Alan Gerber]] and [[Donald Green]] — the Yale/Columbia field-experiment researchers whose work "The Victory Lab" traces; the Obama 2008 and 2012 analytics teams.
- **Analytical contrast:** [[Cambridge Analytica]] — Issenberg's documented account of campaign science is the baseline against which CA's psychographic claims can be tested.
- **Adjacent commentators:** [[Eitan Hersh]] — political scientist whose work on campaign data and skepticism of psychographic effectiveness complements this lineage.

## Public statements

- Issenberg described modern campaigns as deploying "research from behavioral psychology, data-mining, and randomized experiments that treat voters as unwitting guinea pigs" — framing his subject in "The Victory Lab."
- In interviews after the Cambridge Analytica scandal he argued that data analytics in campaigns was real and consequential, but that CA's specific psychographic-targeting claims were oversold relative to the demonstrated science.
