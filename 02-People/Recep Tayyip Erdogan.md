## Summary

Recep Tayyip Erdoğan rose from humble origins in Istanbul to become Turkey's longest-serving leader. He co-founded the Justice and Development Party (AKP) in 2001 and has governed Turkey since 2003 — first as Prime Minister, then as President from 2014. His tenure divides into two recognizable phases: a reformist period (2003–2011) in which Turkey pursued EU accession, improved civil liberties, and curbed military power over politics; and a consolidation period (2011–present) marked by purges of opponents, media takeovers, constitutional changes concentrating power in the presidency, and escalating authoritarian behavior. A 2016 coup attempt accelerated the pace of consolidation dramatically.

**Why the vault holds this figure as a comparative case.** Ben-Ghiat's *Strongmen* places Erdoğan as the paradigm case of 21st-century elected authoritarianism — a leader who arrived through free elections, used democratic institutions instrumentally to consolidate power, and then gradually foreclosed the institutional pathways that could remove him. The vault uses Erdoğan to test several specific mechanisms relevant to the US case: (1) the use of a *failed coup or emergency event* to justify massive purges (the 2016 coup attempt enabled over 150,000 arrests and dismissals, analogous to [[January 6 Capitol Attack]] and emergency-framing questions); (2) the role of *media capture* in sustaining electoral authoritarianism (by 2019, roughly 90% of Turkish media was government-aligned, relevant to the vault's tech-right media analysis); (3) the use of *constitutional referendum* to institutionalize personal rule (the 2017 referendum eliminated the Prime Minister's office and created a hyper-presidential system). Erdoğan is the closest living comparator to the post-2024 US trajectory in the vault's analytical framework.

## Key facts

- Imprisoned briefly in 1999 for reciting a nationalist poem; this is often cited as radicalizing his democratic-reform rhetoric.
- AKP won 34% of the vote in 2002 and gained a parliamentary majority due to the electoral threshold — illustrating how plurality systems can generate authoritarian majorities.
- Early EU reform period (2003–2011) was broadly praised by Western governments and brought Turkey closer to EU candidacy than at any previous time.
- The Gezi Park protests (2013) — against plans to redevelop a public space — were suppressed with tear gas and water cannons; Erdoğan's response marked the visible turning point toward harder authoritarianism.
- The Gülen movement (Hizmet), a parallel Islamic network that had been an AKP ally, was accused of orchestrating the July 15, 2016 coup attempt. Whether the coup was genuine, staged, or something in between remains disputed. (Tier-3: the Turkish government's account is not independently verified; the coup clearly happened; who ordered it is contested.)
- Post-coup purges: over 150,000 people arrested or detained; 150,000+ civil servants dismissed; 160+ media outlets closed; thousands of academics fired.
- The April 2017 constitutional referendum (passed by 51.4% — a narrow margin, with electoral irregularities alleged by monitors) eliminated the Prime Minister's office, gave the president control over the judiciary, and allowed emergency decrees.
- Freedom House downgraded Turkey from "Partly Free" to "Not Free" in 2018.
- Won the 2023 presidential election (first round 49.5%, then runoff 52.2%), defeating Kemal Kılıçdaroğlu — maintaining electoral legitimacy even as institutional constraints on opposition were severe.

## Open questions

- Is Turkey under Erdoğan best classified as a "competitive authoritarian" regime (Levitsky/Way) or a "hybrid regime" — and what does the 2023 genuine election result tell us about whether pure autocracy has been achieved?
- The 2019 Istanbul re-run — the government ordered a re-run after the opposition won, then the opposition won by a larger margin — is an unusual data point: does it represent residual democratic resilience, or tactical calculation by the regime?
