## Summary

John Eldredge is the author of *Wild at Heart: Discovering the Secret of a Man's Soul* (2001), a book that sold millions of copies and became the most widely read popular articulation of evangelical masculine identity theology in the early 21st century. Kristin Du Mez's [[Source - Du Mez 2020 - Jesus and John Wayne]] identifies *Wild at Heart* as a watershed text in the consolidation of evangelical masculinity as a cultural framework — one that preceded, enabled, and provided theological vocabulary for the more overtly political and militant masculinity movements that followed.

Eldredge's core argument in *Wild at Heart* is that God designed men with three core desires: a battle to fight, an adventure to live, and a beauty to rescue. Contemporary Christianity, he argued, had feminized men by suppressing these desires through domesticity, passivity, and niceness, producing spiritually neutered men who had lost their masculine soul. The book called men to reclaim their "wild" essential nature as the path to genuine Christian faith and flourishing relationships. Women were positioned as the "beauty to be rescued" — passive recipients of masculine adventure and protection.

Before writing *Wild at Heart*, Eldredge worked for twelve years at Focus on the Family, the organization James Dobson built into the flagship institution of the evangelical cultural-political complex. He left Focus on the Family in 2000 to found Ransomed Heart Ministries in Colorado Springs, which continues to produce books, podcasts, and retreats based on his framework.

Du Mez's analysis traces how *Wild at Heart*'s framework — appearing soft and therapeutic in tone compared to Mark Driscoll's confrontational style — nonetheless established the same underlying ideological architecture: male authority, female submission, aggressive masculine virtue, and suspicion of "soft" or egalitarian Christianity. That architecture, Du Mez argues, primed millions of evangelical men for the explicitly political and authoritarian masculinity movements that followed.

## Key Facts

- Born June 6, 1960, Los Angeles
- B.A. in theater, California Polytechnic University (Pomona)
- M.A. in biblical counseling, Colorado Christian University (under Larry Crabb and Dan Allender)
- Staff, Sierra Madre Congregational Church, 1983–1988
- Staff, Focus on the Family, 1988–2000 (12 years)
- Founded Ransomed Heart Ministries, Colorado Springs, CO, July 2000
- Published *Wild at Heart* (Thomas Nelson, 2001) — *New York Times* bestseller
- *Wild at Heart* sold millions of copies; translated into dozens of languages
- Follow-up books include *Waking the Dead* (2003), *The Way of the Wild Heart* (2006), *Fathered by God* (2009), and numerous others
- Retreats and online community at wildatheart.org
- *Wild at Heart* drew theological criticism from within evangelicalism for its anthropological claims and treatment of gender

## Relationships

- **Focus on the Family** — Twelve-year institutional home; Dobson's organization gave Eldredge his national platform
- **James Dobson** — Focus on the Family founder; Eldredge's employer for a decade; part of the same evangelical cultural-political complex
- **[[Mark Driscoll]]** — Fellow evangelical masculinity author; Du Mez traces Driscoll's more aggressive version as building on and radicalizing the Eldredge framework
- **[[Bill McCartney]] / [[Promise Keepers]]** — Parallel movement in the same cultural wave; both channeling evangelical masculine energy in the 1990s–2000s
- **Larry Crabb / Dan Allender** — Academic mentors at Colorado Christian University; shaped Eldredge's counseling approach

## Public Statements

From *Wild at Heart* (2001): "Every man longs for a battle to fight, an adventure to live, and a beauty to rescue." — the foundational tripartite framework around which the book is organized.

The book explicitly argues that Christianity has been "feminized" and calls men to "recover the image of a warrior" as authentic Christian manhood.

## Open Questions

- The precise relationship between *Wild at Heart*'s individual-therapeutic framing and the more overtly political masculinity movements it enabled is a live scholarly debate
- Whether Eldredge's framework constitutes a contribution to Christian nationalism's intellectual infrastructure, or primarily a cultural-therapeutic movement with political side-effects, is a question Du Mez addresses but does not fully resolve
- The reception and adoption of *Wild at Heart* outside the United States — in Global South evangelical contexts — is not well documented
