# Alan Gerber

## Summary

Alan S. Gerber is an American political scientist at Yale University, where he is a Sterling Professor of Political Science and has served as Dean of Social Science in the Faculty of Arts and Sciences. Together with [[Donald Green]], Gerber is credited with reviving and modernizing the use of randomized controlled field experiments in political science — a methodological turn that transformed the study of voter behavior from observational inference to causal experiment.

Gerber matters to this vault as the co-architect of the empirical baseline against which [[Cambridge Analytica]]'s claims must be evaluated. The Gerber–Green research program established, with hard causal evidence, what actually moves voters: their landmark 1998 New Haven get-out-the-vote (GOTV) experiment showed that face-to-face canvassing reliably increases turnout while impersonal contact (direct mail, robocalls) does much less. Their 2008 study "Social Pressure and Voter Turnout," a randomized experiment involving roughly 180,000 Michigan voters, showed that mailings threatening to publicize a recipient's voting record to neighbors produced large turnout effects — demonstrating that social-pressure mechanisms, not psychological "personality" profiling, were the empirically robust lever. This body of work is the scientific yardstick the vault uses: it shows that mobilization effects are real and measurable, while the persuasion-by-psychographic-profile model CA marketed has far weaker experimental support.

## Relationships

- **Co-conspirators / collaborators:** [[Donald Green]] — primary research partner across the field-experiment program; Christopher Larimer — co-author of the 2008 social-pressure study.
- **Documented by:** [[Sasha Issenberg]] — "The Victory Lab" traces the Gerber–Green program as the scientific origin of modern campaign analytics.
- **Analytical contrast:** [[Cambridge Analytica]] — CA's psychographic-persuasion claims lack the experimental support that Gerber–Green's mobilization findings have.

## Public statements

- The 2008 study concluded that "substantially higher turnout was observed among those who received mailings promising to publicize ... their turnout to ... their neighbors" — the central finding of the social-pressure experiment.
- Gerber and Green's work consistently emphasizes that personal contact mobilizes, and that many widely used campaign tactics have small or null measured effects.
