## Summary

Patrick Buchanan is a Washington-born conservative politician, syndicated columnist, and television commentator whose three presidential campaigns (1992 and 1996 as Republican, 2000 as Reform Party nominee) established the template for twenty-first-century right-wing populism in the United States. His platform — trade protectionism, immigration restriction, opposition to military interventionism, and explicit cultural nationalism — was marginalized in the 1990s but largely absorbed by the Trump movement after 2015. John Ganz's *When the Clock Broke* places Buchanan at the centre of the 1990s political crisis that revealed deep fractures in the post-Cold War consensus.

## Key facts

- Born 2 November 1938, Washington D.C.; Catholic, Irish-American background.
- Served as special assistant and speechwriter to Presidents Nixon and Ford; White House communications director under Reagan (1985–87).
- 1992 New Hampshire primary: finished strong second against incumbent George H.W. Bush, winning 37% — a humiliating showing for a sitting president.
- His 1992 Republican National Convention speech declared a "cultural war" going on in America, widely seen as alienating moderate voters and contributing to Bush's general-election defeat.
- 1996 campaign won the New Hampshire primary outright, briefly becoming the front-runner before losing to Bob Dole.
- 1999: left the Republican Party to seek the Reform Party nomination, which he secured in 2000; received 449,895 votes (0.4%) in the general election.
- Co-founded *The American Conservative* magazine (2002) as a paleoconservative alternative to neoconservative-dominated publications.
- Intellectual collaborator of [[Samuel Francis]], who provided theoretical grounding for the "Middle American Radicals" base Buchanan courted.

## Relationships

- [[Samuel Francis]] — chief intellectual; Francis wrote the theoretical framework for the [[Middle American Radicals]] base Buchanan mobilised.
- [[Ross Perot]] — Reform Party predecessor; Buchanan took over a party Perot had built.
- [[David Duke]] — Duke endorsed Buchanan in 1992, creating a political liability Buchanan navigated.
- [[Paleoconservatism]] — the broader movement Buchanan became the most prominent public face of.
- [[Steve Bannon]] — Bannon has cited Buchanan as a direct precursor to Trumpism.

## Public statements

- "There is a religious war going on in our country for the soul of America. It is a cultural war, as critical to the kind of nation we will one day be as was the Cold War itself." — RNC, August 1992.
- Buchanan's 1999 book *A Republic, Not an Empire* argued that US entry into World War II was not inevitable and that American intervention in Europe was not in the national interest — a position widely condemned as historically revisionist.
