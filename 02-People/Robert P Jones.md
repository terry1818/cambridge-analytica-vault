## Summary

Robert P. Jones is the founder and president of the [[PRRI|Public Religion Research Institute]] and one of the most influential public scholars of religion and American politics. His research focuses on the relationship between white Christianity and racial power in the United States — work that complements and contextualizes the empirical measurement of [[Christian Nationalism]] done by [[Andrew Whitehead]] and [[Samuel Perry]]. Jones's books combine PRRI survey data with historical analysis and personal narrative, making rigorous research accessible to general audiences.

His 2020 book *White Too Long: The Legacy of White Supremacy in American Christianity* won a 2021 American Book Award. His 2016 book *The End of White Christian America* won the 2019 Grawemeyer Award in Religion — one of the most prestigious awards in the field of religion and theology. His 2023 book *The Hidden Roots of White Supremacy* extended the arc further back in American history.

## Key facts

- Holds a PhD in Religion from Emory University; an MDiv from Southwestern Baptist Theological Seminary; and a BS in Computing Science and Mathematics from Mississippi College.
- Founded PRRI in 2009 because, by his account, there was insufficient nonpartisan public opinion research on religion, culture, and politics.
- Grew up in Mississippi in a white Baptist tradition; this personal history is explicitly woven into his scholarly work, particularly *White Too Long*, which he structured around his family's 1815 Bible alongside contemporary PRRI survey data.
- Before founding PRRI: assistant professor of religious studies at Missouri State University; consultant and senior research fellow at Washington, D.C. think tanks.
- Regularly cited in mainstream media (NYT, Washington Post, NPR) as an expert on white evangelical politics and Christian nationalism.
- Senior fellow [[Jemar Tisby]] and Jones have publicly discussed their overlapping scholarly concerns about race and white Christianity.

## Public statements

Jones has consistently argued that white American Christianity has a structural complicity in racial hierarchy that predates and extends beyond explicit segregationist politics. In *White Too Long* he writes: "As long as white Christians avoid reckoning with white supremacy that remains embedded in the DNA of white Christianity, white churches will continue to be the primary institutional threat to racial justice in the country."

He has also argued that the numerical decline of white Christianity ("The End of White Christian America") does not necessarily mean the decline of white Christian political power — a distinction important for understanding the post-2016 political landscape.
