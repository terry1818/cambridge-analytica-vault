# Kushner family

## Summary

The Kushner family is a New Jersey real-estate dynasty whose trajectory illustrates the fusion of private wealth, criminal-justice controversy, and direct state power that the vault tracks in the patron wing. The family fortune originates with Joseph Kushner, a Holocaust survivor who built a portfolio of New Jersey apartments. His son Charles Kushner (b. 1954) took over and in 1985 founded Kushner Companies, expanding it into a major regional developer. In 2005 Charles pleaded guilty to 18 federal counts — illegal campaign contributions, tax evasion, and witness tampering — the last arising from a notorious scheme to retaliate against a cooperating brother-in-law by hiring a prostitute to entrap him and recording the encounter. He served 14 months in federal prison.

Charles's son Jared Kushner (b. 1981) took over Kushner Companies in 2005 after his father's conviction, and in 2007 led the company's $1.8 billion purchase of the office tower at 666 Fifth Avenue. Jared married Ivanka Trump in 2009 and became a senior adviser in the [[Trump Administration]] (2017–2021), with a wide portfolio including the Abraham Accords. On December 23, 2020, President [[Donald Trump]] — Charles's daughter-in-law's father — issued Charles Kushner a full pardon. In Trump's second term Charles Kushner was named U.S. Ambassador to France. The family is a clear case study in how proximity to presidential power can convert a private fortune and a criminal record into formal state office.

## Key facts

- **Role(s):** Real-estate developers; Kushner Companies founder/chairman (Charles); Senior Adviser to the President (Jared, 2017–2021); diplomat/ambassador (Charles).
- **Affiliations:** Kushner Companies, [[Trump Administration]], Affinity Partners (Jared's post-White-House private-equity firm).
- **Active period:** ~1985–present.
- **Known for:** Kushner Companies; Charles's conviction and pardon; Jared's White House role.
- Charles Kushner founded Kushner Companies in 1985, headquartered in Florham Park, New Jersey.
- In 2005 Charles pleaded guilty to 18 counts; sentenced to two years, served 14 months at Federal Prison Camp Montgomery, Alabama. The prosecutor was then–U.S. Attorney Chris Christie.
- Jared Kushner took over Kushner Companies in 2005; the company purchased 666 Fifth Avenue for $1.8 billion in 2007.
- Jared Kushner served as Senior Adviser to the President 2017–2021; his portfolio included Middle East policy and the Abraham Accords.
- Trump pardoned Charles Kushner on December 23, 2020.
- After leaving the White House, Jared Kushner founded Affinity Partners, which reportedly received a $2 billion investment from a Saudi sovereign wealth fund — raising conflict-of-interest scrutiny.

## Relationships

- **[[Donald Trump]]** — Jared's father-in-law; pardoned Charles Kushner; the relationship that converted the family's private standing into federal office.
- **Ivanka Trump** — Jared Kushner's wife; herself a White House adviser 2017–2021.
- **Joseph Kushner** — family patriarch; built the original New Jersey real-estate portfolio.
- **Chris Christie** — as U.S. Attorney, prosecuted Charles Kushner; later a Trump rival, a tension reported as personal.
- **[[Trump Administration]]** — the institutional vehicle through which Jared exercised state power.
- **[[Adelson family]]** — fellow Trump-aligned Republican patrons; the Adelsons and Kushners both sat in the front row at the 2018 Jerusalem embassy opening.

## Public statements

- Jared Kushner has framed his White House work, especially the Abraham Accords, as a business-style approach to diplomacy.
- Charles Kushner, after his pardon, characterized his prosecution as politically tinged; the trial record and his guilty plea are matters of court record.

## Open questions

- The conflict-of-interest questions around Affinity Partners' Saudi funding and Jared Kushner's prior White House Middle East role remain contested and not fully resolved. `#claim/disputed`
- How much of the family's post-2016 wealth growth is attributable to proximity to presidential power versus ordinary real-estate activity is debated.
- The Kushners are donors and operatives more than network-builders; their precise place in the broader patron-wing infrastructure (versus the Koch/Uihlein institutional model) is a matter of interpretation.
