# Mikhail Khodorkovsky

## Summary

Mikhail Khodorkovsky (born 1963) is an exiled Russian businessman and opposition activist who, before his 2003 arrest, was the wealthiest man in Russia with a fortune estimated at roughly $15 billion. He appears in this vault as a comparative case on two fronts. First, his prosecution and the destruction of his oil company Yukos is the canonical illustration of how Vladimir Putin disciplined Russia's post-Soviet oligarchs — the implicit 2000 bargain that they could keep their wealth so long as they stayed out of politics, and the consequences of breaking it. Second, Khodorkovsky's decade as an exiled opposition figure illustrates what organized resistance to a consolidated authoritarian state looks like from outside its borders, and the lengths to which such a state will go to pursue and delegitimize that resistance.

In 1995, Khodorkovsky's Menatep group acquired Yukos, Russia's second-largest oil company, for roughly $350 million in a controversial "loans-for-shares" privatization auction. When Putin offered the oligarchs a deal in 2000 — keep your industries, stay out of politics — Khodorkovsky rejected it. He funded opposition parties, pushed legislation favorable to private business, and was widely seen as a potential political rival. In October 2003 he was arrested by armed, masked commandos who stormed his plane during a refueling stop in Siberia. He was convicted of fraud and tax evasion, Yukos was broken up with its core assets absorbed by the state-owned Rosneft, and he spent more than a decade in prison before a 2013 pardon. He left Russia, settled in London, and has been a leading Putin critic ever since.

## Relationships

- **Opponents / accusers:** [[Vladimir Putin]] — the central antagonist; the Russian state prosecuted Khodorkovsky, destroyed Yukos, and as of 2025 designates him a "terrorist."
- **Co-conspirators / collaborators:** Fellow exiled opposition figures in the [[Anti-War Committee of Russia]] — including Garry Kasparov, Mikhail Kasyanov, and Vladimir Kara-Murza — with whom Khodorkovsky coordinates anti-war and pro-democracy work.
- **Subordinates / hired:** Through [[Open Russia]] and related funding, Khodorkovsky has bankrolled independent journalism, civil-society projects, and opposition activity inside and outside Russia.

## Public statements

- Khodorkovsky has consistently argued that Putin's system is a personalist dictatorship that cannot be reformed from within and must be opposed by an organized democratic alternative.
- On the 2025 Russian "terrorism" charges, reporting indicates he treated the designation with public defiance — framing it as confirmation of the regime's character rather than a deterrent.

## Open questions

- **The honest oligarch problem:** Khodorkovsky acquired Yukos in a privatization process widely regarded as corrupt and rigged in favor of well-connected insiders. His later reinvention as a democracy advocate is genuine in its activity but does not erase that origin. The vault treats both facts as true: the prosecution was a politically motivated instrument of Putin's power, *and* Khodorkovsky's original fortune came from a tainted privatization. `#claim/disputed` applies to characterizations that flatten this into either pure martyrdom or pure criminality.
- **Effectiveness of exile opposition:** How much real influence exiled Russian opposition figures have on events inside Russia is genuinely uncertain and debated.
