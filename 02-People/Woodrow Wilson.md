# Woodrow Wilson

## Summary

Woodrow Wilson (1856–1924) was the 28th President of the United States and, before that, a political scientist and president of Princeton University. He appears in this vault for two connected reasons. First, his administration created the [[Committee on Public Information]] (CPI) in April 1917 — the first organized, large-scale propaganda apparatus run by the American federal government, and a direct institutional ancestor of state and quasi-state persuasion operations the vault tracks across the twentieth and twenty-first centuries. Second, Wilson is a foundational figure for the *administrative state*: as an academic he argued for professional, expert-led public administration insulated from ordinary politics, and as President he presided over a wartime expansion of federal regulatory and coercive capacity. Both threads — organized persuasion and concentrated administrative power — are central preoccupations of this vault, and Wilson sits at the American origin point of each.

The CPI, headed by [[George Creel]], demonstrated that a democratic government could systematically and deliberately shape public opinion at national scale. It also coincided with — and its messaging amplified — the suppression of dissent under the Espionage Act of 1917 and the Sedition Act of 1918. The vault treats Wilson not as a villain but as a precedent: he shows that mass government persuasion and the contraction of civil liberties are not foreign or modern aberrations but have a domestic American lineage running back over a century.

## Key facts

- **Role(s):** Academic political scientist; Princeton president; Governor of New Jersey; U.S. President (two terms, 1913–1921).
- **Affiliations:** Democratic Party; created the [[Committee on Public Information]] by Executive Order 2594.
- **Active period:** 1885 (first academic writing) – 1921.
- **Known for:** WWI-era propaganda infrastructure; the Espionage and Sedition Acts; Progressive-era administrative reform; the Fourteen Points and the League of Nations.

## Relationships

- **Subordinates / hired:** [[George Creel]] — journalist appointed to chair the Committee on Public Information; the operational architect of the CPI's nationwide persuasion campaign.
- **Reports to / worked under:** As an academic, Wilson worked within the Progressive intellectual movement that advocated expert administration; he is frequently cited (and criticized) as a theorist of the administrative state.
- **Co-conspirators / collaborators:** The CPI mobilized advertisers, journalists, academics, artists, and the "Four Minute Men" volunteer speakers — an early model of public-private persuasion partnership.

## Public statements

- "The Study of Administration" (1887): argued that administration should be run by trained experts on businesslike principles, partly insulated from partisan politics — a text central to debates over the administrative state.
- Wilson framed U.S. entry into WWI as a campaign to "make the world safe for democracy" — the kind of expansive moral framing the CPI then amplified across every available channel.

## Open questions

- **Persuasion vs. propaganda:** The CPI's defenders described it as public information and morale-building; critics, including many contemporaries, concluded it had "oversold" the war and created a climate hostile to legitimate dissent. The vault records both readings and treats the CPI as the documented origin of organized U.S. government persuasion regardless of how its intent is judged.
- **The administrative-state lineage:** Wilson is invoked by the modern right's "constitutional capture" thinkers as the founder of an illegitimate administrative state, and by others as a Progressive reformer. The vault notes this is a live political-historical dispute and does not adjudicate it; the relevant fact is that Wilson is a real node in that genealogy.
- **Race:** Wilson's administration re-segregated parts of the federal civil service. This is documented fact and relevant to any honest assessment of his record, though tangential to the vault's propaganda focus.
