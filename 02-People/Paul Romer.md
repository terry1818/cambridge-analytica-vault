## Summary

Paul Romer is an American economist best known for endogenous growth theory, for which he shared the 2018 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences with William Nordhaus. He is also the originator of the "charter cities" concept — the idea that a poorly governed developing country could create a new city with a different, better-designed legal and institutional framework, potentially administered by a more trusted foreign partner government, to accelerate economic development.

**Important caveat for this vault**: Romer's relationship to the Honduran ZEDE project and the broader [[Plutocratic secession]] movement is one of intellectual origination followed by explicit public withdrawal. He resigned from Honduras's ZEDE transparency committee in 2012, stated clearly that the implemented version crossed his "bright red line" on electoral accountability, and distanced himself from how the concept was deployed by [[Próspera]] and the [[Pronomos Capital]] ecosystem. He should not be cited as an endorser or co-architect of the ZEDE as built.

## Key facts

- Born 1955; PhD University of Chicago (1983).
- Developed endogenous growth theory in the 1980s–90s, revolutionizing how economists model technological change and long-run growth.
- Proposed the charter cities concept publicly around 2009 in a TED Talk and academic work; the core idea was foreign-administered zones with consent of the governed and strong accountability mechanisms.
- Advised the Honduran government under President Lobo Sosa on the Regiones Especiales de Desarrollo (REDs) framework — the precursor to ZEDEs.
- **Resigned in 2012** from the ZEDE transparency commission when the Honduran government began operating in secrecy and passed new law removing electoral accountability from zone governance. Publicly stated: "They have passed a new law that crosses my bright red line."
- Characterized the Honduran corporate interests that took over the project as having "corrupted his original vision."
- Served as World Bank Chief Economist 2016–2018; resigned over disagreements about the Bank's political interference with research.
- Nobel Prize awarded 2018 for growth theory work, not the charter cities concept.

## Relationships

- Intellectually inspired [[Patri Friedman]], [[Pronomos Capital]], and the charter-cities VC movement — but did not endorse their deployment.
- Influenced [[Paul Romer]]'s concept was also cited by Slobodian in [[Source - Slobodian 2023 - Crack-Up Capitalism]] to show how academic ideas become appropriated by plutocratic-exit projects.

## Public statements

- "They have passed a new law that crosses my bright red line." (2015, on ZEDE revision removing electoral accountability)
- The Honduran corporate interests "corrupted my original vision." (various interviews post-2012)
- Original charter cities concept required consent of the governed and transparent, accountable governance — elements he argued were absent from ZEDEs as built.
