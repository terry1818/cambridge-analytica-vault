## Summary

Sean Patrick Hannity (born December 30, 1961, in New York City) has been a Fox News host since the network's founding in 1996, making him its longest-tenured major on-air figure. He co-hosted *Hannity & Colmes* from 1996 to 2009, then launched the solo *Hannity* prime-time show, which became one of the most-watched programs in cable news for much of the 2010s. He also hosts *The Sean Hannity Show*, a nationally syndicated talk radio program. Benkler, Faris, and Roberts in *Network Propaganda* examine Fox News prime-time — with Hannity as its defining figure during the 2016 cycle — as the central distribution mechanism by which fringe right-wing narratives enter mainstream conservative consumption.

## Key Facts

- Grew up in Franklin Square, NY (Long Island); attended NYU and Adelphi University but left without graduating.
- Broke into radio via volunteering at UC Santa Barbara's college station (1989); then WVNN (Athens, Alabama) and WGST (Atlanta).
- Hired by Roger Ailes in September 1996 to co-host what became *Hannity & Colmes* at the Fox News launch.
- Radio program estimated at 15+ million weekly listeners (Talkers Magazine ranking, multiple years); ranked No. 1 in Talkers' "Heavy Hundred" in 2025.
- Inducted into the National Radio Hall of Fame, November 2017.
- Robert Mueller's investigation documented text message exchanges in which Hannity was privately advising Trump during the 2016 campaign and presidency, while publicly acting as a news host.
- Fox News's own internal communications disclosed in the Dominion Voting Systems litigation (2023) showed Hannity privately expressing doubt about stolen-election claims he was amplifying on air.

## Public Statements

- Amplified without qualification multiple narratives later identified as disinformation, including: Uranium One conspiracy theory; Seth Rich murder conspiracy; and repeated 2020 election fraud claims.
- Privately texted Fox colleagues (per Dominion documents): "It's really crazy stuff. And damaging" — about Sidney Powell's stolen-election claims, in contrast to his on-air amplification of those claims.
