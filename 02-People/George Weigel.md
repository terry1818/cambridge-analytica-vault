## Summary

George Weigel is the most prominent American Catholic biographer and one of the most influential public voices in the Catholic neoconservative network documented in Damon Linker's *The Theocons*. His two-volume biography of John Paul II — *Witness to Hope* (1999) and *The End and the Beginning* (2010) — became the definitive American Catholic account of the pontificate and shaped how millions of readers understood the relationship between the Church and liberal democracy. Critics in academic theology have argued that Weigel's reading systematically aligned John Paul II with American neoconservatism in ways the Pope's own writings did not fully support.

Weigel served as president of the [[Ethics and Public Policy Center]] from 1989 to 1996, during which he transformed it into a major Catholic neoconservative institution. He remains its Distinguished Senior Fellow and continues as a prolific commentator on Church affairs and American politics.

## Key facts

- Born 1951; educated at St. Mary's Seminary and University (Baltimore) and the University of St. Michael's College, Toronto.
- President of the [[Ethics and Public Policy Center]] from 1989 through June 1996; built EPPC's Catholic intellectual program.
- Distinguished Senior Fellow at EPPC from 1996 to the present.
- *Witness to Hope: The Biography of Pope John Paul II* (1999) became a *New York Times* bestseller; translated into multiple languages.
- Sequel *The End and the Beginning: Pope John Paul II — The Victory of Freedom, the Last Years, the Legacy* (2010) completed the two-volume project.
- 2017 memoir *Lessons in Hope — My Unexpected Life with St. John Paul II* described the personal relationship behind the biographies.
- Regular contributor to *First Things* magazine and Catholic media outlets including the *Catholic Herald* and *National Catholic Register*.
- Supported the 2003 Iraq War on just-war grounds, placing him in alignment with neoconservative foreign policy positions shared by EPPC's broader network.
- Has written critically about Pope Francis's *Amoris Laetitia* (2016), arguing the document introduced ambiguities that weakened prior Church teaching on divorce and remarriage — placing him in tension with the current papacy.
- Holds honorary degrees from multiple institutions.

## Public statements

- On the papacy and democratic capitalism: Weigel has argued that John Paul II's *Centesimus Annus* (1991) "underwrote" the free market — a reading contested by liberation theologians and some European Catholic social economists.
- On Pope Francis: "The confusion in the Church today stems in no small part from an unwillingness to address directly the [doctrinal] questions that *Amoris Laetitia* has raised." (*Catholic World Report*, 2017)
- On his biography: "I wanted to give the Pope's own understanding of himself back to him — and to show that this was not a man shaped by Western liberalism but by Polish Catholic culture."

## Open questions

- The academic literature has challenged Weigel's biographical interpretation as a "neoconservative misreading" of John Paul II. The degree to which his influential framing shaped US Catholic political alignment (toward the Republican Party) from the 1990s onward is a significant open question for this vault.
- Weigel's hawkish foreign policy positions (Iraq 2003, Ukraine thereafter) track with neoconservative foreign policy networks; full mapping of his national-security adjacencies has not been completed.
