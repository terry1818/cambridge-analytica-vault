## Summary

B.J. Fogg is a Stanford-based research scientist who is the intellectual founder of persuasive technology — the systematic study and design of technologies intended to change human behavior. His 1996 coining of the term "captology" (Computers As Persuasive Technologies), his 1998 founding of the [[Stanford Persuasive Technology Lab]], and his 2003 book *Persuasive Technology: Using Computers to Change What We Think and Do* established the field. The Fogg Behavior Model (B=MAP: Behavior occurs when Motivation, Ability, and a Prompt converge) has been one of the most widely applied frameworks in Silicon Valley product design. Roger McNamee's *Zucked* identifies Fogg's lab as the direct academic origin of the design philosophy that made Facebook and other platforms addictive.

The significance for this vault is that Fogg's lab trained many of the designers who built engagement-maximizing features into [[Facebook]] and other platforms; Instagram's co-founder Mike Krieger took Fogg's class. The chain runs: Fogg's academic framework → Silicon Valley product design → the attention-capture machinery that [[Cambridge Analytica]] and the [[Internet Research Agency (IRA)]] exploited for political targeting.

## Key facts

- Coined "captology" in 1996 from the acronym Computers As Persuasive Technologies.
- Founded the Stanford Persuasive Technology Lab in 1998; it was later renamed the Behavior Design Lab.
- Published *Persuasive Technology: Using Computers to Change What We Think and Do* (Morgan Kaufmann, 2003) — the foundational text of the field.
- The Fogg Behavior Model (first published formally in 2009 at the Persuasive Technology conference) states B = MAP: Behavior requires Motivation + Ability + Prompt converging simultaneously.
- Published *Tiny Habits: The Small Changes That Change Everything* (2019), applying behavior design to personal habit formation — a more consumer-facing work.
- His Stanford course attracted students who went on to found or build major platforms: Instagram (Mike Krieger), and others.
- Has expressed concern about the misapplication of behavior design to manipulation and addiction, though critics argue his framework enabled it.

## Relationships

- **[[Stanford Persuasive Technology Lab]]**: The institution Fogg built; the locus of behavior-design research with highest downstream impact on tech platforms.
- **Mike Krieger** (Instagram co-founder): Took Fogg's class; applied engagement-design principles to Instagram's product.
- **[[Sean Parker]]**: Parker's 2017 "social validation feedback loop" description articulates in lay terms what Fogg's framework describes academically.
- **[[Facebook]]**: Indirect connection via Fogg-trained designers; the attention-capture and engagement mechanics that define Facebook draw on captology principles.

## Public statements

- Has stated he is "freaked out" by the scale at which persuasive design is being deployed for commercial manipulation, and has increasingly focused his work on "ethical" behavior design for health and wellbeing rather than engagement maximization.

## Open questions

- The degree of Fogg's personal responsibility for downstream misuse of his framework is contested. He designed a tool; others weaponized it — the standard "dual-use" question in technology ethics.
- Whether the Behavior Design Lab's current work meaningfully distinguishes beneficial from manipulative application.
