## Summary

Claude C. Hopkins (1866–1932) was the most influential American advertising practitioner of the early twentieth century and the originator of what he called "scientific advertising" — the application of measurement, testing, and data analysis to commercial persuasion. His 1923 book *Scientific Advertising* codified principles that remain foundational to direct marketing, digital advertising, and political microtargeting: that every advertising claim should be tested against measurable outcomes, that audiences respond to concrete benefits rather than abstract appeals, and that return on investment should be tracked at the individual campaign level.

Tim Wu's *The Attention Merchants* (2016) treats Hopkins as a pivotal figure in the history of the attention economy: Hopkins' methodological innovations transformed advertising from art to engineering, making it possible to systematically extract value from mass audiences' attention at scale. This industrialization of persuasion is the commercial precursor to the later industrial-scale persuasion apparatus — from wartime propaganda to Cambridge Analytica's psychographic targeting — that this vault documents.

## Key Facts

- Born circa 1866; exact birthdate not confirmed in public sources.
- Early career in religious revival circuit promoted goods with testimonials — early exposure to mass persuasion.
- Worked at Bissell Carpet Sweeper Company and Swift & Company before entering advertising.
- Hired in 1907 by Albert Lasker, owner of Lord & Thomas (one of the largest US advertising agencies), at a then-extraordinary salary of $185,000 per year.
- At Lord & Thomas, Hopkins developed breakthrough campaigns for Schlitz beer, Palmolive soap, Quaker Oats, Pepsodent toothpaste, and dozens of other consumer brands.
- Invented the "reason-why" copy style: advertisements explain specifically why a product benefits the buyer, rather than relying on image or emotion alone.
- Pioneered coupon-based response tracking — the first systematic method for measuring advertising effectiveness at the individual campaign level (a precursor to the A/B testing and click-through tracking of digital advertising).
- Developed split-run testing — running different versions of an ad in different markets to determine which performs better.
- Rose to president and then chairman of Lord & Thomas before retiring.
- Published *Scientific Advertising* (1923) and *My Life in Advertising* (1927).
- Died 1932.

## Relationships

- [[Edward Bernays]] — Contemporaneous figure working on the same problem from a different direction: Bernays emphasized psychological/sociological theory (mass unconscious, crowd psychology); Hopkins emphasized empirical testing. Together they represent the two methodological pillars of modern persuasion industry — theory and data.
- [[George Creel]] / [[Committee on Public Information]] — The CPI mobilized state resources using many of the same attention-capture techniques Hopkins was developing commercially; the two streams (government propaganda and commercial advertising) used overlapping methods and personnel.
- [[Attention economy]] — Hopkins' scientific advertising is the commercial infrastructure underlying the attention economy: it demonstrated that attention could be made legible, measurable, and tradeable.

## Public Statements

- "Advertising is salesmanship. Its principles are the principles of salesmanship." (*Scientific Advertising*, 1923)
- "Almost any question can be answered, cheaply, quickly, and finally, by a test campaign." (ibid.)

These statements articulate the data-driven, ROI-measurement orientation that runs from Hopkins through direct mail, through digital advertising, and into Cambridge Analytica's psychographic optimization methodology.
