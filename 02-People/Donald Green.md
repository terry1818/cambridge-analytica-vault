# Donald Green

## Summary

Donald P. Green is an American political scientist, since 2011 the J.W. Burgess Professor of Political Science at Columbia University, having previously spent his career at Yale University, where he directed the Institution for Social and Policy Studies (ISPS) from 1996 to 2011. With [[Alan Gerber]], Green is one of the two figures most responsible for establishing randomized controlled field experiments as the methodological standard in political science.

Green matters to this vault for the same reason Gerber does: the Gerber–Green research program defines the empirical baseline against which [[Cambridge Analytica]]'s psychographic claims must be measured. Their 1998 New Haven get-out-the-vote (GOTV) experiment demonstrated, with random assignment and verified turnout records, that face-to-face contact substantially increases turnout while impersonal contact does much less — a finding that overturned decades of guesswork. Their co-authored book "Get Out the Vote: How to Increase Voter Turnout" became the practitioner's manual for evidence-based campaigning, and their 2008 study "Social Pressure and Voter Turnout" (with Christopher Larimer), a randomized experiment of roughly 180,000 Michigan voters, showed that social-accountability mailings produce large, replicable turnout effects. This is the scientific yardstick the vault applies: the *mobilization* effects Green and Gerber documented are robust and causal, whereas the *persuasion-by-personality-profile* model CA marketed has far weaker experimental support. Green has also extended field-experiment methods into the study of prejudice reduction and political behavior worldwide.

## Relationships

- **Co-conspirators / collaborators:** [[Alan Gerber]] — primary research partner across the field-experiment program; Christopher Larimer — co-author of the 2008 social-pressure study.
- **Documented by:** [[Sasha Issenberg]] — "The Victory Lab" traces the Green–Gerber program as the origin of evidence-based campaign analytics.
- **Analytical contrast:** [[Cambridge Analytica]] — CA's psychographic-persuasion model lacks the experimental backing Green's mobilization research has.

## Public statements

- Green and Gerber's body of work argues consistently that personal, social contact is the most reliable turnout lever and that many standard campaign expenditures have small or null measured effects.
- In post-2016 commentary, Green has been among the political scientists urging caution about overstated claims for micro-targeted persuasion.
