## Summary

Alberto Fujimori was elected President of Peru in 1990 as a political outsider, defeating novelist Mario Vargas Llosa. He governed until 2000, when a corruption scandal forced his resignation (by fax, from Japan). He was extradited to Peru in 2007 and convicted in 2009 of ordering death-squad killings and authorizing wiretapping; he served prison time before controversial pardons and legal battles continued until his death in September 2024.

**Why the vault holds this figure as a comparative case.** Levitsky & Ziblatt in *How Democracies Die* use Fujimori as the paradigm case of the "elected autocrat" — a leader who comes to power through legitimate elections and then, step by step, dismantles the democratic institutions that brought him to power. The vault uses the Fujimori case to sharpen the analytic question: at what point does constitutional hardball (norm violation) cross into democratic breakdown? The 1992 autogolpe — where Fujimori suspended the constitution, dissolved Congress, and packed the judiciary with military backing, all while maintaining majority popular support — illustrates how democratic erosion can be *popularly ratified* rather than resisted. This is central to the vault's analysis of whether the post-2024 US trajectory is norm-erosion, hybrid-regime formation, or something else. It also pairs with the [[Hypothesis - Three-wing coalition]] question of which US institutions remain independent constraints.

## Key facts

- Born in Lima to Japanese immigrant parents; was a university rector and agronomist before entering politics.
- Won the 1990 election as a dark-horse candidate with no prior political experience, partly on an anti-austerity platform — then implemented harsh austerity ("Fujishock") immediately after taking office.
- On April 5, 1992, with military support, suspended the constitution, dissolved the democratically elected Congress, and shut down the judiciary — the *autogolpe* ("self-coup"). The move had approximately 70% public approval at the time.
- Convened a new Constituent Assembly that wrote a new constitution (1993) giving him expanded powers and allowing re-election.
- His intelligence chief, Vladimiro Montesinos, ran a pervasive system of bribery, blackmail, and media control — bribing TV channel owners to provide favorable coverage.
- Won re-election in 1995 and controversially sought a third term in 2000 despite the new constitution limiting him to two.
- The 2000 "Vladivideo" scandal — footage of Montesinos bribing a congressman — triggered his downfall; he fled to Japan, faxed his resignation, and was stripped of the presidency by Congress.
- Extradited from Chile in 2007; convicted 2009 of crimes against humanity (Barrios Altos massacre, La Cantuta massacre) and corruption; sentenced to 25 years, later reduced.
- His daughter Keiko Fujimori subsequently became a major political figure, running for president three times (2011, 2016, 2021) — the Fujimorismo movement demonstrates how authoritarian legacies persist electorally.
- Died September 11, 2024, in Lima, after being released from prison on humanitarian grounds.
