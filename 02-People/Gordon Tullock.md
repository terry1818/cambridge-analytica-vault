# Gordon Tullock

## Summary

Gordon Tullock (1922–2014) was an American economist and professor of law and economics who, with [[James M. Buchanan]], co-founded public choice theory — the application of economic self-interest models to voters, politicians, and bureaucrats. Trained as a lawyer rather than an economist (he held a J.D. from the University of Chicago, 1947, and never earned a doctorate in economics), Tullock spent a career at the same Virginia institutional lineage that produced the [[Virginia school of political economy]]: the University of Virginia, Virginia Tech, George Mason University, and later the University of Arizona. He published more than 150 papers and 23 books.

Tullock matters to this vault for two reasons. First, the 1962 book he co-wrote with Buchanan, *The Calculus of Consent: Logical Foundations of Constitutional Democracy*, is a founding text of [[constitutional economics]] — it argued that constitutional rules should be designed in advance to constrain what democratic majorities can later do. Second, in a 1967 paper Tullock identified the phenomenon later named [[Rent-seeking]] by economist Anne Krueger: the diversion of resources into capturing politically created wealth rather than producing it. Both ideas — that democracy systematically produces [[Government failure]], and that political activity is largely predatory — became intellectual scaffolding for the donor-funded movement Nancy MacLean describes in *Democracy in Chains*. Many economists consider Tullock to have deserved a share of Buchanan's 1986 Nobel Prize; he never received it.

## Key facts

- **Role(s):** Economist; professor of law and economics; co-founder of public choice; founding editor of the journal *Public Choice*.
- **Affiliations:** University of Virginia, Virginia Tech, [[George Mason University]], University of Arizona; member of the [[Mont Pelerin Society]].
- **Active period:** ~1958–2008.
- **Known for:** *The Calculus of Consent* (1962); the original economic analysis of rent-seeking (1967); pioneering work on bureaucracy, voting, dictatorship, and revolution.

## Relationships

- **[[James M. Buchanan]]** — primary collaborator; co-author of *The Calculus of Consent*; together they built the Virginia school of political economy across UVA, Virginia Tech, and GMU.
- **Anne Krueger** — independently developed and named ("rent-seeking") the phenomenon Tullock first analyzed in 1967.
- **[[Mont Pelerin Society]]** — member of the international free-market network that linked the Virginia school to the broader libertarian and Koch-funded ecosystem.
- **[[Charles Koch]] / [[Koch Network]]** — Koch money sustained the George Mason economics infrastructure within which Tullock worked from 1983.
- **George Stigler** — Chicago contemporary whose 1971 theory of [[Regulatory capture]] is a sibling concept to Tullock's rent-seeking.

## Public statements

- *The Calculus of Consent* (1962, with Buchanan): framed constitutional choice as the prior selection of "the rules of the game" that bind later majorities.
- "The Welfare Costs of Tariffs, Monopolies, and Theft" (1967): argued that the true social cost of monopoly and protection includes the resources spent competing for the privilege — the insight now called the "Tullock rectangle."
