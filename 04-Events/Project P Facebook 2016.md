## Summary

"Project P" — the "P" standing for propaganda — was a Facebook-internal investigative program launched in the weeks following the November 2016 US presidential election. The probe was a direct response to post-election scrutiny of how fake news and propaganda had circulated on the platform during the campaign. The investigation quickly identified dozens of pages that had peddled false news reports ahead of Trump's election victory. Most of those pages were found to be based overseas, had financial rather than ideological motives, and exhibited a pronounced conservative ideological bias in their content. When Facebook's leadership moved to remove all identified pages, [[Joel Kaplan]], then head of the Washington office and the company's senior Republican, intervened — reportedly warning that removing all of the pages would "disproportionately affect conservatives." The outcome was that the most egregious pages were taken down while the majority remained live. Project P illustrates the pattern documented throughout Frenkel & Kang: where platform integrity goals collided with conservative political sensitivities, enforcement was negotiated downward.

## When & Where

Project P was initiated in the immediate aftermath of the November 8, 2016 election, during the period when public and media criticism of Facebook's role in spreading misinformation had reached a peak. The investigation was headquartered internally at Facebook and run by teams within the security and integrity divisions. The timeline of the internal debate overlapped with [[Alex Stamos]]'s concurrent push for broader disclosure of Russian election interference — two parallel internal battles that both went against the transparency advocates.

## Actors

- **[[Facebook]]** — Platform conducting the investigation into its own content ecosystem
- **[[Joel Kaplan]]** — VP of Global Public Policy; reportedly intervened to limit enforcement, citing conservative-disproportionality concerns
- **[[Alex Stamos]]** — Chief Security Officer; advocate for broader action and disclosure on election-related manipulation
- **[[Mark Zuckerberg]]** — CEO; ultimate decision authority; had notably dismissed suggestions that Facebook influenced the 2016 election in early post-election public statements
- **[[Sheryl Sandberg]]** — COO; presided over the communications and policy environment in which Project P decisions were made

## Sequence of Events

1. **November 8, 2016**: US presidential election; Trump victory surprises media and political establishment; immediate questions about role of social media misinformation
2. **Mid-November 2016**: Facebook publicly criticized; Zuckerberg dismisses the idea Facebook influenced the election as "pretty crazy"
3. **Late November–December 2016**: "Project P" launched internally; rapid identification of dozens of propaganda/fake-news pages
4. **Internal findings**: Most pages are based overseas (often in Eastern Europe or elsewhere), financially motivated, and disproportionately promote conservative misinformation
5. **Executive decision point**: Leadership moves to remove all identified pages
6. **Kaplan intervention**: Kaplan reportedly argues removal would disproportionately target conservatives; pushes back against broad enforcement
7. **Outcome**: Worst-offending pages removed; majority of identified pages left operational on the platform
8. **Parallel**: [[Alex Stamos]] separately attempts to expand disclosure of Russian election interference; also meets executive resistance

## Outcome / Consequences

The limited enforcement scope of Project P meant that a significant body of propaganda infrastructure remained on the platform through the 2017–2018 period. The episode became a key data point in the documented pattern — explored extensively in *An Ugly Truth* — of conservative political considerations consistently overriding platform integrity enforcement at Facebook. It also prefigured the [[Newsworthiness exemption]] debates and the broader [[Facebook Oversight Board]] architecture that emerged from sustained criticism of Facebook's inconsistent moderation.

## Disputed Facts

The characterization of Kaplan's specific intervention language ("disproportionately affect conservatives") comes from insider accounts reported by journalists; Kaplan has not publicly confirmed or denied this framing. The exact number of pages identified versus removed has not been formally disclosed by Facebook.
