## The vote and its data-side architecture

The Brexit referendum of 23 June 2016 produced a 51.9 percent Leave / 48.1 percent Remain split. The vault is interested in the data-side architecture that produced the Leave campaign's persuasion and turnout operation, not in the legitimacy of the result as a political question.

The Leave side ran two officially separate (but operationally entangled) campaigns: [[Vote Leave]] (the official designated campaign, led by Dominic Cummings) and [[Leave.EU]] (the Banks-Wigmore-led unofficial campaign). Both used data operations with documented ties to the broader CA / SCL / [[AggregateIQ]] ecosystem. The Electoral Commission and the ICO later concluded that Vote Leave breached campaign finance rules by coordinating with [[BeLeave]] (a small youth-targeted Leave group) in a way that effectively exceeded the spending cap. The ICO separately concluded that the data-protection practices of multiple Leave-side entities (including CA and AIQ) breached UK data law.

## What is established vs what is contested

Established (high-evidence): AIQ received Leave-side money; AIQ used personal data unlawfully; Vote Leave coordinated with BeLeave in violation of finance rules; CA had a commercial relationship with Leave.EU that was discussed in CA pitches even if the contracted scope was contested.

Contested or unresolved (in the vault sense): the quantitative impact of the data operations on the result; the precise nature of any Russian-state involvement (the ICO declined to confirm the strongest claims, while parliamentary intelligence reports identified Russian state interest in the campaign); the degree to which [[Arron Banks]]'s Russian contacts produced operational support vs informational support.

## Why Brexit matters to the broader vault

Brexit is the concurrent twin of [[2016 US Presidential Election]]: the same operational network, same methodological toolkit, different jurisdiction. The vault treats them as a paired case study because the cross-jurisdictional pattern (a CA-adjacent firm doing analogous work for two separate but ideologically aligned campaigns in the same year) is the strongest single piece of evidence that what happened in 2016 was an operational programme, not a pair of coincidences.

The post-Brexit legal and regulatory record (ICO investigation, Electoral Commission action, DCMS inquiry, Banks litigation) is also the most legally adjudicated piece of the CA story anywhere in the world, which makes Brexit the cleanest evidentiary anchor.

## The pre-referendum data-infrastructure build

The Leave-side data infrastructure did not appear in 2016. The build occurred through 2014-2015 across two parallel tracks. On the Vote Leave side, Dominic Cummings (campaign director) brought a data-driven persuasion philosophy from his earlier work in UK educational reform and from his explicit study of US digital-political operations. On the Leave.EU side, [[Arron Banks]] (principal funder) and Andy Wigmore (communications director) developed an unofficial campaign with a different data-vendor profile.

[[Cambridge Analytica]]'s involvement with Leave.EU was substantial in CA's own marketing claims (CA repeatedly cited Leave.EU as a client in pitch materials and external statements through 2016-2017) but became contested as the regulatory record developed. The [[ICO Investigation]] eventually concluded that CA had done scoping work for Leave.EU but had not signed a final services contract; the relationship was real but operationally less complete than CA's marketing suggested. The Leave.EU operational data work was substantially carried out by other vendors, with CA in a more advisory role.

[[AggregateIQ]]'s involvement with Vote Leave was operationally complete and is the documented core of the Leave-side digital architecture. AIQ received the GBP 3.5M Vote Leave contract plus the GBP 625K BeLeave coordinated transfer, and ran the digital ad operation that targeted UK voters during the referendum period.

## The vote itself

The 23 June 2016 vote produced 51.9 percent Leave (17,410,742 votes) to 48.1 percent Remain (16,141,241 votes), with turnout of 72.2 percent. The result was decisive in absolute terms (a 1.27-million-vote margin) but close in relative terms; the geographic distribution mattered (Scotland and Northern Ireland voted Remain, England and Wales voted Leave, with the most decisive Leave margins in northern English post-industrial areas and the East Midlands).

The result triggered the David Cameron resignation, the Theresa May premiership, the multi-year Brexit-negotiation period, and the eventual UK exit from the EU on 31 January 2020 â€” followed by the ongoing economic, political, and constitutional aftermath that continues to shape UK politics in 2026.

## The DCMS inquiry, the ICO investigation, and the Electoral Commission action

Three separate UK regulatory and parliamentary processes addressed the Brexit-CA-AIQ question:

**The [[DCMS Disinformation Inquiry]]** (House of Commons Digital, Culture, Media and Sport committee) ran from 2017 through 2019, produced two substantial reports, and took sworn evidence from [[Alexander Nix]], [[Christopher Wylie]], [[Brittany Kaiser]], [[Arron Banks]], [[Andy Wigmore]], [[Mark Zuckerberg]] (via written submission rather than appearance), and a long list of other principals. The committee's final report found substantial wrongdoing across multiple Leave-side and Russian-state-actor threads.

**The [[ICO Investigation]]** (UK data protection regulator) was the most operationally consequential of the three. The October 2020 [[Source ICO 2020-10-07 Brexit final report]] is the canonical regulatory disposition. Findings: substantial data-protection violations by CA, SCL, AIQ, and multiple Leave-side data brokers; the most aggressive claims about Russian-state CA coordination were not supported by the available evidence (the ICO declined to confirm or refute, citing investigative limits); enforcement action against AIQ; enforcement action against Facebook (the GBP 500K maximum statutory penalty under pre-GDPR rules).

**The UK Electoral Commission** found that Vote Leave had unlawfully coordinated with BeLeave in a way that effectively exceeded the spending cap; fined Vote Leave GBP 61,000 (statutory maximum); and referred the matter to the Metropolitan Police. The Met investigation did not result in criminal charges; closed September 2019.

## What is established vs what is contested

**Established (high-evidence):** AIQ received Leave-side money via Vote Leave and BeLeave; AIQ used personal data unlawfully; Vote Leave coordinated with BeLeave in violation of finance rules; CA had a commercial relationship with Leave.EU that was discussed in CA pitches even if the contracted scope was contested; substantial Russian-state interest in the Leave outcome was documented in the [[Russia Report 2020]] parliamentary intelligence report.

**Contested or unresolved:** the quantitative impact of the data operations on the result (whether AIQ's digital targeting moved the vote enough to be decisive); the precise nature of any Russian-state operational involvement in the Leave campaign infrastructure (documented contacts, less-documented operational consequences); the degree to which [[Arron Banks]]'s Russian contacts produced operational support vs informational support.
