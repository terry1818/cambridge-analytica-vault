## Summary

Operation LASER (Los Angeles Strategic Extraction and Restoration) was a predictive-policing program run by the Los Angeles Police Department from approximately 2011 to April 2019. It had two interlocking components: a location-based element using [[PredPol]] software to generate daily patrol hot spots, and a person-based element (the LASER component proper) that scored named individuals as likely to reoffend. Officers could add points to an individual's "chronic offender" score for contacts, arrests, and self-reported gang affiliation. Individuals who accumulated enough points were placed on a list subjected to intensified proactive patrol contact. Sociologist [[Sarah Brayne]] conducted the first systematic ethnographic study of the program from inside the LAPD and documented it in *Predict and Surveil* (2020). LAPD cancelled the program in April 2019 — a rare reversal for the department — following years of pressure from the Stop LAPD Spying Coalition.

## When & where

- **Launched**: approximately 2011 within the Newton Division of the LAPD (subsequently expanded).
- **Cancelled**: April 2019.
- **Jurisdiction**: City of Los Angeles, LAPD territory.
- **Initial focus**: Newton Division, which had consistently ranked among the top three LAPD divisions for shooting incidents; more than 40 active gangs.

## Actors

- **LAPD**: Program operator; received federal funding through the Bureau of Justice Assistance Smart Policing Initiative.
- **[[PredPol]]**: Provided the location-based companion module (hot-spot mapping).
- **Stop LAPD Spying Coalition**: Community advocacy group whose sustained pressure led to the 2019 cancellation.
- **[[Sarah Brayne]]**: Sociologist embedded within LAPD who documented LASER in detail.
- **Bureau of Justice Assistance (BJA)**: Federal funder via Smart Policing Initiative.

## Sequence of events

1. **2011**: Newton Division launches LASER as part of the BJA Smart Policing Initiative, pairing the location-based PredPol hot spots with person-based chronic-offender targeting.
2. **2012**: Newton Division reports an all-time low of 16 homicides — a 56% decrease from 2011 (claimed by programme advocates as evidence of efficacy; causation disputed).
3. **2014–2017**: Brayne conducts embedded ethnographic fieldwork within LAPD, documenting how LASER integrates with the [[Secondary surveillance network]] and the [[CalGang database]].
4. **2018–2019**: Stop LAPD Spying Coalition publicly releases internal LASER documents; mounting civil-liberties criticism in local media.
5. **April 2019**: LAPD Chief Michel Moore formally cancels Operation LASER; LAPD also discontinues the PredPol contract in April 2020.

## Outcome / consequences

- Program cancelled without a public accountability process or independent audit.
- Community advocates noted that LAPD's broader data-driven infrastructure (Palantir's Gotham/LAPD integration, license-plate readers, gang databases) remained intact after LASER's cancellation.
- LASER's cancellation became a national reference point for campaigns against predictive policing in Santa Cruz, Oakland, and other cities.
- Brayne's documentation provided the most complete public record of how the system operated.

## Disputed facts

- LAPD attributed a 56% homicide reduction in Newton in 2012 to LASER; independent analysts noted concurrent citywide trends and absence of control comparisons.
- The precise number of individuals placed on the chronic-offender list at peak operation is not publicly confirmed.

## Open questions

- Were LASER individual-score datasets retained within LAPD systems after cancellation, and have they been merged into subsequent databases?
- Did any LASER methodology migrate into Palantir Gotham's LAPD deployment?
- What happened to the civil-liberties documentation compiled by the Stop LAPD Spying Coalition?
