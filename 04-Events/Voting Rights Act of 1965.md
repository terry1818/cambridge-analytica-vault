# Voting Rights Act of 1965

## Summary

The Voting Rights Act of 1965 (VRA) is the landmark federal statute signed by President Lyndon B. Johnson on August 6, 1965, that prohibits racial discrimination in voting. It emerged from the Civil Rights Movement, specifically in the wake of the brutal suppression of the Selma-to-Montgomery marches ("Bloody Sunday," March 7, 1965). The DOJ has called it "the most effective piece of federal civil rights legislation ever enacted." For nearly five decades, the VRA's two primary enforcement mechanisms — the Section 5 preclearance requirement and the Section 2 non-discrimination mandate — provided federal oversight of state and local voting laws. Both have been substantially weakened by Supreme Court rulings: *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) gutted Section 5; *Brnovich v. DNC* (2021) narrowed Section 2. As of 2026, the VRA remains formally on the books but its core enforcement architecture is severely compromised.

## When & Where

Signed into law August 6, 1965, following passage in the Senate (77–19) and House (328–74). Major reauthorizations occurred in 1970 (5 years), 1975 (7 years), 1982 (25 years), and 2006 (25 years, 98–0 in the Senate). The 2006 reauthorization was the last; the coverage formula it retained was struck down by the Supreme Court in *Shelby County* in 2013.

## Actors

- **Lyndon B. Johnson:** Signed the VRA into law following the Selma violence.
- **Civil Rights Movement leaders** including John Lewis, Martin Luther King Jr., and others whose activism directly precipitated the legislation.
- **Congress:** Passed and repeatedly reauthorized the VRA across bipartisan majorities.
- **[[John Roberts]]:** As a Reagan DOJ attorney in the early 1980s, Roberts wrote more than 25 memos opposing the 1982 reauthorization's strengthening of Section 2. As Chief Justice, he authored *Shelby County* (2013) and joined the *Brnovich* (2021) majority.
- **DOJ Civil Rights Division:** Primary federal enforcement body under the VRA.

## Key Provisions

**Section 2 (nationwide):** Prohibits any voting law or practice that results in the denial or abridgment of the right to vote on account of race, color, or language minority status. Applies nationwide. After the 1982 amendment, uses a results test rather than requiring proof of discriminatory intent. Section 2 covers both voting practices (challenged in *Brnovich*) and redistricting (challenged in *Allen v. Milligan*).

**Section 5 (preclearance):** Required "covered" jurisdictions — states and subdivisions with a history of discriminatory voting practices — to obtain preclearance (approval) from either the D.C. District Court or the U.S. Attorney General before implementing any change to voting laws. The covered jurisdictions were determined by Section 4(b), the coverage formula.

**Section 4(b) (coverage formula):** Determined which jurisdictions were subject to preclearance using data on voter registration, turnout, and use of devices like literacy tests. The formula was based on 1964, 1968, and 1972 election data. This formula was declared unconstitutional in *Shelby County* (2013).

**Section 3 ("bail-in"):** Allows courts to impose preclearance requirements on jurisdictions found to have engaged in voting discrimination, even if not covered under Section 4(b). Has been used post-*Shelby County* to cover some jurisdictions.

## Timeline of Enforcement and Erosion

- **1965:** Signed into law; literacy tests banned; federal examiners authorized; preclearance takes effect.
- **1970, 1975, 1982, 2006:** Congress reauthorizes and in 1982 strengthens Section 2 with a results test.
- **2009:** *Northwest Austin Municipal Utility District v. Holder* — Roberts raises but does not decide constitutionality of coverage formula; signals concerns.
- **2013:** *Shelby County v. Holder* — Roberts strikes Section 4(b) coverage formula 5–4; Section 5 preclearance rendered inoperative.
- **2021:** *Brnovich v. DNC* — Alito writes for 6–3 majority narrowing Section 2 voting practices challenges; new "guideposts" raise bar for plaintiffs.
- **2023:** *Allen v. Milligan* — Roberts joins liberal bloc 5–4 to uphold Section 2 redistricting claims and the *Gingles* framework; Section 2 redistricting claims confirmed viable.
- **2025–2026:** John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act repeatedly introduced in Congress; has not passed.

## Outcome / Current Status

The VRA remains on the statute books but its principal enforcement mechanisms are compromised:
- **Section 5 preclearance:** Inoperative since *Shelby County* (2013) due to absence of constitutional coverage formula. Congress has not passed a replacement.
- **Section 2 voting practices claims:** Substantially raised bar after *Brnovich* (2021).
- **Section 2 redistricting claims:** Survive under *Allen v. Milligan* (2023), though Alabama's non-compliance post-decision illustrates enforcement limits.
- **Section 3 bail-in:** Active but limited; courts have imposed new preclearance requirements on some jurisdictions.

## Disputed Facts

- Whether the *Shelby County* majority's claim that discrimination had sufficiently receded is accurate — the congressional record in 2006 documented extensive ongoing discrimination in covered jurisdictions.
- Whether *Brnovich*'s guideposts are consistent with Section 2's text and legislative history — the Kagan dissent argues they are not.

## Open Questions

- Will Congress pass voting rights legislation to restore a coverage formula?
- Will the Supreme Court ever directly address whether Section 2's redistricting application is constitutional (as Justice Thomas's dissent in *Allen v. Milligan* suggests it should)?
- Does the VRA's current status as formally intact but operationally gutted represent a stable equilibrium or a way station to further erosion?
