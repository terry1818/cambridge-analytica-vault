# Event

## Summary

*Coleman v. Miller*, 307 U.S. 433 (1939), is the Supreme Court's most consequential ruling on the scope of judicial review over the constitutional amendment process under Article V. A majority held that whether a state legislature may ratify an amendment it previously rejected, and whether a proposed amendment lapses after an unreasonably long period, are political questions committed to Congress rather than justiciable controversies for courts to resolve. The ruling is pivotal for the [[Article V Convention]] debate: if courts will not police the ratification process, they are even less likely to police a convention's scope or output — a direct implication for the [[Runaway Convention]] risk.

## Sequence of Events

1. **June 2, 1924** — Congress proposes the Child Labor Amendment to the U.S. Constitution. No time limit is specified for ratification.
2. **1925** — Kansas legislature formally **rejects** the amendment and records its rejection.
3. **January 1937** — Twelve years later, the Kansas House passes a resolution **ratifying** the same amendment. The Kansas Senate divides 20–20.
4. **January 1937** — The **Lieutenant Governor**, presiding over the Senate, casts a tie-breaking vote in favor of ratification, over objections that he is not part of the "legislature" within the meaning of Article V.
5. **Opposing legislators sue** in Kansas courts, arguing: (a) the Lieutenant Governor could not vote; (b) Kansas's prior rejection barred ratification; (c) the 13-year lapse rendered the amendment void.
6. **Kansas Supreme Court** rules the legislators have standing to sue and addresses the merits.
7. **June 5, 1939** — U.S. Supreme Court decides. On (a), the justices **split 4–4** and express no opinion. On (b) and (c), a majority holds these are **political questions** for Congress, not courts, to resolve. Congress may — and later does, by joint resolution — determine whether ratifications are timely and effective.

## Outcome / Consequences

The Court's core holding on the amendment process:

> "The question of the efficacy of ratifications by state legislatures, in the light of previous rejection or attempted withdrawal, should be regarded as a political question pertaining to the political departments, with the ultimate authority in the Congress in the exercise of its control over the promulgation of the adoption of the amendment."

Key downstream consequences:

- **No judicial check on ratification validity.** Courts defer to Congress on whether a state's ratification counts.
- **Timeliness is non-justiciable.** Congress, not courts, decides whether a proposed amendment has lapsed — later applied when Congress extended the ERA ratification deadline.
- **Article V convention implications.** Legal scholars on both sides of the runaway-convention debate cite *Coleman* as evidence that courts are unlikely to intervene if a convention exceeds its brief or proposes unauthorized amendments. If Congress controls the ratification end of the process and courts will not supervise it, a convention's output would face minimal judicial scrutiny. See [[Runaway Convention]]; [[Lens - COS as Constitutional Capture Vector]].
- **Ongoing debate on scope.** Only four justices (the Black concurrence) clearly held that *all* Article V questions are political. Four others (Hughes opinion) reached only the specific issues presented. Two (Frankfurter) argued courts lack standing jurisdiction entirely. The precise scope remains contested — a fact exploited selectively by both [[Convention of States Action]] proponents and opponents.

## Disputed Facts

- Whether the Lieutenant Governor was constitutionally part of the Kansas "legislature" for Article V purposes — the Court was evenly divided and issued no ruling.
- Whether *Coleman* should be read broadly (Black view: virtually everything is political) or narrowly (only timeliness and prior-rejection questions are unreviewable).
- Whether *Coleman*'s deference rationale — grounded in Congress's supervisory role — extends to an Article V convention, which is specifically designed to *bypass* Congress. Some scholars argue the rationale inverts in that context.

## Open Questions

- If a convention were called and proposed amendments outside its stated scope, would *Coleman* preclude courts from reviewing the convention's action? No post-*Coleman* case has answered this directly.
- Does *Coleman* insulate state-level pre-convention resolutions (e.g., rescissions) from judicial review?
- Would a future Court, applying *Baker v. Carr* (1962) factors, revisit *Coleman*'s political-question holdings?
