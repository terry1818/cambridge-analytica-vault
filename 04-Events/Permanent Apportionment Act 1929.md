## Summary

The Permanent Apportionment Act of 1929 (formally the Reapportionment Act of 1929) resolved a decade-long constitutional crisis by capping the House of Representatives at 435 members — the size established after the 1910 Census — and creating an automatic mechanism to reapportion those seats among states after each decennial census. The immediate political context was the failure of Congress to reapportion after the 1920 Census, the only such failure in US history, as rural-state representatives refused to accept a process that would have transferred seats to growing urban states. By locking in 435 seats, the Act resolved the deadlock but at a permanent structural cost: as the US population grew from roughly 123 million in 1929 to over 335 million by 2024, each House seat represents an ever-larger population. It also initially omitted requirements for single-member districts, enabling at-large representation until corrected by later legislation.

## When & where

Signed into law June 18, 1929. Passed by the 71st Congress. Preceded by the failed 1921 apportionment attempt and nearly a decade of political deadlock following the 1920 Census.

## Actors

- **Rural-state congressional coalition** — blocked reapportionment throughout the 1920s to prevent seat losses.
- **Urban-state representatives** — pushed for reapportionment reflecting population growth in industrial cities.
- **President Herbert Hoover** — signed the Act.
- **Congress (71st)** — passed the compromise capping seats rather than allowing expansion.

## Sequence of events

1. **1910 Census**: House apportioned at 435 seats based on population; this becomes the permanent cap.
2. **1920 Census**: Population significantly shifted toward urban states; reapportionment would transfer ~11 seats from rural to urban states. Rural-state majority blocks any reapportionment bill for the entire decade.
3. **1921–1928**: Multiple reapportionment bills fail; the House operates on stale 1910 apportionment, in violation of the constitutional requirement for decennial reapportionment.
4. **1929**: Congress resolves the deadlock by capping the House at 435 (preventing rural states from losing seats relative to an expanded House) and automating the mathematical process.
5. **1932**: First automatic reapportionment under the new Act, using the 1930 Census.

## Outcome / consequences

**Representational stagnation**: The US has the second-lowest ratio of representatives to population among major democracies. By 2024, each House member represents approximately 760,000 constituents on average, compared to ~33,000 in 1913 (after the last pre-freeze expansion). The UK's House of Commons has 650 members for ~68 million people; the US House has 435 for ~335 million.

**Minority-rule amplification**: Because each state is guaranteed at least one representative regardless of population (by the Constitution's apportionment rules), small-population states have dramatically higher per-capita representation. Wyoming's ~577,000 residents have one full representative; California's ~720,000 residents per seat receive equivalent representation only by averaging, while small states receive bonus representation. This interacts with [[The filibuster]] and the Electoral College to compound counter-majoritarian structural effects.

**Chemerinsky's analysis**: Erwin Chemerinsky argues the 435-seat cap is a structural democratic defect — an artifact of 1920s political compromise that now systematically dilutes urban and large-state representation, with no constitutional requirement preventing expansion.

**Reform proposals**: Various scholars and advocates have proposed expanding the House to 600–800 seats, which would reduce per-capita representational inequality, dilute the small-state bonus, and make gerrymandering more difficult (more seats = smaller districts). Congress could expand the House by simple statute. As of 2026 no expansion bill has advanced.

## Disputed facts

- The claim that 435 is constitutionally mandated is false: the Constitution sets only a minimum (one per state) and a maximum ratio (no more than one per 30,000 people); 435 is a statutory choice.
- Whether expansion would primarily benefit Democrats (as current large-state underrepresentation skews toward Democratic-leaning states) is contested, though most analyses suggest this directional effect.

## Open questions

- Whether political conditions will ever align to enable a House expansion.
- Interaction with redistricting reform proposals — whether expansion would make independent redistricting commissions more or less effective.
