# Mitrokhin Archive

## Summary

The Mitrokhin Archive is a collection of handwritten notes secretly compiled by KGB Major Vasili Mitrokhin over more than a decade, documenting the covert operations and agent networks of the KGB's foreign intelligence arm (First Chief Directorate) from the 1930s through the 1980s. Mitrokhin began the copying project in 1972 when he supervised the physical transfer of the First Chief Directorate archive from the Lubyanka to the new Yasenevo headquarters. He smuggled notes out in his shoes and pockets, typed them at home, and buried the typed copies in containers under the floor of his dacha. After the Soviet collapse, he defected to Britain via MI6 in 1992, arriving with six trunks of material. The FBI later described it as "the most complete and extensive intelligence ever received from any source." Thomas Rid's *Active Measures* (2020) draws on it extensively for operational detail on [[KGB Service A]] activities.

## When & Where

- **1972–1984**: Mitrokhin compiles notes at Yasenevo as supervising archivist during the archive relocation.
- **1985**: Mitrokhin retires from KGB service; continues work on his notes.
- **1992**: Following the Soviet collapse, Mitrokhin contacts the CIA station in Riga, Latvia — they reject the material as possible fabrication. He then contacts MI6, which arranges his defection to the UK. He arrives with six trunks of notes.
- **1999**: First volume published as *The Sword and the Shield: The Mitrokhin Archive and the Secret History of the KGB* (co-authored with historian Christopher Andrew, Cambridge).

## Actors

- **Vasili Mitrokhin** (5 March 1922 – 23 January 2004): Soviet-era dissident-turned-archivist; his motivations are documented as moral disgust with KGB operations he had witnessed.
- **MI6**: ran the defection and assessed the material; shared it with allied services.
- **CIA**: initially rejected the material in Riga as possibly fabricated.
- **Christopher Andrew** (Cambridge University historian): academic collaborator who organized and published the archive in two volumes.

## Sequence of Events

1. **1956–1985**: Mitrokhin works at KGB headquarters in Moscow, eventually reaching the First Chief Directorate archive.
2. **1972**: During the archive's physical relocation from Lubyanka to Yasenevo, Mitrokhin gains extended access to hundreds of thousands of files.
3. **1972–1984**: He secretly copies documents, hiding handwritten notes in his shoes initially, later using pocket-carried paper; types them at home; buries containers under his dacha floor.
4. **1991**: USSR dissolves; Mitrokhin decides to act.
5. **Early 1992**: Approaches CIA in Riga — rejected. Approaches MI6 — accepted.
6. **1992**: Defects to the UK with six trunks of notes.
7. **1992–late 1990s**: MI6 and allied services conduct operations based on Archive material; multiple agents exposed globally.
8. **1999**: *The Sword and the Shield* published — covers KGB operations in the West, 1917–1991.
9. **2005**: Second volume, *The World Was Going Our Way*, covers KGB operations in the developing world.
10. **2004**: Mitrokhin dies in London.

## Outcome / Consequences

The Archive was immediately actionable intelligence: it led to the exposure and arrest of several Soviet-era agents who had continued operating. It also provided historians with the deepest available look at [[KGB Service A]]'s active-measures operations, forgery production, and agent-of-influence networks — including the mechanics of [[Operation INFEKTION]] and other disinformation campaigns. The Archive is a Tier-1 evidentiary source for Cold War intelligence history, underpinning significant portions of Rid's *Active Measures*.

## Disputed Facts

- The CIA's initial rejection of the material as possibly fabricated has been a point of institutional embarrassment; some analysts have revisited whether the CIA's Ames-era counter-intelligence failures contributed to the skepticism.
- Mitrokhin's notes were handwritten summaries, not photocopies; scholars note that transcription errors or interpretation gaps are possible, though the material's accuracy has been broadly corroborated by subsequent events.

## Open Questions

- Is the full archive publicly available, or do significant portions remain restricted by MI6?
- What proportion of identified agents from the Archive were prosecuted vs. quietly managed?
- Are there digital editions or finding aids available to non-governmental researchers?
