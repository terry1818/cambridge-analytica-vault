# Mueller Report

## Summary

The Mueller Report is the common name for the *Report on the Investigation into Russian Interference in the 2016 Presidential Election*, produced by the Office of Special Counsel led by former FBI Director [[Robert Mueller]]. The investigation ran from May 17, 2017 — when Deputy Attorney General Rod Rosenstein appointed Mueller after President Trump fired FBI Director James Comey — to March 22, 2019. The two-volume, 448-page report was delivered to Attorney General [[William Barr]] and released in redacted form on April 18, 2019. Volume I found that Russian interference in 2016 was "sweeping and systemic," operating along two main vectors: a social-media information-warfare campaign run by the [[Internet Research Agency]] that favored Trump, and the hacking and release (via WikiLeaks and Russian fronts) of Democratic emails. It documented numerous contacts between Trump campaign figures and Russia-linked individuals but did not establish a chargeable criminal conspiracy. Volume II examined obstruction of justice and reached no prosecutorial conclusion, stating it "does not exonerate" the president. The investigation produced 34 indictments and several convictions and guilty pleas.

## When & where

- **Dates:** appointed May 17, 2017; investigation concluded March 22, 2019; report released April 18, 2019.
- **Location:** Office of Special Counsel, U.S. Department of Justice, Washington, D.C.
- **Scope:** over 2,800 subpoenas, nearly 500 search warrants, roughly 500 witness interviews.

## Actors

- [[Robert Mueller]] — Special Counsel; former FBI Director (2001–2013).
- [[Donald Trump]] — subject of the obstruction inquiry; not interviewed in person (answered written questions on Russia matters).
- [[William Barr]] — Attorney General; issued a four-page summary letter before the report's release that critics said mischaracterized its findings.
- [[Paul Manafort]] — Trump campaign chairman; convicted of financial crimes; shared campaign polling data with [[Konstantin Kilimnik]].
- [[Konstantin Kilimnik]] — Manafort associate assessed as tied to Russian intelligence.
- [[Internet Research Agency]] — St. Petersburg "troll farm" indicted for the social-media operation.

## Sequence of events

- `2017-05-09` — President Trump fires FBI Director James Comey.
- `2017-05-17` — Rod Rosenstein appoints Robert Mueller as Special Counsel.
- `2018-02-16` — indictment of the Internet Research Agency and 13 Russian nationals for the social-media operation.
- `2018-07-13` — indictment of 12 GRU officers for the hacking operation.
- `2018-08-21` — Paul Manafort convicted on financial-fraud counts.
- `2019-03-22` — Mueller delivers the report to AG Barr; the investigation ends.
- `2019-03-24` — Barr's four-page summary letter released.
- `2019-04-18` — redacted 448-page report publicly released by the DOJ.
- `2019-07-24` — Mueller testifies before two House committees.

## Outcome / consequences

- **Direct outcome:** 34 individuals and 3 entities indicted, including six former Trump advisers; multiple convictions and guilty pleas.
- **Downstream effects:** the report's findings on Russian information warfare shaped subsequent platform-policy debates and the broader study of election interference; its inconclusive obstruction handling fed years of political contestation.
- **Documented Russian operation:** establishes the [[Internet Research Agency]] social-media campaign and the GRU hack-and-leak as factual — a key Tier-1 anchor for this vault's info-warfare thread.

## Disputed facts

- **"No collusion" framing:** Barr's summary and the Trump White House characterized the report as total exoneration; Mueller publicly objected that the summary "did not fully capture" the report's findings. The report's actual language is that it "did not establish" a conspiracy — a legal finding about chargeable evidence, not a finding of innocence. `#claim/disputed`
- **Obstruction:** the report neither charges nor clears the president, citing in part an Office of Legal Counsel opinion against indicting a sitting president; whether this was the right course is contested. `#claim/disputed`
