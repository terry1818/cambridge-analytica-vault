## Summary

The Immigration Act of 1924 (Johnson-Reed Act) is the legislative culmination of the nativist and eugenicist tradition that Steven Hahn traces in [[Source - Hahn 2024 - Illiberal America]] from the [[Know-Nothing Party]] through the post-WWI "100% Americanism" movement. By anchoring quotas to the 1890 census — before the large waves of Southern and Eastern European immigration — the Act's architects deliberately engineered an ethnic hierarchy of desirable immigrants: Northern and Western Europeans were favored; Italians, Poles, Greeks, and Jews were nearly excluded; all persons "ineligible for citizenship" (Asian immigrants) were barred entirely. The eugenics connection is structural, not incidental: the quota formula was developed by the Eugenics Committee of the U.S. Committee on Immigration; Harry Laughlin testified before Congress as the "Expert Eugenics Agent" of the House Committee on Immigration. The Act remained the governing framework of U.S. immigration until the Hart-Celler Act of 1965.

## When & Where

- Signed into law by President Calvin Coolidge on May 26, 1924.
- Legislative process: Emergency Quota Act of 1921 (precursor, setting 3% of 1910 census); Johnson-Reed Act tightened to 2% of 1890 census and made permanent.
- Formally known as: Immigration Act of 1924; Johnson-Reed Act; National Origins Act.

## Actors

- **Albert Johnson** (R-WA): House author; chairman of the House Committee on Immigration and Naturalization; open eugenics advocate.
- **David A. Reed** (R-PA): Senate author; articulated the quota philosophy as maintenance of "the racial composition of the American people."
- **Harry Laughlin**: Superintendent of the Eugenics Record Office; testified as "Expert Eugenics Agent" to the House Committee; the quota formula drew on his published work.
- **Madison Grant**: Author of *The Passing of the Great Race* (1916); his race hierarchy provided the ideological framework; Adolf Hitler reportedly called Grant's book "my Bible."
- **Calvin Coolidge**: Signed the Act; had earlier written (1921) that "America must be kept American" by preserving "racial composition."

## Sequence of Events

1. **1880s–1910s**: Mass immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe and Asia; nativist restriction campaigns grow.
2. **1917**: Immigration Act of 1917 establishes literacy test and "Asiatic Barred Zone."
3. **1921**: Emergency Quota Act: first national-origins quota system (3% of 1910 census); total cap ~357,000/year.
4. **1922–1924**: Congressional hearings; Laughlin and eugenicists testify; formula refined to 2% of 1890 census.
5. **1924-05-26**: Johnson-Reed Act signed; total cap reduced to 165,000/year (an ~80% reduction from pre-WWI averages); Asian exclusion made total.
6. **1929**: National origins formula fully implemented as permanent annual caps.
7. **1924–1965**: Act governs US immigration; effectively blocks Jewish refugees during Nazi period (1933–1945).
8. **1965**: Hart-Celler (Immigration and Nationality Act) abolishes national-origins quotas; replaces with family reunification and skills framework.

## Outcome / Consequences

- Italy's annual quota reduced from ~42,000 (Emergency Quota Act) to ~5,000.
- Poland's quota reduced to ~6,500; Greece to ~100; most of Southern and Eastern Europe to negligible numbers.
- Complete prohibition on immigration from Asia (persons "ineligible for citizenship").
- U.S. Border Patrol formally established by the Act.
- Consular visa control system created — requiring visas obtained abroad before entry.
- The quota system blocked the vast majority of Jewish refugees fleeing Nazi Germany 1933–1941; historians have documented that the Act's operation contributed materially to Holocaust death tolls by preventing emigration.
- Hart-Celler (1965) abolished the system; demographic composition of US immigration changed dramatically in subsequent decades.

## Disputed Facts

- The degree to which the Act's authors were primarily motivated by eugenics vs. economic protectionism vs. anti-radicalism is contested; historians generally conclude all three reinforced each other.
- Whether Hitler's admiration for US eugenics and immigration law (documented in *Mein Kampf* and letters) constitutes a causal transatlantic link or a parallel development remains a matter of scholarly debate. Evidence of direct influence is suggestive but not conclusive.

## Open Questions

- How does the 1924 Act relate to contemporary immigration restriction arguments? Hahn argues for structural ideological continuity; others emphasize that contemporary restriction advocates rarely use explicitly eugenic language.
- Did the Act's Jewish refugee exclusion during 1933–1945 constitute a policy failure that rises to complicity in the Holocaust, as some historians argue?
