## Summary

The Honduras ZEDE law (Zona de Empleo y Desarrollo Económico Organic Law, Decree 120-2013) was a constitutional and statutory framework creating a new category of near-sovereign private zones within Honduran territory. Passed on 12 June 2013 under President Juan Orlando Hernández, after a controversial 2012 removal of four Supreme Court justices who had blocked an earlier version, the law enabled private operators to establish jurisdictions with their own civil courts, tax codes, police, and regulatory frameworks. [[Próspera]] became the most prominent ZEDE. The framework was unanimously repealed by the Honduran Congress on 20 April 2022 under President Xiomara Castro. The episode is the most significant real-world legislative test of [[Plutocratic secession]] theory.

## When & where

- **Original passage**: 12 June 2013, Honduran National Congress.
- **Constitutional amendment enabling it**: adopted 2012 after Supreme Court justices who blocked an earlier version (REDs — Regiones Especiales de Desarrollo) were removed from office.
- **Repeal**: 20 April 2022, voted 128–0 by the Honduran National Congress.
- **Supreme Court ruling**: 2024 — Honduran Supreme Court declared the ZEDE framework unconstitutional with retroactive effect (divided 3–2 ruling).
- **Honduras ICSID withdrawal**: August 2024, to prevent new arbitration claims; pending claims (including Próspera's) remain active.

## Actors

- **Honduran Congress and executive (2012–2013)**: Lobo Sosa and then Hernández governments drove passage; process included removal of obstructing justices.
- **[[Paul Romer]]**: Nobel Prize-winning economist whose academic "charter cities" concept inspired the legislation; he resigned from the ZEDE transparency committee in 2012 citing lack of accountability and secrecy, and publicly distanced himself from the Honduran implementation.
- **[[Próspera]]**: The primary ZEDE to become operational, backed by US tech investors including [[Peter Thiel]] and [[Marc Andreessen]].
- **Erick Brimen**: Developer of Próspera; chief entrepreneur of the ZEDE ecosystem.
- **Xiomara Castro**: Honduran president (elected 2021) who delivered on her campaign promise to repeal; called the repeal a "retaking of sovereignty."
- **Charter Cities Institute / [[Pronomos Capital]]**: US-based organizations that advocated for and invested in ZEDEs.

## Sequence of events

1. **2009**: Paul Romer proposes "charter cities" concept publicly; Honduras under President Lobo Sosa makes contact.
2. **2011**: Honduras amends constitution to allow Regiones Especiales de Desarrollo (REDs); Supreme Court declares unconstitutional.
3. **December 2012**: Honduran Congress removes four Supreme Court justices who blocked the law.
4. **January 2013**: New Congress passes constitutional amendment enabling ZEDEs.
5. **12 June 2013**: ZEDE Organic Law (Decree 120-2013) enacted; framework established.
6. **2012**: Romer resigns from transparency commission, cites government secrecy and removal of electoral accountability; publicly distances himself from the Honduran project.
7. **2017**: [[Próspera]] established as first operational ZEDE on Roatán.
8. **2019–2021**: US tech investment flows into Próspera; [[Pronomos Capital]] ecosystem grows.
9. **20 April 2022**: Honduran Congress repeals ZEDE Organic Law, 128–0; Castro government announces sovereignty restored.
10. **2024**: Supreme Court voids constitutional basis; Honduras withdraws from ICSID; Próspera's ~$11 billion arbitration claim proceeds.

## Outcome / consequences

- Three ZEDEs (including Próspera) were in operation at repeal; they assert 50-year Legal Stability Agreements under the original Organic Law and bilateral investment treaties.
- Próspera's ICSID claim of approximately $10.7–11 billion against Honduras, if successful, would be larger than Honduras's annual GDP in many prior years — a stark illustration of investor-state dispute mechanisms constraining democratic governance.
- The episode hardened the international debate on charter cities: proponents cite Próspera's operational period as proof of concept; critics cite the legal dispute and sovereignty implications as proof of inherent danger.
- The OECD Forum on Harmful Tax Practices recognized the ZEDE regime as abolished for tax purposes by 2024.

## Disputed facts

- Whether the 2012 removal of Supreme Court justices was legally valid under Honduran constitutional procedure remains contested.
- Romer's level of involvement in the final ZEDE design (as opposed to the earlier RED framework he initially supported) is sometimes misrepresented — he was not involved in shaping the 2013 law that was actually passed. See [[Paul Romer]] for detailed treatment.

## Open questions

- Will the Próspera ICSID arbitration succeed and what quantum of damages will be awarded?
- Do other ZEDE operators have viable claims?
- Does this episode affect the viability of future charter-city legislation in other jurisdictions?
