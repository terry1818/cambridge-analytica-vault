## Summary

The Great Sedition Trial of 1944 was the US government's most ambitious attempt to prosecute the organised American fascist movement. Thirty defendants — ranging from German-American Bund leaders to pro-Nazi agitators to marginal conspiracy theorists — were charged under the 1940 Smith Act with conspiring to undermine military morale and overthrow the government. The trial began 17 April 1944 but rapidly descended into procedural chaos, with defence attorneys and defendants using every available tactic to delay and disrupt proceedings. When presiding Judge Edward C. Eicher died of a heart attack on 29 November 1944, a mistrial was declared. The defendants were never retried. The complete failure of the prosecution is central to Rachel Maddow's *Prequel* argument: American fascism of the 1930s–40s escaped legal accountability, with lasting consequences.

## When & where

- **Indictment**: July 1942 (original indictments); superseding indictment January 1944.
- **Trial commenced**: 17 April 1944, US District Court, Washington D.C.
- **Mistrial declared**: 29 November 1944, following Judge Eicher's death.
- **Jurisdiction**: Federal; charges under the Smith Act of 1940 (Alien Registration Act), which made it illegal to advocate violent overthrow of the government or to undermine military morale.

## Actors

**Defendants (selected)**:
- [[Lawrence Dennis]] — the most intellectually prominent defendant; author of *The Coming American Fascism* (1936)
- [[William Dudley Pelley]] — Silver Legion founder; had already been convicted separately in Indiana
- **Elizabeth Dilling** — antisemitic author and activist (*The Red Network*, 1934)
- **George Sylvester Viereck** — German-American propagandist already convicted of failing to register as a foreign agent
- **Joe McWilliams** — leader of the American Destiny Party, a Bund offshoot
- **Gerald Winrod** — fundamentalist minister and antisemitic pamphleteer ("The Flying Fundamentalist")
- Approximately 24 additional defendants drawn from various far-right organisations and publications

**Prosecution**:
- Attorney General Francis Biddle (oversaw the prosecution; previously had issued grand jury investigation of Coughlin's *Social Justice*)

**Presiding judge**:
- Judge Edward C. Eicher — died of a heart attack 29 November 1944, triggering the mistrial

## Sequence of events

1. **1940–41**: FBI and Justice Department build cases against individuals in the far-right milieu; [[Christian Front]] prosecutions fail.
2. **July 1942**: First indictments under the Smith Act targeting a larger network of fascist sympathisers.
3. **January 1944**: Superseding indictment consolidates approximately 30 defendants.
4. **17 April 1944**: Trial commences. Almost immediately beset by defence delaying tactics: defendants and lawyers make endless motions, disrupt proceedings, and conduct themselves in a chaotic manner designed to make the trial unmanageable.
5. **Summer–Autumn 1944**: Prosecution struggles to prove specific conspiratorial intent (required under Smith Act); defence argues defendants acted independently, not in coordination with Nazi Germany.
6. **29 November 1944**: Judge Eicher dies in office. Mistrial declared.
7. **Post-mistrial**: The Justice Department declines to retry the case; the war was nearly over; political will to prosecute domestic fascists evaporated.

## Outcome / consequences

- **No convictions**: All defendants escaped punishment in this proceeding. Several (Pelley, Viereck) had already been convicted on separate charges.
- **Precedent effect**: The failure reinforced reluctance to use sedition charges against domestic political movements; the Smith Act was subsequently more frequently used against communists than fascists.
- **Accountability gap**: The complete failure of the prosecution left the 1930s–40s fascist network without institutional accounting — a pattern Maddow argues echoes in subsequent cycles.
- **Post-war rehabilitation**: Several defendants re-entered public life; the social stigma of fascist association faded rapidly after 1945.

## Disputed facts

- **Extent of Nazi coordination**: The prosecution alleged defendants were coordinating with Nazi Germany; the defence argued the various defendants barely knew each other. The historical evidence supports a picture of ideological fellow-travellers rather than a tightly organised conspiracy.
- **Dennis's culpability**: Dennis maintained he was describing fascism, not advocating it. This distinction was and remains contested by historians.

## Open questions

- Why did the Justice Department not retry the case under a new judge? Political will, resource constraints, and changing wartime priorities all played roles — the relative weight is debated.
- What became of the defendants after the mistrial? The biographical post-war trajectories of the less-prominent defendants are poorly documented.
