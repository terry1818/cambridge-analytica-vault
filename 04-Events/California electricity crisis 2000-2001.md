## Summary

The California electricity crisis of 2000–2001 was a period of severe electricity shortages and price spikes in California caused by deliberate market manipulation under a partially deregulated electricity market. Wholesale electricity prices rose approximately 800% from April 2000 to December 2000. Rolling blackouts affected millions of customers. Pacific Gas and Electric filed for bankruptcy in April 2001. The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission subsequently documented that multiple energy trading firms — including Enron Corporation and a [[Koch Industries]] subsidiary, Koch Energy Trading Inc. — had manipulated California's electricity markets through strategies with names like "Death Star," "Ricochet," and "Get Shorty." Christopher Leonard's *Kochland* identifies this episode as a key case study in Koch Industries' willingness to use market gaming for profit, and connects it to [[ALEC]]'s earlier push for electricity deregulation.

## When & Where

- **Duration:** Approximately May 2000 through September 2001, with peak crisis in winter 2000–2001.
- **Geography:** California's electricity grid; primarily the wholesale market created by California's 1996 partial deregulation law.
- **Regulatory forum:** Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) conducted investigations 2001–2003.

## Actors

- **[[Koch Industries]] / Koch Energy Trading Inc.** — Koch's energy trading subsidiary was named in FERC enforcement action; found to have engaged in market price manipulation. A judge determined the Koch entity was actively manipulating prices.
- **Enron Corporation** — the most prominently documented manipulator; Enron's strategies (Fat Boy, Death Star, Ricochet, etc.) are the most fully documented in FERC proceedings.
- **[[ALEC]]** — the Koch-funded legislative exchange council had advocated for electricity deregulation in the 1990s, with California as a pioneer test case; Leonard's *Kochland* documents this connection.
- **Pacific Gas and Electric (PG&E)** — California's largest utility; forced into bankruptcy by the crisis.
- **Gray Davis** — California Governor; signed emergency long-term power contracts at inflated prices; recalled in 2003 by voters.
- **FERC** — the federal regulator that ultimately documented market manipulation but was criticized for slow and incomplete enforcement.

## Sequence of Events

1. **1996:** California enacts electricity deregulation law; creates a wholesale electricity spot market. [[ALEC]] had lobbied for deregulation across states.
2. **2000:** Electricity market goes online; energy traders at Koch Industries, Enron, and others begin gaming the new market almost immediately.
3. **May–December 2000:** Wholesale prices spike ~800%; traders use strategies including withholding generating capacity ("economic withholding"), fictitious schedule congestion ("Death Star"), and round-trip trading ("Ricochet").
4. **Winter 2000–2001:** Rolling blackouts across California; state emergency declared.
5. **April 2001:** Pacific Gas and Electric files for bankruptcy.
6. **2001–2003:** FERC investigation; Koch Energy Trading Inc. named in enforcement proceedings; judge finds Koch entity manipulated prices.
7. **2003:** Gray Davis recalled; Arnold Schwarzenegger becomes Governor in part on backlash to crisis.
8. **Post-2003:** Enron's strategies are the most publicly documented; Koch's role remains less prominent in public accounts but is detailed in Leonard's *Kochland*.

## Outcome / Consequences

- Estimated cost to California ratepayers and state government: tens of billions of dollars.
- Demonstrated the vulnerability of partially-deregulated markets to strategic manipulation.
- FERC enforcement was partial; full restitution was never achieved.
- The crisis became a political touchstone for debates about energy deregulation; it did not stop the broader push for deregulation nationally.
- Koch Industries faced regulatory scrutiny but continued operating; no criminal charges against Koch executives resulted.
- The episode illustrates the gap between Koch-funded deregulation advocacy and actual market outcomes — a connection Leonard draws explicitly in *Kochland*.

## Disputed Facts

- The exact magnitude of Koch Energy Trading's manipulation (relative to Enron) is not publicly resolved; FERC documents named Koch but Enron dominated public and legal proceedings.
- The degree to which [[ALEC]]'s deregulation campaign was knowingly designed to create manipulable market conditions is asserted in Leonard's account but has not been established in legal proceedings. Evidence tier: **Tier-2** (documented in regulatory filings and investigative journalism; causal chain to ALEC is Tier-2/3).

## Open Questions

- The full financial benefit Koch Industries derived from the crisis has never been publicly disclosed.
- Whether executives at Koch Industries had advance knowledge that California's deregulated market would be particularly susceptible to the strategies they deployed is unresolved.
- The settlement terms of FERC enforcement actions against Koch have not been fully made public.
