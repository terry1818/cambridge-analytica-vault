## Summary

*Green v. Connally*, 330 F. Supp. 1150 (D.D.C. 1971), was a federal district court ruling in which U.S. District Judge Spottswood Robinson III held that racially discriminatory private schools could not qualify for federal tax-exempt status under Section 501(c)(3) of the Internal Revenue Code. The ruling was upheld by the Supreme Court and gave legal authority to the IRS's 1970 policy change ending tax exemptions for segregated schools.

The case's significance for understanding the religious right extends beyond its legal holding. According to a well-supported scholarly argument advanced by historian Randall Balmer and corroborated by contemporary reporting on operative [[Paul Weyrich]]'s own statements, the IRS enforcement of this ruling — particularly as applied to church-sponsored segregation academies — was the actual galvanizing event that mobilized white evangelicals into the Republican coalition, predating the adoption of abortion as the movement's signature issue. This argument is contested by some historians but is grounded in documented evidence including Weyrich's own account.

## When & where

- **Preliminary injunction:** January 1970 (under the earlier case name *Green v. Kennedy*, before John Connally replaced David Kennedy as Treasury Secretary)
- **District court ruling:** May 1971 (*Green v. Connally*)
- **Nixon IRS policy:** Following the preliminary injunction, President Nixon ordered the IRS in 1970 to deny tax exemptions to private schools practicing racial discrimination
- **Supreme Court affirmation:** The legal principle was definitively upheld in *Bob Jones University v. United States* (1983)
- **Location of originating complaint:** Holmes County, Mississippi — three newly formed all-white private schools targeted by African American parents of public school students

## Actors

**Plaintiffs:** A group of African American parents in Holmes County, Mississippi, who sued the Treasury Department (then headed by David Kennedy, later John Connally) to prevent three newly formed white-only private schools from receiving tax-exempt status.

**Defendants:** The Treasury Secretary and IRS.

**Judge:** U.S. District Judge Spottswood Robinson III (later Spottswood Robinson III of the D.C. Circuit).

**[[Paul Weyrich]]:** Conservative operative and co-founder of the Heritage Foundation who, by his own account, recognized the IRS enforcement as a political organizing opportunity. Weyrich sought to mobilize evangelical leaders into a unified political bloc by framing IRS enforcement on segregated schools as an attack on religious liberty. He explicitly discussed this with [[Jerry Falwell Sr.]] and others in the late 1970s.

**[[Jerry Falwell Sr.]]:** His Lynchburg Christian Academy was among the church-sponsored schools whose tax status was affected by the ruling's logic. Falwell became the public face of the resulting political mobilization through the Moral Majority (1979).

**Bob Jones University:** The most prominent test case; its prohibition on interracial dating triggered IRS revocation of its tax-exempt status in 1976, leading to a decade of litigation culminating in *Bob Jones University v. United States* (1983), which the Reagan administration initially attempted to reverse before public backlash forced retreat.

## Sequence of events

1. **1954–1960s:** Brown v. Board and subsequent federal pressure trigger formation of private segregation academies across the South, many church-sponsored.
2. **1969:** Nixon administration receives political pressure from Southern Republican officials to maintain tax exemptions for segregated schools.
3. **January 1970:** Federal court issues preliminary injunction in *Green v. Kennedy* requiring IRS to deny exemptions to discriminatory schools; Nixon orders IRS to implement new non-discrimination policy.
4. **1971:** *Green v. Connally* ruling formalizes the legal principle.
5. **1975–1976:** IRS begins enforcement against Bob Jones University, revoking its tax-exempt status.
6. **Late 1970s:** Weyrich identifies the IRS enforcement issue as the organizing catalyst. In documented conversations with Falwell and others, Weyrich argues evangelicals must enter politics to defend their schools. This leads directly to formation of the Moral Majority (1979).
7. **1983:** *Bob Jones University v. United States* upheld 8-1 by the Supreme Court; the Reagan administration's short-lived attempt to restore BOJ's exemption causes major public controversy and retreat.

## Outcome / consequences

**Legal:** Tax exemptions for racially discriminatory private educational institutions are denied under federal law. This remains settled law.

**Political:** The political consequences are the subject of the well-supported scholarly argument:
- Weyrich, by his own account, used the IRS enforcement to recruit Falwell and other evangelical leaders into organized political activism. The religious liberty framing — government attacking Christian schools — was more politically viable than a defense of segregation per se.
- Abortion became the movement's public face from 1979 onward, but Weyrich (in statements documented by Randall Balmer and others) acknowledged that the school issue was the actual founding grievance.
- The [[Segregationist origins of Christian schools|Christian school movement]]'s legal and political infrastructure was built in direct response to the IRS threat.
- The [[HSLDA]] was founded in 1983 — the same year as the Bob Jones ruling — partly to extend the religious liberty defense framework to home education.

## Disputed facts

**The Balmer-Weyrich thesis:** The claim that the religious right was primarily galvanized by school desegregation rather than abortion is a scholarly argument with significant evidentiary support (Weyrich's own statements, timing correlations) but is disputed by some historians who argue that abortion, prayer in schools, and other cultural issues were co-equal or primary motivators. The Gospel Coalition and other conservative Christian historiographers have challenged the thesis as overstating the racial motivation.

This vault treats the thesis as: *well-supported argument, attributed to Balmer/Weyrich, not undisputed fact.*

**Weyrich's exact role:** The degree to which Weyrich's strategic framing was causally decisive vs. one input among many into Falwell's political mobilization is not fully resolved in the historical literature.

## Open questions
