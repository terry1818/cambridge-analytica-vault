## Summary

The founding of the Southern Baptist Convention in May 1845 was a direct product of the slavery controversy within American Baptist life. Southern Baptists separated from the national Triennial Convention after the northern-dominated missionary societies refused to appoint slaveholders as missionaries. The precipitating incident was the rejection of James Reeve, a slaveholder, as a missionary candidate. The new denomination, formed at a convention in Augusta, Georgia, from May 8–12, 1845, was created explicitly to defend the right of slaveholders to participate fully in Baptist church life and missionary work. Robert P. Jones's *White Too Long* (2020) uses the SBC founding as a foundational case study in the argument that white Christianity in the South built racism into its institutional DNA.

## When & where

- **Date**: May 8–12, 1845
- **Location**: First Baptist Church, Augusta, Georgia
- **Immediate trigger**: Triennial Convention rejection of James Reeve (a slaveholder from Georgia) as a missionary; Triennial Convention's refusal to appoint any slaveholder as a missionary.

## Actors

- **[[Basil Manly Sr.]]**: Key figure; drafted or was closely associated with the theological arguments defending slavery; opened the founding meeting with prayer; chaplain to the Confederacy at Jefferson Davis's inauguration (1861).
- **William Bullein Johnson**: First president of the Southern Baptist Convention.
- **Richard Furman** (predecessor): Charleston pastor who had written in 1822 that "the right of holding slaves is clearly established in the Holy Scriptures, both by precept and example"; his theological framework provided the intellectual foundation for the SBC's proslavery position.
- **Triennial Convention**: The national Baptist missionary society that refused to fund slaveholder missionaries, triggering the split.

## Sequence of events

1. **1822**: Richard Furman, pastor of Charleston Baptist Church (SC), writes a formal defense of slavery as a Biblical institution, directly addressing South Carolina's Governor after Denmark Vesey's planned slave revolt.
2. **Early 1840s**: Tensions escalate within the Triennial Convention as northern Baptist missionaries increasingly refuse to hold enslaved people and as northern antislavery sentiment grows.
3. **1844**: Georgia Baptist Convention nominates slaveholder James Reeve for missionary appointment; Triennial Convention rejects him. Alabama Baptists formally request assurance that slaveholders may be appointed as missionaries and as officers of mission boards; they are refused.
4. **1845-05-08 to 12**: Representatives from churches in Virginia, North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia, Alabama, Louisiana, Kentucky, Maryland, and the District of Columbia convene in Augusta, Georgia. They organize the Southern Baptist Convention. William Bullein Johnson is elected president.
5. **Post-1845**: SBC becomes an institution explicitly and repeatedly defending slavery; SBC publications and clergy are among the most vocal theological defenders of the "peculiar institution" through the Civil War.

## Outcome / consequences

- The SBC became and remains the largest Protestant denomination in the United States.
- The denomination's proslavery origins shaped its institutional culture, theological tendencies, and racial composition for generations.
- After the Civil War, the SBC did not repudiate its founding racial ideology; it rationalized segregation and opposed civil rights in the 20th century.
- **1995**: The SBC formally apologized for its defense of slavery at its 150th anniversary — 150 years after its founding.
- **2020**: The "Resolution on Racial Reconciliation" and internal debates about name change ("Great Commission Baptists") reflect ongoing reckoning.
- Jones (*White Too Long*, 2020) argues the 1845 founding is the origin point of a persistent "white Christianity" syndrome in which racial hierarchy is embedded in the theology and practice of the denomination.
- The founding is directly connected to the [[SBC sexual abuse crisis]] in the sense that both episodes reveal how the SBC's congregationalist polity and insulation from external accountability have enabled institutional failures.

## Disputed facts

- SBC historians have disputed whether the founding should be characterized as exclusively about slavery, noting that Baptist polity questions were also at stake; most historians accept slavery as the primary cause.
- The degree to which the 1995 apology constituted a full reckoning is contested; critics argue structural racial disparities in the denomination persist.

## Open questions

- How has the SBC's founding history shaped its contemporary political theology, particularly regarding race and Christian nationalism?
- Does the 2020 resolution on racial reconciliation represent a genuine structural break, or primarily symbolic?
