# Rucho v. Common Cause (2019)

## Summary

*Rucho v. Common Cause*, 588 U.S. 684 (2019), decided June 27, 2019, is the Supreme Court's definitive ruling that claims of partisan gerrymandering present non-justiciable political questions that federal courts lack authority to adjudicate. Chief Justice [[John Roberts]] wrote for a 5–4 majority. The decision closed the federal courthouse door to challenges against maps drawn with explicit partisan intent — including the North Carolina and Maryland maps at issue in the case, and by extension the [[REDMAP]]-era maps drawn across multiple states after the 2010 Census. Justice Kagan wrote a pointed dissent arguing the majority had abandoned its duty to protect democratic self-governance.

## When & Where

Decided June 27, 2019. The case consolidated two challenges: (1) *Rucho v. Common Cause*, involving North Carolina's congressional map drawn by [[Thomas Hofeller]] to favor Republicans; and (2) *Benisek v. Lamone*, involving Maryland's congressional map redrawn to disadvantage Republicans in one district. Both parties challenged partisan gerrymandering — the consolidation was designed to insulate the ruling from a purely partisan optic.

## Actors

- **Robert Rucho:** North Carolina state senator and chair of the Senate Redistricting Committee; petitioner defending the Republican-drawn North Carolina map.
- **Common Cause:** Nonpartisan advocacy group; respondent challenging the North Carolina map.
- **[[John Roberts]]:** Wrote the majority opinion.
- **Majority (5 justices):** Roberts, Thomas, Alito, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh.
- **Dissent (4 justices):** Kagan (wrote dissent), Ginsburg, Breyer, Sotomayor.
- **[[Thomas Hofeller]]:** The mapmaker whose North Carolina congressional map was the subject of the challenge. Hofeller's files, discovered posthumously, documented the explicit partisan and racial intent behind the maps.

## Sequence of Events

1. After [[REDMAP]]'s 2010 success, Republican-controlled state legislatures drew maps. [[Thomas Hofeller]] drew North Carolina's congressional map.
2. Challengers sued in federal court arguing the maps violated the Constitution's Equal Protection Clause and other provisions by diluting votes based on party.
3. Lower federal courts in both North Carolina and Maryland sided with challengers, finding partisan gerrymandering unconstitutional.
4. The Supreme Court had previously declined to rule on partisan gerrymandering in *Gill v. Whitford* (2018), sending that case back on standing grounds.
5. The Court granted cert in *Rucho* and *Benisek* and consolidated them.
6. Oral argument March 26, 2019.
7. June 27, 2019: Roberts writes for 5–4 majority that partisan gerrymandering claims are non-justiciable political questions. The majority acknowledges that partisan gerrymandering "may be unjust" and "incompatible with democratic principles" but holds that the Constitution provides no "limited and precise standard" courts can apply to adjudicate such claims.
8. The majority explicitly notes that state courts and state constitutions remain available venues for such challenges.

## Key Legal Points

- **Non-justiciability:** The majority holds that no judicially manageable standard exists for evaluating partisan gerrymandering claims. Courts cannot determine how much partisanship in map-drawing is "too much."
- **Political question doctrine:** The case is classified as presenting a political question — a category traditionally unreviewable by federal courts.
- **State courts preserved:** The ruling explicitly does not prevent state courts from applying state constitutional provisions to partisan gerrymandering claims, which subsequently opened the door to successful state court challenges (notably in North Carolina and Pennsylvania).
- **Kagan dissent:** Argues the majority abandons the Court's role as a constitutional backstop; describes the effect as "gerrymanders [that] entrench the politicians who disfavor majority rule."

## Outcome / Consequences

- Federal courts immediately dismissed pending partisan gerrymandering claims.
- The REDMAP-era maps in North Carolina and several other states were insulated from federal challenge.
- State courts subsequently became more active. The Pennsylvania Supreme Court had already struck down Pennsylvania's map under the state constitution in 2018. North Carolina's Supreme Court struck down its legislative maps under the state constitution in 2022, though a subsequent change in the court's composition led to that ruling being reversed.
- The decision is one component of what Ari Berman (*Minority Rule*) and David Daley (*Antidemocratic*) describe as a coherent, multi-decade judicial project to insulate Republican structural advantages from legal challenge. Combined with [[Shelby County v. Holder (2013)]] and [[Brnovich v. DNC (2021)]], *Rucho* removed the three main federal judicial mechanisms for voting rights enforcement.

## Disputed Facts

- Whether Roberts' "no judicially manageable standard" conclusion was correct — Kagan's dissent laid out several potential standards from social science and electoral law.
- Whether the consolidation with the Maryland Democratic gerrymander was sufficient to neutralize the partisan optic of the ruling.

## Open Questions

- Are state court VRA-analog provisions sufficient substitutes for federal review?
- What is the interaction between *Rucho* and state constitutional amendments that prohibit partisan gerrymandering?
- Did [[Leonard Leo]]-network judicial appointments at the federal appellate level shift the lower-court landscape in ways that made *Rucho* more achievable?
