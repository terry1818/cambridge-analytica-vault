## Summary

*Husted v. A. Philip Randolph Institute*, 584 U.S. ___ (2018), is the landmark Supreme Court decision that green-lighted states using a voter's failure to participate in a single federal election as the trigger for initiating a removal process. Ohio's "Supplemental Process" sent a confirmation notice to any voter who had not voted in one federal election; if the voter did not respond to that notice **and** did not vote in the next two federal elections (a four-year window), the state removed them from the rolls. The Court's 5–4 majority held this did not violate the National Voter Registration Act's prohibition on using "the failure to vote" as grounds for removal. The four liberal justices dissented, arguing Ohio did use non-voting as the trigger and that the majority's reading gutted the NVRA's protections.

## When & where

Decided June 11, 2018. Oral argument: January 10, 2018. Case number: 16-980. Originated in the Southern District of Ohio; the Sixth Circuit had ruled for the plaintiffs before the Supreme Court reversed.

## Actors

- **Jon Husted** — Ohio Secretary of State, defendant/respondent defending the state's voter-roll maintenance practice.
- **A. Philip Randolph Institute** — labor and civil rights organization; lead plaintiff.
- **Demos, ACLU, ACLU of Ohio** — counsel for petitioners.
- **Justice Samuel Alito** — authored the majority opinion (joined by Roberts, Kennedy, Thomas, Gorsuch).
- **Justice Stephen Breyer** — authored the principal dissent (joined by Ginsburg, Sotomayor, Kagan).
- **Justice Sonia Sotomayor** — wrote separately to emphasize the racially disproportionate impact.

## Sequence of events

1. **2015**: APRI and allied organizations challenge Ohio's Supplemental Process in the Southern District of Ohio.
2. **April 2016**: APRI files suit alleging the process violates the NVRA's prohibition on using failure to vote as a basis for removal.
3. **Sixth Circuit** rules in favor of plaintiffs, holding Ohio's process violated the NVRA.
4. **January 2018**: Supreme Court hears oral argument.
5. **June 11, 2018**: 5–4 majority reverses the Sixth Circuit; upholds Ohio's process.

## Outcome / consequences

- **Immediate**: Ohio's voter-roll removal process validated; state retained millions of purge-eligible records.
- **National ripple**: The ruling provided legal cover for other states to adopt or expand "use it or lose it" purge policies. Multiple states — Georgia, Wisconsin, Tennessee, Indiana — had similar processes under challenge or consideration.
- **Anderson's analysis**: Carol Anderson identifies *Husted* as a crucial structural enabler of voter suppression, stripping away an NVRA protection and leaving millions of infrequent — but eligible — voters vulnerable to administrative removal.
- **Justice Sotomayor's dissent** explicitly noted the decision's disparate racial impact: minority, low-income, and disabled voters are most likely to miss a single election and least likely to respond to a mailed notice.

## Disputed facts

- The majority's core holding — that Ohio uses lack of response to a notice (not non-voting itself) as the removal trigger — was disputed by the dissent as a semantic distinction that obscures the fact that non-voting is what initiates the entire sequence.
- The extent of actual double-voting or voter-roll inaccuracies corrected by the process was not quantified in the record.

## Open questions

- Whether *Husted*'s logic extends to shorter non-participation windows if states choose to adopt them.
- Post-*Shelby County*, extent to which federal litigation is the sole remaining check on aggressive purge programs.
