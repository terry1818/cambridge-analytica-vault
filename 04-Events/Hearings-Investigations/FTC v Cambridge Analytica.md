# FTC v Cambridge Analytica

## Summary

On **July 24, 2019**, the US **Federal Trade Commission** filed an administrative complaint against [[Cambridge Analytica]] and accepted consent orders from [[Alexander Nix]] and [[Aleksandr Kogan]]. The action was a parallel and follow-on to the much larger **$5B FTC fine on [[Facebook]]** announced one day earlier (July 23-24, 2019).

## What FTC found

Per [[Source FTC Complaint Cambridge Analytica 2019]]:

- CA engaged in **deceptive practices** by representing it did not collect personally identifiable information when it did
- CA collected personal data from up to 87 million Facebook users
- CA's claims about **EU-US Privacy Shield compliance were false**
- CA's claim that data was deleted (January 2016 certification to Facebook) was false

## Resolution

### Cambridge Analytica LLC (the firm)
- Case proceeded administratively
- CA had already declared bankruptcy May 2018
- Limited practical effect of the corporate-side enforcement

### [[Alexander Nix]] (consent order)
- **20-year restrictions** on:
  - Misrepresenting practices for collecting/using/disclosing data
  - Required deletion of all data collected through deceptive means
  - Required deletion of all "work product" (models, derived analyses)
- Restrictions in effect through 2039

### [[Aleksandr Kogan]] (consent order)
- Same 20-year restrictions
- Required to delete all collected data and any derivative models

## Significance

The FTC consent orders are the **primary US enforcement actions against the individuals**. The 20-year scope is unusually long. The required deletion of "work product" (derived models, not just raw data) was a **novel approach to data-rights enforcement** -- recognizing that even if raw data is deleted, models trained on that data preserve its predictive value.

Whether the deletion was actually carried out is not publicly verifiable. The FTC has not, as of 2026, published any audit confirming destruction.
