# Cadwalladr v Banks (2022)

## Summary

Multi-year UK libel case brought by [[Arron Banks]] against journalist [[Carole Cadwalladr]] over her **2019 TED talk** ("Facebook's role in Brexit â€” and the threat to democracy") and several tweets referencing Banks's documented Russian Embassy contacts during the Brexit campaign period. UK High Court judgment delivered by Justice Karen Steyn on **June 13, 2022**.

## What Cadwalladr said

The contested statements primarily concerned:

- Banks's relationship with the Russian government via Russian Embassy contacts
- Whether Banks lied about the source of his Brexit funding
- Banks's relationship with [[Andrew Wigmore]] and their meetings with Russian Ambassador Alexander Yakovenko

- Cadwalladr ordered to pay portion of Banks's legal costs

## Significance

The case became a **defining test** of:

1. **UK press protections** for investigative journalism on matters of public interest
2. **Chilling-effect concerns** about wealthy subjects of investigation using libel law against journalists
3. **The Defamation Act 2013** public-interest defense in practice
4. **SLAPP (Strategic Litigation Against Public Participation)** concerns prompting subsequent UK anti-SLAPP legislation efforts

## Impact on the broader CA investigation environment

The Banks lawsuit had significant effects beyond Cadwalladr personally:

- **Chilling effect** on other UK journalism on CA-adjacent topics
- **Personal cost** to Cadwalladr (financial, time, emotional)
- **Cautionary example** for other investigators of wealthy CA-era figures
- **Motivated UK anti-SLAPP advocacy**
