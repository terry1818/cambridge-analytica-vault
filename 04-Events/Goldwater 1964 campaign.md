# Goldwater 1964 Campaign

## Summary

Barry Goldwater's 1964 presidential campaign against incumbent Lyndon B. Johnson was among the most consequential electoral defeats in modern American political history — consequential precisely because of what the losing side built from it. Goldwater, the Republican Senator from Arizona and author of *The Conscience of a Conservative* (1960), captured the Republican nomination by defeating the moderate-establishment wing of the party, securing the loyalty of a disciplined ideological faction rather than the broad coalition. He lost the general election catastrophically, winning only his home state of Arizona and five Deep South states, with Johnson taking 61.1 percent of the popular vote. Yet the campaign created the organizational infrastructure, donor lists, volunteer networks, and ideological template that Ronald Reagan and the conservative movement deployed for the next four decades. Historians universally treat 1964 as the founding event of the modern patron-class conservative coalition.

## When & Where

The Republican National Convention was held in San Francisco (Cow Palace), July 13–16, 1964. The general election was held November 3, 1964. The campaign was national in scope, with particular organizational strength in the South and West.

## Actors

- **Barry Goldwater**: Senator from Arizona (1953–1965, 1969–1987); libertarian-leaning conservative; opposed the Civil Rights Act of 1964 on constitutional/states'-rights grounds; author of *The Conscience of a Conservative*
- **Lyndon B. Johnson**: Incumbent Democratic president running on the legacy of JFK and the Great Society program
- **F. Clifton White**: Conservative organizer who masterminded the delegate-acquisition strategy that secured Goldwater the nomination, pioneering modern convention floor operations
- **Denison Kitchel**: Goldwater's campaign manager
- **Ronald Reagan**: Delivered "A Time for Choosing" (October 27, 1964), a nationally televised address for Goldwater drawn from his [[Boulwarism]]-era GE plant speeches; the speech launched Reagan's political career
- **William F. Buckley Jr.**: Provided intellectual legitimation through *National Review*

## Sequence of Events

1. **1960**: Goldwater publishes *The Conscience of a Conservative*, ghostwritten by L. Brent Bozell; becomes the movement's foundational text
2. **1961–1963**: F. Clifton White organizes the "Draft Goldwater" movement at the grassroots, building state-by-state delegate machinery
3. **1964-07-13–16**: Goldwater wins the Republican nomination; his acceptance speech contains the famous line: "Extremism in the defense of liberty is no vice; moderation in the pursuit of justice is no virtue" — a direct rebuke to Eisenhower-era moderate Republicanism
4. **1964-10-27**: Reagan's "A Time for Choosing" televised address raises approximately $1 million for the campaign and establishes Reagan as the movement's next leader
5. **1964-11-03**: Goldwater loses to Johnson 38.5% to 61.1%; carries 52 electoral votes vs. Johnson's 486
6. **1964–1966**: Conservative activists use the donor lists, organizational networks, and volunteer base built for Goldwater to found or expand institutions including the [[Heritage Foundation]] (1973), the [[American Conservative Union]] (1964), and Young Americans for Freedom

## Outcome / Consequences

The defeat was operationally transformative. The Goldwater campaign, which raised over $5.8 million from roughly 650,000 small donors — unprecedented for a Republican campaign — proved that a mass conservative donor base existed outside the country-club Republican establishment. The mailing lists and precinct organizations built by White and others became the seed capital for direct-mail conservative fundraising (developed by [[Richard Viguerie]]) and for the Reagan campaigns. Goldwater's capture of five Deep South states accelerated the partisan realignment that eventually brought white Southern voters fully into the Republican column. Reagan's post-Goldwater ascent to the California governorship (1966) and eventually the presidency (1980) is the direct organizational lineage.

## Disputed Facts

- Goldwater voted against the Civil Rights Act of 1964 on constitutional grounds (commerce clause and federal overreach), not on grounds of supporting segregation — he had desegregated his family business and the Arizona National Guard. However, his vote was exploited by segregationist supporters, and historians disagree about how cleanly his constitutional position can be separated from its political consequences.
- The degree to which the Goldwater campaign itself originated the conservative movement versus merely accelerating an existing trajectory from the [[Foundation for Economic Education]] and Mont Pelerin networks is debated.

## Open Questions

- How much of the Goldwater donor and volunteer network was directly absorbed into the Heritage Foundation and other 1970s institutions versus rebuilt independently?
- The role of the [[William Volker Fund]] and corporate backers in funding the campaign's ideological infrastructure is not fully documented.
