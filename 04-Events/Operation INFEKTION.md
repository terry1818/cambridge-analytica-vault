# Operation INFEKTION

## Summary

Operation INFEKTION — also designated Operation Denver by the Stasi, the codename confirmed in East German archives — was a Soviet-bloc disinformation campaign active from approximately 1983 onward. Its central claim: the US Army had engineered HIV/AIDS as a biological weapon at Fort Detrick, Maryland. It is one of the most extensively documented active-measures operations of the Cold War and a canonical case study in state-sponsored health disinformation. Thomas Rid's *Active Measures* (2020) examines it in depth. In 1992, SVR Director Yevgeny Primakov publicly acknowledged the KGB's role.

## When & Where

The opening shot was an anonymous letter, dated July 1983, sent to the Patriot — a KGB-seeded English-language newspaper in New Delhi — from a purported "American scientist and anthropologist." The letter alleged Fort Detrick was the source of AIDS. In September 1985 the KGB formally informed Bulgarian State Security that it had initiated an active-measures campaign on HIV/AIDS. Through the late 1980s the story was amplified by Soviet and Warsaw Pact media, mainstream international outlets that picked up the claim, and Stasi-coordinated academic forgeries.

## Actors

- **[[KGB Service A]]**: primary architect; ran the active-measures placement network
- **East German Stasi (MfS)**: operational partner; the Stasi codename "Operation Denver" was confirmed in archival research by historians Christopher Nehring and Douglas Selvage
- **Bulgarian State Security**: informed as partner in September 1985
- **Patriot (New Delhi)**: front-outlet used for initial seeding, exploiting its apparent independence from Soviet media

## Sequence of Events

1. **July 1983** — Anonymous letter published in the *Patriot* (New Delhi) alleging Fort Detrick origin of AIDS.
2. **1985** — KGB formally activated coordinated active-measures campaign; East German Stasi designated it Operation Denver.
3. **1985–1987** — Story amplified through Soviet state media (TASS, *Literaturnaya Gazeta*), Warsaw Pact outlets, and sympathetic third-party newspapers across Africa, Asia, and Latin America.
4. **1987** — Soviet scientist Valentin Zhdanov promoted the claim at international venues.
5. **Late 1980s** — US government formally protested; Soviet officials began quietly pulling back under diplomatic pressure.
6. **1992** — SVR Director Yevgeny Primakov acknowledged in a press conference that the KGB had been responsible for the campaign.

## Outcome / Consequences

The immediate propaganda objective — undermining US credibility and stoking suspicion of American military bases — had limited but measurable effect in some developing-world populations. The more lasting consequence was demonstrated proof of concept: a fabricated scientific claim, seeded through a low-credibility front outlet, can migrate into mainstream international media and persist for decades. Belief that AIDS was a US bioweapon remained detectable in surveys well into the 2000s, particularly in African-American communities, which the operation had specifically targeted. The operation became a template studied by subsequent active-measures analysts.

## Disputed Facts

- Historians Nehring and Selvage argue "Operation Denver" is the confirmed Stasi codename; "INFEKTION" was popularized by a 1992 US government report and may not be a primary-source Soviet designation. The vault uses INFEKTION as the common research name.
- The precise boundaries of KGB vs. Stasi operational control remain partially unclear from available archives.

## Open Questions

- To what extent did Soviet leadership directly authorize vs. Service A act on standing doctrine?
- Were similar seeds planted in West European left-wing press beyond identified cases?
- How directly did this operation inform subsequent Russian health disinformation (e.g., COVID-origin narratives)?
