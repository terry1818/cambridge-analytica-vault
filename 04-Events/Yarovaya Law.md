# Yarovaya Law — Russia's 2016 Data-Retention / Anti-Terror Legislation

## Summary

The Yarovaya Law (officially Federal Laws 374-FZ and 375-FZ, signed 7 July 2016) is a pair of Russian statutes amending counter-terrorism law and imposing the most sweeping communications data-retention obligations in Russian legal history. Named for its principal co-sponsor, State Duma deputy Irina Yarovaya, the law requires telecoms and internet operators to store the content of all user communications for six months and associated metadata for three years, physically within Russia, with direct decryption-key access for the FSB on demand and without the operator being entitled to demand a court order at the point of access. Human Rights Watch described it as a "Big Brother" law; the Electronic Frontier Foundation called it technically impossible to fully implement. The law materially reinforced [[SORM]] and completed the architecture documented in Soldatov and Borogan's *The Red Web*.

## When & Where

The bills passed the Russian State Duma on 24 June 2016. President [[Vladimir Putin]] signed them on 7 July 2016. Most criminal-law amendments entered force 20 July 2016. The data-retention obligations for telecoms — the most costly and controversial provisions — entered force 1 July 2018.

## Actors

- **Irina Yarovaya**: State Duma deputy and chair of the Security Committee; primary legislative sponsor. A hardline nationalist with close ties to Kremlin security structures.
- **Viktor Ozerov**: Senator and co-sponsor in the Federation Council.
- **Vladimir Putin**: Signed the legislation despite reported reservations within the industry.
- **FSB**: Primary institutional beneficiary; gains direct, warrantless access to stored content.
- **Russian ISPs and telecoms**: Required to bear compliance costs, estimated by industry at trillions of rubles — widely regarded as prohibitive for smaller operators.
- **[[Roskomnadzor]]**: Regulatory enforcer for the data-localization and storage components.

## Sequence of Events

1. **February 2016** — Draft legislation introduced amid heightened domestic security rhetoric following the 2015 Sinai plane bombing.
2. **June 2016** — State Duma passes both bills; opposition deputies and telecom industry lobby argue costs are impossible and privacy violations unconstitutional.
3. **7 July 2016** — Putin signs. Messaging app providers, including Telegram, publicly state they will not comply with decryption requirements.
4. **July 2016** — Criminal-law amendments (expanded definitions of terrorism, new offence of "international terrorism," criminalization of failure to report suspected terrorism) enter force.
5. **2017–2018** — Telecoms and ISPs begin limited compliance; industry groups publish cost estimates showing full compliance would require capital investment exceeding the sector's annual revenue.
6. **1 July 2018** — Full content-storage provisions enter force; compliance remains partial and uneven.
7. **2019** — Yarovaya obligations are layered with the sovereign internet law DPI requirements, creating a multi-layer surveillance stack.

## Outcome / Consequences

The law achieved its primary political goal: it formalised the FSB's legal claim to all Russian digital communications and signaled to operators that non-compliance is existential. In practice, full technical compliance by all operators has never been independently verified, and selective enforcement has been used as leverage. Telegram was formally ordered to provide decryption keys; it refused; [[Roskomnadzor]] attempted a block (2018) and eventually retreated (2020), illustrating the limits of technical enforcement. The law is a landmark in Russia's trajectory toward a fully nationalized communications stack.

## Disputed Facts

- The actual degree of data-storage compliance across smaller regional ISPs is unknown; no independent audit has been published.
- Whether the FSB actively exploits stored content at scale or primarily uses SORM's real-time access remains unclear.

## Open Questions

- Did the Yarovaya Law's model influence analogous legislation in other post-Soviet states?
- How does the FSB manage the storage and indexing of six months of full communications content from ~100 million users?
