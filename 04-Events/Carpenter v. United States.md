# Carpenter v. United States

## Summary

*Carpenter v. United States*, 585 U.S. 296 (2018), is a landmark U.S. Supreme Court decision concerning the privacy of historical cell-site location information (CSLI). In a 5–4 ruling issued June 22, 2018, the Court held that the government's acquisition of historical CSLI is a Fourth Amendment "search," and that the government must generally obtain a warrant supported by probable cause to acquire seven or more days of such records. The case grew out of a robbery investigation in which prosecutors, using only a court order under the Stored Communications Act — a standard lower than probable cause — obtained 127 days of location data for Timothy Carpenter's phone, cataloguing roughly 12,898 location points and placing his phone near multiple robbery sites. Chief Justice John Roberts, writing for the majority, held that the **third-party doctrine** — the rule that information voluntarily shared with a third party loses Fourth Amendment protection — does not extend to the comprehensive, automatically generated, deeply revealing record of a person's movements that CSLI represents.

## When & where

- **Date decided:** June 22, 2018.
- **Court:** Supreme Court of the United States.
- **Vote:** 5–4; majority opinion by Chief Justice Roberts, joined by the four liberal justices; four separate dissents (Kennedy, Thomas, Alito, Gorsuch).

## Actors

- [[Timothy Carpenter]] — petitioner; convicted in a series of armed robberies; challenged the warrantless acquisition of his location data.
- United States Department of Justice — respondent; argued the third-party doctrine permitted acquisition under the Stored Communications Act.
- [[John Roberts]] — Chief Justice; author of the majority opinion.
- Supreme Court of the United States — decision-maker.

## Sequence of events

- `2011` — a string of armed robberies in the Detroit area is investigated; an accomplice names Carpenter and provides his phone number.
- `2011` — prosecutors obtain court orders under the Stored Communications Act for Carpenter's CSLI, ultimately covering 127 days.
- `~2013-2014` — Carpenter convicted; he challenges the location evidence as obtained without a warrant.
- `2016` — the Sixth Circuit Court of Appeals upholds the conviction, applying the third-party doctrine.
- `2017-06` — the Supreme Court grants certiorari.
- `2017-11-29` — oral argument.
- `2018-06-22` — the Court rules 5–4 for Carpenter.

## Outcome / consequences

- **Direct outcome:** the warrantless acquisition of Carpenter's long-term CSLI was held unconstitutional; the third-party doctrine was held not to reach this category of data.
- **Downstream effects:** *Carpenter* became the central modern precedent for "digital is different" Fourth Amendment arguments — it has been invoked in litigation over geofence warrants, tower dumps, license-plate readers, and the government's purchase of location data from commercial brokers.
- **The "Carpenter loophole":** because *Carpenter* addressed government *compulsion* of records from carriers, it left open whether the government can simply *buy* equivalent location data from data brokers — a gap that directly implicates this vault's data-broker thread.

## Disputed facts

- **Scope:** the majority emphasized the ruling was "narrow" and did not disturb conventional surveillance or the third-party doctrine generally; critics and the dissents read it as either too narrow or as destabilizing settled doctrine. `#claim/disputed`
- **Application to purchased data:** lower courts have split on whether *Carpenter*'s logic restrains the commercial purchase of location data; the Supreme Court has not resolved it. `#claim/disputed`
