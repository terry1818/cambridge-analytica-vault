## Summary

The Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act is a bipartisan US federal bill designed to close what civil liberties advocates call the "data broker loophole" — the legal gap that allows federal government agencies to purchase Americans' location data, browsing histories, communications metadata, and other sensitive personal information from private data brokers without obtaining a judicial warrant. Because the [[Third-party doctrine]] holds that data voluntarily shared with a third party (such as an app or cell carrier) loses Fourth Amendment protection, agencies including the FBI, IRS, DEA, DHS, ICE, and CBP have routinely bought this information commercially rather than seeking judicial authorization. The bill passed the House in 2024 but died in the Senate. It is directly responsive to the surveillance-infrastructure practices documented in [[Source - Tau 2024 - Means of Control]] and is a key policy battleground for the [[Hypothesis - Federal database supersedes CA model]].

## When & where

First introduced in 2021; reintroduced in July 2023 with a broader coalition; passed the US House of Representatives in 2024. The Senate took no action before the congressional term ended. The bill has been introduced in multiple sessions of Congress without becoming law.

## Actors

The bill has notable bipartisan architecture: libertarian-leaning Republicans (Davidson, Massie, Biggs) joined with progressive Democrats (Lofgren, Nadler, Jayapal, Jacobs) — a coalition reflecting unusual cross-aisle alignment on surveillance concerns. The ACLU, EFF, and the Project on Government Oversight (POGO) have been active advocates. The Brennan Center for Justice has published supporting research.

## Sequence of events

1. **2021** — Bill first introduced in the House; does not advance.
2. **July 2023** — Reintroduced with expanded co-sponsorship; bipartisan press conference by Davidson, Lofgren, Nadler, Biggs, Buck, Jayapal, Massie, and Jacobs.
3. **2024** — House passes the bill as part of the reauthorization debate around Section 702 (FISA); Senate fails to act before recess; bill dies.
4. **2025–2026** — Bill has not been enacted; the data-broker loophole remains open. Ongoing advocacy for reintroduction. Davidson published an op-ed in April 2026 reiterating the case.

## Outcome / consequences

The bill has not become law. Federal agencies continue to legally purchase location and behavioral data from brokers including [[Venntel]], [[X-Mode (Outlogic)]], and related vendors without warrants. The Senate's failure to act is widely attributed to intelligence-community opposition and the lobbying power of the data-broker industry.

A parallel state-level effort in Wisconsin (AB-667/SB-679) adopted the same "Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale" framing, indicating state-level adoption of the federal bill's logic.

## Disputed facts

- The scope of the bill and its interaction with foreign-intelligence collection (FISA/Section 702) has been debated; some versions included carve-outs.
- Proponents argue the bill restores Fourth Amendment intent; opponents argue it would impair national security by restricting commercially available data that foreign adversaries also buy freely.

## Open questions

- Will the bill be reintroduced in the 120th Congress (2027+), and will Senate dynamics shift?
- Does the Trump 2.0 administration's expansion of commercial data use (ICE/CBP contracts with [[Clearview AI]]) further entrench the loophole?
- Could *Carpenter v. United States* (2018) precedent on cell-site location data be extended judicially to cover commercial data broker sales, rendering the legislation redundant in part?
