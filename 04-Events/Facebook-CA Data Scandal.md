## Post-2018 enforcement aftermath and the broader policy consequences

The Facebook-CA Data Scandal produced the deepest single privacy enforcement action in US history (the [[FTC v Facebook 2019]] $5 billion settlement) and substantial structural changes to the Facebook developer platform. The full enforcement-and-policy aftermath through 2024-2026:

**Regulatory consequences across jurisdictions.** US: the FTC settlement included structural privacy reforms, board-level privacy oversight, and ongoing FTC compliance reporting; SEC enforcement on disclosure questions produced an additional $100M penalty; multiple state-attorney-general settlements in 2022-2024 produced an additional $725M class-action settlement covering US Facebook users. UK: GBP 500,000 ICO fine (the pre-GDPR statutory maximum). EU: GDPR enforcement under the post-2018 regulatory regime has produced multiple subsequent Meta penalties for related data-processing-failure categories.

**Structural product changes.** Facebook deprecated Graph API v1 in 2015 (pre-CA-public-disclosure) and progressively tightened developer access through v2 and v3; the post-2018 acceleration of those changes included the elimination of friend-data access scopes entirely, the requirement of app-review for any data scope beyond basic profile information, and the introduction of the "Off-Facebook Activity" controls that gave users visibility into the data-collection happening via Facebook tracking pixels and SDKs on third-party websites and apps.

**Industry-wide effects.** The CA scandal substantially restructured the broader social-media-app-developer ecosystem. Many smaller apps that had relied on Facebook social-graph access lost data-access during the post-2018 platform tightening; the broader market for "social discovery" applications declined; the political-targeting market specifically shifted toward more direct first-party data collection and away from the platform-mediated data-access model that CA had exploited.

## What the scandal did and did not change about the underlying capability

The CA scandal eliminated the specific data-access vector that GSR/Kogan exploited (Graph API v1 friend-data exposure) but did not eliminate the underlying capability the vault is built to analyse. The 2024-2026 information environment still supports:

- Individual-level targeting at scale (now principally through Facebook's own internal Custom Audiences and Lookalike Audiences infrastructure, which gives advertisers comparable targeting capability without the third-party-developer-data-access exposure that CA used).
- Psychographic-style audience modelling (now substantially absorbed into Facebook's own machine-learning infrastructure and into commercial data-broker products that aggregate non-Facebook data sources).
- Cross-platform identity resolution (commercial vendors and the platform's own infrastructure both support this).
- Dark-ad deployment at scale (the Facebook Ad Library post-2018 transparency improvements have made the deployment partially visible but have not eliminated it).

The vault's analytical position: the CA scandal was the public-facing reckoning for an infrastructure that has continued operating in a more centralised form. The capability did not disappear; the third-party-developer access to that capability was substantially restricted, with the capability remaining inside the platforms themselves.
