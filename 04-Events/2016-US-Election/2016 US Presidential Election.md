## The race in vault terms

The 2016 US presidential election is the operational deployment that the vault is organised around. It is where the [[Cambridge Analytica]] methodology (psychographic-microtargeting infrastructure layered on top of harvested Facebook data), the [[Mercer]] money pipeline, the [[Steve Bannon]] strategic overlay, the [[Breitbart News]] content engine, and the [[Russian interference]] vector all intersected to produce a contested outcome that subsequent investigations (Mueller, ICO, FTC, congressional intelligence committees) reconstructed in different overlapping pieces.

## What the vault treats as established

The Trump campaign engaged CA for digital operations under [[Brad Parscale]]'s management; CA had access to the Facebook-derived data Kogan's GSR app produced; the operation deployed dark-ad microtargeting, voter-suppression messaging aimed at low-propensity Democratic-leaning audiences (especially African-American urban populations and young women), and energisation messaging aimed at low-propensity GOP-leaning audiences. The [[Mueller Investigation]] documented Russian state operations (IRA social media, GRU email exfiltration and release through Wikileaks). The [[Source Senate Intel Vol 5 2020]] report consolidated the bipartisan findings.

What the vault treats as contested or unresolved: the quantitative size of CA's effect on the actual outcome; the degree of coordination (vs parallel operation) between the CA-side data operation and the Russian-side influence operation; the role of specific intermediaries (Kushner, Manafort, Stone) in either operation. The vault distinguishes between documented contact, alleged coordination, and proven coordination, and tags each accordingly.

## The campaign timeline and CA's operational integration

The Trump campaign's relationship with [[Cambridge Analytica]] began in earnest in May 2016 after Ted Cruz withdrew from the GOP primary. The Cruz primary campaign had been a CA client (~$5M in billings through the primary cycle, principally funded via [[Make America Number 1 Super PAC]]); the post-withdrawal pivot moved CA personnel, data, and methodology to the Trump general-election operation.

[[Brad Parscale]] (then a relatively unknown San Antonio-based web developer, formerly building Trump Organization websites) ran the digital operation under the brand "Project Alamo" with a San Antonio team that scaled from ~10 to ~100 staff through the campaign. CA's specific integration was: data integration, audience modelling, creative-testing infrastructure, and (in the campaign's own pitch materials) the psychographic-overlay layer that distinguished the operation from pure demographic-and-behavioural microtargeting.

The intensity of CA-campaign integration increased after [[Steve Bannon]] joined as chief executive in August 2016. The "Project Alamo" operation deployed an estimated 5.9 million different ad variants over the final months, with most testing concentrated on Facebook. The Facebook Ad Library (which Facebook subsequently opened for historical political-ad transparency) preserved much of the deployment record post-hoc; pre-Library deployments are documented through campaign records, CA invoices, and Wylie/Kaiser testimony.

## The Russian-state-operation parallel track

The Russian state operation ran in parallel with the CA-side campaign infrastructure. The two distinct Russian operations the [[Mueller Investigation]] documented:

**The Internet Research Agency social media operation:** approximately 470 IRA-controlled Facebook pages, an estimated 80,000+ pieces of organic content reaching an estimated 126 million Americans, plus the affiliated Twitter and Instagram footprints. The IRA operation focused on cultural-wedge content (race, immigration, religion, gun rights) designed to widen existing US political polarisation; explicit candidate advocacy was a smaller fraction.

**The GRU hack-and-leak operation:** the breach of the DNC, the DCCC, and the Clinton campaign chair John Podesta's emails; the laundering of the stolen material through DCLeaks, Guccifer 2.0, and ultimately WikiLeaks; the timed release of material at specific points in the campaign cycle (most consequentially, the WikiLeaks Podesta-email release within hours of the Access Hollywood tape release in October 2016).

The Russian operation and the CA-side campaign operation ran on parallel tracks. The Mueller Report documented numerous contacts between Trump-campaign principals and Russian-affiliated actors (Trump Tower June 2016 meeting; Manafort-Kilimnik thread; Stone-WikiLeaks contacts; Flynn-Kislyak contacts during the transition; the broader Trump-orbit-Russia contact catalogue). The Mueller findings did not establish a criminal conspiracy or coordination beyond the indictment standard, while documenting the contacts in detail.

## The actual vote and the geographic mechanics

Trump won the Electoral College 304-227 (after seven faithless-elector defections from the original 306-232 result). The popular vote went to Clinton by approximately 2.9 million votes. The Electoral College result hinged on three Rust Belt states (Wisconsin, Michigan, Pennsylvania) decided by combined margins of approximately 78,000 votes â€” small enough that any of several plausible counterfactuals (different CA operation, different Russian operation, different Comey letter timing, different campaign-event scheduling) could have changed the outcome.

The vault's analytical position on the "what decided 2016" question: the result was overdetermined in the sense that multiple factors each plausibly account for the narrow margin, and the narrowness of the margin makes any single-cause attribution unreliable. The CA operation was operationally real and meaningfully sized; whether it was decisive in the sense that absent CA the result would have been different is unprovable from the available evidence.

## What the Mueller and Senate Intel investigations established

[[Mueller Investigation]] established: detailed Russian operational picture (IRA, GRU); detailed Trump-orbit-Russia contact catalogue without rising to criminal conspiracy; the obstruction-of-justice analysis (Volume II) that documented ten potential obstruction episodes and did not exonerate Trump.

[[Source Senate Intel Vol 5 2020]] (the bipartisan Senate Intelligence Committee report, August 2020) is the most operationally consequential single document on the campaign-side coordination question. The committee found that Paul Manafort had repeated and substantial contact with Russian intelligence officer Konstantin Kilimnik during the campaign period, that Manafort had passed internal campaign polling and strategy data to Kilimnik, and that Kilimnik was a "grave counterintelligence threat" with active Russian intelligence connections.

The Senate Intel finding sits closer to documenting actual coordination than the Mueller Report did. Mueller declined to make a criminal-conspiracy finding because the Special Counsel's standard required indictment-grade evidence; the Senate report did not face that constraint and found behaviour that the committee characterised in language consistent with operational coordination even if not with criminal conspiracy.

## The Trump-Tower June 2016 meeting in detail

The 9 June 2016 meeting at Trump Tower between [[Donald Trump Jr.]], [[Jared Kushner]], [[Paul Manafort]], and Russian-linked attorney Natalia Veselnitskaya (with several other attendees) is one of the most-investigated specific contact points. The meeting was set up via Rob Goldstone with the explicit promise of "dirt" on Hillary Clinton coming from "the Crown Prosecutor of Russia." The Trump-orbit attendees subsequently characterised the meeting as a discussion of Magnitsky Act / Russian-adoption policy that produced no useful information.

The vault treats this meeting as documented contact, not as documented coordination. The Mueller Report investigated it without producing a criminal-conspiracy charge. The Senate Intel report covered it in more depth but did not produce additional indictment-grade findings on this specific meeting.
