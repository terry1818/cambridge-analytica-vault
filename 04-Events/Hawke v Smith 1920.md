# Hawke v. Smith (1920)

## Summary

*Hawke v. Smith*, 253 U.S. 221 (1920), is the foundational Supreme Court precedent on *who* ratifies constitutional amendments. The Court held unanimously that the Ohio constitutional provision requiring a public referendum before the state legislature's ratification of a federal constitutional amendment could take effect was inconsistent with Article V of the U.S. Constitution. The ratification function under Article V belongs to the state legislature as a federal—not state—function, and states cannot reassign it to voters through a referendum mechanism. The case matters to the [[Article V Convention]] debate because it confirms that the amendment process is insulated from direct popular democracy in ways that complicate both COS proponents' "people's convention" framing and the broader question of democratic legitimacy in the Article V process.

## When & Where

Decided June 1, 1920, by the U.S. Supreme Court. The underlying dispute arose in Ohio following the legislature's ratification of the Eighteenth Amendment (Prohibition). Ohio had a state constitutional provision granting citizens the right of referendum on legislative acts, including ratification of federal constitutional amendments.

## Actors

- **Hawke**: Ohio citizen who filed suit seeking to compel a referendum on the legislature's ratification of the Eighteenth Amendment.
- **Smith**: Ohio Secretary of State, named as defendant.
- **U.S. Supreme Court**: Decided unanimously through Justice William R. Day's opinion.

## Sequence of Events

1. Ohio legislature ratified the Eighteenth Amendment.
2. Ohio's constitution provided that any legislative act could be subject to referendum by citizen petition.
3. Hawke sought a writ of mandamus to compel a referendum on the ratification.
4. Ohio courts sided with Hawke; the case reached the Supreme Court.
5. The Supreme Court reversed, holding that Article V ratification is a federal constitutional function, not ordinary state legislation.

## Outcome / Consequences

The Court's reasoning drew a critical line: the state legislature's act of ratification is "not an act of legislation in the proper sense of the word; it is but the expression of the assent of the state to a proposed amendment." Because the function derives from the federal Constitution—not from state authority—states cannot modify it through their own constitutions. Ohio's referendum provision was therefore void as applied.

The ruling had two lasting effects:
1. It hardened the divide between the amendment process and ordinary democratic accountability, which becomes a recurring criticism in the [[Article V aggregation problem]] and [[Article V delegate-selection vacuum]] debates.
2. It was cited in later Article V jurisprudence, including *Coleman v Miller* (1939), which addressed whether ratification could be rescinded, and which partly undermined *Dillon v. Gloss* by treating some Article V questions as political rather than judicial.

Feingold and Prindiville highlight *Hawke* as a foundational precedent that COS proponents must reckon with: the legislature-only ratification rule constrains how an Article V convention's output could be ratified, and any convention product that tried to route ratification through referenda would face this precedent.

## Disputed Facts

The case itself is not factually disputed; the legal holding is clear. Scholarly debate concerns its downstream implications — whether *Hawke*'s logic about the "federal function" of ratification applies analogously to questions about delegate selection and convention rules, or whether those questions remain fully open. Tier-2 academic commentary is divided.

## Open Questions

- Does the *Hawke* principle (ratification is a federal function, unalterable by states) extend to the application side — meaning states cannot add conditions to their Article V applications beyond what Article V prescribes?
- After [[Coleman v Miller 1939]], which declared some ratification questions non-justiciable, how much of *Hawke*'s reasoning survives as judicially enforceable?
- Does *Hawke* provide any constraint on [[Convention of States Action]]'s claim that a convention can be strictly "limited" by state application language?
