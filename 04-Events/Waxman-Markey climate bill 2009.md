## Summary

The American Clean Energy and Security Act of 2009 — commonly called Waxman-Markey after its House sponsors — was the Obama administration's flagship climate legislation. It proposed a cap-and-trade system to reduce greenhouse gas emissions across major economic sectors. The bill narrowly passed the House of Representatives on 26 June 2009 (219–212), a margin that required significant political effort to achieve. It was never brought to the Senate floor; Senate Majority Leader Harry Reid declined to schedule a vote after it became clear a blocking coalition had been assembled. Christopher Leonard's *Kochland* documents how [[Koch Industries]] and [[Americans for Prosperity]] were central to that defeat, using the [[No Climate Tax Pledge]] as the primary legislative blocking tool and deploying town-hall disruption tactics — methods that overlapped with Tea Party organizing against the ACA.

## When & Where

- **House passage:** 26 June 2009 (219–212 vote).
- **Senate fate:** Never scheduled for floor vote; Senate consideration collapsed by late 2010.
- **Koch campaign period:** [[Americans for Prosperity]] opposition campaign ran approximately 2008–2010, including pre-emptive pledge campaigns targeting senators before the House vote and throughout Senate consideration.

## Actors

- **Henry Waxman (D-CA) and Edward Markey (D-MA)** — House sponsors.
- **[[Americans for Prosperity]]** — primary Koch-network vehicle for opposition; deployed the [[No Climate Tax Pledge]] and organized grassroots disruption events.
- **[[Koch Industries]]** — direct financial interest; Koch is one of the largest US carbon emitters; Leonard documents the company's internal analysis that cap-and-trade would cost Koch billions annually.
- **[[Charles Koch]] / [[David Koch]]** — directed the opposition strategy through AFP and [[Koch donor summits]].
- **[[Heritage Foundation]]** — produced economic analyses framing Waxman-Markey as a major tax increase.
- **US Chamber of Commerce** — allied opposition voice.
- **Obama administration and Senate Democrats** — proponents; ultimately unable to assemble 60 votes.

## Sequence of Events

1. **2008:** [[Americans for Prosperity]] launches pre-emptive "No Climate Tax" pledge campaign anticipating cap-and-trade legislation; Supreme Court's 2007 *Massachusetts v. EPA* ruling had established EPA authority to regulate greenhouse gases.
2. **Early 2009:** HR 2454 introduced; Koch network begins intensive lobbying and grassroots campaign framing the bill as "the largest excise tax in history."
3. **June 26, 2009:** House passes the bill 219–212, with significant Democratic defections.
4. **2009–2010:** AFP holds town halls, disrupts Democratic events, and pressures Senate Democrats in fossil-fuel-dependent states; [[No Climate Tax Pledge]] signatories reach a Senate blocking minority.
5. **Late 2010:** Harry Reid abandons Senate climate bill; no floor vote taken.
6. **2010 midterms:** Tea Party wave, partly organized through AFP infrastructure, eliminates many House Democrats who voted for the bill; political cost of the yes vote is widely noted, chilling future climate legislation.

## Outcome / Consequences

- Federal cap-and-trade climate legislation has not been brought to a floor vote since 2010.
- The defeat established the Koch network's capacity to block major legislation through pledge coordination and outside-spending pressure — a template later applied to ACA repeal attempts and tax policy.
- Reinforced the [[No Climate Tax Pledge]] as a binding litmus test for Republican primary viability on climate.
- The episode is a case study in the successful deployment of dark-money infrastructure against a sitting majority government's signature legislative priority.

## Disputed Facts

- The degree to which Koch network spending was the determinative factor (vs. genuine constituent opposition to carbon pricing in fossil-fuel states) is contested. The $400M+ spending figures attributed to the network are estimates, not audited. Evidence tier: **Tier-2** (investigative journalism; not independently audited).
- The claim that AFP called it "the largest excise tax in history" is documented; whether that characterization was accurate is disputed — economists largely regarded it as false.

## Open Questions

- How much Koch Industries spent on direct lobbying (disclosed) vs. undisclosed outside spending against Waxman-Markey has never been fully reconciled.
- Whether the Senate blocking coalition would have held without Koch pressure, given genuine constituent ambivalence in coal and oil states, is counterfactually unanswerable.
