## Summary

In November 1996, the journal *First Things* — edited by [[Richard John Neuhaus]] — published "The End of Democracy? The Judicial Usurpation of Politics," a symposium asking whether federal courts, by claiming final authority over questions of abortion, euthanasia, homosexuality, and obscenity, had so thoroughly displaced democratic self-governance that "conscientious citizens can no longer give moral assent to the existing regime." The symposium generated the most intense intellectual controversy within postwar American conservatism in decades, with several prominent conservatives resigning from the First Things editorial board in protest.

The event is a watershed in the intellectual history of the theocratic wing: it marks the first time a prestigious conservative publication openly entertained the proposition that the United States government had lost its democratic legitimacy — and that resistance, potentially including civil disobedience, was morally justified. The framing anticipates later, more radical arguments in the post-liberal and integralist literature.

## When & where

- Published in the November 1996 issue of *First Things*, distributed nationally.
- Symposium essays were written by contributors located at Princeton, AEI, Fordham Law School, Prison Fellowship, and other institutions.
- The controversy played out in the pages of national publications including *Commentary*, the *Wall Street Journal*, *National Review*, and dozens of smaller journals over subsequent months.

## Actors

**Symposium contributors:**
- **[[Richard John Neuhaus]]** — editor and organizer; framed the central question; wrote introductory and concluding essays.
- **Robert Bork** — former federal circuit judge, Supreme Court nominee; contributed an essay comparing the current situation to the pre-Civil War constitutional crisis.
- **Russell Hittinger** — Catholic legal philosopher; argued courts had become a "theocracy" of secular humanism.
- **Hadley Arkes** — Amherst political philosopher; focused on natural-law grounds for judicial resistance.
- **Charles Colson** — evangelical Prison Fellowship founder; represented the evangelical wing of the theocon coalition.
- **[[Robert P. George]]** — Princeton legal philosopher; one of the symposium's contributors; later distanced himself from the most radical implications while defending the core question.

**Board members who resigned in protest:**
- Gertrude Himmelfarb — historian; objected to the suggestion that regime illegitimacy could justify civil disobedience or resistance.
- Walter Berns — American Enterprise Institute constitutionalist; rejected the question as irresponsible.
- Peter Berger — sociologist; co-author (with Neuhaus) of *To Empower People* (1977); objected to the tone and implication.
- Norman Podhoretz — *Commentary* editor; resigned; published criticism.

## Sequence of events

1. **Autumn 1996**: Neuhaus assembles symposium contributions for the November issue; the framing question explicitly raises whether "the regime" has lost legitimacy.
2. **November 1996**: Issue published; immediate controversy erupts; Neuhaus's editorial framing is seen by critics as an implicit call for resistance to the US government.
3. **Late 1996–early 1997**: Editorial board resignations (Himmelfarb, Berns, Berger, Podhoretz and others); public repudiations by prominent conservatives including William Kristol (*Weekly Standard*) and James Q. Wilson.
4. **1997**: Neuhaus publishes "The Anatomy of a Controversy" — a 90-page response to critics — in *First Things*; defends the legitimacy of the question while clarifying that he was not calling for armed resistance.
5. **1997**: A book collecting the original symposium and responses is published: *The End of Democracy? The Celebrated First Things Debate* (Spence Publishing).

## Outcome / consequences

**Immediate**: The symposium damaged Neuhaus's relationships with several major figures in mainstream conservatism. *National Review* and *Commentary* distanced themselves from the First Things position.

**Medium-term**: Despite the controversy, the symposium succeeded in placing on the table a question that had previously been unspeakable in respectable conservative circles: the moral legitimacy of judicial supremacy. This question would resurface in the early 2000s debates over judicial filibusters and again in the post-*Obergefell* resistance arguments.

**Long-term**: The symposium is now recognized as a precursor to the post-liberal and integralist turn in Catholic conservative thought. [[Patrick Deneen]], [[Adrian Vermeule]], and [[Sohrab Ahmari]] — all writing after 2016 — argue versions of what the 1996 symposium floated: that liberal democracy is not the permanent horizon of politics and that its constitutional arrangements may lack ultimate moral authority. The 1996 symposium did not produce this conclusion, but it created intellectual permission to ask the question.

The symposium also crystallized the theocon coalition's distinctiveness: its willingness to use Catholic natural-law reasoning to question, rather than simply reform, liberal political institutions — a posture that separated it from both libertarian conservatism and mainstream religious-right pragmatism.

## Disputed facts

- The number of editorial board resignations is variously cited as three or four in different sources; Himmelfarb, Berns, Berger, and Podhoretz are confirmed; others may have followed.
- Whether Robert P. George endorsed the most radical implications of the symposium is disputed; he later emphasized that he was raising a question, not advocating regime resistance.
- Neuhaus himself denied the symposium implied armed resistance or revolution; critics argued the framing made that implication unavoidable.

## Open questions

- Was the 1996 symposium a deliberate strategy by Neuhaus to shift the Overton window within conservatism, or was the controversy genuinely unexpected?
- How directly do post-2016 post-liberal arguments (Deneen, Vermeule, Ahmari) cite the 1996 symposium as intellectual precedent? A citation analysis would strengthen the lineage claim.
- The subsequent careers of the board members who resigned — and their continued (or severed) relationship with First Things — have not been fully mapped in this vault.
