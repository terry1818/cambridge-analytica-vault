## Summary

Between early 2023 and mid-2024, House Republicans — led by Representative Jim Jordan (R-OH) as chair of both the House Judiciary Committee and the Select Subcommittee on the Weaponization of the Federal Government — conducted an aggressive campaign of investigation, document requests, and subpoenas targeting academic institutions and researchers engaged in disinformation and election integrity research. The primary targets were the [[Stanford Internet Observatory]] (SIO), the Election Integrity Partnership (EIP), and — through the EIP connection — the [[Center for an Informed Public]] at the University of Washington. The campaign resulted in SIO winding down its election research, the departure of its leadership, and a broadly documented chilling effect on the field.

## When & where

- **April 12, 2023:** House Judiciary Committee issued subpoena to Stanford Internet Observatory for documents related to its election monitoring work.
- **June 2023:** Committee threatened enforcement after Stanford's compliance was deemed insufficient.
- **September 2023:** Washington Post reported that misinformation research programs were "buckling" under pressure.
- **November 2023:** Alex Stamos, SIO founder and primary fundraiser, departed, citing political pressure.
- **June 2024:** SIO announced it was winding down election research; Renée DiResta's contract was not renewed.

## Actors

- **[[Jim Jordan]]** — primary congressional aggressor; used the Weaponization Subcommittee and Judiciary Committee as dual instruments.
- **House Judiciary Committee Republicans** — framed the investigation as uncovering government-directed "censorship" of conservative speech on social media platforms.
- **[[Stanford Internet Observatory]]** — principal institutional target; co-led the Election Integrity Partnership with the University of Washington, Graphika, and others.
- **[[Alex Stamos]]** — SIO founder; stated publicly that the investigation was "pretty successful" in discouraging the SIO from conducting similar research in 2024.
- **[[Renée DiResta]]** — Research Manager at SIO; contract not renewed June 2024; moved to Georgetown.
- **[[Kate Starbird]]** / **[[Center for an Informed Public]]** — UW side of the EIP; subject to related pressure.
- **Defending rights organizations** — ACLU, Electronic Frontier Foundation, and academic freedom groups filed amicus and public comments challenging the investigation's framing.

## Sequence of events

1. The EIP published analyses of election-related misinformation in the 2020 and 2022 elections, flagging content on major platforms. The EIP used Atlassian Jira to ticket alleged mis- and disinformation for platform review.
2. Right-wing critics, including the conservative nonprofit America First Legal, characterized the EIP's flagging work as government-directed censorship (because EIP had connections to DHS's CISA during 2020 election cycle).
3. House Judiciary Committee, under Jordan, sent broad document requests to SIO and partner institutions in early 2023, framed under the "Weaponization of the Federal Government" mandate.
4. When Stanford declined full compliance, the Committee issued a formal subpoena (April 12, 2023).
5. The Committee also sent sweeping information requests to other universities (documented by ProPublica, September 2023).
6. In parallel, lawsuits by conservative organizations against SIO-affiliated researchers created legal costs and personnel risk.
7. Researchers self-reported decisions not to pursue certain projects; recruitment for the field became more difficult.
8. The SIO announced in June 2024 it would not conduct research into the 2024 election. Stanford maintained the SIO would remain operational in other domains (child safety, Journal of Online Trust and Safety).

## Outcome / consequences

- **Immediate:** SIO stopped election research; lost both founding leaders (Stamos, DiResta) within 8 months.
- **Structural chilling effect:** Documented by Washington Post, ProPublica, NPR, and Inside Higher Ed; researchers at multiple institutions reported self-censoring or redirecting research away from election integrity topics.
- **Political framing victory:** House Judiciary Committee publicized the SIO wind-down as a "big win" (Washington Times, June 2024).
- **Field resilience:** CIP continued operations; DiResta moved to Georgetown; the field did not cease but narrowed its public-facing election work.

## Disputed facts

- The core dispute: whether EIP/SIO work constituted government-directed censorship (Republican framing) or independent academic research with appropriate government engagement (researchers' and Stanford's position).
- Courts and independent legal analysis have not upheld the censorship characterization; the *Murthy v. Missouri* Supreme Court case (2024) significantly narrowed the legal theories underlying the government-censorship claim.

## Open questions

- Whether the chilling effect permanently reduces capacity for election integrity research, particularly in 2026 and 2028 election cycles.
- Whether the SIO will restore election research capacity under new leadership.
- Legal exposure of individual researchers to continued harassment campaigns.
