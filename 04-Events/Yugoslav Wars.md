## Summary

The Yugoslav Wars were the decade-long sequence of armed conflicts accompanying the dissolution of the Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia. They are the paradigm post-Cold War case study for the pathway from [[Anocracy]] to civil war through [[Ethnic outbidding]] — the mechanism at the center of Barbara Walter's [[Source - Walter 2022 - How Civil Wars Start]]. Yugoslavia in the late 1980s was precisely the kind of weakened, semi-democratic state in which ethnic entrepreneurs, led by [[Slobodan Milosevic]], discovered they could use demographic grievance to outcompete democratic rivals and consolidate power. The result was a cascade of secessionist wars, ethnic cleansing campaigns, and ultimately genocide — the worst political violence in Europe since World War II. The ICTY's prosecution of Milošević, Karadžić, and Mladić established international criminal responsibility for the pattern; Srebrenica was ruled genocide by both the ICTY and the International Court of Justice.

## When & Where

- **Slovenia War** (Ten-Day War): June 27 – July 7, 1991. Brief conflict after Slovenia declared independence; Yugoslav People's Army withdrew.
- **Croatian War of Independence**: 1991–1995. Serbian minority in Croatia, supported by Yugoslav forces, seized territory; "Krajina" RSK created; reintegrated by Croatia in 1995 (Operation Storm).
- **Bosnian War**: April 1992 – December 1995 (Dayton Agreement). The central and most destructive conflict; Bosnian Serb forces laid siege to Sarajevo (1992–1996, longest siege in modern warfare), conducted systematic ethnic cleansing, and carried out the Srebrenica massacre (July 1995).
- **Kosovo War**: 1998–1999. Serbian security forces vs. Kosovo Liberation Army; NATO air campaign (March–June 1999) forced Serbian withdrawal; Kosovo under UN administration until 2008 independence.
- **Insurgency in North Macedonia**: 2001. Brief conflict involving ethnic Albanian insurgents; resolved by Ohrid Framework Agreement.

## Actors

- **Slobodan Milošević** (see [[Slobodan Milosevic]]): Serbian president; coordinated paramilitary operations; indicted by ICTY 1999.
- **Radovan Karadžić**: Political leader of Republika Srpska (Bosnian Serb entity); convicted by ICTY of genocide and crimes against humanity (40 years).
- **Ratko Mladić**: Commander of the Army of Republika Srpska; convicted of genocide and crimes against humanity (life sentence).
- **Franjo Tuđman**: President of Croatia; oversaw Croatian nationalist mobilization; also indicted by some analysts for complicity in ethnic cleansing of Serbs, though he died before any indictment.
- **ICTY**: UN war crimes tribunal established 1993; indicted 161 individuals; completed proceedings 2017 (succeeded by IRMCT).

## Sequence of Events

1. **1989–1991**: Federal dissolution; Milošević revokes Kosovo autonomy (1989); Slovenia and Croatia declare independence (June 1991).
2. **1991–1992**: JNA (Yugoslav People's Army) intervention in Slovenia (defeated in 10 days); prolonged war in Croatia; recognition of Slovenia, Croatia, Bosnia by EC (January 1992).
3. **1992–1995**: Bosnian War begins; Siege of Sarajevo; Srebrenica massacre (July 1995, 8,000+ Bosniak men and boys killed — ruled genocide); NATO airstrikes against Bosnian Serb positions (August–September 1995).
4. **1995**: Dayton Peace Agreement ends Bosnian War; Croatia reintegrates Krajina (Operation Storm); Croatian Serbs expelled.
5. **1998–1999**: Kosovo War; NATO air campaign; Serbian forces withdraw; UNMIK administration.
6. **2001**: North Macedonia insurgency; Ohrid Agreement.

## Outcome / Consequences

- Yugoslavia dissolved into seven independent states (Slovenia, Croatia, Bosnia-Herzegovina, Serbia, Montenegro, North Macedonia, Kosovo).
- Estimated 130,000 deaths; 2.2 million displaced in Bosnia alone.
- Srebrenica massacre (1995): 8,000+ Bosniak men and boys killed; ruled genocide by ICTY (2004) and ICJ (2007).
- ICTY indicted 161 individuals; 90 convicted; completed work 2017.
- NATO's intervention established a precedent for humanitarian military intervention without UN Security Council authorization.
- Kosovo declared independence 2008; recognized by ~100 states but not by Russia, China, or five EU members.

## Disputed Facts

- Precise death toll estimates vary (scholars range 100,000–140,000+ for Bosnian War alone).
- Tuđman's personal responsibility for Croatian ethnic cleansing operations is contested; he died before any ICTY indictment.
- Whether Milošević directly ordered Srebrenica is disputed; ICTY determined he had "overall control" of Bosnian Serb forces but the genocide finding did not rest on direct command. The full chamber verdict on Milošević was never rendered.

## Open Questions

- Does the Yugoslav case confirm Walter's "anocracy + ethnic outbidding" model as generalizable, or is Yugoslavia sui generis because of the specific federal-institutional trigger (Tito's death, debt crisis)?
- What institutional design lessons does the Yugoslav failure offer for contemporary multiethnic democracies under stress?
