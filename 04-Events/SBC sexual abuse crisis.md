## Summary

The Southern Baptist Convention sexual abuse crisis is the culmination of a pattern of clergy sexual abuse — and systematic institutional suppression of survivors' complaints — extending from at least the early 2000s. The crisis reached a public inflection point on May 22, 2022, when an independent investigation by Guidepost Solutions, commissioned after survivor advocates and denominational reformers pressed the SBC Executive Committee, released a 209-page report. The report found that SBC leaders had, for nearly two decades, stonewalled and actively denigrated abuse survivors, maintained a secret list of accused clergy, and prioritized institutional liability protection over victims. Timothy Alberta's *The Kingdom, the Power, and the Glory* (2023) addresses the crisis as symptomatic of accountability deficits within the Southern Baptist institutional model.

## When & where

- **Investigative period covered**: January 1, 2000 to June 14, 2021.
- **Report release**: May 22, 2022, during the SBC annual meeting in Anaheim, California.
- **DOJ investigation announced**: August 2022.
- **Scope**: National; the SBC is a 47,000-congregation denomination; abuse cases spanned multiple states.

## Actors

- **SBC Executive Committee**: the governing body found to have been primarily responsible for suppressing abuse disclosures; outsourced responses to outside legal counsel focused on liability avoidance.
- **Guidepost Solutions**: independent forensic investigation firm contracted to conduct the seven-month investigation.
- **Survivor advocates** (e.g., Jules Woodson, Rachael Denhollander, and others): pressed for independent investigation over years; many publicly named.
- **Russell Moore**: former head of the SBC's Ethics and Religious Liberty Commission; departed 2021 after protesting leadership's handling of abuse; his internal memos alleging a cover-up became part of the evidentiary record.
- **US Department of Justice**: opened an investigation in August 2022; status not fully resolved as of this note's date.

## Sequence of events

1. **Pre-2000s**: Abuse by clergy in affiliated churches documented; SBC's congregationalist polity used to deny denominational responsibility ("we can't discipline member churches").
2. **2000s–2010s**: Internal staff member compiled a list of accused clergy names. The list was kept secret from survivors and law enforcement. SBC outside counsel reportedly advised minimizing liability exposure.
3. **2019**: Houston Chronicle and San Antonio Express-News publish a joint investigation identifying 700+ victims and 380+ accused ministers over two decades, prompting internal SBC pressure.
4. **2021**: The SBC annual meeting votes to require an independent investigation; Russell Moore resigns from the Ethics and Religious Liberty Commission, citing leadership's hostility.
5. **2022-05-22**: Guidepost Solutions releases its 209-page report. Key findings:
   - SBC leadership "stonewalled and denigrated sex abuse survivors" for nearly 20 years.
   - Secret internal list contained 703 accused names; 409 believed SBC-affiliated at some point; 9 remained in active ministry.
   - Executive Committee was "singularly focused on avoiding liability."
6. **2022-05-27**: SBC releases the previously secret list of accused clergy.
7. **2022-06**: SBC annual meeting votes to create a tracking mechanism for accused clergy and launches a new oversight task force.
8. **2022-08**: Department of Justice opens investigation.

## Outcome / consequences

- The Guidepost report produced a significant, if contested, institutional reckoning within the SBC.
- SBC membership has declined throughout this period; the crisis accelerated departures.
- [[Russell Moore]] left for Christianity Today; his departure itself became evidence of institutional dysfunction.
- The DOJ investigation remains a live legal exposure.
- Reform measures adopted are viewed by survivors as insufficient; congregationalist polity — churches are formally autonomous from the convention — continues to limit enforcement mechanisms.

## Disputed facts

- The SBC contests the characterization that the Executive Committee deliberately "covered up" abuse as opposed to making policy errors based on polity interpretations.
- The number of abuse cases attributable to SBC-affiliated clergy vs. churches that later left the SBC is disputed.

## Open questions

- What is the status of the DOJ investigation as of 2025–26?
- Have any criminal prosecutions resulted from the list release?
- Has the SBC's tracking mechanism for accused clergy been effectively implemented?
