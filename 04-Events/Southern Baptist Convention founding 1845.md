# Southern Baptist Convention founding 1845

## Summary

In May 1845, roughly 293 delegates representing Baptist churches and missionary bodies of the slaveholding South met at the First Baptist Church of Augusta, Georgia, and organized a new denomination: the Southern Baptist Convention (SBC). The split was a direct consequence of the slavery question. Northern Baptists, increasingly influenced by abolitionism, had refused to appoint slaveholders as missionaries; Southern Baptists, who defended slavery as compatible with — even sanctioned by — scripture, broke away to form their own missionary and denominational structure. The SBC would grow into the largest Protestant denomination in the United States.

The event belongs in this vault as a foundational moment in the history of religiously framed American politics. The SBC was, quite literally, founded on the theological defense of slavery. That origin is not merely a historical embarrassment: it established a denomination whose self-understanding was bound up with regional, racial, and political identity, and which became a central institution of white Southern Protestantism. The vault tracks the long arc from this 1845 schism through the SBC's twentieth-century "conservative resurgence," its role in the [[Christian right]], and its place in contemporary debates over [[Christian nationalism]] — including the denomination's own 1995 formal apology for its pro-slavery origins and its more recent controversies over race and critical race theory.

## When & where

- **Date(s):** May 8–12, 1845.
- **Location:** First Baptist Church, Augusta, Georgia.
- **Duration:** A multi-day organizing convention.

## Actors

- **The Triennial Convention** (formally the General Missionary Convention of the Baptist Denomination in the United States) — the national Baptist body from which the Southern churches split.
- **American Baptist Home Mission Society** — the Northern-dominated mission body whose refusal to appoint slaveholders was a precipitating cause.
- **James E. Reeve** — the Georgia slaveholder whose proposed missionary appointment Georgia Baptists advanced as a deliberate test case; Northern Baptists declined to act on it.
- **William Bullein Johnson** (1782–1862) — South Carolina Baptist leader elected the SBC's first president.
- **The 293 delegates** — representatives of Southern Baptist churches, missionary societies, and seminaries who organized the new convention.

## Sequence of events

- `1844` — Tensions sharpen as Northern Baptists move against the appointment of slaveholding missionaries.
- `1844` — Georgia Baptists put forward James E. Reeve, a slaveholder, as a missionary candidate — an intentional test of the national body's position. The Home Mission Society's board declines to appoint him.
- `1845` (early) — Alabama Baptists separately demand assurance that slaveholders are eligible for appointment; the national board responds that it will not appoint anyone whose appointment would imply approval of slavery.
- `1845-05-08` to `1845-05-12` — Southern delegates convene at First Baptist Church, Augusta, Georgia, and formally organize the Southern Baptist Convention.
- `1845-05` — William Bullein Johnson elected the first president of the SBC.

## Outcome / consequences

- **Direct outcome:** The creation of a separate Southern Baptist denomination on an explicitly pro-slavery basis; Northern Baptist bodies continued as what is today the American Baptist Churches USA.
- **Downstream effects:** The SBC grew into the largest Protestant denomination in the United States. The 1845 schism is one of the pre-Civil-War denominational splits (alongside Methodist and Presbyterian divisions) that foreshadowed national rupture. The denomination's racial origins shaped its institutions for well over a century.
- **Later reckoning:** In 1995, on its 150th anniversary, the SBC adopted a formal resolution apologizing for its defense of slavery and for racism. Race-related controversy continued into the 2020s, including disputes over critical race theory.

## Disputed facts

- The relative weight of the Reeve test case versus the Alabama resolution as the "trigger" is a matter of historiographical emphasis, not fundamental dispute. `#claim/interpretive`
- Some twentieth-century SBC historians framed the 1845 split primarily as a dispute over mission-organization methods rather than slavery; modern scholarship treats slavery as the central and decisive cause. `#claim/disputed`
