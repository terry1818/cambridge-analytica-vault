# Allen v. Milligan (2023)

## Summary

*Allen v. Milligan*, 599 U.S. ___ (2023), decided June 8, 2023, is the Supreme Court ruling in which a 5–4 majority upheld the application of [[Voting Rights Act of 1965]] Section 2 to redistricting claims and affirmed a lower court's injunction requiring Alabama to draw a congressional map with a second majority-Black district. Chief Justice [[John Roberts]] — who had authored *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) and sided with the *Brnovich* (2021) majority — joined the four liberal justices to form the majority. The decision was widely noted as an unexpected limit on the post-*Shelby*/*Brnovich* erosion of voting rights protections, preserving the *Gingles* framework for Section 2 redistricting claims. Alabama subsequently drew a new map; the adequacy of that map was itself the subject of further litigation.

## When & Where

Decided June 8, 2023. The case arose from Alabama's 2021 post-Census congressional map, which contained one majority-Black district (District 7) despite Black voters constituting approximately 27% of the state's population. Plaintiffs — Black Alabama voters and advocacy organizations — argued a second majority-Black district was required under Section 2.

## Actors

- **Wes Allen (Alabama Secretary of State):** Named petitioner after predecessor John Merrill; defended the state's map.
- **Evan Milligan and other plaintiffs:** Black Alabama voters; respondents. NAACP Legal Defense Fund represented plaintiffs.
- **[[John Roberts]]:** Wrote the majority opinion. This was notable given Roberts' authorship of *Shelby County*.
- **Majority (5 justices):** Roberts, Jackson (joined in full), Kagan, Sotomayor, and Kavanaugh (joined in part, excluding Part III-B-1).
- **Dissent (4 justices):** Thomas (wrote dissent), Gorsuch, Barrett (in part), Alito (in part).

## Sequence of Events

1. Alabama draws 2021 congressional map with one majority-Black district.
2. Plaintiffs sue under Section 2, arguing the map dilutes Black voting power and that the state can draw a remedial map with two majority-Black or majority-Black-opportunity districts.
3. Three-judge district court panel unanimously enjoins use of the map (January 2022), finding plaintiffs substantially likely to succeed.
4. Alabama appeals to the Supreme Court, which stays the injunction and agrees to hear the case.
5. Supreme Court oral argument October 4, 2022.
6. June 8, 2023: Roberts writes for a 5–4 majority affirming the district court and upholding the injunction; the *Gingles* (1986) framework for Section 2 redistricting claims is preserved and reaffirmed.
7. Alabama draws a new map (2023) that again does not include a second majority-Black district; further litigation ensues.

## Key Legal Points

- The majority affirmed the three-part *Thornburg v. Gingles* (1986) framework for Section 2 redistricting claims, rejecting Alabama's argument that the framework should be modified.
- Roberts' opinion explicitly upheld the consideration of race in drawing remedial maps as required by Section 2.
- The majority did not address Alabama's argument that Section 2 redistricting claims are unconstitutional; it declined to reach that question.
- Justice Thomas's dissent argued the Court's *Gingles*-based Section 2 redistricting jurisprudence is itself unconstitutional as a racial classification.

## Outcome / Consequences

- Alabama was ordered to produce a remedial map. The state legislature's first remedial attempt — which plaintiffs and the district court found inadequate — led to further court proceedings.
- The ruling is significant for what it preserved: Section 2 redistricting claims remain viable, even as Section 5 preclearance is gone and Section 2 voting practice claims are heavily constrained under *Brnovich*.
- The 5–4 lineup — with Roberts in the majority — limited the scope for a maximally expansive Thomas dissent to become law in a near-term case.
- The case was closely watched by [[Convention of States Action]] and adjacent organizations tracking the Court's treatment of structural majoritarianism, given its implications for state legislative map constraints.

## Disputed Facts

- Whether Alabama's remedial maps complied with the Court's order is actively disputed in subsequent proceedings.
- The scope of the *Gingles* framework's application to future redistricting cases remains contested.

## Open Questions

- Will Alabama's non-compliance eventually force a court-drawn remedial map?
- Will the Roberts majority hold in future VRA Section 2 redistricting cases, or was this a one-off idiosyncratic alignment?
- Does the Thomas dissent's constitutional challenge to Section 2 redistricting claims foreshadow a future ruling?
