# Dillon v. Gloss (1921)

## Summary

*Dillon v. Gloss*, 256 U.S. 368 (1921), is a pivotal Article V precedent establishing that Congress may attach a time limit to a proposed constitutional amendment's ratification window, and that Article V implicitly requires ratifications to reflect a "contemporaneous consensus" — meaning all ratifying states must be acting within the same period of popular will. The decision is cited repeatedly in debates about the [[Article V Convention]] process because it exposes a tension: if ratification must be contemporaneous, what happens when state applications toward a convention threshold were filed over decades under entirely different political circumstances? Feingold and Prindiville invoke the "contemporaneous consensus" principle as one reason why the current accumulation of Article V applications — some dating to the 1970s — is constitutionally problematic.

## When & Where

Decided May 16, 1921, by the U.S. Supreme Court. The case arose under the Volstead Act (Prohibition enforcement), with the petitioner challenging his arrest on the grounds that the Eighteenth Amendment was not validly ratified.

## Actors

- **Dillon**: Arrested under the Volstead Act; challenged the validity of the Eighteenth Amendment's ratification.
- **Gloss**: Deputy Collector, federal officer.
- **Justice Willis Van Devanter**: Wrote the unanimous opinion.

## Sequence of Events

1. Congress proposed the Eighteenth Amendment with a seven-year ratification window embedded in the text.
2. The amendment was ratified by 46 states; Dillon was arrested for a Volstead Act violation.
3. Dillon challenged his conviction by arguing the amendment was improperly ratified — specifically that the seven-year time limit was unconstitutional.
4. The Supreme Court unanimously upheld the time limit and affirmed the conviction.

## Outcome / Consequences

The Court held on two points:
1. **Congress's power to set time limits**: Article V grants Congress latitude to specify a "reasonable time" for ratification, and seven years is reasonable.
2. **Contemporaneous consensus doctrine**: The Court stated that Article V implies ratifications must be "sufficiently contemporaneous in that number of states to reflect the will of the people in all sections at relatively the same period." This is often called the "contemporaneous consensus" requirement — the idea that an amendment must represent a unified moment of national agreement, not a slow accretion of state ratifications spanning very different political eras.

**Interaction with *Hawke v. Smith***: Taken together, *Hawke* and *Dillon* create a framework in which Article V operates as a specialized federal process, separate from ordinary democratic mechanisms, governed by implied temporal constraints.

**Partial supersession**: In [[Coleman v Miller 1939]], the Court partly retreated, holding that whether ratification occurred within a reasonable time was a political question for Congress, not the courts. This undermined *Dillon*'s implication that courts could police the timeliness requirement — but the contemporaneous-consensus *concept* retained rhetorical and scholarly force even after *Coleman*.

**Vault relevance**: The contemporaneous-consensus doctrine is directly relevant to the [[Article V aggregation problem]]. Many of the ~34 balanced-budget amendment applications from states were submitted between the 1970s and 2010s, under very different economic and political conditions. Critics argue that aggregating those applications violates the spirit of *Dillon*'s requirement that a convention threshold reflect a unified moment of national consensus.

## Disputed Facts

The case's holding is settled. The contested question is whether *Dillon*'s contemporaneous-consensus reasoning survived *Coleman v. Miller*. Constitutional law scholars disagree: some treat *Dillon* as still operative; others treat it as having been relegated to mere dicta by *Coleman*'s political-question holding.

## Open Questions

- After *Coleman v. Miller*, is the contemporaneous-consensus requirement a judicially enforceable rule or purely a political question for Congress?
- If Congress must judge contemporaneity, would a politically aligned Congress simply ignore the requirement?
- Does the *Dillon* principle apply to state *applications* for a convention (not just ratifications of an amendment), potentially invalidating decades-old BBA applications?
