# Brnovich v. Democratic National Committee (2021)

## Summary

*Brnovich v. Democratic National Committee*, 594 U.S. ___ (2021), decided July 1, 2021, is the Supreme Court case in which a 6–3 majority upheld two Arizona voting rules under the [[Voting Rights Act of 1965]] Section 2 and, in doing so, articulated new restrictive "guideposts" for how courts should evaluate Section 2 claims. The decision dramatically raised the bar for proving voting practice violations under Section 2 and, in the view of voting rights advocates, effectively rendered Section 2 challenges to voting rules largely unwinnable after *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) had already gutted Section 5. Justice Alito wrote for the majority; Justice Kagan wrote a notable dissent joined by Justices Breyer and Sotomayor.

## When & Where

Decided July 1, 2021. The case arose from Arizona, which had two voting practices challenged as racially discriminatory:
1. An out-of-precinct rule under which ballots cast in the wrong precinct are entirely discarded.
2. A ballot collection restriction (H.B. 2023) prohibiting third parties — other than mail carriers, election officials, family members, or caregivers — from collecting and returning early ballots.

## Actors

- **Mark Brnovich:** Arizona Attorney General who defended the restrictions.
- **Democratic National Committee:** Challenged the restrictions as violating Section 2.
- **Justice Samuel Alito:** Wrote the 6–3 majority opinion.
- **Majority (6 justices):** Alito, Roberts, Thomas, Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, Barrett.
- **Dissent (3 justices):** Kagan (wrote dissent), Breyer, Sotomayor.

## Sequence of Events

1. Arizona enacted H.B. 2023, restricting third-party ballot collection.
2. DNC sued, arguing both the out-of-precinct policy and H.B. 2023 disproportionately burdened minority voters and violated Section 2.
3. Ninth Circuit ruled for DNC, finding Section 2 violations; Arizona appealed.
4. Supreme Court granted certiorari; oral argument March 2021.
5. July 1, 2021: Alito writes 6–3 opinion reversing Ninth Circuit and upholding both rules. The majority creates five "guideposts" for Section 2 analysis.

## Key Legal Points

The Alito majority's five guideposts for Section 2 "totality of circumstances" analysis substantially raise the bar for plaintiffs:
1. The "size of the burden" imposed by the rule, with small burdens unlikely to violate Section 2.
2. The "degree to which a State's rules depart from the standard practices in place" at the time of the VRA's enactment.
3. The "size of any racial disparities."
4. The "total package" of a state's voting opportunities.
5. The "strength of the state interests served by the challenged rule."

Critics — including Justice Kagan's dissent — argued these guideposts had no basis in Section 2's text or legislative history and effectively rewrote the statute in a manner Congress never intended.

## Outcome / Consequences

- The immediate outcome upheld Arizona's restrictions; both rules remain in effect.
- The broader consequence is the narrowing of Section 2 nationally. Voting rights attorneys reported that following *Brnovich*, winning Section 2 voting practices challenges became extraordinarily difficult, and no successful major Section 2 voting practices case was won after the decision through 2026 (by Alito's own standard for measuring burden).
- *Brnovich* combined with *Shelby County* (2013) effectively created a condition in which: (a) covered jurisdictions no longer need preclearance (Shelby County), and (b) the Section 2 corrective remedy for discriminatory practices is now extremely hard to invoke (Brnovich).
- The decision is frequently cited alongside *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019) as part of a coherent federal-court withdrawal from voting-rights enforcement.

## Disputed Facts

- Whether H.B. 2023 was motivated by racial animus or partisan advantage (the record showed Republican officials were concerned about Democratic ballot collection operations, particularly in Native American communities).
- Whether the Alito guideposts fairly derive from Section 2's text or constitute judicial amendment of the statute.

## Open Questions

- Can any Section 2 voting practices challenge succeed under the *Brnovich* framework?
- Does *Brnovich* affect Section 2 redistricting challenges (as opposed to voting practice challenges) — the issue later addressed in [[Allen v. Milligan (2023)]]?
