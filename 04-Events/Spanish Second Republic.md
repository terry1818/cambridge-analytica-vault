## Summary

The Second Spanish Republic was proclaimed on 14 April 1931, following the departure of King Alfonso XIII. It was Spain's first fully democratic system, with universal suffrage (including women's suffrage from 1933), a new liberal constitution, separation of church and state, agrarian reform, and civil liberties. Within five years it had collapsed under the weight of intense polarization, reciprocal norm violations by both left and right, and a military coup that triggered a three-year civil war ending in Francisco Franco's dictatorship, which lasted until 1975.

**Vault relevance — comparative case:** Levitsky and Ziblatt use the Second Republic in *Tyranny of the Minority* (2023) as a case study in how democracy fails not through a single dramatic rupture but through the gradual breakdown of mutual restraint and institutional forbearance. Both the left (radical socialist factions) and the right (the CEDA, Falange, and ultimately the military) used democratic institutions as instruments to be exploited rather than norms to be preserved, creating escalating cycles of provocation and counter-provocation. The case maps onto Levitsky and Ziblatt's broader argument about US constitutional design and minority-faction capture: Spain's system lacked the procedural guardrails (coalition governments, robust party systems, executive-legislative balance) that might have contained extremist actors.

## When and where

- Proclaimed: 14 April 1931, Madrid
- Constitutional adoption: 9 December 1931
- First conservative-led period: 1933–1936 (the "bienio negro")
- Popular Front election victory: February 1936
- Military coup: 17–18 July 1936
- Civil War ends: 1 April 1939; Franco declares victory

## Actors

- **Manuel Azaña** — dominant Republican politician; prime minister 1931–33; president 1936–39; architect of reform agenda
- **Francisco Franco** — nationalist general; directed coup from Canary Islands; led Nationalist forces to victory; ruled Spain until 1975
- **CEDA** (Confederación Española de Derechas Autónomas) — mass conservative Catholic party; used constitutional means to obstruct reform; some historians argue it was itself a vehicle for anti-democratic ends
- **Falange Española** — Spanish fascist party, founded 1933 by José Antonio Primo de Rivera; smaller but provided street violence
- **Popular Front** — left-republican coalition that won February 1936 elections
- **Spanish Army officer corps** — monarchist and Catholic conservative; key base for the 1936 coup
- **Soviet Union / Nazi Germany / Fascist Italy** — foreign powers that intervened on opposing sides of the Civil War

## Sequence of events

1. **1931** — Republic proclaimed; reformist coalition takes power; new constitution adopted guaranteeing secular state, land reform, and civil liberties
2. **1932** — Sanjurjada (failed right-wing military coup attempt); demonstrates military's willingness to act against the republic
3. **1933** — Conservative coalition wins elections; reverses key reforms; two years of paralysis and backlash ("bienio negro")
4. **October 1934** — Left-wing uprising in Asturias crushed by army, including General Franco; deepens mutual distrust
5. **February 1936** — Popular Front wins narrow election victory; instability accelerates; political violence escalates on both sides
6. **July 1936** — Military coup launched from Spanish Morocco; coup partially fails (Republican forces hold Madrid, Barcelona) triggering full civil war rather than swift takeover
7. **1936–1939** — Civil War; Nazi Germany and Fascist Italy support Nationalists; Soviet Union supports Republic; ~500,000 estimated deaths
8. **April 1939** — Nationalist victory; Franco establishes dictatorship lasting 36 years

## Outcome and consequences

- Immediate: dictatorship, mass executions, and the repression of Spanish Republican culture, labor, and secularism for four decades.
- Analytical: the Republic's failure has been studied as a canonical case of democratic breakdown caused by: extreme ideological polarization; the weaponization of democratic institutions by actors unwilling to accept their terms; a Catholic Church and military establishment that placed their institutional interests above constitutional order; and foreign intervention that escalated the conflict beyond domestic resolution capacity.
- For Levitsky and Ziblatt: Spain exemplifies how democratic failure occurs not when rules are openly abolished but when key actors — elected politicians, parties, and military officers — collectively abandon the norms of mutual toleration and institutional restraint.

## Disputed facts

- Whether the CEDA was itself proto-fascist or merely conservative-authoritarian (debated among historians of Spain).
- The exact electoral arithmetic of February 1936 — some historians argue the Popular Front margin was narrower than long assumed.
- The extent to which Soviet arms and political influence distorted Republic strategy.

## Open questions

- How much does the Spanish case travel to contemporary US contexts with different constitutional structures? (Levitsky and Ziblatt's methodological question.)
- Whether the 1934 Asturias uprising represents a symmetrical failure of democratic norms by the left, or a proportionate response to provocation — a contested historiographical point.
