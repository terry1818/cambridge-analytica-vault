## Summary

From approximately 2012 to 2016, [[Peter Thiel]] secretly funded multiple lawsuits against Gawker Media, including the case brought by professional wrestler Hulk Hogan (Terry Gene Bollea) over Gawker's publication of a sex tape. Thiel's motive was retaliation for a 2007 Gawker article outing him as gay. He engaged attorney Charles Harder and spent approximately $10 million on litigation. The Hogan jury awarded $140 million in March 2016; Gawker filed for bankruptcy in June 2016 and was sold at auction. Thiel revealed his role to the New York Times in May 2016, framing it as a deliberate strategic campaign. The episode is the defining real-world example of the [[Plutocratic secession]] playbook applied to press suppression: using private capital to achieve through civil courts what censorship law could not.

## When & where

- **Trial**: St. Petersburg, Florida, Circuit Court (Bollea v. Gawker)
- **Gawker founding**: 2003, New York
- **Thiel outed by Gawker**: 2007
- **Thiel secretly begins funding lawsuits**: approximately 2012
- **Hogan trial verdict**: 18 March 2016 — $115M compensatory, $25M punitive
- **Gawker Chapter 11 filing**: 10 June 2016
- **Univision auction**: 16 August 2016 — $135M
- **Settlement with Hogan**: 2 November 2016 — $31M
- **Gawker.com shutdown**: August 2016 (never relaunched)

## Actors

- **[[Peter Thiel]]**: PayPal co-founder; secretly funded multiple Gawker lawsuits; self-described motivation was personal retaliation and broader campaign against what he characterized as a "journalistic terrorist" organization.
- **Terry Gene Bollea (Hulk Hogan)**: nominal plaintiff in the sex tape invasion-of-privacy case; received Thiel's funding through intermediaries; ultimately settled for $31M.
- **Charles Harder**: Los Angeles-based attorney engaged by Thiel to pursue Gawker litigation strategy; went on to represent other high-profile plaintiffs in media cases.
- **Nick Denton**: founder and CEO of Gawker Media; the primary target; Gawker had also published other material embarrassing to tech-billionaire class.
- **Univision Communications**: acquired Gawker Media's assets (excluding Gawker.com) for $135M at bankruptcy auction; did not revive Gawker.

## Sequence of events

1. **2007**: Gawker publishes article outing [[Peter Thiel]] as gay.
2. **~2012**: Thiel begins covertly funding a portfolio of lawsuits against Gawker, managed through Harder and an undisclosed litigation finance vehicle.
3. **2012**: Gawker publishes Hogan sex tape clip; Hogan sues for invasion of privacy.
4. **March 2016**: Florida jury awards Hogan $140M ($115M compensatory + $25M punitive).
5. **May 2016**: New York Times reports Thiel's role; Thiel publicly confirms, characterizing Gawker as a "bully" and the litigation as a "philanthropic" exercise.
6. **June 2016**: Gawker files Chapter 11 bankruptcy; unable to post appeal bond.
7. **August 2016**: Univision buys assets for $135M at auction; Gawker.com shut down.
8. **November 2016**: Hogan–Gawker $31M settlement reached.

## Outcome / consequences

- Gawker Media permanently destroyed as an institution; the flagship Gawker.com never relaunched.
- Nick Denton personally bankrupted.
- Thiel publicly declared the campaign successful and expressed no regret.
- The case ignited a national debate about litigation finance as a weapon of press suppression: critics argued that any sufficiently wealthy individual could bankrupt any media organization through litigation, regardless of the merits, if cases were chosen strategically.
- Raised structural concerns about the First Amendment's limits when circumvented through civil tort rather than prior restraint.
- Thiel's revelation linked the campaign directly to the tech-right's adversarial posture toward the legacy press; [[Blake Masters]], [[J.D. Vance]], and others in the Thiel orbit later similarly characterized mainstream media as an enemy.

## Disputed facts

- Whether Thiel's funding constituted legitimate litigation finance or improper third-party litigation funding that should be disclosed in court; legal standards at the time did not require disclosure.
- The sex tape publication: whether it met the "newsworthy" standard for First Amendment protection was genuinely contested; the jury found it did not.

## Open questions

- Did Thiel fund other undisclosed litigation campaigns against media organizations?
- What legal reforms, if any, should govern secret third-party litigation finance in media cases?
