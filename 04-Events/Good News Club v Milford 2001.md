## Summary

*Good News Club v. Milford Central School*, 533 U.S. 98 (2001), is a landmark US Supreme Court decision that dramatically expanded religious organizations' access to public school buildings. The Court ruled 6-3 that Milford Central School District in New York violated the First Amendment's Free Speech Clause when it denied the Good News Club — a Christian children's organization affiliated with Child Evangelism Fellowship — the use of school facilities for after-school meetings. Justice Clarence Thomas wrote the majority opinion, holding that the exclusion constituted viewpoint discrimination: the school allowed secular groups to discuss morals and character development but excluded the Good News Club for doing so from a religious perspective. Katherine Stewart's *The Good News Club: The Christian Right's Stealth Assault on America's Children* (2012) analyzes the decision as a legal architecture enabling the systematic placement of evangelical recruiting programs inside public elementary schools.

## When & where

- **Decision date**: June 11, 2001
- **Case citation**: 533 U.S. 98
- **Lower court**: US Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit had upheld the school's exclusion; SCOTUS reversed.
- **Originating school**: Milford Central School, Milford, New York.

## Actors

- **Good News Club / Child Evangelism Fellowship**: Petitioner; a private Christian organization operating after-school Bible clubs for children ages 6–12; targets elementary school children.
- **Stephen and Darleen Fournier**: District residents who applied to use school facilities for Good News Club meetings.
- **Milford Central School District**: Respondent; denied the application on grounds that the proposed activities (singing songs, hearing Bible lessons, memorizing scripture, praying) constituted religious worship prohibited by its community use policy.
- **Justice Clarence Thomas**: Wrote the majority opinion.
- **Justices Stevens, Souter, Ginsburg**: Dissented (partial or full); raised Establishment Clause concerns about the perception of school endorsement of religion.
- **Alliance Defending Freedom** (then Alliance Defense Fund): Filed amicus briefs; this case was part of ADF's broader litigation strategy to secure religious access to public school facilities.

## Sequence of events

1. **1996**: Milford Central School adopts a community use policy allowing residents to use school buildings for "instruction in education, learning, or the arts" and for "social, civic, recreational, and entertainment uses."
2. **1996**: The Fourniers submit a request for the Good News Club to hold weekly after-school meetings in the school cafeteria.
3. **1996**: Milford denies the request, ruling that singing songs, hearing Bible lessons, memorizing scripture, and praying constitute "religious worship" excluded by the policy.
4. **Lower courts**: Child Evangelism Fellowship sues; district court grants summary judgment for the school; Second Circuit affirms.
5. **2001-01**: SCOTUS hears oral argument.
6. **2001-06-11**: SCOTUS rules 6-3 for the Good News Club. Thomas majority: the school created a limited public forum and cannot exclude speech based on its religious viewpoint without violating the Free Speech Clause. The Establishment Clause does not require exclusion because allowing access does not constitute school endorsement.
7. **Post-decision**: Child Evangelism Fellowship rapidly expands Good News Club presence in public elementary schools nationwide.

## Outcome / consequences

- The ruling effectively allows religious groups — including evangelical recruiting organizations — to use public elementary schools as venues for after-school religious instruction, so long as the schools allow other community groups access.
- Child Evangelism Fellowship's Good News Club expanded from a relatively small presence to thousands of schools nationally after the ruling.
- Stewart (*The Good News Club*, 2012) documents the consequences: Good News Clubs are designed to be indistinguishable from the regular school environment, which Stewart argues creates de facto school endorsement for young children unable to distinguish after-school programs from school-sponsored activities.
- The decision is part of a broader legal project — executed through the [[Alliance Defending Freedom]] and related litigation shops — to secure maximum religious access to public education institutions.
- Combined with [[Released-time religious education]], the ruling creates a multi-pathway system for religious instruction within or immediately adjacent to public school settings.

## Disputed facts

- The majority held that no reasonable observer would perceive the Good News Club as school-endorsed; the dissenters argued that for young children, the distinction between school-time and after-school religious activities is not meaningful.
- Child Evangelism Fellowship describes its Good News Clubs as voluntary; critics argue the presence of clubs in school buildings, at the same time as school activities, creates implicit coercive pressure on children.
