## Summary

In 2006, Indiana Governor Mitch Daniels signed a ten-year, approximately $1.37 billion contract with IBM Corporation to privatize the state's welfare eligibility system. The goal was to replace face-to-face caseworker interactions with automated call centers and online application processing, with [[Affiliated Computer Services]] (ACS) as the technology subcontractor. The system was designed to apply algorithmic rules to food-stamp, Medicaid, and cash-assistance applications. By 2009, the project had generated a documented crisis: thousands of eligible applicants were losing benefits due to automated errors, documents were being lost, and the US Food and Nutrition Service threatened fines of $1–2 million for food-stamp miscalculations. Indiana terminated the contract and sued IBM. Virginia Eubanks documented the Indiana case as the opening chapter of *Automating Inequality* (2018), framing it as the clearest demonstration of her [[Digital poorhouse]] thesis — that automated systems applied to poor populations enact the same moral logic as 19th-century institutions, at unprecedented scale.

## When & where

- **Contract signed**: 2006
- **Contract cancelled**: 2009 (approximately 3 years into a planned 10-year term)
- **Jurisdiction**: State of Indiana; primary impact on food-stamp (SNAP), Medicaid, and Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF) applicants.

## Actors

- **Indiana FSSA (Family and Social Services Administration)**: State agency that contracted the system.
- **Governor Mitch Daniels (R)**: Champion of privatization; signed the contract; subsequently cancelled it.
- **IBM Corporation**: Prime contractor; responsible for overall system delivery.
- **[[Affiliated Computer Services]] (ACS)**: Technology subcontractor; operated the call centers and document processing.
- **US Food and Nutrition Service**: Federal oversight body that threatened fines for systematic food-stamp errors.
- **[[Virginia Eubanks]]**: Political scientist who documented the human impact and embedded the case within her digital-poorhouse framework.

## Sequence of events

1. **2006**: Indiana FSSA signs 10-year, ~$1.37 billion contract with IBM (prime) and ACS (subcontractor).
2. **2006–2009**: System operates; automated call centers replace county caseworkers; document scanning replaces face-to-face eligibility interviews.
3. **2008–2009**: Crisis: approximately 1 million benefit denials reported; documents routinely lost; food-stamp error rates prompt federal scrutiny.
4. **2009**: Governor Daniels cancels the contract, citing "a system plagued with problems." IBM and Indiana file mutual lawsuits.
5. **2017**: Indiana Supreme Court rules IBM breached the contract; court awards Indiana $78 million in damages.

## Outcome / consequences

- Approximately one million benefit actions (denials or terminations) affected Indiana welfare recipients during the automated period (Eubanks's estimate, drawn from FSSA data; contested by IBM).
- The automated system's defining feature: any error on an application — regardless of cause — was treated as "failure to cooperate" and generated automatic denial.
- The case became a landmark in scholarship on algorithmic governance, documented by Eubanks as the definitive US example of what she calls the [[Digital poorhouse]].
- Federal food-stamp fines of $1–2 million were imposed for systematic miscalculations.
- Conduent (the ACS successor) continued operating similar public-sector automation contracts in other states.

## Disputed facts

- Indiana officially characterized the failure as an implementation problem; IBM argued Indiana failed to meet its contractual obligations. The Indiana Supreme Court ruled for Indiana.
- The precise number of wrongfully denied benefits is contested; Eubanks uses FSSA data but Indiana did not conduct a systematic audit of wrongful denials.

## Open questions

- Did the Indiana failure generate any lasting changes to procurement standards for welfare automation at the federal level?
- Are similar "failure to cooperate" automated denial rules still embedded in current SNAP eligibility systems in other states?
- Does the Trump 2.0 administration's push for automated benefit verification replicate the Indiana model?
