# Shelby County v. Holder (2013)

## Summary

*Shelby County v. Holder*, 570 U.S. 529 (2013), is the Supreme Court decision that effectively ended the preclearance regime of the [[Voting Rights Act of 1965]]. In a 5–4 ruling authored by Chief Justice [[John Roberts]], the Court held that Section 4(b) of the VRA — the formula used to determine which jurisdictions were "covered" and therefore required to obtain preclearance from the Department of Justice or a federal court before implementing any changes to voting laws or procedures — was unconstitutional as applied. Because Section 5 (the preclearance requirement itself) has no operative effect without a constitutional coverage formula, the decision for practical purposes suspended the entire preclearance system. Within hours of the ruling, states previously covered under the VRA began implementing voting changes — including restrictive voter ID laws and polling place closures — that would previously have required federal approval.

## When & Where

Decided June 25, 2013. The case arose from Shelby County, Alabama, a covered jurisdiction that challenged the constitutionality of the reauthorized VRA's coverage formula. The District Court and D.C. Circuit had upheld the formula; the Supreme Court reversed.

## Actors

- **[[John Roberts]], Chief Justice:** Wrote the majority opinion. His opposition to VRA preclearance had a paper trail dating to the Reagan administration, where he wrote more than 25 memos opposing the 1982 VRA reauthorization.
- **Majority (5 justices):** Roberts, Scalia, Kennedy, Thomas, Alito.
- **Dissent (4 justices):** Ginsburg (wrote dissent), Breyer, Sotomayor, Kagan. Ginsburg's dissent is notable: "Throwing out preclearance when it has worked and is continuing to work to stop discriminatory changes is like throwing away your umbrella in a rainstorm because you are not getting wet."
- **Shelby County, Alabama:** A covered jurisdiction that initiated the constitutional challenge.
- **Eric Holder, U.S. Attorney General:** Represented the federal government defending the VRA's coverage formula.

## Sequence of Events

1. The VRA was reauthorized by Congress in 2006 with overwhelming bipartisan support (98–0 in the Senate, 390–33 in the House); coverage formula retained.
2. Shelby County, Alabama filed suit challenging the constitutionality of Sections 4(b) and 5.
3. The Supreme Court had previously expressed concerns about the coverage formula in *Northwest Austin Municipal Utility District No. 1 v. Holder* (2009) but declined to rule on constitutionality there.
4. June 25, 2013: Roberts writes for a 5–4 majority holding that Section 4(b) is unconstitutional because the coverage formula relies on data from 40+ years prior and is therefore no longer "rationally related" to current conditions. The Court did not strike Section 5 directly but held that Section 5 cannot operate without a constitutional Section 4(b).
5. Congress is invited to draft a new coverage formula; as of 2026, no new formula has been enacted.

## Outcome / Consequences

The immediate and well-documented consequences:

- Within **24 hours** of the ruling, Texas announced it would implement a strict voter ID law that had been blocked under preclearance. North Carolina and other previously covered states followed rapidly.
- The Brennan Center documented that states previously covered under the VRA closed more than **1,600 polling places** in the years following *Shelby County* — a rate far exceeding non-covered states.
- Redistricting maps that would previously have required DOJ preclearance could now be implemented without federal review. This directly interacted with the [[REDMAP]] maps being drawn in the same period.
- Congress has repeatedly introduced the John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act and related bills to create a new coverage formula, but none has passed as of 2026.

## Disputed Facts

- The majority's claim that discrimination had sufficiently receded to make the coverage formula obsolete was directly contested by Ginsburg's dissent, which cited extensive Congressional record-finding of continued discrimination in covered jurisdictions.
- Critics argue Roberts' "equal sovereignty" rationale — that it is unconstitutional to treat some states differently — was a novel doctrine not grounded in prior precedent.

## Open Questions

- Will Congress ever pass a new coverage formula? What would be the legal durability of such a formula given the current Court's composition?
- How many voting changes implemented in formerly covered jurisdictions since 2013 would have been blocked under preclearance?
- Does *Shelby County* interact with *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019) to create a zone of effective federal non-review for redistricting and voting changes?
