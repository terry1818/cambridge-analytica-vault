# Presidential Advisory Commission on Election Integrity

## Summary

The Presidential Advisory Commission on Election Integrity (PACEI) — widely called the "voter fraud commission" — was a federal advisory commission established by President [[Donald Trump]] via executive order on May 11, 2017, and disbanded on January 3, 2018. It was created against a specific factual backdrop: after losing the 2016 popular vote, Trump claimed without evidence that millions of people had voted illegally. The commission was, in the assessment of one of its own Democratic members, designed to manufacture legitimacy for that false claim. Chaired nominally by Vice President [[Mike Pence]] and run day-to-day by Kansas Secretary of State [[Kris Kobach]] as vice chair, it broke from the bipartisan tradition of past election commissions — Republicans held a 7–5 (originally 8–5) membership advantage. Its most consequential act, a June 2017 request for sweeping personal voter data from every state, triggered bipartisan refusal: 44 states and the District of Columbia declined to provide some or all of the requested information. The commission produced no findings of widespread fraud and was dissolved after roughly eight months.

## When & where

- **Established:** May 11, 2017, by executive order.
- **Dissolved:** January 3, 2018, by a second executive order.
- **Location:** Washington, D.C.; held public meetings (July 2017 in D.C.; September 2017 in New Hampshire).

## Actors

- [[Donald Trump]] — President; created the commission and supplied its premise (the false millions-of-illegal-votes claim).
- [[Mike Pence]] — Vice President; commission chair.
- [[Kris Kobach]] — Kansas Secretary of State; vice chair and de facto operational head; longtime promoter of restrictive voting measures and the Crosscheck program.
- [[Matthew Dunlap]] — Maine Secretary of State; Democratic commission member who later wrote that the commission appeared designed to legitimize, not test, Trump's fraud claims.

## Sequence of events

- `2016-11 to 2017` — Trump repeatedly claims, without evidence, that 3–5 million illegal votes cost him the popular vote.
- `2017-05-11` — Trump signs the executive order creating PACEI.
- `2017-06-28` — Kobach sends every state a request for detailed voter-roll data, including names, partial Social Security numbers, party affiliation, and voting history.
- `2017-07` — bipartisan backlash; 44 states plus D.C. decline to supply some or all data, citing privacy and state law; lawsuits (including by EPIC) challenge the data collection.
- `2017-07-19` — first commission meeting; discussion dominated by voter-fraud claims.
- `2017-09` — second meeting, in New Hampshire.
- `2017-11` — commission member Matthew Dunlap sues for access to the commission's own documents.
- `2018-01-03` — Trump dissolves the commission, citing state resistance; refers the matter to the Department of Homeland Security.

## Outcome / consequences

- **Direct outcome:** the commission produced no report and no evidence of widespread voter fraud; it was dissolved after about eight months.
- **Downstream effects:** documents later released to Dunlap showed no proof of meaningful fraud. The commission nonetheless normalized the "election integrity" label for a federal body premised on a false claim — branding that the post-2020 movement (see [[Election Integrity]] and the [[Election Denial Infrastructure]]) adopted wholesale.
- **Data-privacy fallout:** the mass voter-data request became a case study in the surveillance implications of centralized voter files.

## Disputed facts

- **Purpose:** the administration framed the commission as a neutral inquiry into election integrity; member Matthew Dunlap and outside critics concluded its purpose was to lend "false credence" to Trump's fraud claims. This vault treats the absence of fraud findings as established and the commission's stated neutral purpose as contested. `#claim/disputed`
- **Reason for dissolution:** the White House attributed dissolution to state non-cooperation; critics argued it was disbanded because it could not produce the fraud it was created to find. `#claim/disputed`
