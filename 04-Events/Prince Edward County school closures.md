## Summary

On 26 June 1959, Prince Edward County, Virginia, became the only jurisdiction in American history to close its entire public school system to avoid court-ordered racial integration — a position it held for five years. The county Board of Supervisors simply declined to appropriate any funds for the public school system after federal courts ordered integration following *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954). White children were provided education through the Prince Edward Academy, a private segregation school financed by state tuition grants and county tax credits. Black children — approximately 1,700 students — were left with no formal schooling at all, though community members created informal "training centers." The county finally reopened its schools on an integrated basis in September 1964 after the US Supreme Court ruled in *Griffin v. County School Board* that the tuition-grant scheme was unconstitutional.

Nancy MacLean's *Democracy in Chains* opens with this episode because it was while researching it that she encountered the work of James M. Buchanan, the Nobel Prize-winning economist at the University of Virginia who, with Warren Nutter, had published a 1959 paper on school vouchers. MacLean argues this paper provided intellectual cover for the privatization strategy. **Note:** This causal claim is contested by libertarian scholars; see Disputed Facts.

## When & Where

- **Schools closed:** June 26, 1959.
- **Schools reopened:** September 1964, under integrated operation.
- **Location:** Prince Edward County, Virginia (Farmville is the county seat).
- **Duration:** Five school years (1959–60 through 1963–64).

## Actors

- **Prince Edward County Board of Supervisors** — the local governing body that refused to fund public schools.
- **Virginia General Assembly** — enacted the Massive Resistance laws (1956) and later provided the tuition-grant mechanism for private segregation academies.
- **Prince Edward Academy** — the private whites-only school funded by state grants and county tax credits.
- **NAACP Legal Defense Fund** — litigated on behalf of Black students; the case was consolidated into *Griffin v. County School Board of Prince Edward County* (1964).
- **James M. Buchanan and Warren Nutter** — University of Virginia economists who published a 1959 paper on school vouchers; MacLean argues this provided intellectual framing for privatization as a desegregation workaround. (See Disputed Facts.)
- **US Supreme Court** — ruled in *Griffin* (1964) that closing public schools while funding private white schools violated equal protection.
- **Black community teachers** — organized informal learning centers in homes and churches during the closure.

## Sequence of Events

1. **1954:** *Brown v. Board of Education* (I and II) rules racial segregation in public schools unconstitutional.
2. **1956:** Virginia adopts "Massive Resistance" as official policy; enacts laws to close any school ordered to integrate.
3. **May 1, 1959:** Two federal courts order Prince Edward County to integrate its schools.
4. **June 26, 1959:** County Board of Supervisors refuses to fund public schools for the 1959–60 academic year; all public schools close.
5. **Fall 1959:** Prince Edward Academy opens using state tuition grants and county tax credits; enrolls white students only. Black community members begin organizing informal learning centers.
6. **1959–1963:** Black children largely denied formal education; some send children to relatives in other counties or states.
7. **1963:** President Kennedy administration and private foundations organize "free schools" for Black students (operating one year).
8. **May 25, 1964:** US Supreme Court rules in *Griffin v. County School Board* that the state tuition-grant scheme is unconstitutional.
9. **September 1964:** Public schools reopen on an integrated basis.

## Outcome / Consequences

- Approximately 1,700 Black children lost up to five years of formal schooling; documented long-term educational and economic impacts on the affected cohort.
- The episode became a landmark case in civil rights law (*Griffin v. County School Board*, 1964).
- MacLean uses it as the origin point for her argument that libertarian school-choice theory served as an intellectual instrument for segregation, connecting Buchanan's voucher work at the University of Virginia to the Koch network's later education-privatization agenda.
- The Prince Edward County closure is frequently cited in contemporary debates about school vouchers and public education privatization.

## Disputed Facts

- **MacLean's Buchanan connection:** MacLean argues that Buchanan's 1959 voucher paper provided intellectual cover for segregationist privatization. Libertarian scholars (Phillip Magness and others) dispute this, arguing MacLean misreads archival evidence and that Buchanan did not support segregation. This dispute is scholarly and unresolved; MacLean's causal claim should be treated as **Tier-2** (argued and contested) rather than established fact.
- Whether Buchanan attended meetings at which Prince Edward County's closure was strategized is disputed and has not been established in documents available to date.

## Open Questions

- The full financial flow of state tuition grants to Prince Edward Academy and its total enrollment during 1959–1964 has not been comprehensively compiled in a single public source.
- Long-term quantitative studies of educational and economic outcomes for the affected Black cohort remain incomplete.
- The degree to which the Prince Edward model influenced subsequent school-choice advocacy (as MacLean argues) or whether this is an overextended causal claim remains an open scholarly question.
