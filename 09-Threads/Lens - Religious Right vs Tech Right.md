# Lens - Religious Right vs Tech Right

## Pitch (one paragraph)

The modern conservative coalition fuses two wings whose ultimate visions are incompatible: a theocratic religious right that wants the state to enforce a Christian moral order, and a tech right that is libertarian, transhumanist, and indifferent or hostile to traditional piety. The alliance is not built on shared belief; it is built on shared enemies, shared money, and a shared willingness to weaken majoritarian checks. That makes it powerful in the short term and structurally unstable in the long term.

## Draft

**Two wings.** The **religious right** in its most assertive contemporary form — the [[New Apostolic Reformation]], dominionist and Seven Mountains theology, Christian-nationalist activism — wants government to actively enforce a Christian moral order: abortion bans, restrictions on LGBTQ life, Christian education, prayer in public institutions. The **tech right** — Peter Thiel, the venture-capital and crypto donor class, the "theo-bro"-adjacent but largely secular Silicon Valley reactionaries — is libertarian on personal morality, fascinated by exit, longevity, AI, and transhumanism, and contemptuous of the "woke" administrative state.

**What they share.** Four things hold them together. First, **shared enemies**: both treat universities, mainstream media, and the federal administrative bureaucracy as a hostile elite to be dismantled. Second, **money**: tech fortunes — Thiel's prominent among them — are an electoral lever conservatives cannot replace, and that financial dependency is a strong binding agent. Third, **anti-institutionalism**: both wings distrust the existing liberal-democratic institutional order, though for different reasons. Fourth, and most important for this vault, **a shared willingness to weaken majoritarian checks** — to entrench outcomes via courts, structural reform, and constitutional change (see [[COS as Constitutional Capture Vector]]) rather than win durable majorities.

**Where they clash.** The worldview gap is real and deep. Christian nationalists want federally mandated piety; the tech right's libertarianism rejects exactly that kind of moral coercion. Transhumanism — life extension, mind uploading, AI as a successor intelligence — is, from a Christian-dominionist standpoint, close to blasphemy. Religious-right figures can see plainly that "the tech guys are actually pretty damn godless." The tech right, in turn, has little use for biblical literalism. Their long-term utopias are not merely different; they are mutually exclusive.

**Bridge figures and instability.** The coalition is held together by bridge figures who personally embody both poles — most visibly [[J.D. Vance]], a Thiel-backed venture capitalist and Catholic convert who fuses tech money with religious conservatism. Such figures paper over the gap rather than resolving it. The analytical conclusion: the alliance is a marriage of convenience optimized for the destructive phase — dismantling regulatory states, capturing courts, weakening democratic checks — on which both wings agree. It is far less equipped for a constructive phase, because the two wings want to build incompatible things. The vault's working position: expect the coalition to remain effective at tearing down, and to fracture if it ever has to govern toward a positive vision.
