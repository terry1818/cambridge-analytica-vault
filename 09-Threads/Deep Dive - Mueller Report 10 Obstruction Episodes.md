# Deep Dive: Mueller Report 10 Obstruction Episodes

Volume II of the Special Counsel's Report on the Investigation Into Russian Interference in the 2016 Presidential Election (released March 2019, the "Mueller Report") documented **10 specific episodes of potentially obstructive conduct** by Donald Trump. Mueller's team explicitly stated they did not reach a conclusion on whether Trump committed obstruction-of-justice crimes, citing the [[OLC opinion]] that a sitting president cannot be indicted. Mueller wrote: "while this report does not conclude that the President committed a crime, it also does not exonerate him."

The 10 episodes are summarized below with the elements analyzed in Volume II: obstructive act, nexus to a proceeding, and intent.

## Background

Mueller analyzed each episode under three statutory elements common to obstruction offenses (18 USC Â§Â§ 1503, 1505, 1512):
1. **Obstructive act**: conduct that would naturally tend to obstruct
2. **Nexus to an official proceeding**: connection to a pending or foreseeable proceeding
3. **Intent**: corrupt purpose

For each episode, Mueller documented whether each element was supported by substantial evidence.

## Episode 1: Trump's conduct concerning the FBI investigation of Michael Flynn

**Period**: January-February 2017

**Conduct**: Trump asked Acting Attorney General Sally Yates about the Flynn investigation; on January 27, 2017 dined privately with FBI Director [[James Comey]] and reportedly demanded loyalty; on February 14, 2017 (the day after Flynn's resignation) cleared the Oval Office of all others including AG Sessions to speak privately with Comey, telling Comey: "I hope you can see your way clear to letting this go, to letting Flynn go."

**Evidence**: Comey's contemporaneous memos written immediately after each interaction; Comey's later sworn testimony to Senate Intelligence Committee June 2017.

**Mueller analysis**: Substantial evidence that Trump sought to "have the FBI Director take steps to end the criminal investigation." Obstructive act, nexus, and intent all supported by substantial evidence.

## Episode 2: Trump's reaction to Comey's March 20 2017 testimony

**Conduct**: After Comey publicly testified March 20, 2017 confirming the FBI was investigating Russia-Trump campaign coordination, Trump began an escalating series of efforts to obtain a public statement from Comey or DOJ that Trump personally was not under investigation.

**Evidence**: Multiple White House witness accounts; documented Trump calls and statements.

**Mueller analysis**: Pattern of pressure on FBI/DOJ leadership.

## Episode 3: Trump's firing of James Comey

**Period**: May 9, 2017

**Conduct**: Trump fired Comey. Initial White House and DOJ memos cited Comey's handling of the Clinton email investigation. Trump told NBC's Lester Holt two days later: "I was going to fire Comey... regardless of recommendation, I was going to fire him... in fact when I decided to just do it, I said to myself, I said you know, this Russia thing with Trump and Russia is a made-up story." Trump also told Russian officials in the Oval Office May 10: "I just fired the head of the F.B.I. He was crazy, a real nut job. I faced great pressure because of Russia. That's taken off."

**Evidence**: NBC interview transcript; White House notes of May 10 Russia meeting; congressional testimony from White House officials; Trump's contemporaneous statements.

**Mueller analysis**: Substantial evidence of all three elements. Comey's role in the Russia investigation was a "but-for" reason for the firing.

## Episode 4: Trump's efforts to remove Mueller

**Period**: June 2017

**Conduct**: After Mueller's May 17, 2017 appointment, Trump on June 17, 2017 instructed White House Counsel [[Don McGahn]] to call Deputy AG [[Rod Rosenstein]] and tell him Mueller had conflicts of interest and must be removed. McGahn refused and prepared to resign rather than comply.

**Evidence**: McGahn's grand jury testimony (multiple sessions); contemporaneous McGahn notes; testimony from other White House lawyers.

**Mueller analysis**: Substantial evidence of obstructive act and nexus. Trump's intent supported by Trump's own statements about Mueller and the investigation.

## Episode 5: Trump's efforts to curtail Mueller's investigation

**Period**: June 2017 forward

**Conduct**: Trump directed his former campaign manager Corey Lewandowski (then in private sector) to deliver a message to AG Sessions instructing Sessions to limit Mueller's investigation to only future election interference. The instruction was dictated by Trump and written down by Lewandowski. Lewandowski never delivered it.

**Evidence**: Lewandowski testimony; the dictated note; testimony from Trump's longtime aide Rob Porter.

**Mueller analysis**: Documented direction to limit the investigation's scope.

## Episode 6: Trump's directive to McGahn to deny he was told to fire Mueller

**Period**: January-February 2018

**Conduct**: After the New York Times reported January 25, 2018 that Trump had directed McGahn to fire Mueller (story sourced to McGahn's account), Trump pressured McGahn to publicly deny the story and to write a memo for the file denying it. McGahn refused.

**Evidence**: McGahn testimony; testimony from Staff Secretary Rob Porter and others; documentary record of communications.

**Mueller analysis**: Trump's efforts to have McGahn change his contemporaneous account of an interaction that had been characterized as obstructive.

## Episode 7: Trump's conduct toward Michael Flynn

**Period**: throughout

**Conduct**: After Flynn began cooperating with Mueller's office, Flynn's lawyers told the White House they could no longer share information; Trump's personal counsel left a voicemail communicating displeasure and seeking continued information about Flynn's cooperation. Earlier, Trump had asked Comey to "let it go" specifically regarding Flynn (Episode 1).

**Evidence**: Voicemail recording; Flynn cooperation record; communications between Flynn's counsel and Trump's counsel.

**Mueller analysis**: Pattern of conduct potentially designed to encourage Flynn not to cooperate.

## Episode 8: Trump's conduct toward Paul Manafort

**Period**: throughout

**Conduct**: Trump publicly praised Manafort during the investigation and made statements about pardons that prosecutors viewed as potentially encouraging Manafort not to cooperate. Trump told Manafort's family attorney "stay strong" and Manafort's defense team and the White House had a joint defense agreement.

**Evidence**: Trump public statements; communications with Manafort defense team; cooperation history.

**Mueller analysis**: Pattern of public pressure and signaling.

## Episode 9: Trump's conduct toward Michael Cohen

**Period**: 2017-2018

**Conduct**: After Cohen began cooperating, Trump's pattern of public statements shifted from defending Cohen to attacking him as a "weak person," a "Rat," and threatening Cohen's family members. Trump's attorney coordinated with Cohen's attorney on Cohen's earlier false congressional testimony about the Trump Tower Moscow project.

**Evidence**: Cohen testimony; communications between attorneys; Trump public statements pre- and post-Cohen cooperation.

**Mueller analysis**: Substantial evidence of pressure on a cooperating witness.

## Episode 10: Trump's outreach to other potential witnesses

**Period**: throughout

**Conduct**: Pattern of pardons or public discussion of pardons for witnesses; public statements characterizing cooperation as betrayal; threats to investigators' careers.

**Evidence**: Trump public statements; pardon discussions documented in White House sources.

**Mueller analysis**: Broader pattern of witness-tampering-adjacent conduct.

## Mueller's overall conclusion on obstruction

Mueller did not reach a conclusion on whether Trump committed obstruction crimes, citing the [[OLC opinion]] that a sitting President cannot be indicted. The Report states (Vol II, p. 8): "the evidence we obtained about the President's actions and intent presents difficult issues that prevent us from conclusively determining that no criminal conduct occurred. Accordingly, while this report does not conclude that the President committed a crime, it also does not exonerate him."

Mueller declined the option of a "prosecution memo" that would have explicitly stated obstruction was committed, on the ground that doing so would be unfair to a President who could not respond in a trial setting.

## Post-Report congressional and DOJ disposition

- AG [[William Barr]] released a March 24, 2019 letter summarizing Mueller's findings, including his own conclusion that the evidence "is not sufficient to establish that the President committed an obstruction-of-justice offense"
- The Mueller Report was released (with redactions) April 18, 2019
- Mueller publicly testified to House Judiciary and Intelligence Committees July 24, 2019
- 1,000+ former federal prosecutors signed a letter May 2019 stating that Trump's conduct, in their professional view, would have resulted in multiple felony obstruction charges if Trump were not President
- Trump was never charged with obstruction; the SCO referred several matters to other US Attorneys' offices; Trump's pardon of associates (Stone, Manafort, Flynn) further closed some downstream proceedings

## What the Mueller Report established (Tier 1)

- 10 episodes of conduct supported by substantial evidence under standard obstruction-statute elements
- Trump's intent to interfere with the investigation supported across multiple episodes
- Mueller's documented conclusion that the evidence did NOT exonerate Trump
- Subsequent professional consensus (1,000+ former federal prosecutors) that the conduct would warrant charges if Trump were a private citizen

## What was not resolved

- Whether a jury would convict on any specific episode (no trial)
- Whether the OLC opinion's no-indictment-of-sitting-president rule will continue
- Whether any of the episodes could form the basis of a future prosecution post-presidency (statutes of limitations)
