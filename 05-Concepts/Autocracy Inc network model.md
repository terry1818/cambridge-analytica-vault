# Autocracy Inc Network Model

## Definition

Anne Applebaum's central analytical thesis in *Autocracy, Inc.* (Doubleday, 2024): contemporary authoritarian regimes do not cohere as an ideological bloc in the manner of the Soviet-led Communist International. Instead, they operate as an *agglomeration* — a loose, transactional network of mutual benefit analogous to a conglomerate of companies. Relations are based on interest, not shared doctrine, which is precisely why they can collaborate across vast ideological, religious, cultural, and geographic differences (Russia, China, Iran, Venezuela, Belarus, Saudi Arabia, UAE, and others).

## Origin / History

The model emerged from Applebaum's reporting and research for *Autocracy, Inc.* (2024), building on her earlier work on Eastern European authoritarianism and on the broader literature of "illiberal internationalism." The book was recognized as one of *The Economist*'s Books of the Year for 2024. It synthesizes trends identified across investigative journalism, scholarship on authoritarian diffusion, and international relations research on great-power competition.

## How It Works

**What autocracies share and exchange**:

- **Financial infrastructure**: Shared offshore banking, investment channels, mutual capital flows that protect elites from Western sanctions and accountability.
- **Surveillance technology**: China exports surveillance hardware and software to autocratic partners; shared repression tools diffuse rapidly.
- **Disinformation expertise**: Russia exports information warfare know-how; mercenary disinformation services operate across regimes.
- **Military hardware and personnel**: Iran provides drones and weapons; Russia provides mercenaries (Wagner/Africa Corps); smaller regimes provide staging grounds.
- **Diplomatic cover**: Mutual UN vetoes and support at international bodies; refusal to extradite; joint opposition to ICC investigations.
- **Propaganda and legitimation**: Shared media amplification networks, mutual legitimation visits, and support for each other's electoral processes.

**What they do NOT share**:

- A common ideology. Russia promotes Orthodox nationalism; China promotes economic development authoritarianism; Iran promotes Shia theocracy; Saudi Arabia promotes Sunni conservatism. These are mutually incompatible doctrines.
- A formal command structure or alliance with binding obligations.

The network's logic is purely transactional: cooperation serves each party's interest in regime survival and wealth protection.

## Application in This Domain

This framework allows the vault to interpret relationships between US-based political actors and foreign autocratic networks without requiring proof of formal coordination. The question is not "Is there a conspiracy?" but "Are there transactional relationships that serve mutual interests in reducing democratic accountability?" Gulf capital flows into Trump family businesses, Russian-adjacent actors in the Mercer network, Emirati lobbying relationships — these can be mapped against the network model as evidence of the transactional layer that Applebaum describes. The model also reframes the question of why multiple autocracies simultaneously appear to benefit from reduced US democratic capacity: they need not coordinate for alignment to emerge from shared interest.

## Examples

- China-Russia: No formal alliance but deepening economic, military, and diplomatic partnership post-2022 based on mutual interest in undermining US-led order.
- Russia-Iran: Russian drones supplied to Iran; Iranian drones to Russia for Ukraine war — with no shared ideology.
- Russia-Belarus: Mutual survival arrangement — Russia protects Lukashenko; Belarus provides staging ground.
- UAE-Russia: Financial flows maintaining despite Western sanctions; UAE serving as a sanctions-avoidance hub for Russian capital.

## Effectiveness / Critique

- **"Network" vs. "bloc" distinction useful**: The core insight — that shared interest, not ideology, binds the network — is well-grounded and supported by investigative reporting.
- **Overstates coherence**: Some international relations scholars argue Applebaum underestimates genuine tensions within the network (China-Russia competition in Central Asia; Saudi-Iran hostility) and that calling it a "network" implies more coordination than exists.
- **Underweights democratic culpability**: Critics argue the framework lets Western financial systems, arms dealers, and lobbying industries off the hook by framing democracies as purely passive targets.
- **Policy implication embedded**: The model implies democracies need their own solidarity network to counter Autocracy Inc. — some scholars see this as a useful policy prescription, others as ideologically loaded.
