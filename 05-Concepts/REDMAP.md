# REDMAP — the Republican Redistricting Majority Project

## Definition

REDMAP (Redistricting Majority Project) was a coordinated, multi-cycle electoral strategy conceived and executed by the [[Republican State Leadership Committee]] (RSLC) to win Republican majorities in targeted state legislatures in the 2010 midterm cycle — the last election before the post-Census redistricting round — so that Republicans would control the redrawing of both congressional and state legislative maps for the decade 2011–2020.

## Origin / History

The concept emerged from a strategic insight recognized by RSLC executive director [[Chris Jankowski]] and RSLC chairman [[Ed Gillespie]] around 2009–2010: control of a state legislature translates directly into control of that state's congressional map. A New York Times article in summer 2009 about Republican paths back to power prompted Jankowski to formalize the redistricting opportunity. With the 2010 Census redistricting cycle approaching, the window was unusually clear.

Gillespie and Jankowski reoriented the RSLC — previously a sleepy vehicle for modest state-level contributions — into a well-funded precision operation. The project was publicly named "REDMAP" and operated as a project of the RSLC.

## How it Works

REDMAP operated in two phases:

**Phase 1 — 2010 state legislature targeting.** RSLC researchers identified chambers where Republicans were close to majority control. They concentrated roughly $30 million in targeted spending on 107 state legislative races in 16 states, focusing on swing states with large congressional delegations: Pennsylvania, Ohio, Michigan, Wisconsin, North Carolina, and Florida. The investments were micro-targeted to races where small spending margins could tip the balance.

**Phase 2 — Map drawing.** After the 2010 wave gave Republicans control of the targeted chambers, Republican mapmakers — most notably [[Thomas Hofeller]] — drew congressional and state legislative maps that packed Democratic voters into as few districts as possible and distributed Republican voters for maximum efficiency. The maps were engineered to be durable across multiple election cycles.

## Application in this Domain

REDMAP is a direct structural enabler of the [[Hypothesis - COS is the permanent capture arm of Project 2025]] thesis. The three-wing coalition's constitutional-capture arm depends on Republican control of state legislatures. REDMAP is the mechanism that produced and sustained that control in the critical 2010–2020 decade. The 2011 maps drawn after REDMAP's success produced an immediate structural advantage: in the 2012 Congressional elections, Republican candidates collectively received approximately 1.4 million fewer votes nationally than Democratic candidates yet retained a 33-seat House majority.

The RSLC's successor effort, REDMAP 2020, aimed to replicate the strategy for the 2020 Census cycle. The [[National Democratic Redistricting Committee]] (NDRC), chaired by Eric Holder, was established in 2017 as a direct Democratic counter-response.

## Examples

- **Pennsylvania 2010:** Republicans gained the state legislature; the resulting 2011 congressional map was among the most extreme gerrymanders in the country, eventually struck down by the Pennsylvania Supreme Court in 2018.
- **North Carolina 2010:** Republicans took both chambers; [[Thomas Hofeller]] drew maps later found to be racial and partisan gerrymanders in multiple court challenges.
- **Wisconsin 2010:** Republicans drew the WISCONSIN Act 43 legislative maps; the resulting advantage persisted through multiple electoral cycles despite Democrats winning statewide offices.

## Effectiveness / Critique

REDMAP's effectiveness as pure electoral strategy is well-documented and essentially uncontested. The 2016 book *Ratf**ked* by David Daley is the definitive account. Critics note that REDMAP exploited a structural feature of American democracy — that state legislatures draw their own maps — to entrench minority-rule dynamics at the federal level. The strategy's durability was subsequently limited by two developments: the Pennsylvania Supreme Court's 2018 intervention, and the Supreme Court's ruling in [[Rucho v. Common Cause (2019)]] that federal courts may not police partisan gerrymanders, which paradoxically insulated REDMAP-era maps from federal challenge while also closing the federal door for Democrats seeking relief.

The strategy is now widely studied as a model and has been replicated or attempted by both parties at the state level where they control legislatures.
