# Democratic backsliding

## Definition

Democratic backsliding is the gradual, incremental erosion of democratic institutions, rules, and norms — typically carried out by elected leaders working within, and exploiting, the legal system itself. It is defined by contrast with the older model of democratic *breakdown*: there is no tank in the street, no single coup, no abrupt seizure. Instead democracy is hollowed out through "a discontinuous series of incremental actions" — packing courts, capturing referees, rewriting electoral rules, delegitimizing opponents and the press — each step individually defensible, the cumulative trajectory anti-democratic. The phenomenon is sometimes called "soft" or "stealth" authoritarianization precisely because it preserves democratic forms (elections still happen) while draining their substance (the outcomes are increasingly pre-determined).

## Origin / history

The concept matured in comparative political science in the 2000s and 2010s as scholars studied Hungary under Orbán, Poland, Turkey, Venezuela, and others — cases where elected leaders dismantled checks rather than abolishing elections. Nancy Bermeo's influential 2016 essay "On Democratic Backsliding" formalized the distinction from classic breakdown. The term reached a mass audience with Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt's *How Democracies Die* (2018), which argued that modern democracies more often die at the hands of elected leaders than of generals. The 2021 *American Political Science Review* article "Laboratories of Democratic Backsliding" extended the analysis to U.S. states, arguing backsliding can occur subnationally before it appears at the national level.

## How it works

Backsliding proceeds through identifiable moves. Levitsky and Ziblatt offer a four-part "litmus test" of authoritarian behavior: (1) rejection of, or weak commitment to, democratic rules of the game; (2) denial of the legitimacy of political opponents; (3) toleration or encouragement of violence; and (4) readiness to curtail the civil liberties of opponents, including the media. Mechanically, backsliding leaders capture the institutional "referees" (courts, election administrators, inspectors general), tilt the playing field (gerrymandering, restrictive voting laws, media concentration), and erode the unwritten norms of mutual toleration and institutional forbearance that formal rules cannot enforce. Each step is defended as legal or as a response to a manufactured threat.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed effectiveness:** as an analytic frame, backsliding successfully captures cases that the older coup/breakdown model missed.
- **Empirical evidence:** indices such as V-Dem and Freedom House have recorded a sustained global decline in democratic quality since roughly 2006, including U.S. backsliding indicators; the U.S. case is debated in degree but widely cited.
- **Academic critique:** scholars dispute thresholds — when does "polarization" become "backsliding"? Some warn the term is over-applied or used as a partisan cudgel. Wolkenstein (2023) and others have pressed for tighter conceptualization. This vault uses backsliding as a structured analytic lens, not a verdict, and flags contested applications. `#claim/disputed`
