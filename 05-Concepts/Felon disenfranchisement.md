## Definition

Felon disenfranchisement refers to the body of state and federal laws that remove or restrict the right to vote from individuals convicted of felony offenses. In the United States, these laws vary dramatically by state: two states (Maine and Vermont) allow incarcerated people to vote; most restore rights at some point after sentence completion; and some states impose permanent or near-permanent disenfranchisement requiring individual petition for restoration. The practice is distinct from the incarceration itself and can extend for years, decades, or a lifetime after release.

## Origin / history

**Common law roots**: The concept derives from the English doctrine of "civil death," under which serious criminal conviction stripped an individual of civil rights including property ownership and the right to sue.

**Post-Reconstruction surge**: Most modern US felon disenfranchisement laws were adopted or significantly strengthened in the 1860s and 1870s — precisely the period when the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments were extending voting rights to Black men. Scholars have documented that Southern states crafted felony definitions and disenfranchisement provisions to target conduct disproportionately associated with Black people (vagrancy, petty theft, minor breach-of-peace offenses), functioning as a replacement mechanism for the direct racial exclusions the amendments prohibited.

**Constitutional framework**: In *Richardson v. Ramirez* (1974), the Supreme Court interpreted Section 2 of the Fourteenth Amendment — which mentions "participation in rebellion, or other crime" as grounds for limiting suffrage — as affirmatively permitting states to disenfranchise people with criminal convictions. This ruling removed federal constitutional barriers and left the law entirely to the states.

## How it works

The scope of disenfranchisement varies along several axes:

| Stage | States/jurisdictions |
|-------|---------------------|
| Incarceration only | ~2 states; voting rights restored upon release |
| Incarceration + parole | ~16 states |
| Incarceration + parole + probation | ~19 states |
| Post-sentence waiting periods or individual restoration | ~9 states |
| Lifetime disenfranchisement for some/all felonies | ~4 states (as of 2023, reduced from a higher number) |
| No disenfranchisement (including while incarcerated) | Maine, Vermont, DC, Puerto Rico |

People in "pending" restoration status who do not know their rights are often effectively disenfranchised even where restoration is legally available, due to lack of notification and bureaucratic barriers.

## Application in this domain

Carol Anderson documents felon disenfranchisement as a structural voter-suppression mechanism inseparable from the racial architecture of the criminal justice system. Because Black Americans are incarcerated at approximately five times the rate of white Americans — a disparity rooted in discriminatory enforcement, sentencing, and policing practices — felon disenfranchisement laws have a massively disproportionate racial effect even when facially race-neutral.

The Sentencing Project estimated that as of 2020, approximately **4.6 million Americans** were disenfranchised due to a felony conviction; of those, approximately **2 in 5** had completed their sentence entirely, meaning they were disenfranchised in community — not incarcerated. An estimated 1 in 16 Black adults of voting age is disenfranchised, compared to 1 in 59 non-Black adults.

## Examples

- **Florida (pre-2018)**: Among the most restrictive states; permanent disenfranchisement required gubernatorial clemency. Approximately 1.5 million Floridians were disenfranchised. In 2018 voters approved Amendment 4 to restore rights automatically upon sentence completion (excluding murder and sexual offenses), but the Republican legislature subsequently added a requirement to pay all fines and fees before restoration — effectively a poll tax for the poor.
- **Iowa**: Until 2020 the only state requiring individual gubernatorial pardon for all felony offenses; Governor Reynolds restored automatic restoration by executive order in 2020.
- **Virginia**: Historically required individual petition; Governor Terry McAuliffe restored rights by executive order for over 200,000 people (2016).

## Effectiveness / critique

**Stated rationale**: Preserving electoral integrity; ensuring civic engagement is limited to those who have demonstrated law-abiding citizenship.

**Critique**:
- The laws have documented racial origins and racially disparate effects; their application through a racially biased criminal justice system amplifies racial exclusion.
- No empirical evidence that disenfranchisement reduces recidivism or improves public safety; some studies suggest restoration correlates with lower recidivism.
- The US is an international outlier: most democracies restore voting rights automatically upon release, if not sooner.
- The fees-and-fines restoration condition (as in Florida post-Amendment 4) has been characterized by federal courts as an unconstitutional poll tax (though the 11th Circuit ultimately reversed district court holdings on this).
