## Definition

Persuasive technology (also called "captology" — Computers As Persuasive Technologies) is the study and practice of designing interactive technologies to change human attitudes, behaviors, or both, without coercion and without deception in Fogg's original formulation. The term and field were pioneered by [[B.J. Fogg]] at the [[Stanford Persuasive Technology Lab]] beginning in the late 1990s. In practice, the field's frameworks have been applied by Silicon Valley companies to maximize user engagement, time-on-platform, and emotional investment — outcomes that serve commercial advertising models but that critics argue cross into manipulation and addiction.

## Origin / history

- **1996**: Fogg coins "captology" from Computers As Persuasive Technologies while a doctoral student at Stanford.
- **1998**: Stanford Persuasive Technology Lab founded.
- **2003**: Fogg publishes *Persuasive Technology: Using Computers to Change What We Think and Do* — the founding text.
- **2009**: Fogg Behavior Model (B=MAP) formally published; rapidly adopted in product design.
- **2010s**: Silicon Valley companies apply persuasive design at scale — variable reward schedules (slot-machine mechanics), social validation signals (likes, comments), notification timing designed to maximize return visits.
- **2017**: [[Sean Parker]]'s public remarks describe the "social validation feedback loop" — a lay articulation of persuasive technology at Facebook scale.

## How it works

The Fogg Behavior Model holds that a target behavior occurs when three elements converge simultaneously:

- **Motivation** (desire to do the behavior)
- **Ability** (ease of performing it)
- **Prompt** (a trigger at the right moment)

Designers can influence behavior by increasing motivation (making the outcome more rewarding), increasing ability (making the action frictionless), and timing prompts optimally (notifications, visual cues). Social media platforms deploy all three simultaneously: variable reward schedules (unpredictable likes) maximize motivation; one-tap engagement maximizes ability; push notifications serve as continuous prompts.

The persuasive technology toolkit includes:
- **Variable reward schedules** (from behavioral psychology / operant conditioning): unpredictable rewards are more reinforcing than predictable ones.
- **Social proof**: showing user counts, like tallies, follower numbers.
- **Reciprocity triggers**: notifications of others' actions create felt obligation to respond.
- **Commitment and consistency**: small initial commitments (profile completion, first post) escalate engagement.

## Application in this domain

The relevance for this vault is structural: persuasive technology is the design layer that made [[Facebook]] and other platforms effective attention-capture machines, and it is that attention-capture capacity that made them valuable for political micro-targeting. [[Cambridge Analytica]]'s model depended on Facebook users spending large amounts of time on platform, generating rich behavioral data. The [[Facebook Exchange (FBX)]] and [[Custom and Lookalike Audiences]] tools operated on top of an audience that persuasive design had already rendered highly engaged and behaviorally legible.

[[Siva Vaidhyanathan]]'s *Antisocial Media* and Roger McNamee's *Zucked* both treat persuasive technology as a root cause of Facebook's democratic harms: the design that maximizes engagement also maximizes emotional provocation, filter-bubble reinforcement, and susceptibility to disinformation.

## Examples

- Facebook's "Like" button: a variable reward mechanism; users check for likes, creating compulsive return behavior.
- Instagram notification design: timed to create anticipation and reward cycles.
- Autoplay on YouTube: removes friction (increases ability) for continued consumption of progressively more engaging — and potentially more extreme — content.
- LinkedIn endorsements and skill notifications: reciprocity triggers that drive return visits.

## Effectiveness / critique

- Effectiveness as an engagement maximizer is well-documented — time-on-platform metrics for social media platforms increased dramatically with the deployment of persuasive design features.
- Ethical critique focuses on the gap between Fogg's original framing (no coercion, no deception) and actual commercial deployment, which critics characterize as coercive and deceptive at scale.
- The persuasive technology framing has informed regulatory discussions about "addictive design" in proposed legislation in the U.S. and EU.
