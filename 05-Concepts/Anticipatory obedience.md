# Anticipatory obedience

## Definition

Anticipatory obedience is the practice of conforming to what an authoritarian power is expected to want *before* being commanded to do so. Rather than waiting for explicit orders, individuals, corporations, or institutions predict what the regime will require and voluntarily comply in advance. The effect is to teach power what it can extract from society without cost.

## Origin / history

[[Timothy Snyder]] formulated the concept in *On Tyranny: Twenty Lessons from the Twentieth Century* (2017), opening the book with the injunction "Do not obey in advance." He grounded it in twentieth-century European history, particularly the German annexation of Austria in 1938. Austrian officials, police, and civilians immediately acted on what they assumed the Nazi occupiers would want regarding Jews — often going further than the Nazis had yet demanded. Snyder writes that the "anticipatory obedience of Austrians taught the high Nazi leadership what was possible." The concept synthesises observations from scholars including [[Hannah Arendt]] on bureaucratic complicity and Robert Paxton on the [[Five-stage model of fascism (Paxton)]].

## How it works

The mechanism operates in three phases:

1. **Threat perception** — An actor reads ambient signals of authoritarian power (executive threats, purges of rivals, informal signals) and concludes that resistance will be costly.
2. **Pre-compliance** — The actor aligns its behaviour, rhetoric, or policy with the inferred preference — without being asked, often without a direct threat.
3. **Ratchet effect** — The authoritarian observes compliance and treats it as evidence of what is possible, emboldening further demands. Anticipatory obedience thus expands the Overton window of authoritarian action.

The mechanism is self-reinforcing: as compliance becomes widespread, the social norm against obeying shifts, and each new act of pre-compliance raises the baseline expectation for everyone else.

## Application in this domain

The vault can use anticipatory obedience as an analytic lens to assess institutional behaviour in response to the Trump 2.0 administration (2025–). Relevant questions: Did media organisations, universities, law firms, and corporations modify their behaviour before receiving explicit legal threats? Does the pattern of corporate pledges, editorial self-censorship, and DEI rollbacks match the anticipatory obedience template? The concept sits alongside [[Normalization (fascist politics)]] and [[Conservative complicity thesis]] in explaining how authoritarian power expands without always using direct coercion.

## Examples

- **Austria 1938**: Austrian bureaucrats and civilians implemented anti-Jewish measures before German administrators formalised them.
- **US corporate sector 2025**: Multiple major law firms reached pre-emptive accommodations with the Trump administration in response to threatened executive orders, before cases were filed (documented; see reporting by *The New York Times* and *The Guardian*, 2025).
- **University administrators**: Several institutions pre-emptively restricted DEI programs ahead of federal enforcement actions.
- **Media self-censorship**: Editorial decisions to soften critical coverage in anticipation of White House retaliation.

## Effectiveness / critique

Snyder's formulation is analytically powerful but has attracted two critiques. First, it may underweight rational risk calculation — actors obeying in advance may be engaging in strategic cost-benefit reasoning rather than psychological conformity, which has different remedies. Second, distinguishing anticipatory obedience from ordinary political prudence is methodologically difficult: how do researchers determine that compliance was anticipatory rather than merely responsive? Some political scientists (e.g., Dahl's tradition on "rule of anticipated reactions") would argue the phenomenon is older and less distinctively tied to authoritarian contexts than Snyder implies. The concept is most diagnostically useful when compliance *precedes* any concrete threat, where there is a clear ratchet dynamic, and where the compliance normalises further demands.
