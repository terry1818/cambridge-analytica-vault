## Definition

Militant evangelical masculinity is the theological-cultural model that frames Christian manhood as inherently warrior-like: strong, dominant, protector of family and nation, and spiritually authorized to exercise hierarchical authority over women, children, and lesser men. The term captures a specific tradition within white American evangelicalism that treats masculine aggression, combat readiness, and patriarchal authority not as departures from Christian ethics but as their highest expression. Kristin Kobes Du Mez's *Jesus and John Wayne* (2020) is the definitive scholarly and journalistic treatment of this tradition.

## Origin / History

Du Mez traces the genealogy across several phases:

**1. Muscular Christianity (late 19th–early 20th century)**: A reaction against perceived feminization of Victorian Christianity; men like Theodore Roosevelt and organizations like the YMCA rebranded Christianity as vigorous, masculine, and imperialist. Jesus was recast as a strong man, not a meek one.

**2. Cold War evangelical masculinity (1940s–1960s)**: Billy Graham's model of Christian manhood was shaped by Cold War anxieties; the Christian man was a bulwark against godless communism, the provider and protector of family against spiritual and political threat.

**3. The 1970s backlash**: Second-wave feminism triggered an intense reactive push within evangelicalism to reassert male headship in family and church. Francis Schaeffer's cultural critique provided intellectual framing; popular writers like James Dobson (*Dare to Discipline*, 1970) codified patriarchal child-rearing and family structure.

**4. The Edwin Cole / Promise Keepers moment (1977–late 1990s)**: [[Christian Men's Network]] founder Edwin Cole systematized the "real manhood = Christlikeness" equation; [[Promise Keepers]] mobilized millions of men around promises of sexual purity and family headship while simultaneously presenting Christianity as muscular and aggressive.

**5. Post-9/11 warrior faith (2001–2014)**: The War on Terror provided a new militarized template. Mark Driscoll at [[Mars Hill Church]] preached a Jesus who "could have called down legions of angels" and framed pacifist Christianity as effeminate. The special forces soldier became an evangelical masculine ideal. Authors like John Eldredge (*Wild at Heart*, 2001) reached mass audiences with the claim that every man has a warrior heart given by God.

**6. Trump-era consolidation (2015–present)**: Du Mez's central argument is that evangelical support for Donald Trump — whose personal conduct violated almost every stated evangelical moral standard — is explained by the preceding decades of militant masculinity culture. Trump embodied the dominant male protector archetype the culture had been constructing for forty years, regardless of his personal piety or lack thereof.

## How It Works

The ideology operates through several mechanisms:

- **Theological naturalization**: Masculine dominance is framed as God-ordained; submission to male authority is framed as obedience to God
- **Enemy construction**: Cultural feminism, LGBTQ acceptance, and progressive Christianity are presented as existential threats requiring masculine defense
- **Media and cultural products**: Novels (Left Behind series), music (Christian rock with martial imagery), film (firearm culture + faith crossovers), and celebrity pastors construct and reinforce the warrior-faith aesthetic
- **Institutional embedding**: Churches, men's ministries, homeschool curricula, and Christian schools transmit the model to successive generations

## Application in This Domain

Within the theocratic-wing research frame, militant evangelical masculinity is relevant as:

1. A cultural substrate that made the MAGA coalition possible: white evangelical men were primed by decades of warrior-faith culture to respond to Trump's dominant-male-protector persona
2. The theological basis for the anti-feminist and anti-LGBTQ policy agenda of organizations like [[Family Research Council]], [[Alliance Defending Freedom]], and [[World Congress of Families]]
3. A recruitment and retention mechanism for theocratic-wing organizations — the warrior identity provides emotional and cultural rewards that bind men to these networks

## Effectiveness / Critique

The militant evangelical masculinity model has been extraordinarily effective as a cultural-political formation. By fusing religious identity with a masculine archetype, it creates strong emotional and communal bonds and makes defection — from the faith, from the political tribe — feel like both spiritual failure and masculine weakness.

Feminist theologians, egalitarian evangelicals, and scholars of gender argue that the model is theologically constructed rather than biblical: it selectively reads scripture, ignores counter-examples (Jesus weeping, washing feet, consorting with women), and reflects cultural anxieties rather than textual mandate. Du Mez's contribution is to demonstrate that the construction is recent, historically contingent, and deliberately shaped by specific political actors and media ecosystems.
