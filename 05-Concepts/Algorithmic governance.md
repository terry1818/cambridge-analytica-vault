# Algorithmic governance

## Definition

Algorithmic governance is the use of algorithms and AI systems to support, structure, or outright automate decisions made by public institutions — the point at which data-driven classification and prediction models become "the de facto infrastructure of public policy." It covers welfare eligibility screening, fraud-risk scoring, predictive policing, school placement, immigration triage, child-welfare risk assessment, and citizenship verification. The defining feature is not merely that computers assist government, but that the *substance of a public decision* — who gets a benefit, who is flagged, who is investigated — is determined or heavily shaped by an automated system.

## Origin / history

Governments have used statistical scoring for decades (credit-style risk models, actuarial sentencing tools). The term "algorithmic governance" entered legal and public-administration scholarship in the 2010s as machine learning, cheap cloud compute, and large administrative datasets converged. Wikipedia catalogues the phenomenon under "Government by algorithm." The 2020s sharply accelerated it: the EU's AI Act (adopted 2024) created a regulatory category of "high-risk" public-sector AI; in the U.S., the integration of agency data under firms like [[Palantir Technologies]] — and DOGE-era data-consolidation initiatives in 2025 — pushed federal administration toward unified, queryable, algorithmically mediated systems. Public-administration research (e.g., Hillo et al. in *Public Administration*, 2025) now studies how citizens and administrators perceive the legitimacy of automated decisions.

## How it works

An algorithmic-governance system has three layers. (1) **Data layer**: administrative records — tax, benefits, immigration, voter, license, criminal-justice — increasingly merged across agencies (see [[Federal-state data-sharing pipelines]]) and supplemented with [[data brokers|brokered]] commercial data. (2) **Model layer**: classifiers and predictors that assign risk scores, eligibility determinations, or priority rankings. (3) **Decision/action layer**: the score is consumed by a caseworker, an enforcement system, or an automated workflow — e.g., flagging a benefits claim as likely fraud, ranking a person for immigration enforcement, or verifying citizenship for voter-roll maintenance. The political stakes lie in the action layer: an opaque model can effectively *make* a coercive decision while diffusing human responsibility for it.

## Application in this domain

Algorithmic governance is where the vault's threads converge. The surveillance-capitalism and data-broker pipelines produce the data; algorithmic governance is what the *state* does with it. [[Palantir Technologies]]' Foundry platform acting as "connective tissue" across IRS, SSA, DHS and immigration records (2025–2026 reporting) is algorithmic governance in its most consequential current form: it allows cross-referencing of previously siloed records and automated targeting at population scale. ICE's "ImmigrationOS" is a documented example — an automated deportation-prioritization system fed by IRS, Social Security, passport and license-plate data. This is the basis for the vault's hypothesis that a *governmental* algorithmic apparatus now does, structurally, what a private firm like [[Cambridge Analytica]] once promised.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed benefits:** Proponents argue algorithmic governance increases efficiency, consistency, fraud detection, and the precision of service delivery.
- **Documented problems:** Automated welfare and fraud systems have produced large-scale wrongful determinations (e.g., the Dutch childcare-benefits scandal; Australia's "Robodebt"). Predictive policing reproduces historical bias. Opacity blocks contestation.
- **Critique:** Scholars argue the most consequential effect is not efficiency but the reshaping of power, accountability, and democratic legitimacy — automated decisions are hard to explain, hard to appeal, and dilute human responsibility. This is the concern [[algorithmic accountability]] tries to answer. The vault treats algorithmic governance as a documented and accelerating trend, and its democratic consequences as a central open question.
