# Virginia school of political economy

## Definition

The Virginia school of political economy (also "Virginia Political Economy") is a research tradition centred on [[public choice theory]], constitutional economics, and law and economics that originated at the University of Virginia (UVA) in the late 1950s and migrated through Virginia Tech (Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University) to George Mason University (GMU), where it remains institutionally anchored. Its defining commitments are: applying rational-choice economic methodology to political institutions; treating constitutions as contracts that rational individuals would design to limit government; and scepticism toward majoritarian democracy unconstrained by constitutional rules. The school is closely associated with [[James M. Buchanan]], its founding figure and Nobel laureate, and Gordon Tullock, his principal collaborator.

## Origin / history

The Virginia school traces to 1957, when Buchanan and G. Warren Nutter co-founded the **Thomas Jefferson Center for Studies in Political Economy and Social Philosophy** at UVA. The center's explicit mission was to promote classical liberal political economy in an environment Buchanan and Nutter viewed as increasingly hostile to free-market ideas within mainstream academia.

Buchanan's pivotal intellectual move was his 1962 collaboration with Gordon Tullock, *The Calculus of Consent*, which established [[public choice theory]] as a rigorous research program. In 1963, Buchanan published "What Should Economists Do?" — a methodological manifesto arguing economics should study exchange processes, not resource allocation — further differentiating the Virginia approach from Chicago school positivism.

The center was reconstituted as the **Center for Study of Public Choice** at Virginia Tech in 1969 after faculty tensions at UVA. In 1983, Buchanan moved the Center to **George Mason University**, a less prestigious but more receptive institutional home that was simultaneously being cultivated by the [[Koch Network]] as a node in its policy infrastructure. This move proved transformative: GMU became the primary university home of the Virginia tradition, and [[Charles Koch]]'s 1997 $10 million pledge to the Buchanan Center, followed by the development of the [[Mercatus Center]] in 1999, embedded the school's institutional infrastructure within the broader donor-funded constitutional-capture project.

## How it works

The Virginia school operates as an intellectual tradition defined by several methodological and normative commitments:

1. **Methodological individualism**: All collective political outcomes must be explained by the choices of individual actors — no recourse to "the public interest" as an autonomous force.
2. **Constitutional political economy**: The appropriate level of analysis for political reform is constitutional rules, not ordinary legislation. Majorities can and do enact harmful policies; the solution is to change the rules of the game.
3. **Symmetry principle**: Economic and political actors should be analysed with the same self-interest assumptions; "government failure" is as real as "market failure."
4. **Contractarian ethics**: Just rules are those that rational individuals would agree to ex ante, behind a veil of ignorance about their position — drawing on Rawls but arriving at much more limited-government conclusions.

These commitments produce a distinctive policy vocabulary: balanced-budget amendments, supermajority requirements, spending caps, tax limitation, anti-delegation doctrines — all framed as constitutional discipline rather than ideological preferences.

## Application in this domain

The Virginia school's relevance to this vault's core subjects is structural, not incidental:

- **[[Convention of States Action]] / [[Article V Convention]]**: The movement's intellectual DNA is saturated with Virginia school constitutional economics. The argument that Congress will never self-limit, therefore a constitutional convention is necessary, is a direct application of public choice theory to constitutional change.
- **[[ALEC]] model legislation**: Many ALEC fiscal restraint templates — balanced budget, tax and expenditure limits — draw on GMU-affiliated scholars.
- **[[Project 2025]]**: Several contributors were trained in or affiliated with GMU departments shaped by the Virginia tradition; the document's administrative-state critique reproduces public choice arguments.
- **[[Koch Network]] infrastructure**: The Virginia school's institutional homes (Center for Study of Public Choice, [[Mercatus Center]], GMU Economics Department) are among the most heavily Koch-funded academic institutions in the United States, with $48 million documented between 2011 and 2014 alone.

## Examples

- The **Mercatus Center** at GMU is the most prominent institutional heir of the Virginia school; it produces regulatory impact studies and policy papers that consistently apply public choice and constitutional economics frameworks.
- **GMU Economics Department** has housed multiple MPS members and Koch-funded scholars; its doctoral program has produced economists placed throughout the think-tank network.
- **Public Choice journal** (founded 1966 by Tullock as *Papers on Non-Market Decision Making*) remains the field's flagship peer-reviewed outlet.
- **Atlas Network** member think tanks globally adopt and translate Virginia school arguments for national contexts.

## Effectiveness / critique

**Influence**: The Virginia school's impact on American policy debate since the 1980s is substantial and documented. Its vocabulary — rent-seeking, regulatory capture, constitutional constraints — has become the lingua franca of anti-regulatory politics. The school produced a Nobel laureate and shaped legal and economic thinking on administrative law.

**Critiques**:
- *Donor dependency*: The school's migration to GMU and subsequent Koch funding raises questions about the independence of research agendas. Critics (including MacLean) argue the school's conclusions too conveniently aligned with its funders' regulatory interests to be purely scholarly.
- *Normative loading*: The "constitutional economics" framework systematically privileges existing property distributions and treats majoritarian redistribution as a rights violation, a normative choice embedded in ostensibly technical analysis.
- *Institutional insularity*: The school's self-referential journal network and think-tank placement of graduates has been criticised for creating an echo chamber that amplifies ideological conclusions rather than subjecting them to genuine peer scrutiny.
- The contested nature of MacLean's specific claims about Buchanan's intent (see [[James M. Buchanan]] note) does not resolve the documented alignment between the school's institutional trajectory and the strategic interests of its major funders.
