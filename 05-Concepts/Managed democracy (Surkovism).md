# Managed Democracy (Surkovism)

## Definition

"Managed democracy" (also rendered as *upravlyaemaya demokratiya*) is the governing doctrine associated with [[Vladislav Surkov]] and the Putin Kremlin, in which the outward structures of democratic governance — competitive elections, registered parties, independent civil society, free press — are preserved as legitimising facades while the actual selection of leaders, outcomes of votes, and terms of political debate are controlled backstage. The term "Surkovism" (used informally by analysts) denotes the broader set of political-technology methods that operationalised the doctrine.

A closely related term, "sovereign democracy" (*suverennaya demokratiya*), was Surkov's preferred public framing: the claim that Russia's democracy was simply adapted to its own national conditions rather than subject to universal norms imposed from outside — a rhetorical move that repackaged authoritarian control as cultural self-determination.

## Origin / History

The doctrine emerged from the convergence of three currents in post-Soviet Russia:

1. **1990s political technology**: Russia's chaotic democratic transition produced a class of political consultants who learned to manufacture election results, create "technical parties" to split opposition votes, and use television as a weapon. Surkov was trained in PR and advertising in this milieu.

2. **Kremlin consolidation (1999–2003)**: Putin's first term required neutralising the oligarch-controlled media, marginalising regional political power, and transforming the State Duma from a genuinely contested arena into a controlled chamber. Surkov served as the operative who engineered this consolidation.

3. **Colour revolution anxiety (2003–2004)**: The Rose Revolution in Georgia (2003) and Orange Revolution in Ukraine (2004) demonstrated that managed elections could fail when civil society mobilised. Surkov drew the lesson that civil society itself needed to be pre-empted and captured — hence the creation of Nashi and other pseudo-NGOs.

The term "sovereign democracy" became prominent in Surkov's public writing and speeches roughly 2005–2007, as the Kremlin sought to articulate a theoretical justification for rejecting EU/US democratic norms.

## How It Works

Managed democracy functions across five interlocking mechanisms:

1. **Electoral engineering**: Creating fake opposition parties (e.g. "technical" liberal or nationalist parties) that absorb votes without threatening power; manipulating ballot access to exclude genuine challengers; using state administrative resources (budgets, civil servant mobilisation) to guarantee ruling party results.

2. **Media capture**: Converting major television channels into state-adjacent propaganda organs while maintaining the surface appearance of plural media. Keeping a small number of genuine opposition outlets alive as a pressure valve and as proof of "freedom."

3. **Captured civil society**: Founding or co-opting NGOs, youth movements (Nashi), think tanks, and civic organisations that appear to represent independent voices but are scripted from the Kremlin. This colonises the civil society space that colour revolutions depend on.

4. **Cognitive overload / "nothing is true"**: Deliberately flooding the public information space with contradictory narratives so that citizens lose confidence in any account of reality, become cynical, and disengage from political life. Peter Pomerantsev's analysis (cited below) emphasises this as a deliberate effect, not a side effect.

5. **Performative pluralism**: Staging debates, opposition appearances, and dissent within boundaries set by the Kremlin, so that the performance of democracy provides legitimacy internationally and exhausts domestic oppositional energy in harmless theatre.

## Application in This Domain

In the context of this vault's focus on information warfare, managed democracy is the foundational domestic template that later Russian influence operations exported or adapted internationally:

- The **[[Internet Research Agency]]** operations in the US (2014–2016) applied mechanism 4 — cognitive overload — to American audiences via social media, amplifying divisive content across the political spectrum not to persuade but to confuse and fragment.
- The **[[Doppelganger]]** campaign similarly cloned Western media brands to flood information environments with contradictory narratives, replicating the domestic Russian strategy in European target countries.
- **Donbas political technology**: Surkov directly applied managed-democracy methods to construct the theatrical separatist "republics" in eastern Ukraine after 2014 — casting leaders, scripting narratives, and stage-managing the appearance of a popular uprising.

Snyder's *Road to Unfreedom* (2018) places Surkovism in a longer intellectual genealogy running through Ivan Ilyin's statist philosophy, and argues that its export to Ukraine and the West represents a shift from "politics of inevitability" (liberal-democratic end-of-history assumptions) to "politics of eternity" (cyclical national mythology in which no progress is possible and only power matters).

## Examples

- **Nashi**: Kremlin-sponsored youth movement (est. 2005) modelled on colour-revolution civil society groups but controlled from above. Deployed to intimidate journalists and demonstrate "youth support" for Putin.
- **United Russia**: Built as a "party of power" with no independent ideological base, serving as an administrative machinery rather than a genuine political movement.
- **2011–12 Duma elections**: Official results showed United Russia winning ~49% despite widespread visible fraud; the resulting protests became a test case for whether managed democracy could survive a genuine civic challenge. (Short-term answer: yes, through crackdown and co-option.)
- **Donbas "people's republics"** (2014–): Surkov personally curated the political personnel and narratives of the DPR and LPR, exemplifying managed democracy's export as a tool of hybrid warfare.

## Effectiveness / Critique

**Effectiveness**: For roughly a decade (2000–2011), managed democracy was highly effective at maintaining Kremlin control while preserving international legitimacy. Russia was able to hold EU/US criticism at arm's length by pointing to its formal democratic institutions. The model was studied and replicated in varying forms by other authoritarian-adjacent governments (Hungary's Viktor Orbán, described by analysts as a "managed democracy" adaptation, has borrowed elements).

**Limits and critique**:
- The 2011–12 Bolotnaya protests demonstrated that even managed civil society can generate genuine grievances that escape containment.
- Pomerantsev argues that the doctrine ultimately hollows out not only opposition but also state capacity, because no one inside the system can speak truthfully — the performance colonises the state.
- The full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022 effectively ended managed democracy's function as international legitimacy-maintenance; Russia no longer needs the facade internationally, and domestically the wartime state has moved toward overt repression rather than managed spectacle.
- Critics from both liberal and nationalist directions have noted that "sovereign democracy" was always primarily a negative doctrine (rejecting Western norms) rather than a positive vision of governance.
