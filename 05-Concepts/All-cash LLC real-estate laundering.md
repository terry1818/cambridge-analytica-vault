## Definition

All-cash LLC real-estate laundering is a money laundering method in which a legal entity — typically a limited liability company (LLC), shell company, or trust — purchases real property with funds that do not pass through the mortgage lending system, thereby avoiding the anti-money-laundering (AML) checks banks impose on financed transactions. Because US states have historically required minimal beneficial ownership disclosure when forming LLCs, and because real estate professionals (agents, title companies, attorneys) were long excluded from Bank Secrecy Act reporting requirements applied to banks, this method created a significant regulatory gap. The New York Times estimated in 2015 that nearly half of US residential purchases above $5 million were made by shell companies; the FBI and FinCEN found that approximately 30% of transactions in their targeted pilot programs involved a beneficial owner or representative who was also the subject of a prior Suspicious Activity Report.

## Origin / History

The method has been practiced since at least the 1980s but became a significant concern for US law enforcement in the post-Soviet period as oligarchic and organized-crime capital sought placement in stable Western assets. The structural vulnerability was identified in US anti-money-laundering frameworks but real estate was repeatedly left outside Bank Secrecy Act coverage due to industry lobbying. The UK had analogous gaps; London's nominee ownership and offshore company holding of property was documented by Transparency International and the National Crime Agency from at least 2015.

Craig Unger's *House of Trump, House of Putin* (2018) uses this mechanism to explain how Russian-origin funds could have entered US luxury real estate through vehicles like [[Bayrock Group]] and direct condominium purchases without triggering AML scrutiny.

## How It Works

1. **Entity formation**: An LLC or equivalent is formed in a low-disclosure US state (Delaware, Nevada, Wyoming) or offshore. Beneficial owner information is not required at formation in most jurisdictions prior to the Corporate Transparency Act.
2. **Property identification**: A target property is identified — luxury condominiums and high-value residential properties preferred, as they appreciate, are liquid relative to commercial real estate, and attract minimal scrutiny as "normal" purchases.
3. **Cash purchase**: Funds are wired or transferred to the LLC, which pays cash at closing. No mortgage lender reviews the transaction; no bank AML checks are triggered by the property purchase itself.
4. **Title insurance**: Required in most US jurisdictions; historically, title insurance companies were not required to identify beneficial owners of purchasing LLCs (the gap that FinCEN's Geographic Targeting Orders targeted from 2016).
5. **Layering and integration**: Over time, the property appreciates, is rented for income, or is sold — with the proceeds now appearing as legitimate real estate gains.

## Application in This Domain

In the Russia-influence context, this mechanism is how investigators believe large volumes of post-Soviet oligarchic and organized-crime capital entered US and UK property markets in the 1990s–2010s. Specific documented applications:
- Trump properties in New York, Florida, and elsewhere: Multiple investigative reports (Miami Herald, BuzzFeed News, NYT) found patterns of LLC purchases by individuals with Russian connections.
- [[Bayrock Group]]: Investigated for potential use of this mechanism to move FSU capital into US development projects.
- UK luxury property: Systematic use of offshore companies to hold London properties, documented by Transparency International UK's Faulty Towers reports.

## Effectiveness / Critique

**Effectiveness**: Highly effective for approximately 1990–2020 in the US; similar timeline in the UK. The combination of low entity-formation requirements, exclusion from Bank Secrecy Act, and real estate professional exemptions created a near-complete regulatory gap.

**Regulatory responses**:
- US: [[FinCEN]] Geographic Targeting Orders (GTOs) from 2016 required title insurance companies to identify beneficial owners in specific metro areas for cash purchases above $300,000.
- US: Corporate Transparency Act (2021) required beneficial ownership registration — but enforcement was contested and in 2024–2026 faced legal challenges.
- US: FinCEN Residential Real Estate Rule (effective March 2026) required nationwide reporting of all-cash purchases by entities and trusts — but was vacated by a Texas federal court in March 2026 as exceeding FinCEN's authority.
- UK: Economic Crime (Transparency and Enforcement) Act 2022 introduced a Register of Overseas Entities.

**Critique**: Enforcement gaps remain substantial. The Texas court decision vacating the 2026 rule represents a significant setback for US regulatory efforts.
