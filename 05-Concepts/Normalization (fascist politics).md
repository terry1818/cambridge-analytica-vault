# Concept

## Definition

In Jason Stanley's analytical framework, **normalization** in fascist politics refers to the process by which the boundary of the politically and morally acceptable is incrementally shifted until what was once unthinkable becomes ordinary. Stanley's definition: normalization "transforms the morally extraordinary into the ordinary" and "makes us able to tolerate what was once intolerable by making it seem as if this is the way things have always been."

Normalization in this sense is not a passive cultural drift but an active, strategic feature of fascist politics. Stanley argues that fascism's project is specifically to "normalize the unthinkable" — to move the Overton window (the range of ideas considered acceptable for mainstream political discourse) so that increasingly extreme policies, rhetoric, and behaviors no longer provoke the resistance they would have provoked at an earlier baseline.

The concept is closely tied to Stanley's broader argument that fascist politics operates through **ten characteristic pillars** — including an appeal to a mythic past, propaganda, anti-intellectualism, a hierarchy of human worth, victimhood claims by the dominant group, law and order rhetoric applied selectively, and the dismantling of public goods. Normalization is not one pillar among ten; it is the *meta-mechanism* that makes the other pillars progressively harder to resist. Each incremental normalization step makes the next step easier to accept.

## Origin / History

Stanley developed the concept of normalization as central to fascist politics in *How Fascism Works: The Politics of Us and Them* (Random House, 2018). Stanley is Jacob Urowsky Professor of Philosophy at Yale University (subsequently at the University of British Columbia), specializing in epistemology, language, and political philosophy. His framework draws on Hannah Arendt's analysis of totalitarianism (particularly the *banality of evil* — the capacity of ordinary people to participate in atrocity through incremental accommodation), on Frankfurt School critical theory, and on the historical record of European fascism.

The concept has roots in social psychology's "boiling frog" literature (incremental desensitization) and in Overton window political theory, but Stanley integrates these into a specifically fascist-politics context: the normalization is not random but *directed* — it serves the strategic goals of the fascist movement.

Stanley's 2018 publication was directly responsive to the 2016 US political moment, but he grounds the concept in comparative historical cases (Nazi Germany, Mussolini's Italy, and contemporary cases from Hungary, Brazil, and elsewhere) to avoid the appearance of mere partisan commentary.

## How It Works

**The incremental mechanism:**

1. **Baseline establishment.** Every political culture has a baseline of acceptable discourse and policy. Fascist politics begins by identifying the boundary and then testing it with statements or actions that are extreme but not quite beyond it.
2. **Habituation.** Repeated exposure to extreme rhetoric or policy, even if initially shocking, reduces the shock response. The brain normalizes the stimulus. Each instance that passes without decisive collective rejection becomes the new baseline.
3. **Goalpost movement.** As the baseline shifts, what previously counted as "extreme" now appears moderate by comparison. Critics who continue to apply the original baseline are portrayed as hysterical or partisan — and this portrayal has some rhetorical force because the *current* moment has indeed become somewhat more normal than the critics are claiming relative to the *recent* past (even as it remains extreme relative to the more distant past).
4. **The normalization trap.** Stanley's key insight: the very success of fascist normalization makes it harder to call out. Because charges of fascism always seem extreme relative to the current, normalized moment, those who use the label are easily depicted as alarmists. The warning bell rings loudest when people have been conditioned to distrust the bell. This is what Stanley calls "one of the ironies of fascist politics" — the normalization of fascist politics makes talk of fascism appear outlandish.
5. **Participation expansion.** As norms shift, more people participate in or acquiesce to behaviors they would previously have condemned. This is not mere cowardice; it is the genuine redefinition of what counts as normal political behavior. Ordinary institutional actors — judges, civil servants, journalists, business leaders — find themselves accommodating things they would once have refused.

**Relationship to propaganda:** Normalization works in tandem with propaganda. Propaganda establishes the alternative reality (e.g., the nation is under existential threat from internal enemies); normalization makes the extreme responses to that claimed threat seem proportionate.

## Application in This Domain

The vault uses normalization as a **process-tracking framework**:

- **Documenting the baseline.** Establish clearly what the US political discourse and legal norms were at specific baseline points (e.g., 2015, 2020, 2024) so that subsequent changes can be measured against an explicit prior norm, not against the current normalized moment.
- **Incremental change tracking.** Map specific shifts in discourse, policy, and institutional behavior that would have been unacceptable at the baseline and are now routine. Examples to investigate: presidential claims of immunity from criminal prosecution; executive branch defiance of court orders; political violence characterized as legitimate protest.
- **Resistance to normalization's own trap.** The vault must be aware that: (a) over-labeling risks crying wolf and discrediting the analysis; and (b) under-labeling risks accommodating the incremental process Stanley describes. Strict evidence tiering is the discipline that keeps the vault in (a) and (b)'s gap.
- **COS-specific application.** Has the [[Convention of States Action]] campaign normalized the concept of a constitutional convention outside its originally framed scope? Has rhetoric that would once have been characterized as extreme constitutional revisionism been normalized as mainstream conservative policy? See [[Lens - COS as Constitutional Capture Vector]].

## Examples

**Historical (Tier 1)**
- The progressive normalization of antisemitic discourse and policy in Germany, 1933–1938: from Nuremberg Laws (shocking at the time to many) to Kristallnacht (less shocking to a population habituated to prior steps).
- The normalization of authoritarian controls in Hungary under Orbán: each electoral law change, each media acquisition, each court-packing measure was individually explicable as a legal reform; collectively they represent a systematic elimination of democratic competition.

**Contemporary US (Tier 3 — for analysis)**
- Shifts in acceptable presidential conduct between 2016–2026 that would have previously triggered bipartisan institutional response — document specific instances with evidence tier.
- Shift in the Overton window on topics including: independent civil service, judicial independence, citizenship by birth, treaty obligations. Record as pattern, not isolated events.

## Effectiveness / Critique

**Strengths:** Normalization explains an empirical puzzle: how mass populations come to accept or demand things they would previously have rejected. It provides a framework for the incremental, non-coup pathway to authoritarianism.

**Criticisms:**
- *Left Voice* and other critics argue Stanley's framework is too focused on rhetoric and cultural politics and underweights material class contradictions and state violence.
- The normalization concept risks a form of baseline fetishism — treating any departure from an earlier norm as alarming, even when the earlier norm was itself problematic. (Were the pre-2016 norms actually democratic baselines worth defending, or were they also sites of structural violence and exclusion?)
- Without precision about which baselines matter and why, normalization can be invoked to argue against any political change, including progressive change. The vault should apply the concept specifically to democratic procedure and constitutional norms.
