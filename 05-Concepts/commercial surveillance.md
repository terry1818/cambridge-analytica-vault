# commercial surveillance

## Definition

Commercial surveillance is, in the U.S. Federal Trade Commission's working definition, "the business of collecting, analyzing, and profiting from information about people" — more precisely, the systematic practice of collecting, aggregating, analyzing, retaining, transferring, and monetizing consumer data and its direct derivatives. The term is deliberately framed around *the business model* rather than around any single technology. Where [[surveillance capitalism]] is [[Shoshana Zuboff]]'s critical-theory frame for the same phenomenon, "commercial surveillance" is the regulatory and legal vocabulary — the language a U.S. agency uses when it considers whether the practice is "unfair or deceptive."

## Origin / history

The phrase has roots in privacy advocacy and scholarship through the 2010s, but it acquired institutional weight on 11 August 2022, when the FTC announced its first-ever rulemaking on "Commercial Surveillance and Data Security" via an Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (ANPR). The ANPR sought a public record on prevalent data practices that might be unfair or deceptive, explicitly naming data brokers, behavioral advertising, automated decision systems, and lax data security. The choice of "commercial surveillance" — rather than the softer industry term "data-driven marketing" — was itself a regulatory signal. The rulemaking did not produce a final rule; subsequent action shifted to targeted enforcement and to the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's separate effort to apply the Fair Credit Reporting Act to [[data brokers]] (the 2024 Regulation V proposal). In February 2026 the FTC reminded data brokers of obligations under PADFAA (the Protecting Americans' Data from Foreign Adversaries Act).

## How it works

Commercial surveillance is best understood as a supply chain. **Collection** happens at the edge: apps, websites, loyalty programs, connected devices, and the advertising-technology "bidstream" all emit personal data. **Aggregation** is performed by [[data brokers]] such as [[Acxiom]], Experian, Epsilon and [[LexisNexis Risk Solutions]], who merge thousands of sources into individual-level dossiers. **Analysis** turns dossiers into scores, segments, and predictions — including [[Psychographics|psychographic]] profiles. **Monetization** sells access to advertisers, insurers, employers, landlords, political campaigns, and — critically for this vault — government agencies. Each handoff is governed by contracts and terms of service rather than by meaningful individual consent.

## Application in this domain

Commercial surveillance is the connective tissue of the vault's argument. [[Cambridge Analytica]] was a *consumer* of commercial-surveillance output: the [[Facebook-CA Data Scandal|harvested data]] was an extreme case of the same extraction. More importantly, commercial surveillance is the mechanism by which the state acquires capabilities it could not lawfully build directly. [[Byron Tau]]'s reporting in *Means of Control* documents how, after 9/11, U.S. agencies began purchasing commercially available data to do what a warrant requirement would otherwise block — the [[Babel Street]]/Locate X and [[Venntel]] location-data contracts being the clearest examples. Senator Ron Wyden's "Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act" targets exactly this purchase-instead-of-warrant move.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Regulatory framing:** The FTC's adoption of "commercial surveillance" reframed a marketing practice as a potential harm subject to consumer-protection law.
- **Evidence:** The supply chain is well-documented through FTC reports, the 2024 FTC action against [[Gravy Analytics]]/Venntel, journalism, and academic work.
- **Critique / limits:** Industry argues the term is pejorative and that data flows enable beneficial services and fraud detection. Civil-liberties critics argue the opposite — that the FTC rulemaking stalled and enforcement is piecemeal, leaving the practice largely unregulated. The vault treats the *existence and scale* of commercial surveillance as documented and the question of *adequate regulation* as unresolved.
