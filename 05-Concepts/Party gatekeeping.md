# Party Gatekeeping

## Definition

Levitsky and Ziblatt's term for the function party elites historically performed in filtering candidates — blocking extremists, demagogues, and would-be authoritarians from winning nominations and thus gaining the legitimacy that party endorsement confers. In their framework, political parties are "democracy's gatekeepers." When gatekeeping fails, extreme candidates access the party label, its fundraising networks, and eventually state power.

## Origin / History

Developed in *How Democracies Die* (Crown, 2018) as part of Levitsky and Ziblatt's comparative analysis of how democracies fell in interwar Europe and 20th-century Latin America. The authors argue that in most historical cases of democratic breakdown, the enabling variable was not the extremist's strength alone but the decision by mainstream party leaders to include, normalize, or ally with them — the "Faustian bargain."

## How It Works

In the pre-reform era of US politics (before the widespread primary system), party bosses and elites selected nominees through conventions and backroom negotiations. This gave insiders veto power over candidates who might damage democratic norms. Levitsky and Ziblatt identify three specific gatekeeping tools:

1. **Denial of the party label** — refusing to endorse or nominate the extreme candidate.
2. **Isolation** — refusing to form electoral alliances or governing coalitions with them.
3. **Unification against** — mainstream parties of left and right temporarily cooperating to prevent the demagogue's ascent.

Historical success cases: Belgium blocked Léon Degrelle's Rexist party (1936); Finland's mainstream parties isolated the Lapua Movement; Austria's Norbert Hofer was defeated partly through cross-partisan opposition.

Failure cases: German conservatives believed they could control and use Hitler; Italian liberals brought Mussolini into a governing coalition expecting to manage him.

**Weakening of gatekeeping**: The expansion of primary elections from the 1970s onward shifted candidate selection power from party insiders to the primary electorate — a reform made in the name of democracy but which removed the elite filter. Celebrity, media presence, and fundraising capacity replaced party vetting as the path to nomination.

## Application in This Domain

The vault can apply this framework to evaluate the Republican Party's behavior toward Trump (2015–present) as a gatekeeping case study: initial resistance (many establishment figures opposed his nomination), followed by capitulation and normalization after 2016, and eventual full subordination. L&Z's framework predicts this arc — it matches the "Faustian bargain" pattern of conservatives who believed they could control an extreme figure and extract policy benefits while managing the democratic damage. The vault can also track whether any gatekeeping function re-emerged (e.g., 2024 primary challengers) and assess its effectiveness.

## Examples

- The 2016 Republican primary: No major party institution effectively blocked Trump's nomination despite widespread private misgivings.
- German conservatives (DNVP, Catholic Centre) enabling Hitler's chancellorship (1933) as a canonical gatekeeping failure.
- The 2016 Austrian presidential election, where pro-democratic parties united to narrowly prevent far-right FPÖ candidate Norbert Hofer's victory.

## Effectiveness / Critique

- **Elitist implication**: Critics argue the framework implicitly endorses elite control of political parties and is skeptical of participatory democracy — primaries were themselves reforms intended to democratize parties captured by corrupt machines.
- **Misdiagnoses root cause**: Some structural critics argue that gatekeeping failure is a symptom of underlying economic and social conditions (inequality, cultural backlash) and that focusing on party elites' choices overstates their agency.
- **Comparative limits**: Cases where gatekeeping "worked" (Belgium, Finland) involved different institutional contexts, which L&Z acknowledge but critics say are under-theorized.
- **Post-2016 application under debate**: Whether the Republican Party's capitulation to Trump represents a "Faustian bargain" or reflects genuine preference alignment among the base is contested in the empirical literature.
