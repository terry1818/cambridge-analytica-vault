# Spiritual warfare

## Definition

Spiritual warfare is the belief — and the associated practices — that Christians are engaged in a literal, ongoing battle against Satan and a hierarchy of evil spirits, and that this battle can be fought through prayer, fasting, worship, exorcism, the laying on of hands, and the verbal "binding" of demonic powers. While the broad idea that Christians struggle against evil is ancient, the specific contemporary framework — with its named demonic hierarchies, "territorial spirits," and aggressive prayer techniques — is recent, developed primarily within the Pentecostal and charismatic movements from the 1970s onward. Most modern spiritual-warfare theology cites Ephesians 6:10–18 ("the whole armor of God").

## Origin / history

The *term* "spiritual warfare" appears to date only to the 1970s; some scholars attribute its early usage to the English charismatic leader Michael Harper (1931–2010). Before that, the concept lacked a major role in mainstream evangelicalism.

The decisive figure in turning it into a structured movement was [[C. Peter Wagner]], a Fuller Theological Seminary missiologist. In the 1980s Wagner founded the **Spiritual Warfare Network**, which rapidly made the new ideas central to charismatic and then broader evangelical practice. Wagner and associates such as the missionary George Otis Jr. developed "**strategic-level spiritual warfare**" — the claim that specific demonic "territorial spirits" rule over particular cities, regions, and nations, and that mapping and confronting them ("spiritual mapping") is necessary for evangelism. As one analysis notes, these ideas became "so entrenched that many Christians would have had no conception that they were only recently implemented." Wagner went on to coin the term [[New Apostolic Reformation]], and spiritual warfare became foundational to NAR theology.

## How it works

At the personal level, spiritual warfare is practiced through intercessory prayer, fasting, worship music, deliverance ministry (exorcism), and anointing with oil. At the "strategic" level it scales up: practitioners identify demonic strongholds over geographic territories or institutions and conduct coordinated mass prayer ("prayer warfare," "prayer walks," "warfare worship") intended to break those strongholds.

Mechanically, the framework does three things that matter politically. First, it **literalizes** conflict — disagreements are not differences of opinion but skirmishes in a real, invisible war. Second, it **maps the demonic onto the visible world** — institutions, ideologies, and people can be identified as demonically controlled. Third, it **sanctions extraordinary action** — if a city, an election, or a government is under demonic control, ordinary political or legal remedies are insufficient and "warfare" is required.

## Application in this domain

Spiritual warfare is the connective theology of the modern theocratic wing. Within the [[New Apostolic Reformation]], "apostles" and "prophets" frame their political project — the [[Seven Mountains Mandate]] — explicitly as warfare to retake territory from demonic powers. After the 2020 election, NAR figures including [[Dutch Sheets]] cast the contest over the result as literal spiritual warfare against a demonic "stolen election"; the [[Cyrus Anointing]] framing and warfare framing reinforced each other. Mass "warfare worship" events led by figures such as [[Sean Feucht]], and the apostolic-revival format of the [[ReAwaken America]] tour, deploy the same vocabulary.

Politically, the consequence is that opponents — election officials, Democrats, the press, LGBTQ people — can be recast not as fellow citizens but as agents or instruments of the demonic. This both raises the stakes (compromise becomes complicity with evil) and licenses responses outside democratic norms. Scholars of [[Christian nationalism]] treat this cosmic framing as a significant driver of the willingness to support anti-democratic action.

## Examples

- [[New Apostolic Reformation]] — built its entire missiology and political theology on strategic-level spiritual warfare.
- [[ReAwaken America]] — stages mass prayer and "army of God" rhetoric in a warfare idiom.
- [[Joel's Army]] — a militant variant teaching that an end-times generation will wage warfare to take dominion.
- [[January 6 2021]] — prayer-warfare framing of the post-election period preceded and accompanied the Capitol attack.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed effectiveness:** Proponents claim spiritual warfare produces revival, healing, and societal transformation; these claims are theological, not empirically testable.
- **Empirical evidence:** As a *mobilization* technology it is demonstrably effective — it sustains intense commitment and reframes political setbacks as winnable battles.
- **Academic critique:** Theologians outside the charismatic movement (including within evangelicalism) argue strategic-level spiritual warfare lacks biblical warrant and that its territorial-spirit claims are extrabiblical innovations. Scholars of democracy warn that its core logic — demonizing opponents and sanctioning extraordinary measures — corrodes the norms pluralist democracy depends on.
