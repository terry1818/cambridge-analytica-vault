## Definition

The public stack is a concept developed by [[Marietje Schaake]] in *The Tech Coup* (2024) to describe a model of digitally governed infrastructure that is built, owned, and operated under democratic accountability rather than private corporate control. The concept is Schaake's constructive answer to her diagnosis that technology companies have conducted a de facto "tech coup" — seizing governance functions (content moderation, identity verification, payment infrastructure, AI-driven decision systems) that should belong to democratically accountable institutions. The public stack encompasses public cloud infrastructure, publicly developed AI systems, and interoperable digital services built on open standards, financed by governments and civic institutions rather than private capital.

## Origin / history

Schaake developed the public-stack concept during her decade as an MEP (2009–2019) and refined it in the post-parliament period at Stanford. The conceptual grounding is rooted in the history of the internet itself — originally ARPANET, a US Defense Department research project, transitioned to public and academic use before commercial privatization — and in European precedents such as public scientific cloud initiatives and national digital identity systems (Estonia's e-governance infrastructure being a frequently cited example). The concept appears as a prescriptive conclusion in *The Tech Coup* (Princeton University Press, September 2024).

## How it works

Schaake envisions the public stack as operating across several layers of the digital infrastructure:

1. **Physical and logical infrastructure**: Publicly funded or regulated cloud computing, fiber networks, and domain name systems that are not subject to unilateral corporate control.
2. **Identity and authentication**: Government-backed digital identity systems (analogous to physical passports) that do not depend on private platforms for verification.
3. **AI systems for public functions**: Government AI systems for welfare eligibility, healthcare, justice — built with transparency obligations, auditable by independent bodies, subject to legislative oversight.
4. **Civic technology**: Non-commercial digital services for democratic participation, news, and public information that are not dependent on advertising revenue or private-platform hosting.
5. **Open standards and interoperability**: Mandatory interoperability requirements that reduce lock-in to private platforms, enabling users to move data and communication across services.

## Application in this domain

The public stack is the positive counterpart to the vault's core research on how private tech infrastructure has been weaponized for surveillance, microtargeting, and algorithmic governance. Where [[Palantir Technologies]], [[Flock Safety]], and the [[Federated Master Database]] represent the privatization of state surveillance capacity, the public stack represents a theoretical model in which those functions would instead be built under democratic accountability, transparent procurement, and civil-society oversight.

The concept also connects to Schaake's critique of the Trump 2.0 administration's acceleration of public-private surveillance partnerships — the alignment of [[Peter Thiel]]'s Palantir, Elon Musk's DOGE apparatus, and the federal data systems as a privatized state power structure that has no public-stack analog.

## Examples

- Estonia's e-residency and digital identity infrastructure: a functioning example of government-built, publicly accountable digital services.
- The original Internet (ARPANET/NSFNet): public-sector origin before commercial handoff, cited as a model for what a reclaimable public digital layer looks like.
- Schaake cites NGO-driven open-source civic technology projects as small-scale public-stack analogs.

## Effectiveness / critique

**Sympathetic critics** (including the *Tandfonline* review of *The Tech Coup*) note that Schaake's public-stack examples remain underdeveloped: the gap between aspirational framing and concrete institutional design is significant. Building a public AI stack capable of competing with OpenAI or Google requires capital and technical capacity that most democratic governments do not currently possess.

**Structural challenge**: The [[EU Digital Services Act]] (which Schaake helped shape) is a regulatory rather than infrastructural intervention — it constrains private power but does not build public alternatives. The public stack would require affirmative investment, not just regulation.

**Political challenge**: In the US context, there is no political coalition currently capable of funding a public digital stack; the EU context is more plausible but still faces procurement and capability constraints.

Evidence tier for public-stack effectiveness as a policy prescription: Tier 4 (conceptual proposal; no operating implementations at the scale envisioned).
