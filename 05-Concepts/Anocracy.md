# Anocracy

## Definition

An anocracy is a political regime that combines elements of both democratic and autocratic governance, falling in the middle range of the [[The Polity score|Polity scale]] (approximately −5 to +5 on the −10 to +10 scale). Anocracies have nominal democratic institutions (elections, legislatures, formal rights) but also significant executive dominance, limited constraints on power, and inconsistent rule of law. They are neither consolidated democracies nor stable autocracies. Barbara Walter's central argument in *How Civil Wars Start* (2022) is that anocracy — not autocracy — is the regime type most prone to internal armed conflict.

## Origin / history

The term derives from the Polity Project, a long-running political science data collection effort originating in the 1970s and maintained by the Center for Systemic Peace (currently Polity5 covers 1800–2018). Polity researchers developed the autocracy-to-democracy spectrum and identified the middle zone as analytically significant. The term "anocracy" itself gained wider usage with the empirical finding that this zone carries disproportionate civil war risk — documented in studies using the Polity dataset (Fearon & Laitin 2003; Hegre et al. 2001). Barbara Walter synthesises and popularises this finding in *How Civil Wars Start and How to Stop Them* (2022), using it as the primary structural variable in her civil war prediction model, alongside the [[Sons of the soil]] dynamic and the role of ethnic entrepreneur factions.

## How it works

The civil-war risk of anocracy operates through several mechanisms:

1. **Institutional inconsistency**: Anocracies lack the strong authoritarian control that suppresses dissent in full autocracies, and lack the robust rule of law and institutional legitimacy that reduces violence in full democracies. This creates a zone where conflict can emerge.
2. **Democratic transition vulnerability**: Countries transitioning toward or away from democracy pass through the anocracy zone — the most dangerous period, because expectations have changed but institutions have not yet consolidated.
3. **Factional opportunity**: Partial openness creates political space for organised factions to compete, but without sufficient rule-of-law constraints to channel that competition peacefully.
4. **Incumbent resistance**: Leaders of anocratic regimes frequently resist further democratisation because it threatens their power, leading to crackdowns that can trigger insurgency.

Walter specifically identifies the combination of anocracy + [[Sons of the soil]] dynamics (dominant group perceiving displacement) + organised factions as the three-variable profile most predictive of civil war onset.

## Application in this domain

The vault can apply this concept to assess democratic backsliding in the US context. Walter noted that the Polity project downgraded the United States from a full democracy (score +10) to a partial democracy (score +5, the boundary of anocracy) following the events of 2020–21, placing the US in what she calls the "anocracy zone" and triggering the civil conflict watch indicators. The concept provides a quantitative framework — anchored in systematic cross-national data — for evaluating the severity of US democratic backsliding, as a complement to qualitative accounts (Levitsky & Ziblatt, Snyder). It also applies to any comparative analysis of other countries the vault tracks (Hungary, Brazil, Philippines, Turkey) that have undergone similar transitions.

## Examples

- **United States 2020–21**: Polity5 downgrade to +5 following January 6 Capitol attack; Walter argues this placed the US on the watch-list threshold.
- **Russia 1990s**: Transition from Soviet system produced a prolonged anocracy period, with associated internal conflicts (Chechnya).
- **Rwanda pre-1994**: Anocratic transition period accompanied the conditions for the genocide.
- **Venezuela**: Progressive slide from democracy through anocracy to competitive authoritarianism under Chávez/Maduro.
- **Hungary**: Orban-era consolidation — scholars debate whether Hungary has passed through anocracy into [[Competitive authoritarianism]].

## Effectiveness / critique

Walter's application of the anocracy concept to the United States has been both influential and contested:

1. **Methodological concern**: The Polity scale was designed for cross-national comparison; applying it to the US with the precision required to trigger "watch list" thresholds involves interpretive choices about how to code institutional performance.
2. **Predictive validity**: Civil war prediction from regime type alone has limited precision — many anocracies do not experience civil war. The model improves with additional variables (ethnic factions, declining group status) but remains probabilistic, not deterministic.
3. **Definition debates**: Political scientists disagree about what counts as an anocracy versus a "backsliding democracy" versus a [[Competitive authoritarianism|competitive authoritarian]] regime; the Polity scale provides one operationalisation among several.
4. **Update lag**: The Polity Project has discontinued regular updates as of 2018 dataset; subsequent coding relies on researchers' applications rather than the institutional project.
