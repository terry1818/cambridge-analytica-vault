# Unitary Executive Theory

## Definition

A constitutional law theory holding that Article II of the US Constitution vests the entire executive power of the federal government in the President alone. Under the strong form of the theory, the President may direct all executive branch officials in any policy matter, may remove any executive branch official at will without restriction by Congress, and no independent executive agency (e.g., the FTC, FEC, CFPB, or DOJ career prosecutors operating under independent authority) can exist constitutionally. The theory is invoked extensively in Project 2025's *Mandate for Leadership* as the legal foundation for a sweeping reorganization of the federal executive branch under direct presidential control.

## Origin / History

The roots are in Article II ("The executive Power shall be vested in a President of the United States") and debates at the Constitutional Convention of 1787 over whether the executive should be singular or plural. However, the **modern doctrine** was explicitly developed by lawyers in the Reagan administration's Justice Department Office of Legal Counsel — including John Roberts (later Chief Justice) and Samuel Alito (later Associate Justice). It was articulated as a formal legal theory through Reagan-era signing statements and OLC memoranda pushing back against independent agencies.

The theory gained academic development through scholars Steven Calabresi and Christopher Yoo, and Calabresi and Kevin Rhodes. It has been invoked in Supreme Court cases including *Morrison v. Olson* (1988, where the majority *rejected* the strong-form version, with Scalia alone dissenting in a now-famous unitary executive opinion), *Seila Law v. CFPB* (2020, where the Court embraced a partial version), and ongoing litigation.

Project 2025's legal architects — many of them alumni of the Federalist Society — treat the theory as settled and use it as the constitutional basis for Schedule F, mass dismissal of career civil servants, and subordination of all executive agencies to direct White House control.

## How It Works

**Core claims**:

1. **Vesting clause**: "The executive Power shall be vested in a President" — the word "the" and "vested" mean all executive power belongs to the President, not to subordinate officials who can exercise independent discretion.

2. **Take care clause**: The President "shall take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed" — implies supervisory authority over all execution of law.

3. **Removal power**: If the President cannot remove executive officials at will, those officials hold power independent of the President, which the theory holds is unconstitutional. This is the most contested and practically consequential claim.

**Institutional implications**:
- Independent agencies (FTC, SEC, FEC, CFPB, FCC) whose commissioners serve fixed terms removable only for cause would be unconstitutional under the strong form.
- Independent counsels and special prosecutors would be unconstitutional without direct presidential supervision.
- Career civil servants (protected by merit system protections) can be converted to at-will employees via Schedule F or equivalent executive orders.
- The DOJ cannot act independently of the White House on prosecutorial decisions.

## Examples

- Reagan administration OLC memos asserting removal power over independent agency heads (1980s).
- Trump's first-term Schedule F executive order (Oct 2020) reclassifying career civil servants as Schedule F (at-will) — a direct unitary executive implementation. Biden rescinded; Trump 2.0 reinstated.
- *Seila Law v. CFPB* (2020): SCOTUS held the CFPB's single-director-removable-only-for-cause structure unconstitutional — a partial unitary executive win.
- Trump 2.0 mass firings of career officials at FBI, DOJ, CFPB, and other agencies claimed under unitary executive authority.

## Effectiveness / Critique

- **Constitutional text contested**: Critics argue "the executive Power shall be vested" does not mean *all* executive power flows from and is controlled by the President — Congress also has constitutional authority to structure the executive branch.
- **Historical practice contradicts**: Independent agencies and merit civil service protections have existed since the late 19th century with constitutional acquiescence; the theory requires dismissing 150 years of practice.
- **Supreme Court has not embraced strong form**: Even the conservative Court has not fully adopted the strong-form theory — *Morrison v. Olson* (1988) majority explicitly rejected it; recent cases move in that direction but have not gone all the way.
- **Democratic accountability critique**: Critics argue the theory, if fully implemented, would make the federal bureaucracy a vehicle for the President's personal and political will rather than for faithful execution of law passed by Congress — which is precisely its appeal to Project 2025 architects and precisely the concern of its critics.
