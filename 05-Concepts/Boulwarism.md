# Boulwarism

## Definition

Boulwarism is a corporate labor-relations strategy, pioneered by Lemuel R. Boulware, Vice President of Employee and Public Relations at General Electric from the late 1940s through 1961, characterized by two interlocking practices: (1) presenting unions with a research-backed "full and fair" initial contract offer that management then declared its final position, refusing to negotiate upward; and (2) conducting systematic ideological education of the entire workforce — and the broader public — in the values of free enterprise, productivity, and managerial legitimacy, while delegitimizing union power. The National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) and the US Second Circuit Court of Appeals ruled in 1969 that the practice constituted an unfair labor practice under the Wagner Act by circumventing good-faith bargaining.

## Origin / History

GE suffered a damaging 1946 strike that Boulware attributed not only to union militancy but to what he saw as workers' economic illiteracy and susceptibility to union propaganda. Appointed to lead labor and public relations, Boulware designed a comprehensive counter-program. The strategy bore his name by the early 1950s and was formally described and debated in labor law literature throughout that decade. Boulware's approach was not merely a bargaining tactic; it was an explicit attempt to reshape the ideological baseline of the American workforce by treating free-market economics as a form of employee education that served both the worker's and the company's interests.

## How It Works

Boulwarism operated on two simultaneous tracks:

**Track 1 — "Right from the start" bargaining:** GE conducted extensive research into what it could afford to pay and what the market required, then presented unions with an offer framed as objectively determined and fair. Management refused to move from this position through normal give-and-take, reserving the right to revise only in response to genuinely new information. The intent was to make the union appear unnecessary by demonstrating that management could arrive at a fair outcome without union pressure.

**Track 2 — Ideological saturation:** GE ran mandatory economic education sessions for all 190,000 employees, distributed pamphlets on free enterprise, held captive-audience meetings, ran advertisements in newspapers and on radio, and trained plant managers as ideological communicators. The goal was to convince workers that their interests were aligned with GE's profitability and opposed to union disruption.

## Application in This Domain

Boulwarism is directly relevant to the prehistory of the patron-class network documented in this vault because:

1. It demonstrates that systematic corporate-funded ideological education preceded and informed the later think-tank and dark-money ecosystem.
2. GE's Boulwarism was the training ground for Ronald Reagan. From 1954 to 1962, Reagan served as GE's television spokesman and corporate ambassador, traveling to GE plants and delivering Boulwarist free-enterprise speeches. Boulware's political and ideological tutoring of Reagan during this period is credited by historians, including Kim Phillips-Fein, with Reagan's conversion from New Deal Democrat to Goldwater-style conservative — the same conversion that ultimately produced the Reagan Revolution.
3. The direct line runs: Boulwarism (GE, 1947–1962) → Reagan's ideological formation → [[Goldwater 1964 campaign]] → Reagan 1980 → supply-side policy era → [[Lewis Powell Memo]] infrastructure.

## Examples

- GE's 190,000-employee mandatory economic education program, late 1940s–1960s
- Distribution of free-market pamphlets to all GE plant workers
- Reagan's GE Theatre television program (1954–1962), which functioned simultaneously as entertainment and soft ideological advertising
- Reagan's "The Speech" (1964), derived from his GE plant tour talks, delivered for Goldwater and later rebranded as "A Time for Choosing"

## Effectiveness / Critique

Boulwarism successfully contained GE wage growth through the 1950s and built Reagan's conservative ideological persona, making it highly effective on its own terms. However, the NLRB ruled in 1964 (upheld by the Second Circuit in 1969) that GE's refusal to bargain in good faith violated the Wagner Act. The 1969–1970 GE strike, the largest in US history at that point, was partly a reaction to the accumulated resentments Boulwarism had produced. As a formal bargaining strategy, it was thus legally constrained. As an ideological education model, it was enormously influential — the FEE/Mont Pelerin/Heritage think tank model can be seen as Boulwarism scaled from the corporation to civil society.
