## Definition

"Exact match" is a voter-registration verification protocol that rejects or places in "pending" status any registration application where the applicant's name, date of birth, or identification number does not match character-for-character with the same fields in a government database — typically the state Department of Driver Services (DDS) or the Social Security Administration (SSA). A single discrepancy — a transposed letter, a missing hyphen, a truncated middle name, a data-entry error in either the registration form or the underlying database — can cause an application to fail and require the voter to take additional corrective action within a deadline.

## Origin / history

Georgia is the primary state associated with exact match in recent electoral history. The practice was first implemented by Secretary of State [[Brian Kemp]] in 2010. A legal challenge led to a 2016 settlement suspending the process. The Georgia legislature then **revived** exact match in 2017 despite documented evidence of its disproportionate racial impact. This revival, absent [[Voting Rights Act of 1965]] Section 5 preclearance (eliminated by [[Shelby County v Holder 2013]]), could proceed without federal DOJ pre-approval.

Exact match variants also appeared in other states including North Carolina, Alabama, and Wisconsin, often under different names but sharing the same structural feature: a verification mismatch triggers rejection or a corrective-action burden.

## How it works

1. An applicant submits a voter registration form with name, date of birth, and an identification number (driver's license or SSN last four).
2. The election office runs the data against the DDS or SSA database.
3. If any field fails to match exactly — including due to hyphenation, name suffix, spacing, or database error — the registration is flagged.
4. The applicant is notified and given a window (40 days in Georgia) to resolve the mismatch; if they do not, the application is rejected or placed in permanent "pending" status.
5. Voters in "pending" status may vote a provisional ballot, which may or may not be counted.

## Application in this domain

In Georgia under Kemp's administration, the exact-match process placed ~53,000 registration applications in pending status in the weeks before the 2018 gubernatorial election — the election Kemp himself was contesting. Approximately 80% of those pending applications were from Black, Latino, and Asian-American registrants.

Analysis of a prior Georgia exact-match cycle (July 2013–July 2015) found that of ~34,000 cancelled registrations, approximately 76.3% were from minority registrants.

## Examples

- **Georgia 2018**: 53,000 applications pending pre-election; ~70–80% minority applicants; Kemp wins over Abrams by ~55,000 votes.
- **Georgia 2017 database analysis**: 80.15% of 51,111 pending registrations were from Black, Latino, or Asian-American applicants.
- **North Carolina**: a version of exact match applied to absentee ballot request signatures, generating similar disparity concerns.

## Effectiveness / critique

**Stated rationale**: Ensuring the person who registers to vote is the same person as in the government identity database; preventing fraudulent registrations.

**Documented critique**:
- Mismatches arise overwhelmingly from database errors, data-entry inconsistencies, and name formatting (hyphens, apostrophes, suffixes, compound surnames) rather than from fraudulent intent.
- Compound surnames and names with diacritical marks — disproportionately common in Latino and Asian-American communities — fail more often.
- The error rate is not random: structural features of the system (limited name formats in older databases, data-entry inconsistencies) systematically disadvantage non-white registrants.
- After the process was abandoned in Georgia, the Campaign Legal Center and Lawyers' Committee noted it was "largely broken" with no evidence of the fraud it purported to prevent.
