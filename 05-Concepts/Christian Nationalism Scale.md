## Definition

The Christian Nationalism Scale (CN Scale) is a six-item additive survey instrument developed by sociologists [[Andrew Whitehead]] and [[Samuel Perry]] to quantify the extent to which an individual holds Christian nationalist beliefs. It was introduced in peer-reviewed journal articles prior to 2020 and received its canonical formulation in their 2020 book *Taking America Back for God*. The scale enables researchers to place respondents on a continuum from strong rejection to strong embrace of Christian nationalism and to analyze how that position correlates with voting behavior, racial attitudes, gender ideology, and support for authoritarian politics.

The instrument represents a methodological advance over impressionistic accounts of Christian nationalism by providing a reproducible, validated measure that can be tracked across survey waves.

## Origin / history

Whitehead and Perry built the scale from six items in the [[Baylor Religion Survey]], a large national survey of American religious life conducted by Baylor University. The items were selected to capture the core claim of Christian nationalism: that the United States is, or should be, a Christian nation in a substantive civic and legal sense — not merely a society with a Christian cultural heritage.

The scale has been replicated and extended by other researchers. It has been used in studies of COVID-19 compliance, January 6 participation attitudes, anti-vaccine sentiment, and authoritarian political preferences.

## How it works

Respondents indicate agreement on a 5-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree, 5 = strongly agree) with six statements:

1. "The federal government should declare the United States a Christian nation."
2. "The federal government should advocate Christian values."
3. "The federal government should enforce strict separation of church and state." *(reverse coded)*
4. "The federal government should allow religious symbols in public spaces."
5. "The federal government should allow prayer in public schools."
6. "The success of the United States is part of God's plan."

Scores are summed to produce a 0–24 range (after zero-centering), with higher scores indicating greater Christian nationalist adherence. The reverse-coded item (#3) distinguishes CN from generic religiosity: highly religious people who affirm church-state separation score lower on the CN Scale even if they score high on other measures of religious practice.

## Application in this domain

In the context of this vault's focus on the theocratic wing and influence operations:

- The scale provides empirical grounding for claims about how large the Christian nationalist constituency actually is, avoiding both over- and under-estimation.
- It establishes that CN belief is measurable and predictive: Ambassadors (highest tier) show the strongest support for authoritarian candidates and for the use of state power to enforce Christian norms.
- It is the methodological foundation for the [[Whitehead Perry CN typology]] (Ambassadors / Accommodators / Resisters / Rejecters).
- Research using the scale has documented that CN correlates with racial resentment, opposition to immigration, and support for January 6, 2021 — findings relevant to understanding the theocratic wing's political behavior.

## Examples

- Ambassadors (score 18–24, ~19.8% of the adult population in 2020 data) constitute the most politically active Christian nationalist bloc; Whitehead and Perry document their outsized role in mobilizing for Trump in 2016 and 2020.
- The scale has been used by researchers at [[PRRI]] and others to monitor shifts in CN sentiment over time.
- A 2021 study found that the scale predicted willingness to change one's vote for Trump in 2020 based on perceived Christian nationalist stakes.

## Effectiveness / critique

**Strengths:** The scale is validated, reproducible, and has been widely adopted in peer-reviewed literature. Its use of Baylor Religion Survey data means it draws on a large, nationally representative sample.

**Critiques:**
- Some researchers argue the scale conflates distinct dimensions (e.g., "ceremonial deism" vs. genuine theocratic intent) and call for "conceptual splitting" (Smith and Adler, 2022).
- The 2023 Li study (*Journal for the Scientific Study of Religion*) proposed separating "religious traditionalism" from "Christian statism," suggesting the six-item scale may bundle two empirically distinct syndromes.
- The scale was developed from items available in the Baylor survey rather than items purpose-built for CN measurement, which limits construct validity claims.
- Critics from within conservative Christianity argue the scale operationalizes a contested political label as a neutral empirical one.

The critiques are substantive and ongoing; the scale remains the most widely used instrument in the field but should be understood as a research tool, not a definitive classifier.
