# Rent-seeking

## Definition

Rent-seeking is the use of resources to obtain wealth through the political process rather than by producing goods or services — securing a subsidy, a tariff, a license, a monopoly grant, or a regulation that hampers competitors. The "rent" is income created not by adding value but by manipulating the legal and political environment. Crucially, rent-seeking is socially wasteful in two ways: the resulting privilege itself misallocates resources, *and* the effort spent competing for the privilege is itself a dead loss.

## Origin / history

The concept was originated by [[Gordon Tullock]] in his 1967 paper "The Welfare Costs of Tariffs, Monopolies, and Theft." The label "rent-seeking" was coined later, in 1974, by development economist Anne Krueger in her study of import-license economies. The two together made it one of the foundational ideas of public choice theory, alongside [[Gordon Tullock]] and [[James M. Buchanan]]'s broader work in [[constitutional economics]].

## How it works

Tullock's central insight was a correction to standard monopoly analysis. Traditional economics measured the social cost of a monopoly as the "deadweight loss triangle" — the value of trades that don't happen because prices are too high. Tullock pointed at the *profit rectangle* instead: the monopolist's excess profit. He asked what self-interested actors would do to capture that rectangle. The answer: they would spend real resources — lobbying, legal fees, campaign contributions, bribes — up to the value of the prize. That competitive expenditure, the **"Tullock rectangle,"** is pure social waste, added on top of the conventional deadweight loss.

The mechanism generalizes: wherever government can create a valuable privilege (a protected market, a license, a regulatory barrier), rational actors will invest in obtaining it. The cumulative spending produces no new value; it merely redistributes existing wealth and consumes resources in the contest. In aggregate, the total cost of rent-seeking can exceed the value of the rents themselves.

## Application in this domain

Rent-seeking is a load-bearing concept for the vault's patron wing. It supplied the public-choice movement with a powerful rhetorical and analytic tool: any government program, subsidy, or regulation can be reframed as a captured "rent" rather than a public good, and any interest group — unions, regulated industries, welfare recipients — can be cast as a rent-seeker. This reframing underwrites the case for [[constitutional capture]]: if ordinary politics is structurally a rent-seeking competition, then policy should be locked into constitutional rules beyond the reach of that competition. The concept is genuinely double-edged, however — it is equally usable to critique corporate lobbying, [[American Legislative Exchange Council]]-style model legislation, and the donor networks themselves, all of which are textbook rent-seeking by Tullock's own definition.

## Examples

- [[Regulatory capture]] — a captured agency is a vehicle for industry rent-seeking.
- [[American Legislative Exchange Council]] — industry-drafted model bills seeking favorable regulation.
- Tariffs, occupational licensing, and monopoly franchises — standard textbook cases.
- Corporate lobbying for tax carve-outs — large-scale contemporary rent-seeking.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed validity:** Widely accepted in economics; the Tullock "welfare cost of rent-seeking" insight is a standard result.
- **Empirical evidence:** Robust evidence across trade protection, licensing, and monopoly franchises.
- **Critique:** Critics note the concept is often applied selectively — used to attack social spending and labor while ignoring corporate and donor-network rent-seeking. Some economists also argue measuring the "Tullock rectangle" empirically is difficult, since not all of a prize's value is necessarily dissipated in the contest.
