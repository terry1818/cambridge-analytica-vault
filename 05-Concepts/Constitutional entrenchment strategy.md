## Definition

Constitutional entrenchment strategy refers to the deliberate effort to embed preferred political and economic policies in constitutional or quasi-constitutional frameworks — supermajority rules, explicit fiscal limitations, strengthened property rights provisions, structural changes to the amendment and legislative processes — that are resistant to reversal through ordinary majority-vote democracy. The goal is to make policy outcomes durable beyond electoral cycles: even if political coalitions shift, the constitutional framework constrains what future majorities can do.

This vault uses the term to describe both the intellectual tradition (primarily James Buchanan's constitutional political economy) and its operational expression in the Koch network's infrastructure — including the [[Convention of States Action]] campaign as its current most ambitious vehicle.

## Origin / History

The intellectual foundations are in James Buchanan and Gordon Tullock's *The Calculus of Consent: Logical Foundations of Constitutional Democracy* (1962), which analyzed constitutional rules as the product of rational choice under a "veil of uncertainty." Buchanan argued that constitutional constraints — including supermajority requirements — serve rational individuals better than simple majority rule for decisions with significant distributional consequences.

Buchanan's later *The Limits of Liberty* (1975) and *Constitutional Economics* essays more directly addressed the problem of fiscal excess in democratic government, calling for a constitutional balanced-budget amendment. These arguments were developed through the [[Thomas Jefferson Center]], [[Center for Study of Public Choice]], and associated scholars.

The operational translation began with:
- **1978–1982**: Koch network engagement with Buchanan; early funding of constitutional economics research
- **1980s**: Balanced Budget Amendment push in Congress (never achieved two-thirds majority)
- **1990s–2000s**: State-level Taxpayer Bill of Rights (TABOR) campaigns (Colorado, 1992) testing fiscal entrenchment
- **2010s**: [[Convention of States Action]] launch (2010: BBA Task Force; 2013: COSA proper) as the most ambitious Article V campaign in US history
- **2025–present**: Post-Trump 2.0 acceleration with ALEC support and [[Project 2025]] alignment documented in EXPOSEDbyCMD reporting

## How It Works

The strategy has three interdependent layers:

**1. Intellectual legitimation**: Fund academic work (Buchanan's institutions, [[Mercatus Center]], [[Heritage Foundation]]) that frames constitutional constraints on government as economically rational and normatively desirable, rather than as interest-group outcomes. This provides a scholarly vocabulary that insulates advocacy from dismissal as mere class interest.

**2. Legal pathway development**: Use the [[Federalist Society]]-to-judiciary pipeline and [[Law and Economics movement]] to place judges and legal scholars favorable to expansive property rights, economic liberty doctrine, and originalist interpretations that resist regulatory expansion. Create the legal-intellectual environment in which constitutional entrenchment arguments are receivable by courts.

**3. Article V campaign**: Pursue an actual constitutional convention through the Article V process, using state legislatures (more conservative than Congress, and subject to [[Koch Network]] and [[American Legislative Exchange Council]] influence) to bypass the federal legislative process. If 34 states pass applications on a similar subject matter, Congress must call a convention; 38 states can ratify amendments. As of 2026, the COS campaign has passed applications in 19 states (exact count varies; contested applications exist).

Nancy MacLean argues this three-layer strategy represents a coherent, decades-long plan to entrench minority-rule economics beyond democratic reversal. Critics of MacLean, including several academic economists and legal scholars, dispute the degree of coordination and intent she attributes to the actors, and contest her reading of Buchanan's texts as anti-democratic rather than constitutionalist.

**Vault evidence note**: The documented facts — Koch network funding of Buchanan-associated institutions, COSA campaign architecture, ALEC coordination — are Tier-1. MacLean's interpretive claim that these constitute a unified conspiratorial plan to defeat democracy is Tier-3 (her inference, contested).

## Examples

- Colorado TABOR (1992): constitutional balanced-budget constraint; serves as model and proof of concept
- [[Convention of States Action]]: active Article V campaign targeting fiscal, regulatory, and term-limits amendments
- **Janus v. AFSCME** (2018): Supreme Court decision eliminating mandatory union fees for public employees — a structural weakening of organized labor's political funding capacity, reducing the opposition's financial infrastructure
- State legislative redistricting entrenchment: REDMAP (2010) Republican state legislature campaigns to control post-Census redistricting

## Effectiveness / Critique

**Effectiveness**: The strategy has had substantial partial success. The federal judiciary has been reshaped; several states have enacted fiscal limitations; the Article V campaign is closer to 34-state threshold than at any prior point.

**Critique**: The Article V convention pathway carries risk for its own proponents: a "runaway convention" could adopt amendments not intended by the sponsoring coalition. Constitutional law scholars across the political spectrum note the uncertainty about convention procedures, scope, and ratification dynamics. The Brennan Center and Common Cause have extensively documented these risks from a progressive perspective.
