# Concept

## Definition

The conservative complicity thesis is Robert O. Paxton's central claim about *how* fascist movements achieve state power. The thesis holds that:

1. Fascist movements **cannot approach power** without the active assistance of mainstream conservatives willing to sacrifice the rule of law for security and order.
2. Conservatives partner with fascists not because they share fascist ideology but because they fear the Left more than they fear the fascists — and because they believe, incorrectly, that they can use and then control the fascist movement.
3. The decisive moment is Stage 3 of Paxton's model: the **invitation into coalition government**. Fascists are not installed by a coup against the resistance of the establishment; they are *invited in* by establishment figures who hold the keys.
4. Once inside power, fascists outmaneuver their conservative partners and consolidate control — but the moral and political responsibility for enabling the regime rests substantially with the conservatives who opened the door.

Paxton states the thesis directly: fascist parties "could not approach power without the complicity of conservatives willing to sacrifice the rule of law for security." He further identifies a specific warning sign: "Fascists are close to power when conservatives begin to borrow their techniques, appeal to their 'mobilizing passions,' and try to co-opt the fascist following."

## Origin / History

The thesis emerged from Paxton's comparative study of fascist success and failure across interwar Europe. His 1972 *Vichy France: Old Guard and New Order* was an early formulation: the Vichy regime was not imposed by German occupiers alone but was actively constructed by French conservatives who saw in defeat an opportunity to reverse the Third Republic's liberal order. The generalization across Italy, Germany, Hungary, Romania, and Spain appears in the 1998 *Journal of Modern History* article and is fully developed in *The Anatomy of Fascism* (2004). The thesis was controversial with conservative historians who resisted the implied indictment of the respectable Right.

## How It Works

The mechanism has several interlocking components:

**a) Political deadlock as precondition.** Conservative parties find themselves unable to govern majorities on their own and face a growing electoral Left they consider unacceptable as a governing partner. A choice presents itself: democratic accommodation of the Left or authoritarian partnership with the fascists. They choose the latter.

**b) Underestimation.** Conservative elites — Hindenburg and the Junker class in Germany; Giolitti, Victor Emmanuel III, and Italian liberals — believe fascist leaders are vulgarians who can be tamed, used to smash the Left, and then discarded. Paxton documents how consistently this calculation fails.

**c) Legitimation function.** The conservative invitation provides the fascist movement with crucial legitimacy it could not obtain by force alone. Being invited into government, appearing in cabinet photographs alongside respectable figures, signals to the public and to state institutions (army, civil service, courts) that the fascist movement is an acceptable governing force.

**d) The complicity gradient.** Paxton distinguishes between active collaboration (Hindenburg's appointment of Hitler; Victor Emmanuel's refusal to sign martial law orders against the March on Rome) and passive acquiescence (business communities that funded both mainstream parties and fascist movements as insurance; courts that declined to prosecute political violence). Both forms of complicity matter.

**e) Borrowing as a warning signal.** Before the formal invitation, there is a diagnostic: mainstream conservatives begin *borrowing* fascist rhetoric, organizational methods, and popular mobilization techniques — treating them as useful electoral tools. This borrowing both normalizes fascist politics and signals to the fascist movement that it has leverage.

## Application in This Domain

This vault uses the conservative complicity thesis to structure inquiry into the post-2020/2024 US alignment. The analytic questions are:

- Did elements of the established Republican Party function as the "traditional conservative elites" in Paxton's model — inviting or enabling an authoritarian-nationalist movement into power rather than containing it?
- Is there evidence of the "borrowing" signal: mainstream conservatives adopting movement rhetoric, techniques, or mobilizing passions to co-opt the movement's following?
- What is the role of institutional conservatives — federal judiciary, donor class, business community — in legitimating rather than checking the movement?
- How does [[Hypothesis - Three-wing coalition]] map onto the complicity structure? A three-wing coalition (movement base, establishment enablers, institutional legitimators) is structurally consistent with Paxton's model.
- Does [[Convention of States Action]] represent an institutionalized form of conservative complicity in constitutional restructuring — providing respectable legal cover for a more radical political project? See [[Lens - COS as Constitutional Capture Vector]].

**Evidentiary discipline:** The thesis is a framework for structuring evidence, not a conclusion. The vault should record: (a) documented instances of establishment conservative actors choosing movement alliance over democratic opposition; (b) documented instances of rhetorical and tactical borrowing; (c) counterevidence of genuine conservative resistance. Tier all evidence.

## Examples

**Historical (Tier 1 — well-established)**
- *Germany, 1933:* Hindenburg's appointment of Hitler as Chancellor on January 30, 1933 is the paradigm case. German conservatives (Papen, Hugenberg, the Junker class) calculated they could dominate a coalition cabinet where Nazis held only 3 of 11 posts. Within weeks, the calculation failed.
- *Italy, 1922:* Victor Emmanuel III declined to sign a martial law order that would have dispersed Mussolini's March on Rome. Instead, he invited Mussolini to form a government. Italian liberals voted for the Acerbo Law (1923), cementing fascist electoral dominance.

**Partially analogous (Tier 3 — contested, for investigation)**
- *US, 2016–2025:* The GOP establishment's post-2016 accommodation; Senate confirmation of nominees advancing movement priorities; post-January 6 decisions by congressional conservatives not to convict. Whether these rise to "complicity" in Paxton's sense or constitute normal democratic politics is disputed and is a core analytical question for this vault.

## Effectiveness / Critique

**Strengths:** The thesis explains why fascism succeeded in some countries (where conservative establishments chose partnership) and failed in others (where they did not). It is empirically grounded in well-documented historical sequences.

**Criticisms:**
- In cases where the conservative party *merges with* or *becomes* the movement rather than partnering with it as a distinct entity, the complicity framing may not fully apply — the distinction between conservative enabler and fascist movement collapses.
- The thesis may place too much causal weight on elite decisions and too little on structural forces (economic crisis, state failure, mass radicalization) that constrain elite choices.
- Applying the framework to a two-party system where there is no numerically distinct fascist party creates analytic complications absent from the interwar European cases.
