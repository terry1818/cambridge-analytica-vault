## Definition

Biblical capitalism is the theological claim that laissez-faire, deregulated, free-market capitalism is ordained by scripture and that government intervention in the economy — through regulation, taxation, labor protections, or welfare programs — therefore constitutes interference with God's visible hand in human affairs. In its strongest form, the doctrine holds that poverty is a consequence of individual sin or spiritual failure, and that redistribution is not merely bad policy but an affront to divine law.

Jeff Sharlet's *The Family* (2008) coins and deploys the term as a precise descriptor of the theological-economic synthesis promoted by [[The Fellowship]] and related elite fundamentalist networks: Christianity applied to economics produces not the social gospel of the poor but the prosperity and power of the wealthy as evidence of divine favor.

## Origin / History

Biblical capitalism draws on several strands:

1. **Calvinist prosperity theology** — the idea that financial success signals divine election (Max Weber's "Protestant ethic" thesis in its vulgarized popular form)
2. **Christian Reconstructionism** — R.J. Rushdoony and Gary North's framework that Biblical law provides the correct template for all social and economic organization, including property rights, contracts, and the abolition of usury laws
3. **The Fellowship's anti-labor origins** — [[The Fellowship]] (the Family) was founded in 1935 by Abraham Vereide explicitly as a Christian counterweight to the labor movement; its founding theology framed union organizing as godless collectivism

The concept was formalized in practice before it had a label. In the 1930s and 1940s, American business leaders and sympathetic clergy developed a vocabulary that presented the New Deal and labor organizing as spiritually as well as economically dangerous. This infrastructure — including the National Association of Manufacturers' use of clergy as business spokespeople — is documented by historians including Kevin Kruse (*One Nation Under God*, 2015).

## How It Works

Biblical capitalism operates through several mechanisms:

- **Scriptural proof-texting**: Key passages (the parable of talents, Romans 13, Proverbs on diligence) are cited as endorsing private property, entrepreneurship, and submission to earthly authority including economic authority
- **Delegitimizing opposition**: Labor organizing, welfare policy, and progressive taxation are framed not as policy disagreements but as rebellion against God-ordained order
- **Elite theological cultivation**: [[The Fellowship]] in particular operated by cultivating congressmen, CEOs, and military leaders in a theology that told them their wealth and power were signs of divine favor and that they therefore had an obligation to use that power to resist "godless" economic regulation
- **Pastoral deployment**: Organizations like [[American Family Association]] and Salem Communications broadcast this framing to mass audiences, connecting individual economic aspiration to spiritual identity

## Examples

- [[The Fellowship]]'s founding by Abraham Vereide in 1935 explicitly as an anti-labor movement
- The National Prayer Breakfast's consistent promotion of pro-business, anti-regulation theology
- Christian Reconstructionist Gary North's biblical economics texts arguing for gold standard, abolition of central banking, and elimination of welfare on scriptural grounds
- The Heritage Foundation's and American Enterprise Institute's use of religiously inflected language to frame market fundamentalism as morally and spiritually superior to welfare-state alternatives

## Effectiveness / Critique

Biblical capitalism has been highly effective as a political coalition-binding ideology. It allows economic libertarians and cultural traditionalists to find common cause on a theological basis rather than merely on shared political interest, creating a durable alliance between business conservatives and evangelical voters.

Critics — including progressive evangelicals, mainstream biblical scholars, and historians — argue that the biblical capitalism reading is highly selective and implausible: the Hebrew Bible contains extensive economic regulation (jubilee laws, gleaning obligations, prohibitions on interest), and the New Testament's economic passages (Acts 2–4 on communal sharing; Jesus on wealth and the kingdom) are at least as compatible with socialist as with free-market readings. The use of biblical authority to sacralize laissez-faire is therefore theologically contested, not established.
