# Article V aggregation problem

## Definition

The **Article V aggregation problem** refers to the unresolved constitutional and procedural question of whether state applications for an Article V constitutional convention, submitted at different times and with different wording, purposes, and scopes, can be counted together toward the 34-state (two-thirds of state legislatures) threshold that would trigger Congress's obligation to call a convention.

Article V states that Congress "shall call a Convention for proposing Amendments" when "the Legislatures of two thirds of the several States" apply for one. The text says nothing about whether the applications must be identical in wording, contemporaneous, or limited to the same subject. This silence creates the aggregation problem.

## Origin / History

The aggregation problem did not become pressing until three major Article V campaigns began simultaneously accumulating state applications in the 21st century:
1. **[[Balanced Budget Amendment Task Force]]**: Seeks a balanced-budget amendment (BBA); has applications from approximately 27–28 states, some dating to the 1970s–1990s.
2. **[[Convention of States Action]]**: Seeks a broader multi-topic convention covering fiscal restraints, federal overreach, and term limits.
3. **[[U.S. Term Limits]]**: Seeks a single-subject convention limited to congressional term limits.

Each set of applications uses different language and envisions a different convention. None, individually, has reached 34 states. The aggregation question is whether Congress could count applications from all three campaigns together.

Feingold and Prindiville in [[Source - Feingold 2022 - The Constitution in Jeopardy]] treat the aggregation problem as one of the two critical unresolved legal questions (alongside the [[Article V delegate-selection vacuum]]) that make the Article V convention path constitutionally dangerous. Their concern is not merely theoretical: a sufficiently motivated Congress (for example, one with a strong ideological interest in calling a convention) could declare 34 states' worth of applications sufficient by aggregating disparate applications, and it is not clear what judicial check would apply.

The SCOTUS case [[Dillon v Gloss 1921]] is indirectly relevant: its "contemporaneous consensus" doctrine implies that applications reflecting the political will of radically different eras should not be aggregated — but *Coleman v. Miller* (1939) subsequently held that ratification timeliness questions are political, not judicial, raising the risk that aggregation decisions would also be treated as non-justiciable.

## How It Works

**The threshold**: Article V requires applications from two-thirds of state legislatures (34 of 50 states) to call a convention.

**The gap between applications**: Current applications span three campaigns with different subject matters and different operative language. If every state that has filed *any* Article V application for *any* purpose since 1960 were counted together, some analysts argue the threshold could be approached or exceeded.

**The role of Congress**: Once 34 applications are filed, Congress "shall call" a convention. If Congress declares the threshold met by aggregating dissimilar applications, there is no explicit constitutional mechanism for judicial review — especially post-*Coleman v. Miller*.

**The wording variation**: Even within a single campaign (e.g., the BBA campaign), different states passed applications with different operative language, different conditions, and different time periods. This creates a sub-problem: even same-topic applications may not be aggregable if their language is substantively different.

## Application in This Domain

In the [[Lens - COS as Constitutional Capture Vector]] analysis, the aggregation problem is central: the right-wing Article V campaign does not need to reach 34 states uniformly on any single topic. It needs only for a sympathetic Congress to declare the threshold met by combining applications from COS, BBATF, USTL, and older outstanding applications. Post-2024, with Republican trifectas in 28+ states, the political conditions for such a declaration have improved substantially.

The aggregation problem also interacts with the [[Hypothesis - COS is the permanent capture arm of Project 2025]]: if Project 2025's goal is structural constitutional change, the aggregation problem is a *feature*, not a bug — a threshold that can be reached through political will rather than constitutional consensus.

## Examples

- Mississippi's BBA application prohibits congressional appropriations exceeding revenues AND requires national debt repayment within a specified timeline. Other states' BBA applications contain neither the debt-repayment requirement nor the same spending-limitation language. Congress would face a genuine question whether these count as "same subject" applications.
- COS applications call for a convention on "fiscal restraints on the federal government, limiting the power and jurisdiction of the federal government, and limiting the terms of office for its officials and for members of Congress" — a three-topic scope incompatible with the BBATF's single-BBA scope.

## Effectiveness / Critique

**COS/BBATF position**: The aggregation problem is manageable because the organizations are working toward identical or near-identical application language within their own campaigns, and Congress will make a good-faith determination.

**Feingold/Prindiville position**: Congress's determination would be a purely political act with no binding legal standard. If the determining Congress has an ideological interest in calling a convention, it would face no effective judicial check.

**Scholarly positions**: The Congressional Research Service (CRS Report R42589) has documented the aggregation problem without resolving it. The Article V Information Center (a pro-convention resource) acknowledges that differing language "gives Congress an excuse to refuse to aggregate" but frames this as a problem for convention proponents to solve through language uniformity — not as a constitutional constraint per se.
