# Mobilizing Passions

## Definition

Robert O. Paxton's term for the cluster of emotional dispositions that drive fascist movements and bind followers to the leader. In Paxton's framework, fascism is propelled far more by "gut" feeling than by reasoned doctrine. The mobilizing passions function as the ideological substrate of fascism — not a formal program but a set of shared emotional states that make collective action possible without logical consistency.

## Origin / History

Paxton introduced the concept in his 1998 *Journal of Modern History* article "The Five Stages of Fascism" and elaborated it in *The Anatomy of Fascism* (Knopf, 2004). The argument was a direct challenge to the historiographical tradition that sought a coherent fascist ideology comparable to liberalism or socialism. Paxton contended that looking for doctrine misses what is actually distinctive: the emotional repertoire that fascism mobilizes.

## How It Works

Paxton identifies several core passions, including:

1. **Sense of overwhelming crisis** beyond reach of traditional solutions.
2. **Primacy of the group** — duties to the collective supersede individual rights.
3. **Victimhood** — the group has been humiliated, betrayed, or weakened; this sentiment justifies action without legal or ethical limits against enemies.
4. **Dread of decadence** — anxiety about the group's degeneration under cosmopolitan liberalism and individualism.
5. **Need for closer integration** — a brotherhood forged by conviction or, where needed, by exclusionary violence.
6. **Compensatory cult of unity, energy, and purity** expressed through charismatic leadership and mass ritual.
7. **Right to domination** — one's own community is naturally superior and entitled to override others without guilt.

These passions, not a platform, are what fascist movements offer. They explain why fascism can simultaneously contradict itself on economics or foreign policy while maintaining cohesion.

## Application in This Domain

This framework allows the vault to test political movements against an emotional-behavioral checklist rather than a doctrinal one. When evaluating whether a contemporary movement exhibits fascist *tendencies*, the question to ask is: which of the mobilizing passions are present and how intense? The absence of an explicit fascist manifesto is not exculpatory — Paxton would predict the absence of one. The vault can map specific rhetoric (e.g., victimhood claims, crisis framing, in-group purity language) against the passion list and note density and escalation over time.

## Examples

- Mussolini's squadrismo combined victimhood (Italy betrayed at Versailles), group primacy, and violence as brotherhood-building.
- The January 6, 2021 Capitol assault has been analyzed through the passions lens: stolen election = victimhood; "real Americans" = group primacy; crisis framing.
- Paxton himself in later interviews (2021 onward) stated that Trump's actions after the 2020 election led him to revise his prior reluctance to apply the fascism label to American politics.

## Effectiveness / Critique

Paxton's framework is widely influential in fascism studies but draws several critiques:

- **Over-broadness**: Critics argue the passions list could describe populist movements that are not fascist — nationalism, victimhood, and group solidarity appear across the political spectrum.
- **No threshold specified**: Paxton does not say how many passions, or at what intensity, qualify a movement as fascist rather than merely authoritarian-nationalist.
- **Omits structural factors**: The framework emphasizes psychology and emotion; critics from a structural tradition argue it underweights the role of class alliance, capitalist crisis, and institutional breakdown.
- **Application to US contested**: Many comparative scholars (Levitsky, Ziblatt, Norris, Inglehart) argue that applying the fascism label to contemporary US politics inflates it and obscures the more precise diagnosis of democratic backsliding or competitive authoritarianism.
