# Mutual Toleration and Institutional Forbearance

## Definition

Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt's central analytic pair from *How Democracies Die* (2018). **Mutual toleration** is the norm that political competitors — however much they disagree — accept each other as legitimate rivals who have an equal right to contest and hold power. **Institutional forbearance** is the norm of self-restraint in the use of constitutional powers: acting within the spirit rather than merely the letter of the law, even when the letter would permit aggressive power seizure.

Together, these are the "soft guardrails" — unwritten norms that prevent day-to-day political competition from escalating into a zero-sum war of annihilation.

## Origin / History

Levitsky and Ziblatt derived the framework from their comparative study of democratic erosion across Latin America and Europe during the 20th century, published in *How Democracies Die* (Crown, 2018). The book became a widely read scholarly-public crossover and the norms framework was adopted quickly in political science teaching and journalism. The pair elaborated related structural arguments in *Tyranny of the Minority* (Crown, 2023).

## How It Works

**Mutual toleration** breaks down when political actors begin to treat opponents as existential threats — enemies of the nation, criminals, or traitors — rather than legitimate competitors. Once rivals are existentially delegitimized, the logic shifts: normal electoral defeat becomes catastrophic, which licenses extraordinary countermeasures.

**Institutional forbearance** breaks down when actors use every legal tool available to gain advantage, regardless of precedent or effect on the system's functioning. Examples include: packing courts, weaponizing investigations, exploiting legislative technicalities, refusing to seat opponents' nominees, or using emergency decrees for routine governance.

The two norms are interdependent: politicians who view rivals as illegitimate are less likely to show forbearance, and norm violations compound — once one side breaks a guardrail, the other faces pressure to retaliate in kind, creating a ratchet of escalation.

## Application in This Domain

The vault can use this framework to date the onset of democratic erosion with more precision than broad characterizations like "authoritarianism." Specific events — a party refusing to seat a duly elected rival, a Senate leadership blocking a Supreme Court nomination for a year, a president characterizing political opponents as criminals deserving imprisonment — can each be evaluated against these norms and coded as **mutual toleration failure** or **institutional forbearance failure**. The framework does not require labeling the endpoint; it maps the process.

## Examples

- The US Senate's refusal to hold hearings on the Merrick Garland nomination (2016) — forbearance failure.
- Trump's characterization of Hillary Clinton ("Lock her up") and of political opponents as enemies of the state — mutual toleration failure.
- The January 6, 2021 effort to reverse a certified electoral outcome — compound failure of both norms.
- Levitsky and Ziblatt point to the US South's refusal to accept Republican governance after 1860 as an early mutual toleration failure that produced civil war.

## Effectiveness / Critique

- **Normative loading**: Critics note that the norms framework implicitly privileges stability over justice — historically, norm-breaking by minority parties has sometimes been necessary to achieve democratic inclusion (e.g., Civil Rights movement).
- **US-centric blind spot**: Some scholars argue L&Z's framework is calibrated to the specific history of US and European liberal democracy and may misread cases where these "norms" masked exclusion of large populations.
- **Asymmetry ignored**: Critics from the left argue L&Z's framework treats norm erosion as bipartisan or symmetric when the empirical record of recent decades is heavily one-sided; L&Z have responded to this in later writing.
- **Descriptive, less predictive**: The framework describes erosion well in retrospect but offers limited tools for predicting when a ratchet becomes irreversible.
