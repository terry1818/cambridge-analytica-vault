# Sons of the soil

## Definition

"Sons of the soil" refers to a conflict pattern in which a group that identifies as indigenous or historically dominant in a region perceives that in-migration by outsiders — often members of a nationally dominant ethnic group supported by the state — is displacing them from land, livelihoods, and political power. The "sons of the soil" group responds with organised violence directed at migrants and/or the state. Barbara Walter uses this concept in *How Civil Wars Start* (2022) as one of the two key factors — alongside [[Anocracy]] — that predict civil war onset. The dynamic is not merely about migration; it is about *perceived status loss by a group that considers itself the rightful occupants of a place*.

## Origin / history

The term originates in the political science of ethnic conflict. Myron Weiner used it in 1978 to describe conflicts in India over regional land rights. The concept was systematised empirically by James Fearon and David Laitin in their 2011 paper "Sons of the Soil, Migrants, and Civil War" (*World Development*), which analysed 139 cases of civil war from 1949–2008 and found that approximately 22–25% of ethnic civil wars followed the sons-of-the-soil pattern. Walter draws on this literature and extends it to the American context, arguing that white Americans' perception of demographic displacement constitutes an analogous dynamic.

## How it works

The escalation sequence identified in the literature:

1. **In-migration**: State-sponsored or economically driven migration brings an outside group into a region historically dominated by another group.
2. **Competition**: Migrants compete for land, jobs, political representation, or cultural recognition — areas the incumbent group had monopolised.
3. **Grievance formation**: The incumbent group frames migrants as invaders or usurpers, constructs an identity narrative centred on prior claim ("we were here first"), and perceives state support for migrants as betrayal.
4. **Mobilisation**: Political entrepreneurs among the incumbent group organise the grievance into political or paramilitary action.
5. **Violence**: Attacks on migrants; state response; potential insurgency.

Walter connects this to the [[Anocracy]] variable: sons-of-the-soil conflicts are most dangerous in anocratic regimes that lack the institutional capacity to mediate grievances or suppress escalation, and which may actually signal openness to the incumbent group's demands.

## Application in this domain

Walter explicitly argues that the United States exhibits a sons-of-the-soil dynamic among a segment of white Americans who perceive demographic and cultural displacement. This is analytically useful for the vault in several ways: (1) it connects CA's psychographic targeting of "fears about cultural change" and "threats to white identity" to a documented conflict-risk variable; (2) it provides a cross-national comparative framework for the "Great Replacement" conspiracy theory as a form of grievance mobilisation; (3) it helps interpret the political significance of anti-immigration rhetoric and white nationalist movements as structural conflict factors rather than merely ideological phenomena. The concept should be used analytically — describing the *perceived* displacement dynamic — not as an endorsement of the incumbent group's claims.

## Examples

- **Assam, India**: Indigenous Assamese vs. Bengali-speaking migrants (1979–85 Assam agitation, 1983 Nellie massacre).
- **Moro, Philippines**: Muslim Moro indigenous population vs. Christian settlers moved to Mindanao under Marcos-era land programmes.
- **Abkhazia, Georgia**: Abkhaz minority vs. Georgian majority following Soviet-era settlement policies; contributed to 1992–93 war.
- **United States (Walter's application)**: White working-class perception of displacement by immigration and demographic change as a structural risk factor; combined with anocracy and organised factions (militia movement, January 6 networks) as civil conflict indicators.

## Effectiveness / critique

The empirical basis (Fearon & Laitin 2011; 139 cases) is solid within the civil war literature. Walter's application to the US has attracted significant scrutiny:

1. **Analogy limits**: The US case differs structurally from the paradigm cases (Assam, Mindanao) in scale, institutional capacity, and the fact that the "incumbent group" (white Americans) is the nationally dominant group, not a regionally subordinate one.
2. **Normative concern**: Some critics argue that framing white demographic anxiety as a "sons of the soil" dynamic risks legitimising the grievance narrative. Walter's response is that accurate description of a risk dynamic is not endorsement of the underlying claim.
3. **Measurement problem**: Identifying which population constitutes "sons of the soil" in a settler-colonial nation-state like the US involves contested historical judgments.
4. **Predictive track record**: The US did not descend into civil war following the 2020 election, raising questions about the model's predictive precision, though Walter would note the model identifies risk factors, not certainties.
