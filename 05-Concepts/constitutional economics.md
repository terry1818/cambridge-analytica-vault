# constitutional economics

## Definition

Constitutional economics (also called constitutional political economy) is a research program that applies economic analysis to the choice of constitutional rules — the durable "rules of the game" that constrain the choices of political and economic actors. It is deliberately distinct from orthodox economics: where ordinary economics studies how actors choose *within* a fixed set of rules, constitutional economics studies how the rules themselves are or should be selected. The program was pioneered by [[James M. Buchanan]] and is an outgrowth of public choice theory.

## Origin / history

The intellectual foundation is *The Calculus of Consent: Logical Foundations of Constitutional Democracy* (1962), co-authored by [[James M. Buchanan]] and [[Gordon Tullock]]. That book distinguished two levels of collective decision: the constitutional level (choosing rules) and the post-constitutional level (acting within them). Buchanan developed this into an explicit named program over the following decades, most fully in *The Reason of Rules: Constitutional Political Economy* (1985, with Geoffrey Brennan). Buchanan received the 1986 Nobel Memorial Prize "for his development of the contractual and constitutional bases for the theory of economic and political decision-making." The program drew on the older classical-liberal conviction — traceable through [[Friedrich Hayek]] and [[Ludwig von Mises]] — that liberty depends on rules that limit what government can do.

## How it works

The core move is a shift in the unit of analysis from policies to rules. Because public choice theory holds that politicians and bureaucrats pursue self-interest, and that democratic processes therefore systematically produce [[Government failure]] and [[Rent-seeking]], constitutional economics argues that the only durable protection is to fix the rules in advance — behind a "veil of uncertainty" where no one knows their future position — so that majorities cannot later exploit minorities. Mechanisms it favors include constitutionally entrenched balanced-budget requirements, supermajority thresholds for taxing and spending, and explicit limits on federal jurisdiction. The technical apparatus is contractarian: rules are legitimate if rational individuals would have agreed to them at the constitutional stage.

## Application in this domain

For this vault, constitutional economics is the academic engine room of the [[constitutional capture]] project. It supplies the respectable, Nobel-credentialed argument that democratic majorities *should* be permanently constrained — converting a political preference (protecting wealth and markets from redistribution) into a claim about good institutional design. Nancy MacLean's *Democracy in Chains* argues that this scholarly program was operationalized into a political strategy by Buchanan's [[Koch Network]]-funded institutional ecosystem at George Mason University. The [[Convention of States]] movement is the most direct present-day application: it seeks to write fiscal-restraint and jurisdiction-limiting rules into the Constitution itself, exactly the kind of constitutional-stage entrenchment the program prescribes.

## Examples

- [[Convention of States]] — campaign to entrench fiscal and jurisdictional limits via an Article V amendments convention.
- [[American Legislative Exchange Council]] — circulates model legislation embodying balanced-budget and tax-limitation rules.
- [[Mercatus Center]] — Koch-funded GMU institution applying public-choice and constitutional-economics analysis to policy.
- State-level supermajority tax requirements and TABOR-style spending caps.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed merit:** Proponents argue that pre-committing to stable rules curbs rent-seeking, fiscal irresponsibility, and majoritarian predation.
- **Academic critique:** Critics including legal scholars on class entrenchment argue that "neutral" constitutional restraints are not neutral — tying the state's distributional hands implicitly locks in pre-existing market inequality, producing *class entrenchment* for the wealthy. The contractarian "veil of uncertainty" is challenged as a fiction that disguises the interests of actual funders.
- **Contested interpretation:** MacLean's claim that the program was *consciously designed* to entrench minority privilege is disputed by critics (Farrell, Teles, Magness, Munger). This vault treats constitutional economics as a real and influential research program (documented fact) and MacLean's strong-intent reading as contested interpretation.
