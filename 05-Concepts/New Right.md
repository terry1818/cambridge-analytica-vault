# New Right

## Definition

The **New Right** is the name for the American conservative movement that coalesced in the early-to-mid 1970s — a network of activists and organizations that built an effective grassroots political force for the Republican Party out of existing conservative networks. It was defined less by a new ideology than by new *methods*: computerized direct-mail fundraising, single-issue organizing, and the deliberate political mobilization of evangelical Christians. It is distinct from the older Goldwater-era "Old Right" and, critically, from the unrelated 2010s–2020s neo-reactionary movement also called the "New Right" (see disambiguation below).

## Origin / history

The 1970s New Right emerged from the wreckage of Barry Goldwater's 1964 presidential defeat; nearly all its leaders had organized for Goldwater. It crystallized between 1973 and 1975 around four or five strategists:

- **[[Richard Viguerie]]** — the direct-mail fundraising pioneer who financed the movement and built its donor lists.
- **[[Paul Weyrich]]** — the institution-builder, founder of the Heritage Foundation and the Committee for the Survival of a Free Congress.
- **[[Howard Phillips]]** — the grassroots organizer, founder of The Conservative Caucus (1974).
- **Terry Dolan** — head of the National Conservative Political Action Committee (NCPAC), which pioneered independent-expenditure attack campaigns.
- **Morton Blackwell** — a Viguerie associate and Republican insider who revived the Conservative Political Action Conference (CPAC).

Weyrich began hosting coalition meetings in 1973; Viguerie ran parallel strategy sessions. The movement organized around opposition to abortion, gay rights, the Equal Rights Amendment, the Panama Canal treaties, affirmative action, and taxation.

## How it works

The New Right's central innovation was *infrastructure*. Rather than relying on candidates or the party apparatus, it built permanent, self-funding institutions: think tanks (Heritage), PACs (NCPAC), grassroots organizations (Conservative Caucus), and the direct-mail machinery that paid for all of it. Its second innovation was the strategic recruitment of religious conservatives. Weyrich, Phillips, Viguerie and Dolan worked together to persuade the Rev. Jerry Falwell to launch the Moral Majority in 1979 — the moment evangelical Protestantism was organized as a Republican voting bloc, the founding event of the modern religious right. The movement's methods proved decisive in Ronald Reagan's 1980 victory.

## Application in this domain

The 1970s New Right is an upstream node for several of this vault's threads. It built the organizational template — donor-funded permanent institutions, single-issue mobilization, the politicized evangelical bloc — that later carried the theocratic wing, the patron wing, and the constitutional-restoration project. [[Howard Phillips]]'s later [[Constitution Party]] and the broader push toward constitutional originalism descend directly from this milieu. The Moral Majority's voter-mobilization machinery is an ancestor of the targeting operations the vault examines.

## Examples

- [[Howard Phillips]] — co-founder; built The Conservative Caucus.
- [[Paul Weyrich]] — co-founder; built the Heritage Foundation.
- [[Richard Viguerie]] — co-founder; built the direct-mail funding model.
- Moral Majority (1979) — the New Right's most consequential creation.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Effectiveness:** Highly effective — the New Right is widely credited with building the coalition that elected Reagan in 1980 and with creating the institutional architecture of modern American conservatism.
- **Critique:** Critics argue its single-issue, grievance-driven methods and its instrumental use of religious mobilization coarsened political discourse and laid groundwork for later democratic-backsliding dynamics.

## Disambiguation — the two "New Rights"

This note covers the **1970s** New Right (Viguerie/Weyrich/Phillips/Dolan). A separate, unrelated movement of the **2010s–2020s** is also called the "New Right": a neo-reactionary current associated with [[Curtis Yarvin]], the "Dark Enlightenment," and figures including [[Peter Thiel]], Steve Bannon, and JD Vance, characterized by openly anti-democratic and "techno-monarchist" thought. The two share a name but not a lineage; the vault keeps them as distinct entities.
