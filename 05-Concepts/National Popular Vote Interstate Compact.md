# National Popular Vote Interstate Compact (NPVIC)

US interstate compact under which member states agree to award their electoral votes to the winner of the national popular vote, regardless of how their own state voted. The compact only takes effect when member states collectively control 270 or more electoral votes (a majority of the Electoral College).

## How it works (Tier 1)

- Each member state passes legislation joining the compact
- The legislation includes a trigger clause: it becomes operative only when states totaling 270+ EVs have joined
- Until trigger, members continue to award EVs by their existing rules (mostly winner-take-all by state)
- After trigger, members award their EVs to the national popular vote winner

## Current status (as of latest reporting)

**Joined (17 states + DC, ~209 electoral votes):**
California, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, DC, Hawaii, Illinois, Maine (2024), Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan (2024), Minnesota (2023), New Jersey, New Mexico, New York, Oregon, Rhode Island, Vermont, Washington

**Threshold gap:** ~61 more electoral votes needed to trigger

**Active in additional states (passed one chamber):** Arizona, Maine (now joined), Nevada, North Carolina, Virginia

## Constitutional basis

- Article II Section 1: "Each State shall appoint, in such Manner as the Legislature thereof may direct, a Number of Electors..."
- Article I Section 10 (Compacts Clause): Generally requires congressional consent for interstate compacts, BUT the Supreme Court has held since Virginia v Tennessee (1893) that compacts not affecting federal supremacy do not require consent
- The NPVIC's constitutionality has not been litigated at the Supreme Court level; legal scholars are divided

## Why it matters in this vault

NPVIC is the most-discussed structural electoral reform that would meaningfully constrain the [[Structural Capture Thesis]] coalition's path to power:

- The contemporary GOP coalition has won the popular vote in only ONE of the past nine presidential elections (2004)
- 2000 and 2016 were Electoral College wins against popular-vote losses
- If NPVIC takes effect, popular-vote-loss-EC-win paths to the presidency end

Therefore NPVIC is the structural-democratic counter to the [[Article V Convention]] structural-minoritarian path. They are competing constitutional-structural projects.

## Opposition arguments (steelmanned)

- Small-state advocates argue NPVIC eliminates the federalist balance the Electoral College preserves
- Some legal scholars argue the Compact Clause requires congressional consent and that NPVIC would be struck down
- Practical concern: a state that voted for Candidate X being forced to send its electors for Candidate Y would create legitimacy strain

## Significance for vault

NPVIC is one of two competing structural-amendment paths visible in contemporary US politics:
- [[Convention of States]] / Article V (minoritarian-coalition path)
- NPVIC + related democracy-reform package (majoritarian-coalition path)

The political question of 2025-2035 may be which structural project reaches its trigger threshold first.
