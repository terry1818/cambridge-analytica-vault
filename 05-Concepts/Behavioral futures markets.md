## Definition

Behavioral futures markets is a term coined by Shoshana Zuboff in *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (2019) to describe markets in which predictions about human behavior are manufactured and traded as commodities. In Zuboff's framework, surveillance capitalism's primary product is not a digital service but a *behavioral prediction*: a scored, probabilistic forecast of what a given individual or population segment will do — click, buy, vote, move, communicate — in the near or medium future. These predictions are packaged as "prediction products" and sold to third parties — advertisers, political campaigns, employers, financial institutions, and government agencies — who wish to anticipate, influence, or act in advance of the predicted behavior.

The behavioral futures market is the downstream commercial destination of [[Behavioral surplus]]: surplus behavioral data is processed, modeled, and converted into prediction scores that are then traded.

## Origin / history

Zuboff argues that Google's discovery of targeted advertising in the early 2000s established the first large-scale behavioral futures market. By auctioning advertisers the ability to reach users who were predicted to be in a given mental state — searching for a car, anxious about health — Google created a commodity whose value derived entirely from behavioral prediction accuracy. The subsequent growth of this model across Facebook, Amazon, and the broader ad-tech ecosystem expanded the market to encompass not just advertising but any context where foreknowledge of human behavior has commercial or political value.

The concept draws on futures market language from financial economics: just as commodity futures markets allow buyers to lock in a price for wheat or oil to be delivered in the future, behavioral futures markets allow buyers to purchase access to predicted human behavior before it occurs. Zuboff argues this market logic drives the entire surveillance capitalism system — the data collection, the algorithmic processing, the behavior modification — as infrastructure for manufacturing accurate behavioral predictions.

## How it works

1. **Data extraction**: [[Behavioral surplus]] is extracted from human-platform interactions and aggregated across billions of data points.
2. **Prediction manufacturing**: Machine learning models process surplus data to generate behavioral predictions — propensities, scores, probabilities — for individuals and segments.
3. **Market transaction**: These predictions are sold. In advertising, this is the real-time auction for ad impressions (Google AdWords, Facebook Ads). In politics, this is the sale of voter behavioral profiles. In employment, this is the sale of credit and personality scores. In government, this is the sale of location and behavioral data by brokers to agencies including ICE, the FBI, and the [[National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (NGA)]].
4. **Behavior modification feedback loop**: The most advanced form of behavioral futures markets extends from *predicting* behavior to *guaranteeing* predictions — i.e., modifying human behavior (through nudges, friction, algorithmic feed curation, targeted messaging) to make predictions come true. Zuboff calls this the "actuation imperative."

## Application in this domain

Behavioral futures markets are the commercial infrastructure that connects Cambridge Analytica-era political targeting to the present federal data-broker ecosystem:

- Cambridge Analytica's psychographic microtargeting was a participation in behavioral futures markets: CA purchased behavioral surplus (Facebook data) and sold behavioral predictions (voter persuasion scores) to political campaigns.
- The post-CA evolution documented in [[Hypothesis - Federal database supersedes CA model]] involves government agencies buying directly from behavioral futures markets — purchasing location histories, browsing patterns, and financial data from commercial brokers — rather than commissioning a private firm to do so.
- The [[Third-party doctrine]] ensures that government purchase from behavioral futures markets requires no warrant, making these markets a legal pathway to mass surveillance.

## Examples

- **Google AdWords / Display Network** — the original behavioral futures market; auctions attention of predicted-intention users
- **Facebook Custom Audiences and Lookalike Audiences** — markets in behavioral predictions about political and consumer behavior (exploited by Cambridge Analytica via GSR data)
- **Voter data marketplaces** (L2, TargetSmart, i360) — political behavioral futures markets selling modeled voter contact and persuasion scores
- **Location data broker markets** ([[Venntel]], [[X-Mode (Outlogic)]]) — selling predicted and actual location behaviors to government agencies
- **Insurance and financial underwriting data markets** — behavioral credit and risk scores derived from digital behavior

## Effectiveness / critique

Zuboff's behavioral futures markets concept has been influential as an analytic frame for understanding what surveillance capitalism actually produces. Critiques include:

- The concept may conflate *prediction* with *behavior modification*; advertising markets primarily claim prediction accuracy, not behavioral control, though the line is blurring.
- The "futures market" analogy may overstate the precision of prediction products; behavioral scores are probabilistic and error-prone at the individual level.
- The framework is strongest as a description of the *logic* of surveillance capitalism's revenue model — it accurately names why data collection is unbounded: every additional data point improves prediction product quality.
