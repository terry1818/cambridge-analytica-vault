# Political Technologists (Polittekhnologi) — Russia's Professional Reality-Management Consultants

## Definition

*Polittekhnologi* (singular: *polittekhnolog*) — loosely translated as "political technologist" — is the Russian term for a class of professional political consultants who manage electoral campaigns, mass mobilization, media narratives, and public opinion not through genuine persuasion but through the technical manipulation of political reality. The term implies that politics is an engineering problem: a technologist applies tools and techniques to manufacture the desired political outcome, just as an engineer applies techniques to build a structure. Unlike Western political consultants who at least nominally operate within competitive democratic frameworks, the polittekhnolog class in Putin-era Russia operated in a context where the outcome of major political contests was predetermined, and their role was to make the predetermined outcome appear legitimate. Pomerantsev's *Nothing Is True and Everything Is Possible* is the most widely read English-language account of this class in practice.

## Origin / History

The polittekhnolog class emerged from the chaos of the post-Soviet 1990s, when Russian politicians faced genuinely contested elections for the first time and imported American-style campaign consulting. Figures like Gleb Pavlovsky and [[Marat Gelman]] built careers in the 1996 Yeltsin presidential campaign, which deployed Western-style media strategy and polling alongside more opaque "administrative resource" manipulation. By the early Putin years, as genuine electoral competition was progressively eliminated, the role of the polittekhnolog shifted: rather than winning real contests, they produced the theatrical performance of democratic competition — creating opposition parties that were actually Kremlin-controlled, managing televised debates, scripting political crises, and manufacturing grassroots movements. [[Vladislav Surkov]] was the supreme practitioner within the Kremlin itself, but he drew on an entire professional ecosystem.

## How It Works

The polittekhnolog's toolkit, as documented by Pomerantsev and academic researchers, includes:

1. **Managed opposition**: Creating or coopting parties and figures that appear to challenge the Kremlin but function to channel and contain dissent, provide a simulacrum of pluralism, and crowd out genuine opposition (the [[Managed democracy (Surkovism)]] doctrine).
2. **Dramaturgiya**: Scripted electoral "themes" — invented threats, emotional narratives, manufactured crises — that frame the ruling candidate as a stabilizing force. The technique borrows from Soviet agitprop but uses modern television production values.
3. **Sociological manipulation**: Commissioning and publishing polls selectively, creating bandwagon effects and self-fulfilling prophecies of majority support.
4. **Kompromat deployment**: Strategic release of compromising material on opponents through sympathetic media channels.
5. **Reality blurring**: The advanced Surkov-era technique documented by Pomerantsev: deliberately creating multiple, contradictory narratives so that no single authoritative account of political events can be established. Citizens are left epistemically disoriented rather than persuaded.

## Application in This Domain

The polittekhnolog class is the human infrastructure layer connecting Kremlin political strategy to mass media manipulation and information warfare. Their techniques prefigure and directly influenced Western-facing operations: the reality-blurring methodology documented in the Putin domestic context is recognizably related to the "firehose of falsehood" doctrine applied internationally. Alumni of the polittekhnolog class — including figures who later left or were purged — describe the system as having developed genuine technical sophistication about epistemic manipulation that was subsequently industrialized through entities like the [[Internet Research Agency]]. [[Gleb Pavlovsky]], after his 2011 break with the Kremlin, gave candid interviews describing the internal logic of the system.

## Examples

- **Gleb Pavlovsky**: The leading polittekhnolog of the 1999–2011 period; ran the Foundation for Effective Politics; helped script Putin's 1999–2000 rise; invented the "managed democracy" operational doctrine with Surkov; dismissed in 2011 for opposing Putin's return to the presidency.
- **[[Marat Gelman]]**: Co-founded the Foundation for Effective Politics with Pavlovsky; also operated as a gallerist, embodying the polittekhnolog class's characteristic blending of political work with cultural prestige.
- **[[Vladislav Surkov]]**: The most powerful practitioner, operating from inside the presidential administration rather than as an external consultant.
- **Sergei Markov**: A more overtly propagandistic polittekhnolog who became a regular Western media commentator offering pro-Kremlin framing.

## Effectiveness / Critique

The polittekhnolog system was highly effective at manufacturing political stability and legitimating outcomes that were actually produced through electoral manipulation and administrative pressure. Its internal failure mode was creative atrophy: by the time genuine protest emerged in 2011–2012, the system had become too scripted to respond to authentic mass sentiment, and the dramaturgiya felt hollow. Pavlovsky's 2014 New Left Review interview is the most candid internal critique: he describes the system as having become a closed loop in which the technologists ultimately lost contact with reality themselves. The 2022 invasion may represent a catastrophic failure of the polittekhnolog epistemology — a system built to manufacture domestic consensus colliding with an international reality it could not manipulate.
