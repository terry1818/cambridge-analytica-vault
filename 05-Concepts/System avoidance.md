## Definition

System avoidance describes the observable pattern by which individuals who have had contact with the criminal justice system — police stop, arrest, conviction, incarceration — subsequently reduce their engagement with institutions that keep formal records. These include healthcare providers, banks, employers who conduct background checks, educational institutions, and government service agencies. The avoidance is not simply a general distrust of institutions; it is specifically calibrated to institutions that generate records that could be accessed by law enforcement or that could trigger immigration or probation consequences.

## Origin / history

[[Sarah Brayne]] first formalized system avoidance in a 2014 article in the *American Sociological Review* — "Surveillance and System Avoidance: Criminal Justice Contact and Institutional Attachment" — drawing on data from the Add Health (National Longitudinal Study of Adolescent to Adult Health) and NLSY97 (National Longitudinal Survey of Youth) datasets. The analysis controlled for sociodemographic characteristics and prior institutional attachment, finding that criminal justice contact was itself the independent predictor of subsequent institutional withdrawal. Brayne extended the concept in *Predict and Surveil* (2020), where she connected it to the growth of data-driven policing: the surveillance expansion documented in that book creates the conditions that generate the avoidance she measured.

## How it works

1. An individual has contact with the criminal justice system (stop, arrest, conviction, etc.).
2. This generates a formal record in one or more government databases.
3. The individual becomes aware (directly or through social network) that institutional participation generates records accessible to law enforcement.
4. The individual strategically reduces engagement with record-keeping institutions — skipping medical appointments, avoiding banks, not enrolling in school — to minimize new record creation.
5. The withdrawal generates downstream harms: untreated health conditions, financial exclusion, disrupted education, employment gaps.
6. Paradoxically, the data-sparse profile created by system avoidance may itself flag the individual as suspicious in algorithmic screening systems, which interpret institutional non-participation as a risk signal.

## Application in this domain

System avoidance has direct relevance to the vault's research concerns. The Trump 2.0 administration's deployment of integrated surveillance architecture — [[Palantir Technologies]], [[Flock Safety]] ALPR networks, the [[Federated Master Database]], immigration enforcement through [[ImmigrationOS]] — creates conditions of generalized surveillance that Brayne's framework predicts will drive widespread system avoidance across immigrant communities and communities of color. This withdrawal then reduces uptake of healthcare, public schooling, financial services, and tax compliance — generating both individual harm and systematic data distortions.

## Examples

- Brayne's empirical finding: individuals with criminal justice contact are statistically less likely to see a doctor, maintain a bank account, or enroll in post-secondary education, controlling for other variables.
- In LAPD fieldwork: community members in high-policing zones avoided hospitals and clinics because they knew paramedic calls generated address records that could be shared with LAPD.
- Immigration context (extension): undocumented individuals avoid school enrollment, tax filing, driver's licensing — all of which generate government records — due to fear of deportation.

## Effectiveness / critique

Brayne's empirical evidence for system avoidance is strong: two large longitudinal datasets, controlled analysis, consistent findings. The concept has been replicated in subsequent research in public health and criminology contexts. Critics of policing reform sometimes argue that system avoidance reflects pre-existing distrust rather than surveillance-induced behavior; Brayne's controlled analysis directly addresses this by isolating criminal justice contact as the independent variable.
