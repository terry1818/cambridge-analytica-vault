# propaganda

## Definition

Propaganda is the deliberate, systematic effort to shape perceptions, manipulate cognitions, and direct behavior in order to advance the aims of the propagandist. Harold Lasswell's classic formulation calls it "the management of collective attitudes by the manipulation of significant symbols." Edward Bernays defined modern propaganda as "a consistent, enduring effort to create or shape events to influence the relations of the public to an enterprise, idea or group." Unlike [[disinformation]] — which is specifically *false* information spread to deceive — propaganda need not be false. It is defined by its **intent and method** (organized, manipulative persuasion for a predetermined end), not by the truth value of its content. True facts, selectively presented and emotionally framed, are propaganda; so is outright fabrication. Disinformation is therefore best understood as one tool within the broader category of propaganda.

## Origin / history

The word derives from the *Congregatio de Propaganda Fide*, a body established by the Catholic Church in 1622 to "propagate" the faith — at that time the term carried no negative connotation. Propaganda as deliberate statecraft is far older, traceable to ancient Greece and Rome. Its modern, theorized form emerged with mass media in the early 20th century:

- **World War I** demonstrated industrialized state propaganda (Britain's Wellington House, the US Committee on Public Information).
- **Edward Bernays** — nephew of Sigmund Freud and a "father of public relations" — published *Propaganda* (1928), arguing that the "conscious and intelligent manipulation of the organized habits and opinions of the masses" was an essential feature of democratic society.
- **Harold Lasswell** — political scientist — produced the foundational academic studies, broadening propaganda's defined aims from changing opinions to changing actions.
- The **Nazi and Soviet regimes** turned propaganda into total state apparatuses, which is the source of the word's enduring negative connotation in the West.

## How it works

Propaganda operates on several levers:

1. **Symbol manipulation** — flags, slogans, enemies, in-group/out-group markers — to attach emotion to political objects.
2. **Repetition and saturation** — making a frame feel like common sense through sheer ubiquity.
3. **Emotional targeting** — fear, grievance, pride, and disgust move audiences more reliably than argument.
4. **Selective framing** — choosing which true facts to foreground and which to omit (propaganda need not lie to mislead).
5. **Channel control and source credibility** — building or capturing trusted-seeming messengers.
6. **Computational propaganda** — the contemporary form: bots, coordinated accounts, big data, and algorithmic micro-targeting used to manage opinion at scale and speed (analyzed by researchers including Renée DiResta and the Oxford Internet Institute).

## Application in this domain

Propaganda is the broadest analytical frame in this vault — the category under which the more specific objects sit:

- **[[Cambridge Analytica]]** industrialized the *targeting* of propaganda: psychographic profiles let messages be matched to the emotional and personality vulnerabilities of individual voters.
- **[[Roger Ailes]]'s** 1970 memo "A Plan For Putting the GOP on TV News" is a near-explicit propaganda blueprint — a plan to deliver "pro-administration" framing to mass audiences — later realized as [[Fox News]].
- The **[[MAGA media ecosystem]]** is a propaganda *distribution system*: a network engineered (and incentivized) to saturate a loyal audience with a coherent frame and insulate it from contradiction.
- **State propaganda** — Russian *active measures*, the Internet Research Agency — represents the computational, foreign-interference end of the same spectrum.

The vault's working position: the techniques are continuous from Bernays's 1920s public relations through Cold War state propaganda to algorithmic micro-targeting. What changed is precision, speed, and the disappearance of editorial gatekeepers — not the underlying logic.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed effectiveness:** Propagandists and their critics both tend to assert powerful, direct effects.
- **Empirical evidence:** The mid-20th-century "hypodermic needle" model of all-powerful propaganda was largely abandoned by communication scholars in favor of "limited" and "conditional" effects models — propaganda works best when it reinforces predispositions and operates in a low-competition information environment. The rise of micro-targeting and engagement-optimized platforms has revived the question of whether contemporary propaganda is more powerful than the mid-century evidence suggested. `#claim/disputed`
- **Academic critique:** Scholars stress that audiences are active interpreters, not passive recipients; that propaganda's effects are hard to isolate from selective exposure and prior belief; and that overstating propaganda's power can itself become a disempowering narrative.
