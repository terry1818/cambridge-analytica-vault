## Definition

The secondary surveillance network is a concept developed by sociologist [[Sarah Brayne]] to describe how data-driven policing technologies expand the perimeter of surveillance far beyond individuals who have had direct law-enforcement contact. In a traditional surveillance model, a person comes under police scrutiny through direct contact — arrest, stop, investigation. In the data-driven model, a person enters the surveillance net simply by appearing in the same database record, communication log, financial transaction, or geographic location as a primary subject of police interest. The secondary network is automatic, invisible, and does not require any legal justification referencing the secondary individual.

## Origin / history

Brayne developed the concept through multi-year ethnographic fieldwork inside the Los Angeles Police Department, documented in *Predict and Surveil: Data, Discretion, and the Future of Policing* (Oxford University Press, 2020). The concept builds on earlier sociological work on surveillance societies (Foucault, Giddens, Lyon) but is grounded in specific empirical observation of how LAPD used commercial data-aggregation platforms — particularly a Palantir system — to map social networks around primary subjects of interest.

## How it works

1. LAPD (and similar departments) ingest data from multiple sources: field interview cards, arrest records, the [[CalGang database]], license-plate-reader logs, commercial data brokers, cell-phone tower dumps, social-media records, and utility/financial records accessible through legal process.
2. A single individual designated as a primary subject of interest serves as an anchor point.
3. The system automatically surfaces all individuals who share a record with that anchor: known associates, family members at the same address, people who parked at the same location, contacts in phone records.
4. Those surfaced individuals become secondary subjects — they may be surveilled, contacted, or logged — without any independent suspicion attaching to them.
5. Because the data ecosystem is continuously updated, the secondary network expands over time as primary subjects interact with new people.

## Application in this domain

In LAPD practice, Brayne documented Palantir's LAPD integration as the primary technical vehicle for the secondary surveillance network. The system allowed analysts to run "social network" queries that visualized all individuals connected to a primary subject within specified degrees of separation. Combined with the [[CalGang database]] and [[Operation LASER]]'s chronic-offender list, the secondary network functioned as an ambient, semi-automated extension of investigative surveillance.

The secondary surveillance network is directly relevant to the vault's core research concern: how data aggregation by [[Palantir Technologies]] and the [[Federated Master Database]] being built by the Trump 2.0 administration under DOGE creates structural secondary networks around immigration enforcement targets, political actors, and civil society organizations.

## Examples

- An individual who has never had police contact may appear in the secondary network because a sibling is a primary LASER subject.
- A person who parks near a known subject's location on a single occasion may be captured in license-plate-reader data and entered into the secondary network.
- A community organizer who attends a meeting with a primary subject may be surfaced through cell-tower location data.

## Effectiveness / critique

The secondary surveillance network is effective from a law-enforcement intelligence perspective precisely because it is invisible and pre-legal: it does not require a warrant, generates no formal record of its own, and produces no notice to those surveilled. Civil-liberties critics argue this constitutes a form of guilt by association at scale, with chilling effects on political association, religious practice, and medical care. Brayne connects this to her concept of [[System avoidance]]: when people become aware (or suspect) that their associations are surveilled, they withdraw from institutions, generating precisely the data-poor behaviour that makes them appear suspicious.
