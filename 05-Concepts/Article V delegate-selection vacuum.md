# Article V delegate-selection vacuum

## Definition

The **Article V delegate-selection vacuum** refers to the total absence of constitutional text or binding precedent governing how delegates to an Article V constitutional convention would be selected, how many each state would send, who would be eligible to serve, how delegates could be recalled or replaced, and who — Congress or the states — has authority over the selection process. Article V of the Constitution states only that Congress "shall call a Convention for proposing Amendments" when two-thirds of state legislatures apply; it says nothing further about convention structure. This silence creates what Feingold and Prindiville identify as one of the two most dangerous unresolved problems (alongside the [[Article V aggregation problem]]) in the current Article V convention campaign.

## Origin / History

The delegate-selection vacuum was not a pressing practical problem for most of U.S. history because no Article V convention has ever been called. The provision has been a constitutional curio for over two centuries. But as the [[Convention of States Action]], the [[Balanced Budget Amendment Task Force]], and [[U.S. Term Limits]] have pressed their campaigns to within plausible distance of the 34-state threshold, the delegate-selection vacuum has moved from constitutional theory to operational risk.

Feingold and Prindiville flag the issue in [[Source - Feingold 2022 - The Constitution in Jeopardy]], citing a critical insight: **"In law, the most powerful person is the one holding the pen."** If convention advocates — specifically those aligned with the right-wing Article V campaign — controlled the selection and credentialing of delegates, they would hold the pen on any proposed amendments.

The issue was also addressed in the Constitution Center's "Report on Article V Constitutional Conventions" (2022) and in Scholars Strategy Network research, both of which document the competing theories without resolving the question.

## How It Works

**Who selects delegates?**

Two competing frameworks exist, with no authoritative resolution:

1. **State legislatures select**: State legislatures would choose their own delegates, as some state Article V application bills have already begun to specify. This aligns with the general principle that the application process runs through state legislatures. Several states aligned with [[Convention of States Action]] have passed legislation establishing delegate-selection procedures that pre-select delegates likely to be aligned with COS's convention agenda.

2. **Congress controls selection**: Article V grants Congress the power to "call" the convention. One interpretation is that "calling" includes setting the rules — including delegate selection. If Congress controls selection, the political composition of Congress at the time of the call would determine who attends.

**Apportionment: equal or proportionate?**

A related and equally unresolved question: would each state get one vote at the convention (as at the 1787 Constitutional Convention), or would representation be apportioned by population (as in Congress)? Equal-state representation would dramatically advantage small, rural, red states. Proportionate representation would advantage large, urban, blue states. The convention's output could differ dramatically depending on which apportionment rule applied.

**Recall and replacement**

If a state's political composition changes between the time it selects delegates and the time the convention sits, can it recall and replace delegates? If a delegate goes "rogue" and votes against the state legislature's instructions, is their vote valid? Article V provides no answers.

**Binding instructions**

Can a state legislature bind its delegates to vote only on specified topics? If a delegate from a COS-application state proposed an amendment to the First Amendment's press freedom clause, would their vote be valid? The constitutional answer is unknown.

## Application in This Domain

The delegate-selection vacuum is directly relevant to the vault's coverage of [[Convention of States Action]] and the [[Hypothesis - COS is the permanent capture arm of Project 2025]]. COS and its allied organizations have been explicit that they intend to pre-position friendly delegates through state-level delegate-selection legislation passed before a convention is called. As of the mid-2020s, multiple COS-aligned states had already passed such legislation. The strategic logic is that by the time a convention is called, the delegate roster would already be populated with COS-aligned figures — effectively turning the "constitutional vacuum" into a captured convention.

This connects the delegate-selection vacuum to the broader vault hypothesis about [[Hypothesis - Multiple right networks converged in Trump 2.0]]: a post-2024 Republican Congress, working with 28+ Republican state trifectas, would face no structural obstacle to declaring 34 states' applications sufficient ([[Article V aggregation problem]]) and then convening a convention with pre-selected, ideologically aligned delegates.

## Examples

- **COS state delegate legislation**: Several COS-aligned states have passed legislation specifying that delegates to an Article V convention shall be members of the state legislature, appointed by legislative leadership, with specified recall provisions — effectively ensuring that delegates are institutionally loyal to the state legislature's majority, which in these states is Republican.
- **The 1787 precedent**: The original Constitutional Convention was called under the Articles of Confederation to "amend" them; it instead produced an entirely new Constitution, discarded the amendment requirement of unanimity, and established a new ratification threshold. COS proponents cite this as precedent for the flexibility of a convention; critics cite it as evidence that a convention cannot be reliably constrained — the core [[Runaway Convention]] concern.

## Effectiveness / Critique

**COS/proponent position**: The delegate-selection vacuum is fillable by state legislation enacted before the convention is called. With enough aligned states passing uniform delegate-selection laws, the vacuum becomes a managed process. Michael Farris and COS have argued explicitly that a "runaway" convention is a myth because state legislatures retain recall authority over their delegates.

**Feingold/Prindiville position**: State recall authority is constitutionally untested. If a convention, once assembled, declared itself sovereign and refused to recognize recall instructions (as the 1787 Convention effectively refused to work within its original mandate), no judicial or legislative mechanism could override that assertion of sovereignty. The convention would hold its own authority.
