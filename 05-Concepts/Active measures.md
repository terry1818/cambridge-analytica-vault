# Active Measures

## Definition

Active measures (*aktivnyye meropriyatiya* in Russian) is the term used by Soviet and subsequently Russian intelligence to describe a category of covert political warfare operations distinct from intelligence collection. Where collection gathers information, active measures use disinformation, fabrication, subversion, and influence to shape the political environments of target countries in favor of Soviet/Russian interests. The doctrine encompasses: planting false stories in foreign media, producing and distributing forged documents, managing front organizations, cultivating agents of influence, conducting kompromat operations, and in some formulations, assassination and sabotage. Thomas Rid's *Active Measures* (2020) is the most comprehensive historical treatment, tracing the doctrine from the 1920s through 2016.

## Origin / History

The institutional origin is the GPU's "special disinformation office" established in 1923 — the first explicit state bureaucracy dedicated to producing and distributing fabricated intelligence. The Cheka/GPU inherited the "Trust" operation as an early model: a fake anti-Bolshevik organization used to penetrate and neutralize genuine émigré resistance networks.

Under the KGB (established 1954), active measures were systematized into a formal doctrine and assigned to a dedicated directorate: initially Service D (*dezinformatsia*), reorganized as [[KGB Service A]] under Yuri Andropov (KGB chairman 1967–1982). Andropov, who believed in active measures' utility from his experience managing the 1956 Hungarian uprising, oversaw the most significant expansion of the capability. By the early 1980s the First Chief Directorate's entire 15,000-officer apparatus was oriented toward active measures in some capacity, with [[KGB Service A]] as the dedicated center.

## How It Works

Active measures operate through several overlapping mechanisms:

1. **Disinformation and forgery**: fabricated documents, fake letters attributed to real people, forged official correspondence. See [[Operation NEPTUNE]] (fake Nazi documents, 1964) and [[Operation INFEKTION]] (AIDS/Fort Detrick claim, 1983+).

2. **Media placement ("black propaganda")**: planting fabricated stories in foreign newspapers through front outlets, paid journalists, or KGB-controlled publications; once in print, the story migrates through legitimate media without further Soviet involvement.

3. **Front organizations**: creating or subsidizing ostensibly independent organizations — peace movements, cultural networks, academic bodies — to advance Soviet positions. Contrast with Western mirror: [[Congress for Cultural Freedom]] (CIA-funded anti-communist cultural network, 1950–1967).

4. **Agents of influence**: cultivating individuals in positions of authority — journalists, politicians, academics — who wittingly or unwittingly promote desired narratives.

5. **Kompromat**: collecting or fabricating compromising material on target individuals for blackmail or to neutralize opponents.

6. **"Reflexive control"**: a related Soviet military-intellectual concept — shaping the adversary's information environment so they make decisions favorable to Soviet interests, believing they are acting independently.

The key feature of active measures that distinguishes them from conventional propaganda is their *covert* character: they are designed to appear as organic domestic expression rather than foreign interference.

## Effectiveness / Critique

The effectiveness of active measures is debated:
- **Pro-effectiveness arguments**: Operations like INFEKTION demonstrably seeded false beliefs that persisted for decades; IRA operations reached millions of US social media users; active measures contributed to measurable shifts in elite opinion in Western Europe during the Cold War.
- **Counter-arguments**: Rid and others note that active measures often failed to achieve their stated objectives, had unintended consequences, or succeeded in amplifying existing divisions rather than creating new ones. The [[Firehose of falsehood]] model trades persuasion for confusion — a different and arguably more durable effect.
- A consistent finding: active measures work best when amplifying real grievances or pre-existing divisions rather than fabricating entirely new ones.
