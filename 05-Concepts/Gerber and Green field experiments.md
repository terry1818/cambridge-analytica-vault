## Definition

The "Gerber and Green field experiments" refers to a program of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) on voter mobilization conducted by Yale political scientists Alan S. Gerber and Donald P. Green beginning in 1998. The experiments randomly assigned registered voters to receive different get-out-the-vote (GOTV) contacts — face-to-face canvassing, direct mail, or telephone calls — and measured actual turnout using post-election voter-roll records. The program revived the experimental tradition in political science (dormant since [[Harold Gosnell]]'s 1924 work) and established a cumulative, replicable evidence base for what voter-contact methods actually raise turnout.

## Origin / History

In **1998**, shortly before the November midterm elections in New Haven, Connecticut, Gerber and Green assigned approximately 30,000 registered voters at random to receive nonpartisan GOTV messages via one of three channels or to an untreated control group. They measured turnout from public voter-roll data, enabling clean causal inference. Results were published in 2000 in the *American Political Science Review*: face-to-face canvassing produced large, statistically significant turnout effects (~9 percentage points); direct mail produced small positive effects; phone banking produced no measurable effect.

The study was deliberately designed to replicate [[Harold Gosnell]]'s 1924 methodology while applying modern statistical standards. Neither Gerber nor Green was fully aware of Gosnell's precedent when they launched; the intellectual connection was established retroactively.

**Subsequent replications**: Gerber, Green, and collaborators (including David Nickerson, Christopher Larimer, and others) ran dozens of follow-up experiments through the 2000s, varying scripts, messengers, social-pressure language, and targeting criteria. Results were widely replicated and synthesized into the academic literature.

## How It Works

Standard design:
1. Take a voter file for a target jurisdiction.
2. Randomly assign individual voters to treatment arms (different contact methods/messages) or a control group.
3. Deliver the contacts as assigned.
4. After the election, merge treatment assignments with public voter-roll turnout records.
5. Calculate intent-to-treat (ITT) and complier average causal effect (CACE) estimates.

The public availability of US voter-roll turnout records is a crucial enabling condition: it removes the need for self-reported survey data on voting, which is subject to social-desirability bias.

## Application in This Domain

The Gerber-Green experiments are the academic foundation for the Democratic experimental-testing ecosystem documented in [[Source - Issenberg 2012 - The Victory Lab]]:

- **[[Analyst Institute]]**: Used the Gerber-Green methodology as its template; founding members include Gerber and Green as affiliated academics.
- **[[Mike Podhorzer]]**: Cited Gerber-Green results to justify AFL-CIO investment in experimental programs over intuition-based targeting.
- **[[Hal Malchow]]**: Applied similar control-group logic to mail testing at MSHC Partners.
- **[[Todd Rogers]]**: Extended the tradition by adding behavioral-science mechanisms (identity-based framing, social-norm effects) to the Gerber-Green contact-method framework.

The GOP parallel — at [[TargetPoint Consulting]] and in the [[Voter Vault and the 2004 72-Hour Task Force]] — ran its own experiments, often without explicit Gerber-Green framing but converging on similar randomized-test logic.

## Examples

- **1998 New Haven**: Canvassing +9pp turnout; mail +0.6pp; phone calls ~0pp
- **2000 Iowa and Michigan** (Gerber, Green, Nickerson): Confirmed canvassing effects; explored repeated-contact dynamics
- **2006 Social pressure experiment** (Gerber, Green, Larimer): Mailing showing neighbors' voting records raised turnout by ~8pp — one of the largest effects ever measured in a GOTV experiment and a direct predecessor to the social-norm messaging [[Todd Rogers]] developed for the Analyst Institute

## Effectiveness / Critique

**Evidence for effectiveness**: Meta-analyses across hundreds of experiments confirm that face-to-face canvassing consistently raises turnout; direct mail has small but real effects; phone banking (especially robocalls) has near-zero effect. The methodology itself is widely regarded as among the most rigorous in social science.

**Critiques**:
- Effects may be smaller in high-salience presidential elections where baseline turnout is already elevated
- External validity questions: lab results from low-turnout local elections may not translate to competitive presidential cycles
- The social-pressure mailer (2006) raised ethical concerns about privacy (publicizing neighbors' voting records) and manipulation
- Proprietary nature of much Analyst Institute research limits academic replication
