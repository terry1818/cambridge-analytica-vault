# Concept

## Definition

**Schizofascism** is Timothy Snyder's neologism for a specific condition: actual fascists who call their opponents "fascists." More precisely, Snyder defines schizofascism as characterized by "actual fascists calling their opponents 'fascists,' blaming the Holocaust on the Jews, treating the Second World War as an argument for more violence."

The term combines "schizo-" (connoting split, contradictory, or inverted reality) with "fascism." It names a form of political accusation that has severed from evidentiary grounding and operates purely as a rhetorical weapon. Snyder writes:

> "Fascists calling other people 'fascists' is fascism taken to its illogical extreme as a cult of unreason. It is a final point where hate speech inverts reality and propaganda is pure insistence. It is the apogee of will over thought."

Schizofascism is thus not merely name-calling or political mudslinging; Snyder treats it as a diagnostic indicator — when a movement that meets substantive criteria for fascism systematically deploys the "fascist" accusation against non-fascist opponents, this inversion is itself a feature of fascist epistemics, not an incidental rhetorical choice.

## Origin / History

Snyder coined and applied the term primarily in the context of Vladimir Putin's Russia and the Russian invasion of Ukraine (2022), though the analytical groundwork appears in *The Road to Unfreedom* (2018). The specific application: Putin's government justified the invasion in part as a "denazification" mission against Ukraine's government — a government that is democratically elected, includes a Jewish president (Volodymyr Zelensky), and has no credible fascist character by any independent measure — while the Russian state itself displays extensive fascist characteristics (ultranationalism, glorification of historical violence, cult of the leader, rejection of democratic legitimacy, imperial aggression).

Snyder applied the term to specific ideologues in *The Road to Unfreedom*, citing figures like **Alexander Dugin** and **Alexander Prokhanov** — Russian intellectuals Snyder describes as "unmistakably fascist" by their writings — who produce texts accusing others (particularly Ukrainians and Western liberals) of fascism.

The post-2022 application to the Ukraine invasion extended the concept into international law and war crimes framing: the schizofascist accusation functions to rhetorically justify fascist violence by projecting fascist identity onto the victim.

## How It Works

**The inversion mechanism:**

1. **Accusation as inoculation.** By pre-emptively labeling opponents "fascist," the schizofascist movement makes it difficult for observers to evaluate the actual distribution of fascist characteristics. When both sides in a conflict are calling each other fascists, some audiences default to "both sides" framing, which benefits the side with more actual fascist characteristics.

2. **Will over evidence.** Snyder places schizofascism in the broader context of [[Politics of eternity (Snyder)]] — a mode in which mythological truth replaces factual truth. The schizofascist accusation does not require evidence; its force comes from repetition and emotional intensity. Denials and counter-evidence are reframed as further proof of the accused's deceptive character.

3. **Historical inversion.** In Snyder's primary examples, the historical record of fascist atrocity (the Holocaust, World War II) is not denied but inverted — its perpetrators are recast as victims, its victims as perpetrators. This is not merely revisionism but a deliberate weaponization of historical guilt.

4. **Analytical paralysis.** For scholars and journalists, schizofascism creates a rhetorical trap: calling a schizofascist regime "fascist" appears to reproduce the very move one is criticizing. Snyder's response is that the analytical term must be applied on the basis of substantive criteria (see [[Five-stage model of fascism (Paxton)]]), not avoided because it has been misused.

## Application in This Domain

The vault uses schizofascism as a **propaganda analysis tool** with two primary applications:

**1. Identifying inversion rhetoric in the US context.** Does any actor in the post-2024 political landscape who can be substantively characterized as authoritarian or proto-fascist (by Paxton's criteria, by Stanley's ten pillars) simultaneously deploy "fascist" or "totalitarian" accusations against democratic opponents? If so, this is a schizofascist pattern worth documenting — not as proof of fascism, but as a diagnostic indicator. Common examples to investigate: "Democrat Socialists," "deep state," "antifa" as alleged terrorist organization while organized right-wing violence is minimized.

**2. Analytical clarity for the vault itself.** Because this research vault is analyzing the "is this fascism?" question, it is subject to the schizofascist trap from the opposite direction — the claim that applying fascism frameworks to the current US situation is itself a form of partisan labeling. The concept of schizofascism helps the vault distinguish between: (a) substantive analytic use of "fascism" grounded in defined criteria and evidence; and (b) rhetorical deployment of "fascism" as an epithet. The vault operates by (a).

**Scope limit:** Snyder developed schizofascism specifically for a case (Putin's Russia/Ukraine) where the fascist characteristics of the accusing party are very well-documented and the non-fascist character of the accused party is clear. Applying the concept to US domestic politics requires first establishing the substantive criteria — otherwise the term becomes the very rhetorical weapon it describes.

## Examples

**Primary (Tier 1 — Snyder's documented cases)**
- Russian government's "denazification" framing of the 2022 Ukraine invasion, applied to a state with a Jewish president and no credible fascist political movement in power.
- Dugin's and Prokhanov's texts calling Western liberalism "fascist" while advancing ultranationalist positions Snyder documents as meeting fascist criteria.

**Secondary / Investigative (Tier 3 — for analysis, not conclusion)**
- Domestic US political rhetoric deploying "fascist," "totalitarian," or "Nazi" against democratic opposition while advancing policies that restrict democratic participation — the vault should document specific instances and assess the criteria.

## Effectiveness / Critique

**Strengths:** The concept names a real rhetorical phenomenon — inversion of fascist accusation — that otherwise lacks precise terminology. It is empirically anchored in Snyder's documented cases.

**Criticisms:**
- The term is highly charged; its use in the vault must be carefully supported by substantive criteria to avoid becoming the rhetorical inversion it describes.
- Snyder's primary application (Russia/Ukraine) involves such clear asymmetry of evidence that the concept's extension to ambiguous cases is analytically risky.
- Some critics argue that Snyder's framework, by centering fascism as the key concept, may underframe other forms of authoritarianism that are not usefully called fascist.
