# Joel's Army

## Definition

"Joel's Army" is a charismatic end-times theology holding that, before the return of Christ, God will raise up a militant, supernaturally empowered generation of believers — "overcomers" — who will function as a literal army to defeat evil and establish God's dominion on earth. The name derives from Joel 2:1–11, which describes a vast, unstoppable army moving across the land; adherents read this army as the victorious end-times church rather than (as most exegetes hold) a plague of locusts or an invading nation. The teaching is closely tied to the parallel concept of the "Manifest Sons of God." It is best understood as a *militant variant* of dominionist [[spiritual warfare]] theology.

## Origin / history

Joel's Army traces to the **Latter Rain movement**, a revival that gained traction in the late 1940s and 1950s among Pentecostals largely outside the established Pentecostal denominations. Latter Rain taught the imminent rise of a victorious, denomination-transcending church endowed with miraculous power. Its teaching that an army of "overcomers" — the "Manifest Sons of God," "Joel's Army" — would wage spiritual warfare, perform miracles, and usher in Christ's return was controversial from the start; the Assemblies of God formally condemned Latter Rain doctrines in 1949 as unscriptural.

The concept did not disappear. It was carried forward by revivalist preachers and re-entered wider circulation through later charismatic revivals. The Latter Rain stream fed into [[Dominionism]] — which from the 1970s taught that Christians are mandated to "take dominion" over civil government — and into the [[New Apostolic Reformation]]. Journalists and researchers documented "Joel's Army" rhetoric especially around the 2008 Lakeland (Florida) revival led by Todd Bentley, where the language of a militant end-times army was prominent.

## How it works

The theology fuses three elements. From Latter Rain it takes the idea of a uniquely empowered end-times generation. From [[Dominionism]] it takes the mandate to seize control of earthly institutions, including government. From militant readings of Joel 2 it takes the *army* metaphor — and the metaphor tends, in practice, to literalize.

A recurring and much-criticized teaching is generational and militant: that young people of "this generation" are God's anointed vessels with divine "favor to take, by force, any ground that is not Christian." Critics — including within Pentecostalism — warn this can slide from metaphorical zeal into actual militancy, spiritual pride, and, when prophecies fail, disillusionment. The Assemblies of God has criticized the doctrine as promoting a "post-denominational militancy inconsistent with Scripture."

## Application in this domain

Joel's Army is less an organized program than a recurring militant *register* that surfaces wherever dominionist charismatic theology turns combative. Its imagery — a generation raised up as God's army to take territory by force — reappears in [[New Apostolic Reformation]] revivalism, in youth-targeted "warrior" rhetoric, and in the militarized framing of Christian-nationalist mobilization. The "army of God" language deployed at the [[ReAwaken America]] tour, and the broader shift among some NAR voices from a liberating "[[Cyrus Anointing]]" frame toward militant "avenger" and "warrior" imagery after 2020, draw on the same theological well. For this vault, Joel's Army matters as the most explicitly militant strand of the dominionist tradition: it is the point where [[spiritual warfare]] metaphor most readily hardens into the language of force.

## Examples

- **Latter Rain movement** (1948–1950s) — origin point; condemned by the Assemblies of God in 1949.
- **Lakeland Revival** (2008, Todd Bentley) — a revival where Joel's Army and militant end-times rhetoric were prominent, drawing journalistic scrutiny.
- [[New Apostolic Reformation]] — carries Latter Rain / Manifest Sons concepts forward in altered form.
- [[ReAwaken America]] — "army of God" recruitment rhetoric echoes the Joel's Army register.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed effectiveness:** Adherents claim it mobilizes a victorious, miracle-working church; these are theological claims.
- **Empirical evidence:** Its documented effect is rhetorical and cultural — it normalizes militant, militarized language within charismatic settings.
- **Academic and theological critique:** Mainstream Pentecostal bodies (notably the Assemblies of God) and many evangelical theologians reject Joel's Army as heretical — a misreading of Joel 2, an over-realized eschatology, and a doctrine prone to militancy and authoritarian leadership. Researchers of religion and extremism note that "take ground by force" framing, applied to politics, is corrosive to democratic norms. The degree to which the theology *causes* (as opposed to colors) political militancy is interpretive and contested. `#claim/disputed`
