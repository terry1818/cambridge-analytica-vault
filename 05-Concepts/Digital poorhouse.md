## Definition

The digital poorhouse is a conceptual framework coined by political scientist [[Virginia Eubanks]] to describe how high-tech tools — data mining, predictive risk models, automated eligibility systems — serve the same social function as the physical poorhouses of the 19th century: they surveil, police, punish, and exclude poor and working-class Americans. The analogy is structural, not metaphorical. Eubanks argues that the moral assumptions embedded in 19th-century poverty management — that poverty reflects moral failure, that the poor require coercion, that assistance should be conditioned on behavioral compliance — were never abandoned. They were digitized. The digital poorhouse can act more quickly, at greater scale, and with greater opacity than its brick-and-mortar predecessor.

## Origin / history

Eubanks first articulated the digital-poorhouse framework in a January 2018 essay in *Harper's Magazine* ("The Digital Poorhouse") and elaborated it across three case studies in *Automating Inequality* (St. Martin's Press / Picador, 2018):

1. **Indiana**: The [[Indiana welfare automation]] privatization scheme (2006–2009), run by IBM and [[Affiliated Computer Services]], which generated approximately one million automated benefit denials.
2. **Pennsylvania**: The [[Allegheny Family Screening Tool]], a predictive risk model used to screen child-abuse referrals.
3. **Los Angeles**: An algorithm-based system for allocating the scarce housing vouchers available to the city's ~58,000 homeless residents.

The concept builds on a tradition of poverty historiography (Michael Katz, Barbara Nelson) that traced how moral judgments about the poor became embedded in institutional design.

## How it works

The digital poorhouse operates through four mechanisms:

1. **Automated eligibility determination**: Rules-based or ML-based systems replace caseworker discretion, applying criteria that systematically disadvantage applicants who cannot conform to bureaucratic norms (stable address, reliable documentation, consistent digital identity).
2. **Predictive targeting**: Risk scores identify individuals for intervention (child-welfare investigation, social-services referral) based on data patterns that correlate with poverty itself, creating circular reinforcement.
3. **Surveillance conditioning**: Receipt of public benefits is conditioned on participation in monitoring systems (drug tests, location reporting, case plans) that treat poverty as a behavioral problem requiring supervision.
4. **Scale and speed**: The digital version of poorhouse logic operates at the scale of entire states or the federal system, processing millions of cases in ways that individual caseworkers would not or could not.

## Application in this domain

The digital poorhouse concept has direct relevance to the vault's core research area. The Trump 2.0 administration's deployment of integrated data systems — [[Palantir Technologies]]' federal contracts, the [[Federated Master Database]] assembled through DOGE, [[ImmigrationOS]] — extends digital-poorhouse logic beyond the welfare context to immigration enforcement, creating analogous structures of automated, opaque, large-scale control over marginalized populations.

## Examples

- Indiana: Automated rules flagged any application error as "failure to cooperate," generating ~1 million benefit actions.
- Allegheny: AFST risk scores may penalize help-seeking behavior (using mental health services) because that behavior generates government records used in the model.
- Los Angeles: Algorithm-based housing voucher allocation systems determine shelter access for thousands of homeless people without meaningful appeals process.

## Effectiveness / critique

Eubanks's framework has been widely adopted in algorithmic accountability and poverty scholarship. It has been critiqued from two directions:
- **Techno-optimists**: Some proponents of the AFST and similar tools argue that algorithmic systems can *reduce* human bias by standardizing decisions; Eubanks's response is that standardizing biased decisions scales the bias.
- **Libertarians**: Some critics argue that eliminating automated systems would restore worse-performing human discretion; Eubanks's counterargument is that the choice is not between algorithms and discretion but between accountability structures.
