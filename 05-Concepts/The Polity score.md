# The Polity score

## Definition

The Polity score is a composite numerical index — scaled from −10 (maximum autocracy) to +10 (maximum democracy) — that measures the level of democratic or autocratic governance in a country in a given year. It is the central output of the Polity Project (currently Polity5), a long-running political science data collection effort maintained by the Center for Systemic Peace. The score is calculated from coded assessments of executive recruitment, executive constraints, and political competition. It is the most widely used quantitative measure of regime type in comparative politics and conflict studies, and is the empirical instrument through which [[Anocracy]] is operationalised.

## Origin / history

The Polity Project was initiated by Ted Robert Gurr at the University of Denver in 1974 (*Polity I*). Subsequent iterations (Polity II, III, IV, and V) expanded coverage and refined coding rules. The project is now housed at the Center for Systemic Peace (formerly at the University of Maryland). Polity5 covers 167 countries from 1800 to 2018, making it one of the longest time-series political datasets in existence. The project has been updated periodically; as of 2026, comprehensive updates beyond 2018 are not available from the original project team, though researchers have produced extended datasets using the Polity methodology.

## How it works

The Polity score is computed from six component indicators:

1. **Executive recruitment** (XRCOMP, XROPEN): How constrained and open is the process by which the chief executive comes to power?
2. **Executive constraints** (XCONST): What institutional constraints limit the executive's authority (legislature, judiciary, military)?
3. **Political competition** (PARCOMP, PARREG): How competitive and regulated is participation in politics?

The democracy score (DEMOC, 0–10) and autocracy score (AUTOC, 0–10) are computed separately from these components and then combined: **Polity = DEMOC − AUTOC**. The result ranges −10 to +10.

Standard thresholds: **Autocracies** = −10 to −6; **Anocracies** = −5 to +5; **Democracies** = +6 to +10. Special codes (−66, −77, −88) denote periods of interruption (foreign occupation, transition, anarchy).

## Application in this domain

The vault can use the Polity score to: (1) provide a standardised, cross-nationally comparable measure of regime type when analysing countries the vault tracks (Hungary, Russia, Turkey, Brazil, Philippines); (2) situate the US democratic backsliding debate within a quantitative framework — the score's movement from +10 to +5 (as noted by Walter) provides a benchmark; (3) assess the severity of democratic erosion relative to historical precedents and other countries at comparable Polity levels. The score is a *proxy* measure and should always be combined with qualitative analysis; it measures formal institutional characteristics, not the actual quality of democratic practice.

## Examples

- **United States, pre-2016**: Polity score +10 (consolidated democracy).
- **United States, 2020–21**: Downgraded to approximately +5 following events of January 6, 2021 (per Walter's application; not an official Polity Project coding, which ended at 2018).
- **Hungary 2010–present**: Has moved from approximately +10 toward +6–7 under Orban; some analysts score it lower depending on interpretation of executive constraint variables.
- **Russia 1990s**: Oscillated in the +3 to +6 range (anocracy) during the post-Soviet transition.
- **Germany 1933**: Scored +4 in 1932, fell to −9 by 1934 as Nazi consolidation completed — illustrating the speed of potential collapse.

## Effectiveness / critique

The Polity score is widely used but has been subject to sustained methodological criticism:

1. **Measurement validity**: The coding of "executive constraints" and "political competition" involves significant researcher judgment; two coders may disagree on the same country in the same year.
2. **Institutional vs. behavioural gap**: The score measures formal institutional characteristics, not actual behaviour. A country can score high on paper while institutions are hollowed out in practice — exactly the backsliding dynamic that Levitsky & Ziblatt and others emphasise.
3. **Discontinuation concern**: The project has not produced official updates beyond 2018; researchers applying the methodology to recent years are making interpretive extrapolations.
4. **Alternative measures**: The V-Dem (Varieties of Democracy) dataset, Freedom House scores, and Economist Intelligence Unit Democracy Index measure related but distinct constructs. Findings sometimes diverge, suggesting the choice of measure matters for conclusions.
5. **US coding controversy**: The downgrade of the US to +5 following January 6 was applied by Walter based on Polity criteria; the Polity Project itself has not officially coded post-2018 US data, so this application should be noted as an analytic inference, not an official score.
