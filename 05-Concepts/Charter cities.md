# Charter cities

## Definition

A **charter city** is a new city — or a designated zone within an existing country — that operates under its own distinct set of laws, regulations, courts, and economic rules, separate from the surrounding national legal system. The host nation grants the city a "charter" delegating broad self-governance, typically to attract investment by promising rule of law, low taxes, light regulation, and efficient administration. In libertarian and tech-right hands, the charter-city idea has been radicalized into a model of **"governance as a service"** or "startup societies": polities founded and run as products, where residents and investors opt in by contract rather than by citizenship, and where founders rather than democratic majorities set the rules.

## Origin / history

The modern charter-cities concept was popularized around 2009 by **Paul Romer**, the economist (later a World Bank chief economist and 2018 Nobel laureate). Romer argued that poor countries could accelerate growth by hosting new cities with imported, credible legal and economic institutions — and proposed that a foreign government or third party could guarantee those rules. Romer's framing was technocratic and development-oriented, not libertarian.

The concept has older roots, however, in **special economic zones (SEZs)** — designated areas with distinct trade and regulatory rules, used widely since the late 20th century (Shenzhen being the iconic example) — and in libertarian "private cities" theory, which long imagined governance provided competitively by private entities. When Romer's idea met that libertarian tradition, and the [[Seasteading Institute]]'s "exit"-focused thinking, it produced the radicalized version the vault tracks.

The clearest real-world test came in **Honduras**: Romer initially advised Honduran officials, but distanced himself in 2012, saying he had been denied the powers and transparency his role required. Honduras then passed a 2013 law creating **ZEDEs** (Zones for Employment and Economic Development) — autonomous zones with their own political, judicial, and economic systems. The best-known ZEDE, **Próspera**, was established on the island of Roatán in 2021 and described as a "crypto-libertarian paradise," backed by tech-right investors.

## How it works

A charter city is created by a host government enacting legislation that carves out a zone and delegates governance powers to a charter authority — which may be a public body, a public-private partnership, or, in the radical libertarian version, a private corporation. Within the zone:

- A distinct legal and regulatory code applies (often much lighter on taxes, labor, and environmental rules than the host nation's).
- Disputes may be resolved through private arbitration rather than national courts.
- Residency and "membership" can be acquired by contract, sometimes for a fee.
- The charter authority effectively functions as a government, but one whose legitimacy derives from the founding charter and the consent of opt-in residents — not from a national democratic process.

Technically, the model treats jurisdiction as **modular and competitive**: governance is "unbundled" from the nation-state and offered as a competing product. This is the conceptual bridge to the [[Seasteading Institute]] (the same logic, applied at sea) and the "network state" idea (the same logic, applied online and across distributed enclaves).

## Application in this domain

For this vault, charter cities matter as the **territorial expression of the constitutional-capture project**. The libertarian movement the vault tracks — from the [[William Volker Fund]] through the [[Koch Network]] to the [[Thiel Foundation]] — has long sought to insulate market and property rules from democratic majorities. Charter cities pursue that aim spatially: rather than only constraining the state from within (courts, constitutional amendments, the [[No Tax Pledge]]), they build *new jurisdictions* where the libertarian rule-set is foundational and democratic revision is structurally limited.

Tech-right patrons have invested heavily. [[Peter Thiel]], Marc Andreessen, and others have funded charter-city and "network state" ventures; Patri Friedman (of the [[Seasteading Institute]]) co-founded Pronomos Capital to invest in such projects. In the U.S., the idea has surfaced politically as proposals for "Freedom Cities" — federally chartered new cities on public land with their own light-touch rules. The model's anti-democratic implication is explicit in critical analysis: it offers wealthy founders and investors a way to exit the obligations and accountability of ordinary democratic governance.

## Examples

- [[Próspera]] (Roatán, Honduras) — flagship ZEDE; a charter-city experiment in corporate governance, now in an ~$11 billion ICSID arbitration dispute with the Honduran government.
- Honduras **ZEDEs** — the 2013 legal framework enabling autonomous zones; later repealed by a subsequent Honduran government, triggering investor legal claims.
- [[Seasteading Institute]] — applies the same opt-in, competitive-governance logic to floating ocean platforms.
- "Freedom Cities" — U.S. proposals for federally chartered new cities with their own regulatory regimes.
- Special economic zones (Shenzhen and others) — the technocratic precursor model.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed effectiveness:** Proponents argue charter cities import credible institutions, attract investment, and let countries experiment with governance to find what drives growth.
- **Empirical evidence:** Thin. No charter city has yet delivered the transformative growth its boosters promised; Próspera remains small and legally embattled.
- **Critique:** Critics — including economists, the Honduran government, and the host populations — describe charter cities as **"neo-colonial"**: wealthy foreign investors carving sovereign enclaves out of poorer nations, exempt from local taxes, labor protections, and democratic accountability. The Honduras case produced an ~$11 billion investor-state claim that threatens to bankrupt a nation that tried to repeal the model. The deeper democratic-backsliding critique is that charter cities normalize the idea that governance is a market product founders sell, rather than a public trust citizens hold. `#claim/disputed`
