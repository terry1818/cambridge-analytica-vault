# Paradox of Tolerance

## Definition

A philosophical problem articulated by Karl Popper in *The Open Society and Its Enemies* (1945): if a society extends unlimited tolerance to all groups including those who are intolerant, those intolerant groups will eventually use the freedoms of an open society to destroy it. Unlimited tolerance is therefore self-defeating. Popper concluded that tolerance of the intolerant is not morally required, and that a tolerant society must claim the right to suppress intolerant ideologies — not by suppressing rational argument, but specifically when groups reject rational argument altogether and resort to "fists and pistols."

Jason Stanley invokes this concept in *How Fascism Works* (2018) in the context of fascist movements' rhetorical use of liberal tolerance norms to advance anti-democratic ends.

## Origin / History

Popper first stated the paradox in a footnote to Chapter 7 of *The Open Society and Its Enemies* (1945), written during his wartime exile and in direct response to the rise of fascism. The exact passage reads: "unlimited tolerance must lead to the disappearance of tolerance. If we extend unlimited tolerance even to those who are intolerant, if we are not prepared to defend a tolerant society against the onslaught of the intolerant, then the tolerant will be destroyed, and tolerance with them."

The paradox has had intermittent influence in political philosophy but became widely discussed in public discourse from around 2016–2018 as debates about platform deplatforming, hate speech, and fascist organizing under liberal free speech protections intensified. Stanley explicitly frames fascist politics' use of free-speech claims as an example of the paradox in action.

## How It Works

The mechanism Popper identifies:

1. A tolerant, open society extends freedoms of speech, assembly, and political participation to all, including anti-democratic movements.
2. Intolerant movements use these freedoms to organize, recruit, and propagandize.
3. Unlike their democratic opponents, intolerant movements are not bound by rational argument — they can use democratic tools while rejecting democratic norms.
4. If the tolerant society fails to respond, the intolerant movement can accumulate enough power to destroy the conditions of tolerance itself.

**Popper's qualification**: He did NOT argue that intolerant ideas should simply be banned. His criterion for justified suppression was specifically groups that "forbid their followers to listen to rational argument" and resort to violence — not unpopular or offensive ideas.

**Stanley's application**: In *How Fascism Works*, Stanley notes that fascist politics systematically exploits tolerance norms — claiming free speech rights while working to deny speech to opponents, claiming democratic legitimacy while organizing to end democratic competition, and using the language of victimhood (being "silenced," "cancelled") to mobilize support even as the movement advances anti-democratic goals.

## Application in This Domain

The vault can apply this framework when analyzing cases where movements invoke liberal tolerance norms (free speech, religious freedom, pluralism) as shields for anti-democratic organizing. The diagnostic question is Popper's: does the movement accept rational argument as a legitimate form of political contestation, or does it systematically delegitimize fact-based institutions (media, universities, courts, elections) while claiming the procedural benefits of an open society? The latter pattern is a potential indicator of the paradox in action. This is directly relevant to the vault's analysis of how Project 2025 and allied movements frame judicial capture, media delegitimization, and academic defunding.

## Examples

- Fascist movements of the 1930s used the Weimar Republic's press freedom, assembly rights, and electoral participation to organize their destruction.
- Contemporary: Anti-democratic movements claiming platforms' deplatforming is "censorship" while working to remove press freedom and academic independence.
- The "cancel culture" framing: Stanley would read this as invoking tolerance norms to protect intolerant organizing from consequences.

## Effectiveness / Critique

- **Risk of authoritarian inversion**: Critics argue the paradox can be weaponized in reverse — governments claiming they must suppress "intolerant" speech can use this to justify authoritarian censorship of legitimate dissent. The paradox provides no bright-line test.
- **Popper's own qualifier often dropped**: Public invocations of the paradox frequently omit Popper's specific criterion (rejection of rational argument + resort to violence) and apply it to any speech one finds objectionable.
- **Stanley's application contested**: Some scholars argue Stanley's deployment of the paradox in *How Fascism Works* conflates fascist movements with right-wing movements more broadly, over-extending the label.
- **Not a policy prescription**: Popper himself treated it as a philosophical observation, not a policy manual; translating it into content moderation or legal restrictions raises separate empirical and normative questions.
