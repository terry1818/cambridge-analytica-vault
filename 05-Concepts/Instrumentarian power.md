## Definition

Instrumentarian power is a concept introduced by Shoshana Zuboff in *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (2019) to describe a historically novel form of social power that operates through pervasive behavioral instrumentation rather than through the violence, terror, or ideological indoctrination characteristic of earlier forms of authoritarian control. Instrumentarian power shapes human behavior at scale by monitoring behavior comprehensively, predicting behavioral futures, and intervening with precisely calibrated stimuli — notifications, prices, cues, frictions, rewards — to produce desired behavioral outcomes. Where totalitarianism sought to reshape consciousness, instrumentarianism seeks to shape behavior directly, at the level of action rather than thought, and to do so invisibly and continuously.

Zuboff presents instrumentarian power as the third pillar of her surveillance capitalism framework, following [[Behavioral surplus]] (the raw material) and [[Behavioral futures markets]] (the revenue engine). Instrumentarian power is the *political* consequence of allowing behavioral futures markets to mature: the accumulation of behavioral prediction capacity becomes, at scale, a capacity to govern populations.

## Origin / history

Zuboff introduces the concept partly through contrast with Hannah Arendt's analysis of totalitarianism. Where Arendt documented total power exercised through violence and the obliteration of private life, Zuboff argues that instrumentarian power is total in a different sense: it is total because it operates *below* the level of consciousness, shaping behavior without requiring the subject to hold any particular belief or to be aware of being controlled.

The concept also draws on B.F. Skinner's behavioral psychology — the "Other One" in Zuboff's text is the instrumentarian ideal of a society operating on behavioral modification principles — and on Foucault's analysis of surveillance as a disciplinary technology (the panopticon), though Zuboff argues instrumentarianism goes further because it is not merely a threat of visibility but an actual continuous behavioral nudge.

## How it works

Instrumentarian power operates through five overlapping mechanisms:

1. **Ubiquitous sensing**: Instruments embedded in smartphones, IoT devices, platforms, and physical infrastructure continuously capture behavioral data, producing the [[Behavioral surplus]] that feeds the system.
2. **Behavioral prediction**: Machine learning processes surplus data into prediction scores — propensities, likely choices, vulnerabilities — at the individual level.
3. **Behavioral modification**: Algorithmic systems deliver precisely timed stimuli (push notifications, price changes, feed curation, social validation or ostracism signals) designed to nudge individuals toward predicted-or-preferred behaviors. This is what Zuboff calls "actuating" predictions — making them come true.
4. **Population-scale governance**: At scale, the modification of millions of individual behaviors produces predictable aggregate outcomes — electoral, economic, social — without any individual being aware of being governed.
5. **Asymmetric knowledge**: The power of instrumentarianism rests entirely on the asymmetry between the detailed behavioral knowledge held by platform and government actors and the ignorance of the monitored subject about how that knowledge is used.

## Application in this domain

Instrumentarian power is the end-state framing for the vault's core thesis:

- Cambridge Analytica's psychographic microtargeting was an early application of instrumentarian power logic to electoral politics: using behavioral prediction to deliver individually calibrated influence at scale.
- The post-CA development documented in [[Hypothesis - Federal database supersedes CA model]] is the absorption of instrumentarian capacity by the state itself: rather than contracting a private firm, government agencies directly purchase behavioral data from brokers, building an in-house instrumentarian infrastructure.
- The combination of [[Clearview AI]]-style biometric identification, [[Venntel]]-style location tracking, and Palantir-style integration platforms constitutes the technical architecture of instrumentarian power in the hands of a government — one that, unlike surveillance capitalism's commercial version, does not need the subject's commercial participation to function.
- [[Instrumentarian power]] differs from the CA model in that its behavioral modification capacity is not limited to advertising and political messaging; it extends to physical access control, benefits eligibility, immigration enforcement, and criminal justice — domains where behavioral prediction translates directly into coercive state action.

## Examples

- **Facebook's news feed algorithm** — curates emotional content to maximize engagement; at scale, produces predictable shifts in mood, political attitudes, and behavior
- **Cambridge Analytica voter targeting** — applied instrumentarian logic to electoral microtargeting
- **China's social credit system** — cited by Zuboff as an extreme state instantiation of instrumentarian power; behavioral scoring systems directly linked to privileges and punishments
- **ICE's use of commercial location data** — purchasing behavioral surplus from brokers to predict and locate undocumented migrants
- **Predictive policing systems** — converting behavioral surplus into law enforcement targeting scores

## Effectiveness / critique

Zuboff's concept has been influential in articulating what distinguishes contemporary surveillance from earlier forms of state monitoring. Critics note:

- The concept risks overstating the efficacy of behavioral nudging; human behavior is more resistant to manipulation than the model implies.
- The distinction between instrumentarian and totalitarian power may be less sharp in practice: authoritarian states employ both.
- Some critics argue Zuboff's framework is too US/West-centric and underweights state surveillance in non-capitalist contexts.

Defenders respond that the framework is about the *logic* of the system, not its perfection: instrumentarian power does not need to be perfectly effective to be structurally novel and politically consequential.
