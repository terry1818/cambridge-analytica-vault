# surveillance capitalism

## Definition

Surveillance capitalism is the term coined by Harvard Business School professor emerita [[Shoshana Zuboff]] for an economic order that, in her phrasing, "unilaterally claims private human experience as free raw material for translation into behavioral data." That data is then computed into "prediction products" — forecasts of what people will do now, soon, and later — which are traded in what Zuboff calls "behavioral futures markets." The customers are businesses with a commercial interest in knowing and shaping future behavior. Crucially, in Zuboff's account the human beings who generate the data are not the customers and not the product; they are the *raw-material source*, mostly unaware of the extraction.

## Origin / history

Zuboff introduced the term in a 2014 academic essay and developed it fully in her 2019 book *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism: The Fight for a Human Future at the New Frontier of Power*. Her historical claim is that surveillance capitalism was *invented*, not inevitable: she locates its origin at Google around 2001–2003, when the company discovered that the exhaust data of search behavior — far more than it needed to improve the search service — could be used to predict and target advertising with unprecedented precision. Zuboff calls this excess data "behavioral surplus." Google's successful monetization of behavioral surplus, she argues, was then adopted by Facebook and propagated across the tech economy as the default business model of the consumer internet.

## How it works

The mechanism has a recognizable cycle. (1) **Extraction**: digital products and services are designed to capture far more behavioral data than is needed to deliver the service — clicks, location, dwell time, social graph, voice, biometric signals. (2) **Behavioral surplus**: this excess is the proprietary input. (3) **Prediction products**: machine-learning models convert surplus into forecasts of behavior, sold to advertisers and other clients. (4) **Behavioral futures markets**: the trade in those forecasts. Zuboff's most pointed claim is that prediction eventually slides into *modification* — "the surest way to predict behavior is to intervene at its source and shape it" — so the system acquires an incentive not merely to anticipate behavior but to nudge, herd, and condition it. This is the "instrumentarian power" she warns undermines individual autonomy and democratic self-governance.

## Application in this domain

Surveillance capitalism is the macro-frame that makes the Cambridge Analytica story legible. CA did not create the data it weaponized; it exploited a data ecosystem that surveillance capitalism had already built — Facebook's behavioral-surplus extraction made the [[Facebook-CA Data Scandal|harvested profiles]] possible. The vault's broader argument extends Zuboff: the same extraction logic feeds [[data brokers]], the location-data trade ([[Gravy Analytics]], [[Babel Street]]), and ultimately government surveillance. Where Zuboff focuses on private-sector behavioral modification for profit, the vault tracks how that commercial infrastructure becomes available to state actors — the "[[commercial surveillance]]"-to-government pipeline documented by [[Byron Tau]].

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed scope:** Zuboff presents surveillance capitalism as a systemic, civilization-level mutation of capitalism, not merely a privacy problem.
- **Empirical basis:** The extraction-and-targeting business model is thoroughly documented; the term has become standard in policy and scholarship.
- **Academic critique:** Critics (e.g., economist Will Rinehart; some media scholars) argue Zuboff over-generalizes — that "surveillance capitalism" lumps distinct practices together, that she overstates behavioral-modification efficacy, and that the framing is more polemic than testable theory. Others fault her for treating Google's path as the only possible one. The vault treats the *extraction model* as documented fact and the strongest *behavioral-modification* claims as contested — the same evidentiary posture it applies to [[psychographic microtargeting]].
