# SORM — Russia's Mandated Lawful-Interception Surveillance System

## Definition

SORM (Sistema Operativno-Rozysknykh Meropriyatiy — System for Operative Investigative Activities) is a Russian legal and technical framework that compels all telecommunications operators to install FSB-supplied hardware giving the security service warrantless, real-time access to communications content and metadata. Unlike Western "wiretap" systems that require court approval and operator cooperation at the moment of interception, SORM grants the FSB a direct, operator-independent tap: the hardware box is installed on the ISP or carrier's own infrastructure, and the agency keys in remotely without informing the operator of the target or legal basis.

## Origin / History

SORM originated in KGB research from the mid-1980s. Its first statutory mandate — SORM-1 — came into force in 1995, initially covering analogue telephone lines. A second generation, SORM-2, extended the requirement to internet service providers in 1998, predating wide Russian internet adoption. SORM-3, whose requirements were codified around 2014, expanded collection to all media types including Wi-Fi hotspots and social-network traffic, and required data retention of up to three years. The [[Yarovaya Law]] (2016) reinforced SORM by adding mandatory six-month storage of communications content, cementing the most expansive surveillance mandate in Russian history. Soldatov and Borogan's *The Red Web* (2015) provides the most detailed English-language account of SORM's origins, tracing it from KGB design institutes through to its internet-era expansion.

## How It Works

1. **Hardware mandate**: Every licensed Russian telecom and ISP must purchase and install an FSB-specified SORM device ("black box") at their own expense. Refusal means loss of operating licence.
2. **Direct access**: The FSB connects to the box remotely and can intercept without notifying the carrier. Operators are explicitly prohibited from disclosing that surveillance is occurring.
3. **No judicial pre-authorisation at the point of access**: Russian law requires an order be obtained, but the FSB is not required to present it to the operator; it holds the order internally. Courts rarely, if ever, deny requests.
4. **Expanding scope**: Each SORM generation widened the data types collected. SORM-3 adds aggregation of metadata across sessions to build long-term behavioural profiles.

## Application in This Domain

SORM is the domestic hardware layer underneath broader Russian information-control architecture. It interfaces directly with [[Roskomnadzor]]'s blocking orders (content filtered at the network level) and provides the FSB raw intelligence on journalists, dissidents, and opposition figures. It is the structural reason Russia was able to surveil Novaya Gazeta journalists and Navalny supporters with minimal legal friction. In the info-warfare frame, SORM represents the domestic surveillance half of a system whose offensive arm is entities like the [[Internet Research Agency]].

## Examples

- Nokia subsidiary was reported in 2019 (TechCrunch) to have supplied SORM-compliant equipment to Russian carrier VEON/Beeline, raising Western export-compliance concerns.
- SORM was used to monitor communications of journalists covering the 1999 Moscow apartment bombings, a key episode in Soldatov and Borogan's account.
- Post-2022 invasion, SORM data is believed to have been used to identify Russians sharing anti-war content on messaging apps.

## Effectiveness / Critique

SORM is highly effective as a tool of targeted political surveillance and a deterrent to open dissent. Its weakness is volume: blanket collection at scale generates noise that degrades analytical utility. Civil liberties critics note the complete absence of meaningful judicial oversight as the system's defining structural flaw. The Yarovaya Law's data-retention requirements were widely regarded by ISPs as technically and financially impossible to comply with fully, creating selective enforcement that itself becomes a tool of selective repression.
