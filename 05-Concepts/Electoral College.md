# Electoral College

## Definition

The Electoral College is the body of 538 electors that formally elects the President and Vice President of the United States. Each state receives a number of electors equal to its total congressional delegation (House seats plus two senators); the District of Columbia receives three under the Twenty-third Amendment. A candidate needs a 270-vote majority to win. Crucially, the system is indirect: voters in each state choose a slate of electors pledged to a candidate, and in 48 states plus D.C. the statewide popular-vote winner takes all of that state's electors. Maine and Nebraska allocate by congressional district. Because every state receives two "senatorial" electors regardless of population, the College systematically over-weights small-population states and can — and repeatedly does — produce a president who lost the national popular vote.

## Origin / history

The Electoral College was created at the 1787 Constitutional Convention as a compromise among delegates who distrusted both direct democracy and election of the president by Congress. It was entangled with slavery: the three-fifths clause inflated the elector count of slave states. The original design (Article II) had each elector cast two undifferentiated votes for president; the chaotic 1800 election prompted the Twelfth Amendment (1804), which separated presidential and vice-presidential balloting. The system has generated five "wrong-winner" elections — 1824, 1876, 1888, 2000, and 2016 — in which the popular-vote loser took the presidency.

## How it works

Mechanically, voters do not vote for a candidate; they vote for a pledged elector slate. After the November election, electors meet in their state capitals in December to cast votes, which are transmitted to Congress and counted in a joint session in January. The Electoral Count Reform Act of 2022 — passed after January 6 — clarified that the Vice President's role in the count is purely ministerial and raised the threshold for congressional objections. A "faithless elector" is one who breaks a pledge; per the National Archives there have been roughly 165 instances historically, none decisive. In *Chiafalo v. Washington* (2020) the Supreme Court unanimously held that states may bind electors and penalize or replace faithless ones.

## Application in this domain

The Electoral College matters to this vault on two axes. First, as a **structural minority-rule mechanism** it is a central exhibit in democratic-backsliding analysis: it allows a party to hold the presidency without national majority support, reducing the electoral cost of unpopular agendas. Second, its **indirect, multi-step certification process** created the procedural seams that [[Stop the Steal]] and the broader [[Election Denial Infrastructure]] tried to exploit in 2020 — the fake-elector schemes in seven states, pressure on the Vice President to reject slates, and the theory that state legislatures could substitute their own electors. The College is thus simultaneously a backsliding-enabling feature and an attack surface.

## Examples

- [[Stop the Steal]] — the 2020 fake-elector scheme attempted to manufacture alternative College slates in contested states.
- [[Presidential Advisory Commission on Election Integrity]] — premised on the claim that popular-vote totals (which Trump lost) were fraudulent.
- [[National Popular Vote Interstate Compact]] — reform effort to neutralize the College without amending the Constitution.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Defenders' claims:** the College protects federalism, forces coalition-building across regions, and contains disputes within states.
- **Empirical evidence:** in practice it concentrates campaigning in a handful of swing states and has delivered the presidency to the popular-vote loser twice in the last seven elections.
- **Academic critique:** political scientists widely characterize it as counter-majoritarian; reform proposals range from a constitutional amendment for direct election to the interstate compact. Both routes face the same obstacle — small states and the party currently advantaged have little incentive to consent.
