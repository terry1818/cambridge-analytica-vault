## Definition

Net neutrality is the principle that internet service providers (ISPs) and telecommunications companies that carry internet traffic must treat all data packets equally — without discriminating by content, source, application, user, or website. Under net neutrality, an ISP cannot: (1) block legal content; (2) throttle (slow) specific traffic; or (3) create "paid prioritization" arrangements in which companies pay extra for faster or more reliable delivery of their content to users.

The term was coined by Tim Wu in his 2003 paper "Network Neutrality, Broadband Discrimination" in the Journal of Telecommunications and High-Technology Law. Wu's broader intellectual project — which culminates in *The Attention Merchants* (2016) — argues that control of communication infrastructure is always also control of information flows and therefore political power. Net neutrality is the specific legal doctrine that attempts to prevent ISPs from becoming gatekeepers of the attention economy's distribution layer.

## Origin / History

**Regulatory antecedents**: The "common carrier" principle in telecommunications law (requiring telephone companies to carry all calls without discrimination) pre-dates net neutrality as a concept but provides its legal foundation.

**2003**: Tim Wu publishes "Network Neutrality, Broadband Discrimination," coining the term and framing the argument that ISPs should not be able to discriminate among data packets.

**2005–2009**: FCC adopts informal "open internet" principles, not enforceable rules. Several ISPs (including Comcast) are found to have throttled specific traffic (BitTorrent), triggering regulatory action.

**2015-02-26**: FCC under Chairman Tom Wheeler votes 3–2 (along party lines) to adopt the Open Internet Order, classifying broadband internet access as a Title II telecommunications service (common carrier). This is the high-water mark of net neutrality regulation in the United States. The 2015 order prohibits blocking, throttling, and paid prioritization.

**2017**: FCC under Chairman Ajit Pai (appointed by Trump) reverses the 2015 order, reclassifying broadband as an information service and removing net neutrality rules. The reversal takes effect in 2018.

**Post-2017**: Multiple states (California, New York, others) enact state-level net neutrality laws. Federal litigation continues. FCC under Biden administration attempts to restore net neutrality rules (2024); reversed again in court.

**2026 status**: No federal net neutrality rules in effect; state-level patchwork.

## How It Works

Without net neutrality, ISPs can:
- **Block**: Prevent users from accessing specific websites or services (e.g., blocking a competitor's streaming service).
- **Throttle**: Slow delivery of specific content (e.g., slowing Netflix to make their own video service more attractive).
- **Paid prioritization**: Charge content companies for "fast lane" delivery, disadvantaging smaller or non-commercial content producers who cannot afford premium access.

With net neutrality rules in effect, all of these practices are prohibited; all packets must be delivered at equal speed and priority regardless of source or content.

## Application in This Domain

Net neutrality intersects with the information-warfare and attention-economy themes of this vault in several ways:

1. **Control of information infrastructure as political power**: Tim Wu's framing in *The Attention Merchants* argues that whoever controls distribution of attention ultimately controls political discourse. Net neutrality prevents ISPs from becoming gatekeepers of which voices reach audiences.

2. **Right-wing political economy**: The major ISPs and their investors are aligned with deregulatory politics; the repeal of net neutrality in 2017 was a Republican FCC priority. This is consistent with the broader pattern of right-wing donor networks (Koch Network, Chamber of Commerce) opposing communications regulation.

3. **Platform concentration**: The absence of net neutrality rules advantages large, well-resourced platforms (Facebook, Google) that can pay for preferential treatment, potentially disadvantaging new entrants or alternative media ecosystems — relevant to understanding how the digital attention economy is structured.

4. **FCC regulatory capture**: Brendan Carr, the FCC chairman under Trump 2.0 (a noted opponent of net neutrality), is documented in this vault as a figure in the right-wing regulatory apparatus.

## Examples

- **Comcast/BitTorrent (2007)**: Comcast throttled BitTorrent traffic, leading to FCC sanctions — one of the first documented net neutrality violations.
- **AT&T blocking FaceTime (2012)**: AT&T blocked FaceTime unless users subscribed to a more expensive data plan.
- **2015 Open Internet Order**: The regulatory high point; blocked, throttled, and paid-prioritization practices prohibited nationally.
- **2017 repeal**: Ajit Pai's FCC removes all federal net neutrality protections; major ISPs face no federal constraints on traffic management.

## Effectiveness / Critique

Net neutrality proponents argue that ISP discrimination would consolidate power in the hands of carriers and stifle competition, innovation, and political diversity in online expression. Critics (primarily from ISPs and free-market think tanks) argue that net neutrality regulation is unnecessary (markets will discipline anti-consumer behavior), constitutionally problematic (First Amendment arguments), and chills infrastructure investment.

The empirical record is contested: ISP investment did not obviously decline or increase dramatically after the 2015 or 2017 rules. However, documented incidents of throttling and discrimination pre-2015 provide evidence that the behavior occurs absent regulation.
