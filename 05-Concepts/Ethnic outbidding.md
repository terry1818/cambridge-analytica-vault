# Ethnic Outbidding

## Definition

A competitive dynamic — first theorized in political science by Alvin Rabushka and Kenneth Shepsle (1972) and elaborated by Donald Horowitz (1985) — in which parties or factions within an ethnic or racial group compete to be the most extreme, most "authentic," or most uncompromising champion of that group's interests and identity. Each actor outbids rivals not by offering better policy but by signaling stronger ethnic commitment, usually through increasingly exclusionary or hostile rhetoric toward out-groups. Levitsky and Ziblatt draw on this dynamic in *Tyranny of the Minority* (2023) to explain how a numerically shrinking white electoral coalition can produce intensifying rather than moderating politics.

## Origin / History

The concept originates in Rabushka and Shepsle's *Politics in Plural Societies: A Theory of Democratic Instability* (1972), which modeled how multiethnic democracies tend toward ethnic rather than class-based voting, with parties then competing for the most extreme intra-ethnic position. Horowitz's *Ethnic Groups in Conflict* (1985) gave it a socio-psychological dimension, showing how status anxiety and perceived group decline fuel the bidding dynamic. Classic empirical cases are Sri Lanka (1948–1983), Northern Ireland, and Sudan.

Levitsky and Ziblatt invoke the logic in *Tyranny of the Minority* to argue that the Republican Party, facing a demographic transition that is reducing the relative size of its traditional white voter coalition, faces incentives to intensify ethnic and cultural appeals rather than expand its coalition — outbidding within its existing base rather than competing for a changing electorate.

## How It Works

1. A party perceives its ethnic/cultural in-group shrinking as a share of the electorate, or threatened by out-group gains.
2. Rather than moderate to attract new voters, it competes for ever-higher turnout and commitment from its existing base.
3. Intra-party competition rewards figures who make the most extreme in-group appeals.
4. Rhetoric escalates: from policy disagreement to cultural threat to existential threat to calls for restriction or suppression of out-groups.
5. Once established, the dynamic is self-reinforcing — any figure who moderates is outbid by a more extreme rival accusing them of betrayal.

## Application in This Domain

This framework explains why demographic change might accelerate rather than moderate extreme politics in the short term. Applied to the vault's COS/Project 2025 cluster: the legislative vehicle of the Convention of States is located in state legislatures where structural advantages (rural overrepresentation, gerrymandering) partially insulate it from the national demographic trend. The outbidding dynamic may drive the urgency of the capture timeline — capturing constitutional structures before the demographic window closes. The vault should test: is there evidence that COS/Heritage messaging intensified after demographic projections showed the majority-minority transition approaching?

## Examples

- Sri Lanka: Sinhala-only language legislation (1956) was passed through competitive outbidding between UNP and SLFP, escalating to ethnic civil war.
- Northern Ireland: Unionist parties competed over who would most firmly resist power-sharing with nationalists.
- Levitsky and Ziblatt's contemporary US example: Republican primary competition rewarding more extreme anti-immigration, anti-DEI, and anti-demographic-change positions.

## Effectiveness / Critique

- **Mechanistic**: Critics argue the outbidding model is too mechanical and does not account adequately for ideology, leadership agency, or moments where ethnic politics de-escalates.
- **Requires intra-ethnic competition**: The model predicts most intensely in situations where multiple parties compete for the same ethnic base; it is less applicable where one party has a near-monopoly on ethnic-bloc loyalty.
- **Application to US contested**: Some scholars argue the US case is better explained by partisan sorting and geographic concentration than by classic ethnic outbidding dynamics from developing-democracy contexts.
- **Recent literature**: A 2020 review in *Nationalism and Ethnic Politics* (Tandfonline) argues the outbidding literature has under-theorized the role of ideology versus pure ethnic signaling.
