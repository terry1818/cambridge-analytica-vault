## Definition

The "newsworthiness exemption" (also called the "newsworthiness exception" or "public interest override") is a content-moderation policy mechanism used by [[Facebook]] (and later adopted in variant forms by other platforms) that permits content by politicians and public figures to remain on the platform even when it would ordinarily violate Facebook's Community Standards. The operative logic is that statements by political figures are inherently newsworthy and that the public interest in seeing and debating those statements outweighs the normal rationale for enforcement (preventing harm, suppressing misinformation, etc.). In practice, the exemption has been applied almost exclusively to right-wing political figures and has functioned as a structural mechanism by which platform policy insulates political actors from content enforcement.

## Origin / History

Facebook maintained an informal public-figure/newsworthiness policy as early as 2009, but this was not publicly disclosed. The policy became more formalized and explicitly political during the 2016 US presidential campaign. Facebook published two blog posts in 2016 and 2019 partially disclosing the approach. The 2016 emergence coincided with intense debate about whether to apply community standards to inflammatory content from the Trump campaign and later the Trump presidency. Research based on internal Facebook documents and interviews with current and former employees (referenced in Frenkel & Kang, 2021) shows that [[Joel Kaplan]]'s policy team was the primary internal champion for the exemption's expansive application.

## How It Works

When a piece of content from a politician or prominent public figure is flagged for violating community standards, Facebook's content review and policy teams can apply a newsworthiness determination that overrides normal enforcement. This determination is made by humans in Facebook's policy division (not automated systems). The criteria for "newsworthiness" are not publicly defined with precision. According to internal documents and reporting, Facebook gave politicians 13 documented exemptions in a single year (2021–2022 period reported by Washington Post). The exemption operates in addition to the broader [[Newsworthiness exemption]]-adjacent policy of allowing politicians' ads to contain false or misleading content (a related but distinct policy, the political ads exemption).

## Application in This Domain

In the context of this vault's focus on political targeting and information warfare, the newsworthiness exemption functions as a structural asymmetry in platform enforcement:

1. **Trump content**: The exemption was invoked most visibly for Donald Trump's posts during his presidency, shielding content that included voter-fraud misinformation, inflammatory rhetoric, and (pre-January 6) content that would have been removed from a non-political account.
2. **Conservative publishers**: The exemption logic was extended beyond politicians to cover content shared by right-wing media outlets, as [[Joel Kaplan]] and his team argued that removing such content would suppress conservative speech.
3. **Counterpoint**: The exemption was rarely if ever invoked for left-wing politicians or progressive content creators, creating a documented asymmetry that progressive civil society organizations cited as evidence of structural right-wing bias in Facebook's enforcement.

## Examples

- Trump posts containing voter-fraud claims during the 2020 election were initially protected under newsworthiness logic before Facebook ultimately applied limited labels
- Content from Brazilian President Jair Bolsonaro regarding COVID-19 misinformation was subject to similar debates about newsworthiness overrides
- In documented internal cases (Frenkel & Kang), Trump's "when the looting starts, the shooting starts" post in May 2020 was left up after executive intervention citing newsworthiness, overruling integrity team recommendations

## Effectiveness / Critique

Critics argue the newsworthiness exemption:
- Creates a two-tier enforcement system in which politicians — uniquely dangerous actors with large audiences — are least subject to content standards
- Is inherently asymmetric in practice (applied more often to right-wing political figures)
- Undermines the credibility of community standards as universal rules
- Transfers responsibility for norm-setting from clear rules to ad hoc human judgment in Facebook's policy division, which is staffed with conservative political operatives (per [[Joel Kaplan]]'s network)

The [[Facebook Oversight Board]] in its May 2021 ruling on the Trump account specifically criticized Facebook's application of the newsworthiness exemption and the "arbitrary" use of the indefinite suspension, calling for clearer rules.
