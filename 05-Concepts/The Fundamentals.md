## Definition

*The Fundamentals: A Testimony to the Truth* is a twelve-volume series of ninety essays published between 1910 and 1915, funded by [[Lyman Stewart]] (co-founder of Union Oil Company of California) and his brother Milton Stewart. The essays were written by sixty-four authors representing major conservative Protestant denominations from the United States, Canada, and Great Britain, and were distributed free of charge to an estimated 300,000+ English-speaking pastors, theology professors, seminary students, missionaries, and YMCA/YWCA workers. The series gave the term "fundamentalist" to the world — though the word itself only came into wide use when journalist Curtis Lee Laws coined it in 1920 to describe those willing to "do battle royal" for the fundamentals of the faith.

*The Fundamentals* is the foundational document of what became the Protestant fundamentalist movement, and it is the earliest large-scale instance of oil-industry capital being deployed to shape the theological direction of American Christianity.

## Origin / history

- **Context**: By the early 20th century, Protestant denominations were increasingly divided between "modernists" who accepted higher biblical criticism, evolution, and social reform theology, and "conservatives" who defended biblical inerrancy, the virgin birth, the bodily resurrection, and traditional Christian doctrine.
- **Conception**: Lyman Stewart, deeply committed to biblical conservatism and alarmed by the drift of seminary education, conceived a project to distribute a definitive statement of orthodox Protestant doctrine to every English-speaking clergyman and religious educator he could reach.
- **Execution**: Stewart commissioned A.C. Dixon (later R.A. Torrey) as editor. The Testimony Publishing Company in Chicago produced the volumes. Stewart and his brother each contributed $150,000.
- **Distribution**: Over 3 million individual volumes were published and mailed free of charge. The scale of this distribution — private capital funding mass ideological dissemination to clergy — was unprecedented.
- **Editorial content**: The ninety essays addressed biblical inspiration and authority, attacks on higher criticism, defenses of traditional doctrine (virgin birth, atonement, resurrection, second coming), and critiques of evolution, socialism, and Christian Science. The series was notably non-sectarian within Protestant conservatism, including Baptist, Presbyterian, Methodist, Anglican, and other contributors.

## How it works

*The Fundamentals* operated as an ideological infrastructure investment: by identifying, articulating, and distributing a set of "non-negotiable" doctrinal positions, it defined the terrain of theological orthodoxy in ways that made compromise with modernism theologically costly. The effect was:

1. **Naming**: It gave the movement its identity marker; clergy who affirmed the fundamentals could identify themselves as "fundamentalists."
2. **Credentialing**: The range and eminence of contributors gave the essays intellectual authority they might not have achieved from a single author or institution.
3. **Network formation**: The distribution list became a de facto network of theologically conservative clergy who shared a common doctrinal reference point.
4. **Patron model**: It established the template of private industrial capital funding theological conservatism at scale — the model [[J. Howard Pew]] would later apply to [[Spiritual Mobilization]] and [[Christianity Today]].

## Examples

Key doctrines defended in the essays (the "five fundamentals" later crystallized from the series):
1. Biblical inerrancy
2. The virgin birth of Jesus
3. The physical resurrection of Jesus
4. The atoning sacrifice of Jesus
5. The literal second coming of Jesus

Specific essays engaged: refutations of higher biblical criticism (Wellhausen's Documentary Hypothesis), defenses of the historical accuracy of Genesis, critiques of Darwinian evolution, and attacks on what the authors saw as the Social Gospel's substitution of political for spiritual salvation.

## Effectiveness / critique

**Effectiveness**: The series achieved its immediate goal of distributing a coherent conservative Protestant theology to clergy at massive scale. It helped crystallize a distinct fundamentalist identity and delayed the complete modernist capture of major denominations. It remains in print and is cited in conservative evangelical theological education.

**Critique**:
- The framing of the series as a "spontaneous" theological movement obscures the role of Stewart's private oil wealth in funding it — it was, in part, a corporate patron project.
- Scholars note that the essays were not uniformly "fundamentalist" in the later, separatist sense: some contributors were premillennialists, others amillennialists; some were separatists, others mainstream. The label simplified a diverse collection.
- Higher critics of the 20th century identified specific essays as engaging strawman versions of liberal theology.
