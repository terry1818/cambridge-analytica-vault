## Definition

The Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), formerly "One Belt, One Road" (OBOR), is the Chinese government's large-scale global infrastructure and economic connectivity program, inaugurated by President Xi Jinping in September–October 2013. It finances and co-constructs roads, railways, ports, pipelines, fiber-optic networks, airports, and special economic zones across participating countries, primarily in Asia, Africa, the Middle East, and Central and Eastern Europe. The initiative encompasses a "Silk Road Economic Belt" (overland corridors across Central Asia and Europe) and a "21st Century Maritime Silk Road" (port and shipping infrastructure across the Indian Ocean and beyond).

## Origin and history

- Announced by Xi Jinping during a state visit to Kazakhstan (September 2013) and to the Indonesian parliament (October 2013).
- Framed as a connectivity and development initiative echoing the historical Silk Road trade routes.
- By the early 2020s, over 140 countries had signed BRI cooperation agreements with China.
- The program has been restructured since approximately 2021 to reduce the scale of some loans following criticism of "debt-trap diplomacy" and project viability concerns.

## How it works

The BRI operates primarily through:
1. **Chinese state-bank lending** (China Development Bank, Export-Import Bank of China) to host-country governments or state enterprises, often at concessional rates but with Chinese state-enterprise construction contractors
2. **Build-operate-transfer or long-term lease arrangements** for key infrastructure (ports, railroads)
3. **Digital Silk Road component**: fiber-optic cables, 5G infrastructure (Huawei), AI surveillance systems, data centers
4. **Political-diplomatic alignment**: hosting countries typically adopt positions favorable to China in multilateral fora (e.g., not criticizing Xinjiang policies at the UN Human Rights Council)

## Application in this domain (autocratic statecraft)

Applebaum's *Autocracy, Inc.* discusses the BRI as an instrument by which China extends influence over both democratic and autocratic governments without requiring ideological alignment. The mechanism is economic leverage and institutional dependency rather than direct ideological export. Key patterns documented in scholarship:

- **Autocracy concentration**: Between 2014 and 2019, approximately 77% of total BRI construction spending went to autocratic or semi-competitive electoral regimes (documented by academic researchers, Tier-2 finding).
- **Governance norm export**: Chinese BRI packages frequently include surveillance technology (particularly Huawei-supplied "safe city" systems) that gives recipient governments enhanced capacity to monitor their populations — used against protesters and political opponents.
- **Debt leverage**: Several cases (Sri Lanka's Hambantota Port leased to China; Zambia's debt renegotiations; Pakistan's CPEC obligations) are cited as examples of how BRI debt creates political dependencies. The "debt-trap" framing is disputed — some scholars argue the evidence for deliberate strategic debt entrapment is thin — but the dependency effects of concentrated infrastructure debt are documented.
- **Multilateral blocking**: Countries with significant BRI exposure have been documented blocking or weakening EU and UN statements critical of China.

In Venezuela, Applebaum specifically notes Chinese-supplied equipment (including water cannons and surveillance tools) used against protesters — illustrating how Chinese commercial exports function as autocratic-support infrastructure independent of formal BRI loans.

## Examples

- Hambantota Port, Sri Lanka (99-year lease to China Merchants Port Holdings after debt distress)
- CPEC (China-Pakistan Economic Corridor): >$60 billion in infrastructure pledges
- Huawei "safe city" surveillance deployments in Ecuador, Kenya, Zimbabwe, Zambia, and others
- Chinese digital surveillance equipment supplied to Venezuela, Belarus, and Iran — documented in multiple cases

## Effectiveness and critique

**Evidence that BRI extends autocratic influence:**
- Documented correlation between BRI participation and reduced criticism of China in multilateral bodies
- Chinese surveillance technology is documented as being used to monitor political dissidents and ethnic minorities in recipient countries
- Economic dependence creates leverage in bilateral negotiations

**Critiques of the "autocracy promotion" framing:**
- BRI also extends to many democracies (Italy signed a BRI MOU 2019; Greece, Portugal); Chinese statecraft is primarily mercantilist rather than ideologically driven
- "Debt-trap diplomacy" as a strategic frame is contested: some projects are genuinely commercially driven, some recipient governments negotiated BRI agreements despite knowing the risks
- BRI's effectiveness has declined since 2020 due to quality concerns, debt sustainability pressures, and geopolitical pushback

**Applebaum's specific contribution:** Frames BRI not as an isolated Chinese foreign policy tool but as one component of a broader autocratic ecosystem in which Russia provides security backing, China provides economic leverage and surveillance technology, and smaller autocracies like Belarus and Venezuela benefit from the network without being formal BRI members.
