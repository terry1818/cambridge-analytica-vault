## Definition

Released-time religious education (also called "release time") is an arrangement in which public school students are excused from regular school hours, with parental consent, to attend religious instruction offered by private religious organizations. The instruction may take place off school premises (constitutionally permissible under *Zorach v. Clauson*, 1952) or, in an earlier model, on school premises (ruled unconstitutional under *McCollum v. Board of Education*, 1948). Released-time programs are a documented mechanism through which religious organizations gain structured access to public-school children during the academic day.

## Origin / history

- **1914**: The first documented released-time program in the United States begins in Gary, Indiana, under superintendent William Wirt. The program allowed children to leave school for one or more periods per week to attend religious instruction at their own churches, with parental consent and cooperation of church authorities.
- **1920s–1940s**: Released-time programs spread rapidly. By 1922, programs operated in 23 states with 40,000 students. By 1947, enrollment reached 2 million students in 2,200 communities — the historical peak.
- **1948**: *Illinois ex rel. McCollum v. Board of Education*, 333 U.S. 203: the Supreme Court rules that released-time programs conducted *on* school premises violate the Establishment Clause because they use tax-supported facilities for religious purposes. On-premises programs are struck down.
- **1952**: *Zorach v. Clauson*, 343 U.S. 306: the Supreme Court rules 6-3 that released-time programs conducted *off* school premises are constitutional. Justice William O. Douglas writes that "we are a religious people whose institutions presuppose a Supreme Being." The program must use no public funds and involve teachers only in releasing children, not in administering religious instruction.
- **Post-*Zorach***: Released-time programs continue legally as long as they are off-campus and do not use public resources. Many evangelical and Catholic organizations maintain active programs. Significant geographic variation in prevalence.
- **2001**: *[[Good News Club v. Milford Central School]]* expands religious access to school facilities in a complementary but distinct way: after-school programs rather than during-school release.
- **2010s–present**: Released-time programs experience a revival in several states. Katherine Stewart (*The Good News Club*, 2012) documents their contemporary use as part of a broader theocratic-wing education strategy.

## How it works

1. A private religious organization (church, parachurch ministry, evangelical organization) establishes a released-time program in a community.
2. The organization obtains a formal agreement with the local school district.
3. Parents sign consent forms permitting their children to be released for a specified period — typically one hour per week.
4. Released students leave campus during school hours and attend religious instruction at a nearby location (a church, a trailer, a rented room).
5. Students who do not participate remain at school in secular instruction or study hall.
6. Public school teachers are responsible only for releasing the consenting children — they play no role in the religious instruction.

## Application in this domain

Released-time programs are an instrument of what Katherine Stewart calls the "stealth assault" on public education: a legal pathway to bring religious recruitment and instruction into the orbit of compulsory public schooling without formally breaching the Establishment Clause. They are particularly prevalent in rural and suburban communities where a single religious tradition dominates. In such contexts, the social pressure on non-participating children can be substantial despite the formal voluntariness of the program.

The programs connect to the broader theocratic-wing education-capture strategy documented in the vault, which includes:
- Home schooling advocacy ([[HSLDA]])
- Charter school proliferation ([[Hillsdale College]])
- After-school religious clubs ([[Good News Club v. Milford Central School]])
- Classical Christian education networks
- Textbook and curriculum lobbying

Released-time programs occupy a distinct niche: they are the only mechanism that allows religious instruction to occur *during* the regular school day with the school's institutional cooperation.

## Examples

- **Tri-County Released Time Bible Education (Pennsylvania)**: one of the largest contemporary programs; serves multiple school districts in rural Pennsylvania; affiliated with evangelical ministries.
- **Released Time Bible Education (national)**: a network of programs coordinated by evangelical organizations.
- **Utah**: Released-time programs are widespread, primarily serving the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints (LDS) "seminary" programs adjacent to schools.
- **South Carolina**: Multiple districts have active programs; often operated by local evangelical churches.

## Effectiveness / critique

**Supporters** argue that released-time programs:
- Respect parental rights to provide religious education.
- Do not compel participation.
- Operate off public property without public funds.
- Have a long constitutional history.

**Critics** argue that released-time programs:
- Create de facto social coercion in communities where one religion dominates, penalizing non-participating children through social stigma.
- Allow religious organizations to leverage the compulsory attendance structure of public schools to access captive student populations.
- Are frequently used for explicit evangelism and religious recruitment of children.
- Are concentrated in communities without religious diversity, precisely where the children of non-participating families face the greatest social pressure.
- Stewart (*The Good News Club*) documents programs that use school referral lists, grade school contacts, and school-adjacent locations in ways designed to blur the line between school-sponsored and private religious activities.
