# Competitive authoritarianism

## Definition

Competitive authoritarianism is a hybrid regime type identified by Steven Levitsky and Lucan Way in which formal democratic institutions (elections, legislatures, courts, press freedom) exist and are used by genuine opposition, but incumbents violate the rules of competition so systematically — through media manipulation, abuse of state resources, selective prosecution, and harassment — that the playing field is structurally tilted in their favour. The regime is neither fully democratic (the rules are broken too egregiously) nor classically authoritarian (opposition is not eliminated and elections can occasionally be lost). Levitsky and Way classify competitive authoritarianism as a *subtype of authoritarianism*, not a flawed democracy.

**Bibliographic precision**: The concept originates in Levitsky and Lucan Way's 2002 article "Elections Without Democracy: The Rise of Competitive Authoritarianism," *Journal of Democracy* 13(2). It was expanded in their 2010 book *Competitive Authoritarianism: Hybrid Regimes after the Cold War* (Cambridge University Press). The concept is associated with Levitsky and Way jointly, not with Levitsky's later solo or co-authored work with Ziblatt; the vault source note cites *Levitsky 2023 - Tyranny of the Minority* (co-authored with Ziblatt), which discusses competitive authoritarianism but is not the concept's originating text.

## Origin / history

Levitsky and Way coined the term in response to the proliferation of post-Cold War regimes that held elections but were clearly not democracies. Earlier typologies (semi-democracy, pseudo-democracy, electoral authoritarianism) existed but Levitsky and Way argued these were analytically imprecise. Their 2002 *Journal of Democracy* article proposed "competitive authoritarianism" as a specific category defined by the genuine (if unfair) contestation of power. The 2010 book examined 35 cases and developed a theory of why some competitive authoritarian regimes democratised while others consolidated. In *Tyranny of the Minority* (2023), Levitsky (with Ziblatt) applied the concept to analyse the risk of the United States sliding into competitive authoritarianism under Trump-era Republican Party developments.

## How it works

The regime type is characterised by four types of playing-field tilting:

1. **Media control**: Incumbents do not need to shut down all independent media; they selectively reward loyal outlets, harass critics, and control advertising revenue flows to create structural disadvantage for opposition journalism.
2. **State resource abuse**: Government budgets, regulatory approvals, public employment, and state contracts are deployed for electoral purposes.
3. **Legal weaponisation**: Courts, prosecutors, tax authorities, and regulatory agencies are used selectively against opposition figures — not to eliminate all opposition but to impose disproportionate costs on it.
4. **Rule manipulation**: Electoral laws, term limits, constituency boundaries, and campaign finance rules are changed to benefit incumbents.

The result is a regime where opposition can participate, can win local races, and occasionally wins national elections — but faces a structural disadvantage that grows over time if left unchecked.

## Application in this domain

This is among the most directly applicable regime concepts for the vault's analysis of contemporary US politics. Key applications: (1) the *Tyranny of the Minority* (2023) application of the concept to assess whether US Republican Party behaviour constitutes movement toward competitive authoritarianism — minority veto institutions, state-level electoral law manipulation, norm-breaking; (2) the Hungarian model under Orban as a documented case of democratic backsliding into competitive authoritarianism that figures in the international right-wing network documented by the vault (CPAC Hungary, Heritage connections, Yoram Hazony); (3) distinguishing competitive authoritarianism from [[Anocracy]] — they overlap but are distinct; competitive authoritarianism is a regime description while anocracy is a Polity-scale position. Cases can be both simultaneously.

## Examples

- **Russia 1990s–2000**: Levitsky and Way's paradigm case of competitive authoritarianism consolidating into outright autocracy under Putin.
- **Venezuela under Chávez (1999–2013)**: Elections held, opposition participated, but media, state resources, and judiciary increasingly tilted.
- **Hungary under Orban (2010–present)**: Widely cited as a contemporary case of competitive authoritarianism within the EU framework.
- **Philippines under Marcos Jr. / Duterte era**: Elections occur, but incumbents use state resources, media control, and legal harassment systematically.
- **United States (risk assessment)**: Levitsky and Ziblatt in *Tyranny of the Minority* argue US electoral structures give the Republican Party advantages that, combined with norm erosion, create competitive authoritarian risk — not yet a fully tilted system but trending toward the preconditions.

## Effectiveness / critique

The concept has been enormously influential in comparative politics. Key critiques:

1. **Categorical blurring**: Political scientists debate where the line falls between a "backsliding democracy" and a "competitive authoritarian" regime; the classification involves judgment calls that different researchers make differently for the same country.
2. **Static vs. dynamic**: The framework describes a *type* but the more analytically interesting question is the *trajectory* — regimes move through competitive authoritarianism toward consolidation or back toward democracy; the typology can obscure this dynamism.
3. **US application contestation**: Applying the concept to the United States has been controversial. Critics argue US institutions remain more robust than the paradigm cases; proponents (Levitsky, Ziblatt) argue the structural preconditions are being created.
4. **Electoral focus**: The framework centres on electoral institutions; it may underweight non-electoral forms of authoritarian control (surveillance, bureaucratic capture, financial coercion) that are increasingly significant.
