# The right to have rights

## Definition

"The right to have rights" is [[Hannah Arendt]]'s formulation of the foundational precondition for all political and civil rights: membership in an organised political community that recognises and enforces rights. Absent that membership — i.e., when a person is rendered stateless — no specific right (to speech, to fair trial, to life) is practically enforceable, because rights in the modern world are guaranteed by states, not by abstract humanity. The concept identifies a paradox at the heart of human rights discourse: the "rights of man" are supposed to be universal, but in practice they depend on state recognition that can be stripped away.

## Origin / history

Arendt developed the concept in Part Two ("Imperialism"), Chapter Nine ("The Decline of the Nation-State and the End of the Rights of Man") of *The Origins of Totalitarianism* (1951). She wrote from direct personal experience: she had been stateless for eighteen years following her flight from Nazi Germany in 1933, and became a US citizen only in 1951 — the same year the book was published. The formulation is: "We became aware of the existence of a right to have rights (and that means to live in a framework where one is judged by one's actions and opinions) and a right to belong to some kind of organized community, only when millions of people emerged who had lost and could not regain these rights because of the new global political situation." The concept has since been extensively developed in political philosophy (Seyla Benhabib, Étienne Balibar) and international law.

## How it works

The concept operates on two levels:

1. **Descriptive/analytic**: Arendt observes that when states strip people of citizenship — or refuse to recognise refugees — those people fall into a legal void. International human rights instruments exist, but enforcement depends on state apparatus. The stateless person is formally protected by nothing.
2. **Normative**: Arendt identifies a meta-right — the right to political membership — that logically precedes all enumerated rights. Violating this meta-right is uniquely devastating because it nullifies all other protections at once.

Totalitarian regimes weaponise this by denaturalising targeted populations (see [[Statelessness as a political weapon]]), thereby removing them from all legal protection prior to other forms of persecution. The technique transforms political enemies into legal non-persons.

## Application in this domain

The vault can deploy this concept when analysing: (1) denaturalisation and mass deportation programs in the Trump 2.0 era — particularly the revocation of birthright citizenship and denaturalisation-via-administrative-process proposals; (2) the treatment of undocumented populations who exist within US territory but outside its rights framework; (3) historical comparisons with interwar European statelessness. The concept is analytically powerful for distinguishing *reduction of rights* (still within the rights framework) from *removal of rights-bearing status* (which is categorically different and historically associated with totalitarian practice).

## Examples

- **Interwar Europe**: Millions of Jews, Roma, and political dissidents stripped of citizenship by Nazi Germany's Nuremberg Laws (1935), rendering them legally stateless within their own country.
- **Post-WWI refugees**: Mass statelessness of White Russians, Armenians, and others after 1918 — the population Arendt observed firsthand.
- **Contemporary**: Rohingya in Myanmar denied citizenship, effectively placed outside legal protection. Gaza Palestinians under blockade conditions Arendt's framework has been applied to by international legal scholars.
- **US 2025**: Executive orders challenging birthright citizenship for children of undocumented immigrants — courts have blocked, but the attempt illustrates the mechanism Arendt described.

## Effectiveness / critique

The concept is widely accepted in political philosophy and international law scholarship. Critiques include: (1) Arendt's framework assumes the nation-state as the unit of rights protection; post-Westphalian international law (ICC, ECHR, UNHCR) partially transcends this, weakening the absoluteness of her claim. (2) Some scholars (e.g., Giorgio Agamben in *Homo Sacer*) extend Arendt's insight but argue she did not go far enough — that the modern state permanently holds the power to produce "bare life" regardless of formal citizenship. (3) Seyla Benhabib argues the concept can ground a cosmopolitan right of hospitality that Arendt herself did not draw out. The concept remains most useful for cases of *formal* statelessness and denaturalisation; it is less precisely applicable to de facto exclusion from rights within a nominal citizenship framework.
