# Anti-Intellectualism in Fascist Politics

## Definition

One of the ten "pillars" of fascist politics identified by philosopher Jason Stanley in *How Fascism Works: The Politics of Us and Them* (Random House, 2018). In Stanley's framework, anti-intellectualism in fascist politics is not simple ignorance or populist distrust of elites — it is a *structural* attack on the institutional production of shared factual reality. Universities, independent journalism, scientific consensus, and expert bodies are targeted because they generate facts and arguments that challenge the mythologized history and false claims on which fascist politics depends. The attack serves to replace fact-based epistemology with loyalty-based epistemology.

## Origin / History

Stanley, a philosopher at Yale specializing in epistemology and political philosophy, developed the ten-pillar framework in *How Fascism Works* (2018), drawing on political philosophy, linguistics (he builds on his earlier work on propaganda), and comparative historical analysis. Anti-intellectualism as a cultural phenomenon was famously analyzed by historian Richard Hofstadter in *Anti-Intellectualism in American Life* (1963); Stanley specifically develops the *fascist political* use of anti-intellectualism as a governing strategy distinct from mere cultural suspicion.

## How It Works

Stanley's account of the mechanism:

1. **Fascist politics requires myth over truth**: Fascist ideology depends on mythologized history (a glorious past, a victimized people, a corrupt elite), falsified threat assessments, and manufactured crises. Truth-seeking institutions threaten these myths.

2. **Universities as targets**: Universities present complex, critical accounts of history and current reality that challenge dominant narratives. In fascist framing, universities are portrayed as hotbeds of indoctrination by a hostile ideological elite rather than truth-seeking institutions.

3. **Expertise delegitimized**: Scientific expertise, professional consensus, and credentialed authority are reframed as markers of a self-serving "expert class" whose conclusions reflect their class interests, not truth. This decouples public discourse from factual accountability.

4. **Loyalty replaces merit**: In the fascist epistemology that anti-intellectualism enables, loyalty to the leader/movement is the measure of correctness. Those who agree with the movement are "common sense" people; those who dissent are corrupt elites.

5. **Shared facts destroyed**: Without a shared factual substrate that both sides accept, political argument becomes tribal — not a contest over what is true but a signal of which side you are on. This makes democratic deliberation impossible.

## Application in This Domain

This framework is directly applicable to the vault's analysis of education capture (Hillsdale, 1776 curriculum, classical Christian education networks, attacks on DEI and social science research funding) and media delegitimization strategies. The key diagnostic question from Stanley is: are attacks on universities and expertise policy disagreements about curriculum or funding, or are they structural attacks on the *institutional capacity* to produce shared factual reality? The latter pattern aligns with Stanley's pillar. The vault can also apply this to Heritage Foundation attacks on "woke" academic research, the 1776 Commission's historical revisionism, and efforts to defund social science and public health research.

## Examples

- Trump's characterization of the press as "enemy of the people" — classic anti-intellectual delegitimization.
- Defunding of academic social science and public health research programs under the Trump 2.0 administration.
- The 1776 Commission report (2021) presenting an alternative US history explicitly countering mainstream academic historiography.
- Hillsdale College's K-12 charter network built around a curriculum explicitly opposing "progressive" academic consensus.
- Rhetoric attacking epidemiologists, climate scientists, and election security experts as a class with corrupt institutional interests.

## Effectiveness / Critique

- **Ten pillars as checklist**: Some scholars argue Stanley's framework is better understood as a diagnostic heuristic than a theory; the pillars are not an integrated causal model but an observed co-occurrence pattern.
- **"Fascism" label contested**: As with other applications of the fascism label to contemporary US politics, many comparative scholars argue that "anti-intellectualism" and "populist attacks on expertise" are features of many non-fascist populist movements — the pillar is not unique to fascism.
- **Epistemological emphasis**: Stanley's background in philosophy means the framework foregrounds epistemological mechanisms (how truth is attacked) more than structural factors (economic conditions, class alliances) that other scholars prioritize.
- **US-specific application**: Stanley's examples are largely US-focused; application to other national contexts requires additional theorization.
