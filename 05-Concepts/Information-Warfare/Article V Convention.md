# Article V Convention

## Definition

**Article V of the US Constitution** provides two paths for proposing constitutional amendments:

1. **The traditional path:** Two-thirds of both houses of Congress propose an amendment, which then requires ratification by three-fourths (38) of state legislatures
2. **The convention path:** Two-thirds (34) of state legislatures apply for a convention; Congress is then required to call one; the convention proposes amendments; same 38-state ratification threshold

**The convention path has never been used.** All 27 existing amendments came through the traditional path. **Convention of States Action is attempting to trigger the first-ever Article V convention.**

## Key thresholds

- **34 of 50 state legislatures** must apply on the same general subject to compel Congress to call a convention
- **One-state-one-vote** at the convention (default CRS interpretation)
- **38 of 50 states** must ratify any proposed amendment

## Current state (2026-01)

- **20 of 34 needed** have passed COS-aligned applications
- **14 more needed** to trigger the convention
- **~28 of 50 state delegations** would be GOP-controlled under current state-legislature balance
- **Republicans alone could compose the convention's output** if convened under current state balance

## Unresolved legal questions

The Article V convention path is constitutionally unprecedented in modern times (last in 1787). **Multiple critical questions remain unresolved:**

1. **Can applications be limited in scope?** COSA claims yes; many legal scholars disagree
2. **Who selects delegates?** Each state legislature decides; some states have already enacted delegate-selection statutes
3. **Can a state recall its delegates?** Yes per Texas statute and Robert Natelson framework; contested
4. **Equal or proportional state representation?** Default is one-state-one-vote; convention can change its own rules
5. **Is delegate behavior at the convention legally binding to the resolution scope?** Convention can adopt its own rules
6. **Can Congress refuse to call?** Most scholars say no (judicially enforceable duty)
7. **Can a state rescind a prior application?** Disputed (Delaware, Maryland, others have tried)
8. **Are amendments subject to judicial review?** Constitutionally, ratified amendments are part of the Constitution; not subject to judicial strike

## Why this matters

Constitutional amendments emerging from a convention:

- **Cannot be reversed by future presidents**
- **Cannot be struck down by courts** (once ratified)
- **Require another constitutional amendment to repeal** (38-state threshold)
- **Permanent capture** vector compared to [[Project 2025]]'s reversible executive actions
