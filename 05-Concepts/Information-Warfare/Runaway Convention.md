# Runaway Convention

## Why it's the central risk

The Article V text does **not** specify:

- Whether convention scope can be limited by state applications
- Whether delegates are bound by state instructions
- What rules govern convention proceedings (the convention sets its own rules)
- Whether and how delegates can be recalled

**This means once a convention is convened, its scope is functionally indeterminate.** [[Convention of States Action]] argues the convention CAN be limited to the application scope; opponents argue this is unenforceable.

## Empirical evidence: 2023 Williamsburg mock convention

In **August 2023**, [[Citizens for Self-Governance]] held a mock convention in Williamsburg, Virginia, to road-test convention procedures. Among the proposals drafted:

- **Federal Legislative and Executive Jurisdiction Proposal 3:** compels federal government to transfer large swaths of federal lands to the states
- **Other proposals beyond the stated three-pillar (fiscal restraint / term limits / federal power) scope**

This mock convention is **the strongest empirical evidence that a real convention would exceed stated scope** Ã¢â‚¬â€ the proponents themselves modeled an expanded-scope convention.

## Legal scholarship

**Robert Natelson** (favored COS legal theorist) argues runaway convention is mostly myth and conventions can be limited. **Brennan Center for Justice**, **Common Cause**, **Lawrence Lessig**, and many constitutional law scholars argue the runaway risk is real and unsmitigable. The legal question is unresolved because **no Article V convention has occurred in 239 years**.

## Why this is harder to mitigate than COS proponents suggest

- The convention adopts its **own rules** (Article V is silent)
- One-state-one-vote default means small/rural states dominate
- Republican-majority delegation count would dominate
- Once amendments are proposed, the question shifts to ratification (38 states needed); but the amendments are already in motion
- **Courts almost certainly would not interfere** with a convention in session; political question doctrine
