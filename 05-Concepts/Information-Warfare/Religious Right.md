# Religious Right

## Definition

The **organized US Christian-right political movement** that emerged in the late 1970s and consolidated through the 1980s — encompassing the **evangelical political organizing infrastructure**, the **Catholic-conservative-traditionalist alliance**, the **Christian-nationalist intellectual movement**, and the **broader theocratic political project** in the contemporary US. The Religious Right is the **historical and contemporary patron-class organizing base** for [[Project 2025]], [[Convention of States Action]], and adjacent theocratic-political infrastructure.

For the comparative-analytical framing of how the Religious Right intersects with the Tech-Right and broader conservative ecosystem, see [[Compare - Religious Right vs Tech Right]].

## Historical formation

The contemporary Religious Right emerged from **discrete organizational events** in the late 1970s:

- **1979:** [[Jerry Falwell Sr.]] founds the **[[Moral Majority]]** — the first mass-membership Christian-right political organization
- **1980:** Religious Right organizing helps elect Ronald Reagan; the **white-evangelical-political-mobilization template** is established
- **1989:** Pat Robertson founds the **Christian Coalition** following his 1988 presidential primary campaign
- **1990s-2000s:** consolidation of policy / litigation infrastructure ([[Alliance Defending Freedom]], Family Research Council, Focus on the Family)
- **2007-present:** convergence with the **[[Council for National Policy]]** and the donor-advised-fund infrastructure that funds large-scale political organizing

The movement's intellectual base draws on **Christian Reconstructionism** (R.J. Rushdoony and successors), **Dominion Theology** (the theological framework that Christians should occupy and govern earthly institutions), the **Seven Mountains Mandate** (a related framework specifying seven cultural spheres for Christian dominion), and **Christian Nationalism** as a contemporary political identity.

## Significance for the vault

The Religious Right is a **base node** in the vault's analytical framework because:

- It is the **organizational substrate** for the **Christian-right cluster** identified in the [[Hypothesis - Three-wing coalition|three-wing coalition]] thesis
- It provides the **mass-organizing capacity** that the patron-class strategy (Heritage / Mercer / Koch / DAF infrastructure) depends on for political execution
- It is the **historical antecedent** to the [[Hypothesis - COS is the permanent capture arm of Project 2025|COS-Project-2025 coordinated patron-class strategy]] — without Religious Right organizing infrastructure built since 1979, the contemporary patron-class strategy could not deploy at the scale it now does

The movement is **not monolithic** — there are real theological, tactical, and generational fractures between (e.g.) traditional Catholic-conservative-traditionalists, Pentecostal / New Apostolic Reformation organizers, evangelical political pragmatists, and the broader Christian-nationalist intellectual movement. The vault treats it as a **named cluster** rather than as a single unified actor.

## Key contemporary organizational nodes

- **Mass-organizing infrastructure:** [[Moral Majority]] (historical, now succeeded), Faith and Freedom Coalition, Family Research Council
- **Litigation infrastructure:** [[Alliance Defending Freedom]] (the Dobbs-delivery legal vehicle), Becket Fund, First Liberty Institute
- **Policy infrastructure:** [[The Heritage Foundation]] (policy and Project 2025 publisher), [[Conservative Partnership Institute]] (personnel pipeline), Family Research Council Action
- **Donor / patron infrastructure:** [[Council for National Policy]] (donor-coordination), [[Mercer Family Foundation|Mercer network]] adjacencies, DonorsTrust / Donors Capital Fund (DAF infrastructure)
- **Constitutional-capture vehicle:** [[Convention of States Action]] / [[Citizens for Self-Governance]]
- **Theological apparatus:** New Apostolic Reformation (NAR), Capitol Ministries, broader Christian-nationalist intellectual infrastructure

## Contested framings

- **Religious Right vs. Christian Nationalism:** these are overlapping but **distinct** terms in the academic literature. **Religious Right** is a sociological / political-mobilization category; **Christian Nationalism** is more specifically an ideological identity (Christians believing the US should be a Christian nation, sometimes formally, sometimes culturally). Many Religious-Right participants are not formally Christian-Nationalist by this stricter definition; conversely, some Christian-Nationalist intellectuals are not embedded in Religious-Right organizing infrastructure.
- **Theocratic-project vs. religious-liberty framing:** internal Religious-Right framing often emphasizes religious-liberty defense; external academic / journalistic framing often emphasizes theocratic-project ambitions. The vault treats both framings as analytically present in the movement, with weight depending on which segment is being discussed.
