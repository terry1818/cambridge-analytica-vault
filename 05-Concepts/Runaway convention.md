# Runaway convention

Concern that an Article V Convention of States, once called, could not be effectively limited to its stated subject matter and might propose constitutional amendments far beyond the original applications.

## Historical precedent

- 1787: The Constitutional Convention was called explicitly to revise the Articles of Confederation but produced an entirely new Constitution. This is the founding-era precedent for runaway convention concerns.

## Opposition framing

- Articulated by legal scholars across the political spectrum including [[Lawrence Lessig]] (left), Lawrence Tribe (left), and Phyllis Schlafly (right, before her death) and [[John Birch Society]] (right)
- The concern: once a convention is in session, there is no clear legal mechanism to prevent it from proposing amendments outside its stated scope

## [[Convention of States Action]] response

- Maintains the convention can be legally limited to its application subjects
- Cites scholarship from Robert Natelson and others supporting subject-limited conventions
- The dispute is unresolved at the Supreme Court level (no case has reached the Court)
