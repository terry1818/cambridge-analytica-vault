## Definition

The Whitehead-Perry typology classifies the American adult population into four categories — **Ambassadors**, **Accommodators**, **Resisters**, and **Rejecters** — based on scores on the [[Christian Nationalism Scale]]. It is not a demographic classification but an ideological one: people in each category may belong to any race, denomination, or socioeconomic group, though each category has statistically distinct demographic profiles.

The typology is the primary analytical output of *Taking America Back for God* (2020) and has been widely adopted in subsequent research on Christian nationalism.

## Origin / history

Whitehead and Perry derived the four categories from the score range of the [[Christian Nationalism Scale]] (0–24 points). The category boundaries are:

| Category | Score Range | Approximate Population Share (2020 data) |
|----------|-------------|------------------------------------------|
| Ambassadors | 18–24 | ~19.8% |
| Accommodators | 12–17 | ~32.1% |
| Resisters | 6–11 | ~26.6% |
| Rejecters | 0–5 | ~21.5% |

These proportions are derived from national survey data available at the time of publication; subsequent survey waves may show shifts, and the typology should be applied to specific data years when making population estimates.

## How it works

**Ambassadors** are outspoken advocates of Christian nationalist ideology. They hold that the United States was founded as a Christian nation and should be governed by Christian principles; they support state enforcement of Christian values, display of religious symbols in public spaces, school prayer, and Christian exceptionalism in foreign policy. Ambassadors are the core activist base of the theocratic wing. Research finds they are disproportionately white, Southern, male, lower-education, and Republican, but these are statistical tendencies, not requirements.

**Accommodators** occupy the sympathizer tier. They do not actively champion Christian nationalism but do not reject it either. They absorb Christian nationalist rhetoric without acting as organizers. Their support is contingent and movable; they are important to any analysis of political coalition-building because they constitute the largest single category and their tilt determines electoral outcomes.

**Resisters** are uncomfortable with Christian nationalism but do not reject it in every particular. They may support school prayer while opposing a declared Christian state, for example. They represent a zone of partial resistance that can be engaged by targeted counter-messaging.

**Rejecters** strongly disagree with all six CN Scale items. They tend to be more secular, urban, educated, and politically progressive. They form the natural constituency for pluralist counter-movements.

## Application in this domain

For this vault's purposes — mapping the theocratic wing as infrastructure — the typology is most useful in three ways:

1. **Scale estimation:** The Ambassador category (~1-in-5 American adults) is large enough to constitute a viable mass political constituency. This contextualizes the political viability of explicitly Christian nationalist platforms without overstating or understating the base.
2. **Coalition dynamics:** The Accommodator majority (~1-in-3 adults) explains how Christian nationalist politicians can win general elections: Ambassadors provide the activist core, Accommodators provide the electoral margin.
3. **Persuasion targeting:** Resisters and Accommodators are analytically distinct from Rejecters. Influence operations — both pro-CN and counter-CN — would rationally concentrate on these swing categories.

The typology maps onto [[Paul Weyrich]]'s model of movement infrastructure: Ambassadors are the activated base; Accommodators are the demographic reservoir that Weyrich's mobilization machine was designed to reach.

## Examples

- Studies following January 6, 2021 found Ambassador-level CN scores strongly predicted approval of the Capitol assault, while Accommodator scores showed more ambivalence.
- Analyses of the 2016 and 2020 elections found Ambassador-tier Christians had unusually high turnout rates and strong support for Trump regardless of their own church attendance.
- The typology has been used to analyze vaccine hesitancy, anti-LGBTQ legislation support, and [[Project 2025]] constituent polling.

## Effectiveness / critique

The typology is a useful analytical shorthand but carries the same caveats as the CN Scale itself. Score cut-points (18/24 for Ambassador tier, etc.) are somewhat arbitrary — the underlying distribution is continuous, not categorical. Researchers have cautioned against treating category membership as a fixed identity rather than a snapshot of survey responses at a point in time.

Additionally, the typology was built on predominantly white-respondent survey data. Research by Frontiers in Social Psychology (2024) finds that non-white Christians can score as Ambassadors by survey items while having meaningfully different political orientations — suggesting the typology may underperform as a predictor outside white evangelical communities.
