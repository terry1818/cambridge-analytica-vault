# The constitution of knowledge

## Definition

"The constitution of knowledge" is Jonathan Rauch's term for the distributed, decentralised set of rules, norms, and institutions that Western liberal societies have developed to distinguish reliable knowledge from conjecture, propaganda, and falsehood. Just as a political constitution structures the competition for power within legitimate limits, the "constitution of knowledge" structures the competition for truth — subjecting claims to empirical testing, peer evaluation, professional scrutiny, and iterative correction. Rauch argues this system is the epistemic infrastructure of liberal democracy and is now under systematic attack.

## Origin / history

Rauch developed the concept in *The Constitution of Knowledge: A Defense of Truth* (Brookings Institution Press, 2021). The work builds on his earlier *Kindly Inquisitors* (1993), which defended liberal science against both political correctness and religious orthodoxy. The 2021 book was written in direct response to the disinformation environment of the Trump era and the rise of coordinated [[Epistemic nihilism]] — what Rauch calls "reality manipulation" as a deliberate political strategy. Rauch draws on Karl Popper's fallibilism and Friedrich Hayek's epistemology of dispersed knowledge, but applies them specifically to the institutional infrastructure that validates truth claims in practice.

## How it works

The constitution of knowledge operates through several mutually reinforcing components:

1. **Empirical submission**: Claims must be submitted to public scrutiny and must be falsifiable in principle.
2. **Peer evaluation**: Knowledge claims are evaluated by expert communities using professional standards — not by political authority or popular vote.
3. **Fallibilism**: No claim is permanently settled; all accepted knowledge remains open to revision on the basis of new evidence.
4. **Error correction**: The system has explicit mechanisms for identifying and correcting errors — retraction, appeal, debate.
5. **Institutional embodiment**: These norms are instantiated in specific institutions: academic journals, courts, newsrooms, regulatory agencies, professional licensing bodies.

Rauch argues these institutions are imperfect but self-correcting. The crisis he identifies is not that the institutions fail occasionally, but that they are being deliberately undermined — targeted not for their errors but for their function of producing shared, verifiable knowledge.

## Application in this domain

The vault can use this concept as an overarching framework for analysing the *epistemic* dimension of the CA/info-warfare research domain — the goal of which is not merely to persuade but to destroy the shared reality that makes persuasion legible. Key applications: (1) connecting [[Bespoke realities]], [[Firehose of falsehood]], and psychographic micro-targeting as coordinated attacks on the constitution of knowledge; (2) analysing the targeting of specific knowledge-validating institutions (universities, mainstream media, the FBI, the CDC) as a coherent strategy; (3) assessing platform-level changes (algorithm changes at [[X (Twitter)]], Meta's content moderation rollbacks) as institutional enablement of epistemic attack. The concept pairs directly with [[Epistemic nihilism]], which names the attack strategy.

## Examples

- **COVID-19 misinformation ecosystem**: Systematic delegitimisation of the CDC, FDA, and peer-reviewed vaccine research — not primarily through counter-evidence but through attacks on institutional credibility.
- **"Alternative facts" (2017)**: Kellyanne Conway's phrase exemplifies the explicit rejection of the constitution of knowledge's basic premise.
- **Academic freedom attacks**: Legislative restrictions on what can be taught in universities (Florida, Texas 2021–25) as direct interference with the peer-evaluation node of the system.
- **Cambridge Analytica operations**: Psychographic targeting designed not only to persuade but to fragment shared factual baselines across audience segments.
- **Media ecosystem fragmentation**: Right-wing parallel media construction (OAN, NewsMax, Breitbart, Bannon's War Room) as substitute knowledge-validating institutions that do not operate by Rauch's epistemic norms.

## Effectiveness / critique

The concept is analytically useful and has been widely cited. Limitations:

1. **Ideological embedding**: Critics (from both left and right) note that the institutions Rauch defends — mainstream journalism, Ivy League academia, federal regulatory agencies — are themselves not ideologically neutral. The "constitution of knowledge" may systematically advantage certain perspectives while framing that advantage as epistemic objectivity.
2. **False equivalence risk**: Rauch's framework can imply a symmetry between left and right attacks on knowledge-validating institutions that the evidence does not fully support — the scale and coordination of right-wing epistemic attack is significantly larger in recent decades.
3. **Institutional ossification**: The system's conservatism (resistance to revision) can protect established errors as well as established truths; movements that challenge orthodox consensus are not automatically nihilistic.
4. **Descriptive vs. normative**: Rauch's "constitution" is partly descriptive (what these institutions do) and partly normative (what they should do); the gap between the two is substantial.
