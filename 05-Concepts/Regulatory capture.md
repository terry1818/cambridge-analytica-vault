# Regulatory capture

## Definition

Regulatory capture is the phenomenon in which a regulatory agency, created to act in the public interest, instead comes to be dominated by — and to act for — the industry it is supposed to regulate. The captured agency advances the regulated firms' commercial interests (limiting competition, raising prices, blocking entrants) rather than protecting consumers or the public.

## Origin / history

The concept was formalized in 1971 by economist **George Stigler**, a Nobel laureate of the University of Chicago, in his paper "The Theory of Economic Regulation." Stigler argued, against the prevailing "public interest" theory of regulation, that "as a rule, regulation is acquired by the industry and is designed and operated primarily for its benefit." His key insight was that regulated industries have far more concentrated incentive — and so invest far more effort — in influencing regulators than the diffuse, weakly motivated general public. Although the underlying pattern had been observed earlier (in critiques of railroad and utility commissions), Stigler gave it rigorous economic form, and it became a pillar of the broader public-choice account of [[Government failure]].

## How it works

Capture follows from asymmetric incentives and asymmetric information:

1. **Concentrated vs. diffuse interests** — a regulated industry of a few firms gains a large per-firm benefit from favorable regulation; consumers each lose a small amount, so they do not organize. The industry therefore lobbies intensively while the public does not.
2. **Information dependence** — regulators rely on the industry for technical data and expertise, importing the industry's framing.
3. **The revolving door** — regulators are recruited from, and return to, the industries they oversee, aligning their long-term interests with those firms.
4. **"Deregulatory capture"** — a later refinement notes that capture can also produce the *absence* of needed regulation, not just industry-friendly rules.

The result is regulation that, in Stigler's framing, raises prices and protects incumbents at consumers' expense.

## Application in this domain

Regulatory capture is a sibling concept to [[Rent-seeking]] and a building block of the libertarian / public-choice case central to this vault's patron wing. Stigler's argument supplied an academically respectable case that government intervention is *inherently* corruptible — which feeds two political conclusions. The first is deregulation: if agencies are captured, abolish or shrink them. The second, more important for the vault, is [[constitutional capture]]: if ordinary regulatory and legislative processes are unavoidably captured, then policy should be locked into constitutional rules beyond their reach. Stigler's Chicago analysis and [[James M. Buchanan]] / [[Gordon Tullock]]'s Virginia public choice converge on this point: democratic government cannot be trusted, so it must be structurally constrained. Notably, the concept cuts both ways — anti-capture reasoning is also used by progressives to critique industry influence over agencies like the SEC or FAA.

## Examples

- Historic critiques of the Interstate Commerce Commission as captured by the railroads.
- [[American Legislative Exchange Council]] — industry-drafted model legislation is a form of legislative capture.
- Vault-relevant: data-broker and surveillance firms operating with minimal effective oversight illustrate "deregulatory capture."

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed validity:** The Stigler model is widely accepted in economics and political science as describing a real and recurrent dynamic.
- **Empirical evidence:** Strong case studies across transport, finance, telecoms, and energy regulation.
- **Critique:** Critics argue the concept is sometimes overextended into a blanket claim that *all* regulation is captured, supporting a deregulatory agenda that ignores genuine public-interest regulation; "deregulatory capture" scholarship complicates the original model.
