# Government failure

## Definition

Government failure refers to situations in which government intervention produces outcomes worse than would have occurred without it, or fails to achieve its stated objectives efficiently. It is the public-choice counterpart to "market failure": where welfare economics had catalogued the ways markets fall short of the ideal, public choice catalogues the parallel ways that government does. The concept does not claim governments always fail — it claims that failure is systematic and predictable, arising from the structure of political incentives rather than from bad luck or bad people.

## Origin / history

The concept emerged from public choice theory in the 1960s and 1970s — the work of [[James M. Buchanan]], [[Gordon Tullock]], and others. Earlier welfare economics had treated market failure as a justification for government correction, implicitly assuming that government acts as a benevolent, well-informed agent of the public good. Public choice attacked that assumption directly. Buchanan called this stripping-away of idealized assumptions "politics without romance." If voters, politicians, and bureaucrats are modeled — as in markets — as self-interested rational actors, then government intervention has no guarantee of improving on the market outcome. The term "government failure" was popularized in subsequent public-choice and free-market literature (e.g., the Cato Institute's primer *Government Failure*).

## How it works

Public choice identifies several recurrent mechanisms:

1. **Bureaucratic self-interest** — agencies and officials pursue larger budgets, job security, and discretion, not efficiency.
2. **[[Rent-seeking]]** — concentrated interest groups capture programs and regulations for private benefit, distorting policy.
3. **Imperfect information and weak monitoring** — voters cannot effectively monitor politicians ("rational ignorance"), allowing officials to serve themselves at constituents' expense.
4. **Electoral short-termism** — politicians discount long-term costs because they face short re-election horizons.
5. **[[Regulatory capture]]** — agencies come to serve the industries they regulate.

Together these imply that political decision-making is subject to its own systematic distortions, just as markets are.

## Application in this domain

Government failure is the keystone premise of the vault's patron wing. The logical chain runs: government failure is systematic → therefore expanding government cannot be assumed to improve on markets → therefore the state must be structurally constrained. That conclusion is what [[constitutional economics]] supplies (rules that bind majorities) and what [[constitutional capture]] operationalizes (entrenching those limits beyond democratic revision). Nancy MacLean's *Democracy in Chains* argues that the government-failure framework, developed by Buchanan, was deployed by the [[Koch Network]] not as neutral analysis but as the intellectual justification for a project to make democratic government structurally incapable of redistribution and regulation. The concept also rationalizes [[Education-capture]]: public schools, on this account, are a textbook government failure best remedied by privatization.

## Examples

- [[constitutional capture]] — the strategy of locking limits into constitutional structure, justified by the government-failure premise.
- [[Convention of States]] — campaign explicitly framed around correcting federal "overreach" and fiscal failure.
- [[Education-capture]] — public schooling reframed as captured government failure.
- [[Mercatus Center]] — Koch-funded GMU institution applying the government-failure framework to policy analysis.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed validity:** The concept is genuinely useful — political and bureaucratic incentives do distort outcomes, and public choice gave this rigorous form.
- **Empirical evidence:** Real cases of bureaucratic inefficiency, capture, and short-termism are well documented.
- **Critique:** Critics argue the framework is applied asymmetrically — government failure is scrutinized while market failure and corporate rent-seeking are minimized — and that it is used to license a sweeping anti-government conclusion the evidence does not support. Many policy successes (public health, basic research, infrastructure) cut against a blanket "government fails" reading. Treating systematic government failure as a reason to *remove decisions from democratic control entirely* is a contested political move, not an empirical finding.
