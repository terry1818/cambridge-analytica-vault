## Definition

"The naked public square" is the central metaphor and thesis of [[Richard John Neuhaus]]'s 1984 book of the same name. The phrase describes the condition of American political discourse after strict separationist interpretations of the First Amendment Establishment Clause had — in Neuhaus's diagnosis — expelled religious language, moral reasoning, and religiously grounded values from legitimate public debate. A "naked" public square, stripped bare of religious reference, is unstable: because human beings are "meaning-seeking animals," the vacuum will be filled by secular substitute religions — ideologies that operate with the absolutism of religion but lack its moral restraints.

## Origin / history

Neuhaus published *The Naked Public Square: Religion and Democracy in America* in 1984, while he was still a Lutheran pastor (he converted to Roman Catholicism in 1990, was ordained a priest in 1991, and went on to found *First Things* in 1990). The book emerged from his engagement with the broader neoconservative intellectual ferment of the late 1970s and early 1980s, particularly the neo-Tocquevillian argument — shared by figures like Peter Berger — that liberal democracy requires religious and moral foundations it cannot itself generate.

The book was addressed to two audiences simultaneously: to secular liberals who dismissed religious reasoning as inherently incompatible with democratic pluralism, and to religious conservatives who retreated into separatist or apolitical postures. Neuhaus argued against both: against secularists who treated religious exclusion as constitutional neutrality, and against Christian fundamentalists who simply withdrew from the mainstream.

The concept proved enormously influential. It provided an intellectual framework for the broader social-conservative effort to reintroduce explicitly religious moral arguments into political debates over abortion, euthanasia, family structure, and education. It was also foundational to the subsequent theocon network documented in Linker's *The Theocons*: [[George Weigel]], [[Michael Novak]], and [[Robert P. George]] all built their policy arguments on the premise that religiously grounded natural-law reasoning has a legitimate place in the public square.

## How it works

The argument proceeds in three steps:

1. **Diagnosis**: The First Amendment, as interpreted by strict separationists and the Supreme Court since the 1940s, has been used not merely to prohibit government establishment of religion but to disqualify religious reasoning from political life altogether. Citizens who ground their policy positions in religious conviction are told their arguments are inadmissible in pluralistic public debate.

2. **Consequence**: This produces a genuinely "naked" square — morally bare, without shared reference points — that cannot sustain democratic deliberation. The majority of Americans, who are "incorrigibly religious," are told their deepest convictions are politically inadmissible. This is both democratically unjust and practically unstable.

3. **Alternative filling**: The vacuum does not remain empty. Secular ideologies — Neuhaus pointed to various forms of progressive liberalism — fill the space with their own implicit moral absolutisms, but without religious accountability or the check of a transcendent standard. The naked square becomes, paradoxically, more ideologically coercive than one in which religious arguments are openly engaged.

## Application in this domain

Within the theocon network and the broader religious-right project, "the naked public square" functions as a recurring legitimation argument: social conservatives who want to legislate against abortion, same-sex marriage, or physician-assisted suicide invoke the concept to argue that their religious motivations are not disqualifying but rather an example of citizens bringing their full moral selves into democratic deliberation.

The concept is also central to the legal strategy of [[Alliance Defending Freedom]] and related religious-liberty litigants, who argue that accommodating religious objectors is not establishment but rather the prevention of a new form of secular establishment. [[Robert P. George]]'s natural-law framework attempts to translate the same intuitions into secular philosophical language accessible to non-believers — a project that implicitly accepts Neuhaus's diagnosis while offering a different remedy.

The concept has been cited in Supreme Court opinions and briefs. Justice Clarence Thomas and (in concurrence) Justice Samuel Alito have articulated positions consonant with Neuhaus's diagnosis, particularly in Establishment Clause cases.

## Examples

- **Abortion debate**: Religious pro-life advocates invoking natural law or scriptural teaching were told by secularists their arguments were inappropriate for public policy. Neuhaus argued this was an asymmetric exclusion — secular moral arguments faced no equivalent disqualification.
- **School prayer jurisprudence**: The series of Supreme Court decisions from *Engel v. Vitale* (1962) onward that removed state-sponsored prayer from public schools were, for Neuhaus, exhibits of the naked public square in action.
- **1996 First Things symposium**: The [[First Things End of Democracy symposium]] extended Neuhaus's analysis to argue that courts had so thoroughly monopolized moral questions that the democratic legitimacy of the regime itself was in question.

## Effectiveness / critique

**In favor**: The concept identified a genuine tension in liberal democratic theory — between the Rawlsian requirement that public reasons be accessible to all citizens and the sociological reality that most Americans ground their moral views in religious tradition. This tension remains unresolved.

**Against**:
- Critics (including secular liberals and some religious progressives) argue Neuhaus's diagnosis was selective: religious arguments were not excluded from public life; they were contested like any other arguments. The Civil Rights Movement operated openly from religious premises.
- The concept has been used to advance a specifically Catholic natural-law agenda under the banner of universal democratic inclusion — critics argue this is its own form of intellectual sleight of hand.
- Post-liberal thinkers like [[Patrick Deneen]] and [[Adrian Vermeule]] have moved past Neuhaus's framework, arguing that liberal democracy cannot be reformed from within; the naked public square was always the destination of liberalism, not a deviation from it.

## Related concepts

- [[Catholic Integralism]] — the more radical position that the state should be ordered to the supernatural end of the Church; goes further than Neuhaus's reform of liberalism.
- [[Christian Reconstructionism]] — holds that biblical law should directly govern society; rejected by Neuhaus as theocratic.
- [[Common Good Constitutionalism]] — [[Adrian Vermeule]]'s legal theory that originalism should be replaced by a jurisprudence oriented toward the common good, as understood by natural law.
- [[Seven Mountains Mandate]] — the NAR doctrine that Christians must take dominion over the seven spheres of society; a charismatic Protestant parallel to theocon arguments for religious engagement in public life.
