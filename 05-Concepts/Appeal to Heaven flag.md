# Appeal to Heaven flag

## Definition

The Appeal to Heaven flag (also called the Pine Tree Flag or Washington's Cruisers flag) is a white banner bearing a green pine tree above the motto "An Appeal to Heaven." In its contemporary usage it functions as a symbol of Christian nationalist political theology — specifically the NAR conviction that American political authority should be subordinated to divine (apostolic) governance, and that God sanctions the use of force if constitutional means fail. The phrase "Appeal to Heaven" derives from John Locke's *Second Treatise on Government*, where it refers to a people's right to armed resistance when civil authority fails them — a framing the NAR has fused with Christian dominion theology.

## Origin / History

**Historical origin (1775):** The flag was first flown by six schooners commissioned as "Washington's Cruisers" under George Washington's authority in October 1775 for naval supply operations during the Revolutionary War. Massachusetts officially codified a version of it — "a white Flag, with a green Pine Tree, and the Inscription, 'An Appeal to Heaven'" — on April 29, 1776. The pine tree had already become a symbol of colonial resistance through the Pine Tree Riot and other clashes with British authority. The phrase "Appeal to Heaven" signals that having exhausted earthly legal remedies, a people may appeal directly to God — which in Lockean terms licenses resistance.

**Dormancy and obscurity (1776–2013):** After the Revolutionary War, the flag largely fell out of mainstream use. It persisted in New England maritime symbolism and Massachusetts state iconography, but held no significant Christian nationalist associations before the 21st century.

**NAR reappropriation (2013):** The flag's transformation into a Christian nationalist symbol traces to a single event. In 2013, [[Dutch Sheets]] received an Appeal to Heaven flag as a gift at a Christ for the Nations Institute graduation ceremony. He publicly declared that he "received a prophecy" that the flag was to be "the symbol of a campaign to restore America to the Christian nation God intended." This act — a NAR apostle declaring the flag a prophetic assignment — injected it into NAR networks as a sacred artifact of the dominion movement. In 2015, Sheets formalized this with his book *An Appeal to Heaven* and made the flag the permanent backdrop of his GiveHim15 broadcasts.

## How It Works

Within NAR theology, objects and symbols function as "prophetic declarations" — physical anchors for spiritual warfare claims. By attaching a prophetic word to the flag in 2013, Sheets made it a vehicle for several interlocking claims:

1. **Christian nationhood:** America was founded as a Christian nation and must be restored as such — a claim against the historical record of constitutional church-state separation.
2. **Divine mandate for political action:** Flying the flag signals alignment with the NAR's apostolic mandate to "take back" the Seven Mountains of cultural influence (see [[Seven Mountains Mandate]]).
3. **Lockean resistance frame:** The "Appeal to Heaven" language, drawn from Locke, implicitly licenses escalation beyond legal norms when apostles declare earthly institutions corrupt. This is the theological mechanism connecting the flag to the January 6 action.
4. **Spiritual warfare:** In contexts of battle and intercession, the flag marks territory as claimed by Christian nationalist forces.

## Application in This Domain

**2020 election period:** The flag appeared as a constant backdrop on [[Dutch Sheets]]'s GiveHim15 YouTube channel throughout the post-election period, visually linking his election-fraud claims and "Operation Valkyrie" prayer mobilization to the NAR's divine-mandate symbolism.

**January 6, 2021:** Religion scholar Matthew D. Taylor documented that the Appeal to Heaven flag was present among the crowds on Capitol grounds on January 6, with at least two documented instances of the flag being carried by rioters who physically breached the Capitol building. Taylor estimates "possibly hundreds" of Appeal to Heaven flags were visible on the grounds. This makes it one of the most visible Christian nationalist symbols at the attack — distinct from (and ideologically complementary to) Confederate and militia flags also present.

**Post-January 6 normalization:** The flag has been hung outside the congressional office of Rep. Glenn Grothman (R-WI) and displayed prominently by Speaker Mike Johnson outside his House office — the latter reported by Rolling Stone in 2023. Justice Samuel Alito flew the flag at his vacation home (reported by New York Times, May 2024), generating a Supreme Court ethics controversy. The flag also flew over the Arizona Capitol during a separate Christian nationalist demonstration.

**Education Department:** Common Dreams (2025) reported the flag hung at the US Department of Education building during the early Trump second term.

## Effectiveness / Critique

The flag's effectiveness as a symbol lies precisely in its dual legibility: to secular audiences it can be presented as a benign Revolutionary War historical artifact; to NAR-aligned audiences it communicates the full doctrine of Christian nationalist dominion and the license to resist "demonic" secular authority. This ambiguity provides political cover — as Alito's defenders demonstrated by insisting it was merely a historical flag — while simultaneously transmitting a specific and radical theological message.

Critics including Baylor University communications professor Leslie Hahner have documented that the "Appeal to Heaven" movement's tenets carry "overtones of both Christian Nationalism and Christian Dominionism." Taylor's analysis goes further: the flag's Lockean resistance framing is precisely what made it appropriate as a visual symbol for the January 6 event, linking the crowd's action to a theology of justified resistance to corrupt earthly authority.
