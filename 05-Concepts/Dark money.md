# Dark money

## Definition

"Dark money" refers to political spending intended to influence elections or public policy where the original source of the funds is hidden from the public. The money is typically routed through organizations that are not legally required to disclose their donors — chiefly tax-exempt "social welfare" nonprofits organized under section 501(c)(4) of the U.S. tax code, but also 501(c)(6) trade associations and certain LLCs and shell entities. The donor knows where the money goes; the public does not know where it came from.

## Origin / history

The term was popularized around 2010 by journalists and watchdog groups (the Center for Responsive Politics, now OpenSecrets, and reporter Jane Mayer, among others). The practice itself is older, but two developments supercharged it. First, the U.S. Supreme Court's 2010 decision in *Citizens United v. FEC* held that independent political expenditures by corporations and unions could not be limited, and the related *SpeedTrap*/*SpeechNow.org v. FEC* D.C. Circuit ruling enabled unlimited contributions to independent-expenditure-only committees ("Super PACs"). Second, the IRS treatment of 501(c)(4) nonprofits — which may engage in political activity so long as it is not their "primary" purpose and which need not disclose donors — created a vehicle for spending that is both unlimited and anonymous. Jane Mayer's 2016 book "Dark Money" gave the concept its definitive popular treatment, documenting the [[Koch Network]]'s decades-long construction of exactly this infrastructure.

## How it works

The mechanism is laundering attribution, not money. A donor gives to a 501(c)(4) nonprofit. That nonprofit may spend directly on "issue ads" and political activity, or it may pass the funds to other nonprofits or to Super PACs. Because 501(c)(4)s do not disclose donors, the trail stops there: a Super PAC's FEC filing may show a contribution from "Americans for Prosperity" or a similarly named group rather than the actual individual or corporation behind it. Networks of interlocking nonprofits — donor-advised funds, "pass-through" entities such as the Donors Trust and Donors Capital Fund, and grant-making foundations — can move money through several layers, making the original source effectively untraceable. The result is unlimited, anonymous political spending: a structural escape from the disclosure regime that the post-Watergate campaign-finance laws were built to guarantee.

## Application in this domain

Dark money is the financial circulatory system of the patron-funded projects this vault tracks:

- The [[Koch Network]] built an integrated dark-money apparatus — Americans for Prosperity, Freedom Partners, and allied 501(c)(4)s and foundations — to fund free-market advocacy, judicial confirmation campaigns, and state-level policy.
- The judicial-selection and confirmation campaigns associated with [[Leonard Leo]] and the [[Federalist Society]] orbit were financed substantially through dark-money vehicles (the Judicial Crisis Network / Concord Fund and a network of linked nonprofits), funding the advertising campaigns behind Supreme Court nominations central to the [[constitutional capture]] story.
- The [[Tea Party]] movement's national infrastructure, while drawing real grassroots energy, was substantially scaffolded by dark-money-funded organizations.
- Religious-right and Christian-nationalist advocacy, anti-voting-rights litigation, and election-administration campaigns have all been funded in part through undisclosed channels.

The vault's analytical interest is precisely the opacity: dark money makes it structurally difficult to prove who is paying for a given campaign — which is the point of the design.

## Examples

- [[Citizens United v. FEC]] (2010) — the decision that, with *SpeechNow*, opened the legal space dark money fills.
- [[Koch Network]] — the most documented builder of a dark-money infrastructure.
- [[Tea Party]] — a movement whose national scaffolding relied on dark-money-funded groups.
- Judicial Crisis Network / Concord Fund — dark-money vehicle for Supreme Court confirmation campaigns.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed effectiveness:** Anonymity shields donors from accountability and lets concentrated wealth shape policy and judicial selection without public scrutiny; the spending totals run into the billions across cycles.
- **Empirical evidence:** OpenSecrets and watchdog tracking document billions in non-disclosed spending since 2010; the exact totals are unknowable by design, which is itself the strongest evidence of the mechanism's effect.
- **Academic / democratic critique:** Critics across the political spectrum argue dark money corrodes the informational basis of democratic accountability — voters cannot evaluate a message if they cannot see who paid for it. Defenders invoke donor privacy and First Amendment association rights (an argument the Court strengthened in *Americans for Prosperity Foundation v. Bonta*, 2021). Both the harm and the constitutional defense are genuine and contested.
