## Definition

The No Climate Tax Pledge is a written commitment circulated by [[Americans for Prosperity]] (AFP), the Koch-funded 501(c)(4), in which signatories pledge to "oppose any legislation relating to climate change that includes a net increase in government revenue." It was launched approximately 2008, one year after the US Supreme Court ruled in *Massachusetts v. EPA* that the Environmental Protection Agency has authority to regulate greenhouse gases — which AFP and Koch Industries recognized as a legal predicate for cap-and-trade legislation.

## Origin / History

- **2008:** AFP, founded by David Koch and Richard Fink, devises the pledge in anticipation of cap-and-trade climate legislation from the incoming Obama administration.
- **2009:** The pledge is deployed to pressure senators against the [[Waxman-Markey climate bill 2009]] after it passes the House; AFP describes cap-and-trade as "the largest excise tax in history," a characterization disputed by economists.
- **2010:** Inside Climate News documents the pledge as "another political hurdle to US climate action"; signatories at this point include significant portions of the Republican congressional caucus.
- **2011–2016:** Pledge becomes a standard primary litmus test for Republican candidates; a signatory list that includes the full House Republican leadership, approximately 140 House members, 26 senators, and 8 governors, plus hundreds of state legislators.
- **Ongoing:** The pledge continues to exist as an AFP instrument; its practical effect diminished somewhat after the Koch network partly rebranded under "Stand Together" and adjusted some advocacy priorities after 2020.

## How It Works

1. **AFP outreach:** AFP field staff contact candidates and incumbent legislators, particularly before primary elections.
2. **Signing:** The candidate signs the pledge publicly; AFP publicizes the list of signatories.
3. **Primary enforcement:** AFP and affiliated groups signal willingness to fund primary challenges against Republicans who vote for climate legislation with revenue components.
4. **Legislative effect:** A sufficient number of Senate signatories can prevent any cap-and-trade or carbon-pricing bill from reaching 60 votes (the cloture threshold).

The pledge is not a legal instrument but functions as a credible commitment device backed by AFP's demonstrated capacity to fund primary challenges and run independent expenditure campaigns against defectors.

## Application in This Domain

The No Climate Tax Pledge is the most documented example of the Koch network using a pledge mechanism to create a binding blocking coalition in Congress. It is analytically comparable to:
- Grover Norquist's Americans for Tax Reform "Taxpayer Protection Pledge" (the model it most closely resembles)
- The [[Convention of States Action]] signature campaigns (a later parallel in the COS context)

In the context of this vault, the pledge represents the patron/dark-money wing's most successful single legislative operation: blocking the only cap-and-trade bill to reach a congressional floor vote.

## Examples

- **Senate blocking coalition (2009–2010):** AFP-coordinated pledge signing by enough senators to deny cloture to Waxman-Markey or any successor Senate climate bill.
- **2012 Republican primary:** AFP uses pledge non-compliance as a signal for primary support decisions; virtually all viable Republican primary candidates are signatories.
- **Ted Cruz, Marco Rubio, Rand Paul, Scott Walker** — among documented presidential-primary candidates who signed the pledge.

## Effectiveness / Critique

**Effectiveness:** Highly effective as a blocking instrument. Federal cap-and-trade legislation has not been brought to a Senate floor vote since 2010. The pledge is the most parsimonious explanation for the failure to advance climate legislation despite majority public support.

**Critique:**
- The "no net increase in government revenue" framing is designed to be broader than cap-and-trade — it would apply to any carbon tax or fee mechanism regardless of how revenue is recycled.
- The pledge's description of cap-and-trade as a "tax" is disputed; the mechanism involves tradeable permits rather than direct taxation, though economists debate whether the distinction is substantive.
- Critics argue the pledge is funded by entities with direct financial interest in blocking carbon pricing (Koch Industries is a major carbon emitter), creating an undisclosed conflict of interest.
- Evidence of coordinated pledge strategy through [[Koch donor summits]] is documented in Christopher Leonard's *Kochland*. Evidence tier: **Tier-1** for pledge existence and signatories; **Tier-2** for full strategy documentation.
