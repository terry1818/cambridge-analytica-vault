## Definition

Supreme Court term limits refers to the proposed reform of replacing the current constitutional arrangement — under which federal judges "hold their offices during good behavior," effectively lifetime tenure — with fixed, non-renewable terms for Supreme Court justices. The most commonly proposed term is 18 years, staggered so that one vacancy arises every two years (one per presidential term). After serving 18 years, justices would rotate to lower federal courts or take senior status. The proposal would require a constitutional amendment if applied to current sitting justices; some legal scholars argue Congress could implement it by statute for future nominees only.

## Origin / history

Life tenure for federal judges was established in Article III of the Constitution (1789) as a mechanism to insulate the judiciary from political pressure. The Framers' assumption was a smaller, less powerful judiciary with shorter expected careers; few anticipated modern longevity or the transformative political significance of individual Court appointments.

Serious academic and political advocacy for term limits began gaining traction in the early 2000s, driven by escalating confirmation battles (Bork 1987, Thomas 1991, Garland 2016, Kavanaugh 2018, Barrett 2020) and the increasing ideological stakes of individual appointments. Chemerinsky, in *No Democracy Lasts Forever* (2024), frames term limits as one of the essential structural reforms needed to make the Court compatible with democratic governance.

The reform gained bipartisan academic support: legal scholars across the ideological spectrum (Steven Calabresi, James Lindgren — conservatives; Chemerinsky, Laurence Tribe — liberals) have endorsed 18-year terms, though their motivations differ. During the 2016 Republican primary, candidates including Ben Carson, Mike Huckabee, and Rick Perry all called for 18-year term limits.

President Biden's Supreme Court reform commission (2021) addressed term limits but the commission did not make a formal recommendation. In 2024, Biden endorsed an 18-year term limit and a binding ethics code as part of a proposed reform package; the proposal received no congressional action before the 2024 election.

## How it works

Under the most prominent model:

1. Each justice serves one non-renewable 18-year active term.
2. Vacancies arise on a predictable schedule — one every two years, aligned with presidential terms.
3. Every president would appoint two justices per term, eliminating the random vacancy lottery that currently allows some presidents (Carter: 0 appointments; Trump first term: 3) wildly unequal influence.
4. After 18 years of active service, justices rotate to the federal Courts of Appeals or take senior status; they can be recalled in case of recusal or death of a sitting justice.
5. Implementation for future nominees could arguably proceed by statute; applying terms to current justices would require a constitutional amendment, which Chemerinsky explicitly acknowledges.

## Application in this domain

Chemerinsky locates the absence of term limits within a broader argument that the Constitution's structural features — including the Electoral College, the Senate's equal-state representation, and now lifetime SCOTUS tenure — produce a system of compounding minority rule. The average tenure of a Supreme Court justice has increased dramatically: early justices served roughly 8 years; current average tenure exceeds 25 years. Justices now regularly serve into their 80s, creating an incentive for presidents to appoint very young ideologues with maximal staying power.

Life tenure also creates a death-lottery quality to Court composition: the accidental vacancy (Ruth Bader Ginsburg's death 46 days before the 2020 election; Scalia's death in an election year) can restructure constitutional law for a generation based on timing rather than democratic mandate.

## Examples

- **Canada**: Supreme Court justices serve until mandatory retirement at age 75.
- **Germany**: Federal Constitutional Court judges serve single 12-year non-renewable terms.
- **US Courts of Appeals**: No term limits, but far less political salience.
- **Garland precedent (2016) / Barrett precedent (2020)**: The reversal on whether a president can appoint a justice in an election year — Garland blocked for ~10 months; Barrett confirmed in ~30 days — illustrates how lifetime-tenure vacancy politics has corroded institutional norms.

## Effectiveness / critique

**In favor**:
- Regularizes appointments, reducing arbitrary variance and the incentive for strategic retirement or death-gaming.
- Reduces the political temperature of individual confirmations (each justice now certain to eventually be replaced, rather than being a potentially permanent ideological lock).
- Consistent with international democratic norms.
- Widely supported across ideological lines by legal scholars.

**Against**:
- Constitutional amendment is extremely difficult; requires two-thirds of both houses and three-fourths of states.
- Some argue independence is undermined if justices know their term ends at a specific date (though non-renewability mitigates this).
- Application to current justices raises due process and separation-of-powers concerns.
- Opponents from the right argue the Court's current conservative majority is legitimate and reform is motivated by results-oriented partisan goals.
