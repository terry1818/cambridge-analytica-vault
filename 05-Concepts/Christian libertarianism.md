## Definition

Christian libertarianism is the ideology holding that laissez-faire capitalism is the only economic system consistent with Christian principles of individual freedom, and that government regulation of the economy — particularly the New Deal welfare state — constitutes a form of spiritual tyranny incompatible with Christian conscience. It should be distinguished from both mainstream evangelical political thought (which has varied positions on economic issues) and from Christian anarchism. Its distinctive claim is that the free market is not merely compatible with Christianity but is positively required by it, while state economic intervention is positively incompatible with it.

The ideology does not originate with concern for sexual ethics, abortion, or cultural issues that later defined the Religious Right; its founding preoccupation was specifically economic: opposition to labor regulation, the minimum wage, progressive taxation, and social welfare programs.

## Origin / history

Christian libertarianism as a named, organized ideology emerged from the corporate-funded clergy networks of the 1930s–50s:

- **1935**: [[James Fifield]] founds [[Spiritual Mobilization]] at his First Congregational Church in Los Angeles, with the explicit goal of recruiting Protestant ministers to oppose the New Deal on theological grounds. Fifield's method is to reinterpret New Testament teachings on wealth — minimizing passages about the dangers of riches, emphasizing individual spiritual responsibility — so that free-market economics becomes Christian orthodoxy.
- **Late 1940s**: Spiritual Mobilization's *Faith and Freedom* magazine gives the ideology its clearest expression, featuring libertarian economists (von Mises, Hazlitt, Leonard Read) alongside theological arguments. The term "Christian libertarianism" circulates in this period.
- **1950**: [[J. Howard Pew]] co-founds the Christian Freedom Foundation to extend the same ideology into a more explicit advocacy organization.
- **1956**: [[Christianity Today]] is founded, in part with Pew's funding, carrying a toned-down but recognizable version of the Christian-libertarian synthesis into the mainstream evangelical intellectual world.

The ideology draws on several pre-existing traditions: Protestant individualism, American exceptionalism, and the Social Darwinist strand of 19th-century Protestant thought. But its specifically organized, corporately funded form is a product of the New Deal era.

## How it works

The ideological mechanism involves several moves:

1. **Reframing regulation as coercion**: Government economic intervention is characterized not as a policy choice but as a violation of God-given individual freedom — making political opposition to regulation a form of Christian witness.
2. **Counter-framing the Social Gospel**: The Social Gospel tradition (which drew on New Testament teachings about the poor to justify progressive social policy) is recast as a theological error, even a form of paganism.
3. **Clergy as distribution nodes**: Rather than making the argument directly to corporate audiences, Christian libertarianism works by converting clergy, who then transmit the ideology to millions of congregants, insulating it from the appearance of being merely business self-interest.
4. **Theological cover for plutocracy**: By making material success a sign of divine favor and poverty a consequence of spiritual failure, the ideology provides theological permission for wealthy congregants to oppose redistribution.

## Application in this domain

Christian libertarianism is the ideological foundation of the mid-20th-century religious-right infrastructure documented in this vault. It precedes and enables the later political mobilizations (Moral Majority, Christian Coalition) by establishing the conceptual framework in which Christianity and conservative politics are treated as identical:

- [[Spiritual Mobilization]] was its primary institutional vehicle in the 1930s–50s.
- [[J. Howard Pew]]'s patronage network spread it through NAE and Christianity Today.
- It reappears in the "Biblical capitalism" concept that runs through later Religious Right and Tea Party discourse.
- The concept of [[Biblical capitalism]] in the vault is the direct descendant of Christian libertarianism.

## Examples

- Fifield's argument that the graduated income tax is a form of theft forbidden by the Eighth Commandment.
- Spiritual Mobilization pamphlets equating Social Security with communist collectivism.
- *Faith and Freedom* magazine publishing Ludwig von Mises essays alongside sermons.
- The Foundation for Economic Education distributing libertarian texts through Spiritual Mobilization's clergy network.

## Effectiveness / critique

**Effectiveness**: The Christian libertarian project achieved several of its medium-term goals: "under God" was added to the Pledge, "In God We Trust" became the national motto, and the conceptual equation of Christianity with anti-statism became a durable feature of American conservative politics. The long-term legacy is visible in the Tea Party movement's fusion of religious and libertarian rhetoric and in the continuing tendency of religious-right organizations to oppose taxation and regulation.

**Critique**: Historians, including Kruse, argue that Christian libertarianism involved a deliberate misreading of Scripture, selectively citing texts that supported its economic conclusions while suppressing those that did not. Mainline Protestant critics of Spiritual Mobilization in the 1940s–50s made this argument contemporaneously. The ideology also served the interests of its corporate funders so directly as to call into question whether the theological argument was sincere or instrumental.
