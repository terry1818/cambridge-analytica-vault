# Redistricting

## Definition

Redistricting is the redrawing of the geographic boundaries of electoral districts — congressional, state-legislative, and local — to reflect population changes recorded by the decennial census. Done neutrally, it equalizes district populations to honor "one person, one vote." Done manipulatively, it becomes **gerrymandering**: the deliberate drawing of lines to advantage one party (partisan gerrymandering) or to dilute the voting power of a racial group (racial gerrymandering). The two principal gerrymandering techniques are "cracking" (splitting a cohesive bloc of voters across many districts so it is a majority in none) and "packing" (concentrating an opposing party's voters into a few districts so its votes are "wasted" on lopsided wins).

## Origin / history

District manipulation predates the United States, but the term dates to 1812, when Massachusetts Governor Elbridge Gerry signed a map with a district shaped like a salamander — a "Gerry-mander." The modern legal framework emerged from the Supreme Court's reapportionment revolution: [[Baker v. Carr]] (1962) made districting justiciable, and *Reynolds v. Sims* (1964) imposed equal-population requirements. The Voting Rights Act of 1965 constrained racial vote dilution. The decisive recent turn was *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019), in which a 5–4 Supreme Court held that **partisan** gerrymandering claims are nonjusticiable political questions — closing the federal courthouse door to partisan-fairness challenges while leaving racial-gerrymandering claims alive.

## How it works

Mapmakers use precinct-level voting history and increasingly granular consumer and demographic data — the same data-broker infrastructure this vault tracks elsewhere — to predict how proposed lines will perform. Software can generate thousands of candidate maps and score each for partisan "efficiency gap" or seats-to-votes skew. The result is that a party controlling a state's redistricting process (usually the legislature, sometimes an independent commission) can lock in a durable majority: in several states a party has won a minority of the statewide vote yet a supermajority of seats. Because state legislatures themselves draw most maps, gerrymandering is self-reinforcing — the manipulated legislature redraws its own districts.

## Application in this domain

Redistricting is, in this vault's framing, a primary engine of structural minority rule and a sibling mechanism to the [[Electoral College]]. After 2010, the Republican REDMAP project concentrated resources on winning state legislatures precisely so the party would control the post-census maps; the result was durable U.S. House and state-legislative advantages. *Rucho* removed the federal check, pushing the fight into state courts, ballot initiatives, and Congress. The [[National Democratic Redistricting Committee]] is the Democratic counter-project. The mid-decade re-gerrymandering wars of 2025–2026 — partisan map redraws outside the normal census cycle — show the practice escalating into an open arms race.

## Examples

- [[Rucho v. Common Cause]] — 2019 ruling making partisan gerrymandering nonjusticiable in federal court.
- [[National Democratic Redistricting Committee]] — organization built to contest gerrymandered maps.
- [[Convention of States]] — a parallel route to entrench outcomes structurally rather than through ordinary politics.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed effectiveness:** highly effective at converting a transient legislative majority into a decade-long structural advantage.
- **Empirical evidence:** the "efficiency gap" and related metrics document large, durable seats-to-votes distortions in heavily gerrymandered states; political scientists broadly agree the effect is real even where they dispute magnitude.
- **Academic critique:** the contested question is judicial manageability — *Rucho*'s majority held courts cannot define "too much" partisanship, while the dissent and many scholars argued workable standards existed. Reform alternatives (independent commissions, proportional metrics) work where adopted but cannot be imposed nationally absent congressional action or a constitutional amendment.
