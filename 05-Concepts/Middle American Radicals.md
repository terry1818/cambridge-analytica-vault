## Definition

"Middle American Radicals" (MARs) is a sociological category describing a segment of the white middle and working class characterised by simultaneous resentment of economic and cultural elites (who they believe look down on them) and of the poor (whom they believe government favours over them). The term was coined by sociologist Donald Warren in his 1976 book *The Radical Center: Middle Americans and the Politics of Alienation* and was later adopted and radicalised by [[Samuel Francis]] into the theoretical core of a potential right-wing populist revolution. In Ganz's framing, MARs are the social base whose political activation defines the 1990s right-wing insurgency.

## Origin / history

Donald Warren, a sociologist at Oakland University (Michigan), identified the MARs category through survey research conducted in the early 1970s. His 1976 book documented a constituency — he estimated as many as one in five Americans — who combined economically progressive views with socially conservative and anti-establishment attitudes: suspicious of big government, the national media, academic experts, and wealthy corporations alike.

The MARs concept first had political expression in George Wallace's 1968 and 1972 presidential campaigns, which drew heavily from white working-class Democrats alienated by the civil-rights transformation of the party. The category was dormant analytically until [[Samuel Francis]] encountered Warren's work and reframed it through James Burnham's elite theory in the late 1980s and 1990s.

Francis's innovation was to argue that MARs were not merely a demographic fact but the potential vanguard of a counter-revolution against the liberal "managerial class." He published "Message from MARs" and related essays in *Chronicles* magazine and advised [[Pat Buchanan]] to explicitly target this constituency. Buchanan's 1992 and 1996 campaigns were the first large-scale electoral tests of the strategy.

## How it works

The MARs worldview rests on a "sandwich" theory of class resentment:

- **From above**: Liberal elites (academics, media, corporate managers, government bureaucrats) impose cosmopolitan values, affirmative action, and globalisation on the middle class while being insulated from the consequences.
- **From below**: The welfare state redistributes middle-class tax money downward to minority and poor populations who do not "earn" it.

This double resentment produces a distinctive political profile: economically nationalist (anti-free trade, pro-tariff, pro-industrial jobs), culturally authoritarian (anti-immigration, anti-affirmative action, anti-"political correctness"), and anti-establishment in a non-partisan sense (hostile to both party establishments).

## Application in this domain

The MARs framework is analytically central to understanding the prehistory of the current authoritarian right. The Trump 2016 coalition — white working-class voters without college degrees in Rust Belt states — matches the MARs profile almost exactly. Cambridge Analytica's [[Psychographic Targeting]] work targeted similar audiences, though without explicitly using the MARs vocabulary. The concept provides a sociological anchor for understanding why disinformation and info-warfare operations find receptive audiences: MARs are epistemically primed to distrust establishment information sources.

Francis's adaptation is also significant for the racial content he added: Warren's original category was sociological and not explicitly racial; Francis explicitly linked MARs identity to white ethnic consciousness, making the concept a bridge between mainstream populism and white nationalism.

## Examples

- George Wallace campaigns, 1968 and 1972
- Buchanan campaigns, 1992 and 1996
- [[Ross Perot]] coalition, 1992 (overlapping but not identical — Perot drew more upscale voters)
- Trump 2016 primary and general election coalition
- Brexit "left behind" voters (structural parallel in UK context)

## Effectiveness / critique

The predictive power of the MARs framework has been validated by subsequent events: the constituencies Francis and Warren described did rupture the two-party system in the ways Francis predicted, though on a 20-year delay. Critics note that the concept risks obscuring real material grievances behind a purely cultural analysis — the economic dislocations of deindustrialisation were real, not merely psychological. Francis's racial framing also converts an analytically useful category into an ideological programme.
