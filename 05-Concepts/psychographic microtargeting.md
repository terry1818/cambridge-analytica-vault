# psychographic microtargeting

## Definition

Psychographic microtargeting is the practice of segmenting an audience into very small groups — sometimes down to the individual — by inferred *psychological profile*, and then delivering message variants designed to resonate with each profile's emotional and cognitive tendencies. It is the operational application of [[Psychographics]]: where psychographics produces the trait scores, microtargeting is the act of matching tailored content to those scores and pushing it through ad-delivery infrastructure. It differs from ordinary microtargeting (which keys on demographics, geography, or past behavior) by claiming to target *who you are psychologically*.

## Origin / history

Microtargeting in politics predates the psychographic variant: by the 2004 and 2008 US cycles, both parties were matching voter files to consumer data to tailor outreach. The *psychographic* form is associated with [[SCL Group]] and its US affiliate [[Cambridge Analytica]], which from roughly 2014 marketed personality-typed messaging as a generational advance. CA's pitch — heavily promoted by CEO [[Alexander Nix]] in conference talks and sales decks — was that knowing a voter's Big Five (OCEAN) profile let a campaign press the exact psychological lever that would move them. The technique drew on the [[Facebook-CA Data Scandal|harvested Facebook data]] processed through models seeded by [[Aleksandr Kogan]]'s personality quiz.

## How it works

A working psychographic-microtargeting pipeline has four stages: (1) **profiling** — assign every targetable person an inferred personality score; (2) **creative variation** — produce many versions of the same political message, each written and art-directed for a particular trait profile (e.g., a gun-rights ad as a fear-of-intruders frame for high-Neuroticism voters versus a heritage-and-family frame for high-Conscientiousness voters); (3) **matching** — link profiles to ad-platform identifiers (Facebook Custom Audiences, cookie/device IDs); (4) **delivery and optimization** — serve each variant to its matched segment as "dark ads" visible only to the recipient, then use engagement feedback to refine. CA reportedly developed dozens of variants on themes such as immigration, the economy, and gun rights for the 2016 cycle.

## Application in this domain

Psychographic microtargeting is the central "weapon" in the Cambridge Analytica story and the reason the vault exists. CA sold it to the [[Ted Cruz]] 2016 primary campaign and the [[Donald Trump]] 2016 campaign; [[Robert Mercer]]'s money and [[Steve Bannon]]'s strategic direction sat behind the firm. The technique's broader significance is structural: it requires (a) mass personal data, (b) a platform that permits granular, opaque ad delivery, and (c) profiling models. After CA's collapse all three remain — data via [[data brokers]] and the [[Gravy Analytics|bidstream]], delivery via platform ad systems, profiling via in-house machine learning — which is why the vault treats the capability as having outlived the firm.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed effectiveness:** CA presented psychographic microtargeting as a decisive persuasion technology.
- **Empirical evidence:** Genuinely contested. A 2025 study (PNAS Nexus) found that *warning* people they are being microtargeted does not eliminate the persuasive advantage, suggesting some real effect; but other research finds the marginal lift of *psychographic* tailoring over ordinary targeting is small or unproven. `#claim/disputed`
- **Academic critique:** Political scientists (Hersh, Karpf, Nyhan) argue CA's psychographic claims were marketing. *The New York Times* (March 2017) reported CA executives conceded psychographics was not used in the Trump general election. The vault's position: the *data and delivery infrastructure* are real and consequential; the specific claim that OCEAN-typed creative reliably swings elections is unproven and probably overstated.
