# Public choice theory

## Definition

Public choice theory is the application of economic methods — particularly the assumption that individuals act from rational self-interest — to the analysis of political institutions and political decision-making. Where mainstream political science and welfare economics traditionally modelled government actors (politicians, regulators, bureaucrats) as benevolent maximizers of the public interest, public choice theory treats them as self-interested agents pursuing votes, budgets, prestige, and ideological goals. The framework generates systematic predictions about government failure that parallel the concept of market failure, and it supplies the theoretical vocabulary for arguing that democratic majorities, regulatory agencies, and the administrative state are structurally prone to inefficiency, capture, and rights violation.

## Origin / history

The theory emerged from the [[Virginia school of political economy]] at the University of Virginia in the late 1950s. Its founding text is *The Calculus of Consent: Logical Foundations of Constitutional Democracy* (1962), co-authored by [[James M. Buchanan]] and Gordon Tullock. The book applied cost-benefit analysis to constitutional choice — asking what voting rules, constitutional constraints, and governance structures rational individuals would agree to behind a veil of uncertainty. A secondary founding text is Gordon Tullock's 1965 *The Politics of Bureaucracy*, which modelled bureaucrats as budget-maximisers.

The concept of *rent-seeking* — individuals or firms expending resources to capture political favours rather than create value — was introduced by Tullock (1967) and named by economist Anne Krueger (1974). Together, Buchanan's constitutional economics and Tullock's rent-seeking analysis gave public choice its twin analytical pillars.

Buchanan received the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences in 1986 explicitly for public choice theory. By the 1970s–1980s, the theory had been adopted and amplified by a network of free-market think tanks — the [[Cato Institute]], the [[Heritage Foundation]], the [[Mercatus Center]], the [[American Enterprise Institute]] — most of them connected through the [[Mont Pelerin Society]] and, increasingly, the [[Koch Network]].

## How it works

Public choice theory models politics as analogous to market exchange among self-interested parties:

1. **Voters** are rationally ignorant — the cost of becoming informed about complex policy exceeds the tiny probability that any one vote is decisive, so voters systematically under-invest in political knowledge.
2. **Politicians** seek re-election above policy goals; they respond to concentrated, organised interests who supply campaign resources, not dispersed publics.
3. **Bureaucrats** seek budget maximisation, job security, and expanded agency scope; they resist efficient outcomes that would reduce their own importance.
4. **Interest groups** engage in rent-seeking — lobbying for regulations, subsidies, and protections that redistribute wealth toward organised minorities (themselves) at the expense of dispersed majorities.
5. **Constitutional constraints** — super-majority rules, balanced-budget amendments, limits on regulatory delegation, property-rights protections — are therefore needed to bind future democratic majorities and limit the damage self-interested actors can do through the political process.

The last step — the leap from public choice diagnosis to constitutional remedy — is where the theory interfaces most directly with the constitutional-capture project.

## Application in this domain

In the context of this vault's core subjects, public choice theory operates on several levels:

- It provides the *intellectual legitimacy* for [[Article V Convention]] / [[Convention of States Action]] proposals: if government is structurally prone to expansion and rights violation, a constitutional convention to impose fiscal constraints (balanced budget, spending limits, term limits) is framed as a principled correction, not a partisan power grab.
- It supplies the vocabulary for attacking the administrative state — EPA, CFPB, OSHA — as inherently captured agencies serving bureaucratic and special-interest goals rather than public welfare.
- It informed [[Project 2025]]'s Schedule F proposal (dismantling civil-service protections) by framing career bureaucrats as self-interested impediments to democratic accountability.
- [[Charles Koch]] and affiliated donors explicitly used Buchanan's framework — documented in MacLean's research — to direct funding toward institutions that would produce and disseminate anti-regulatory, pro-constitutional-constraint arguments at scale.

## Examples

- **Balanced Budget Amendment advocacy**: Public choice arguments are central to ALEC model legislation and Convention of States arguments that Congress will never voluntarily constrain spending, so a constitutional rule is necessary.
- **Regulatory capture doctrine**: The EPA, FCC, and other agencies are routinely described in Koch-affiliated policy papers using public choice's rent-seeking and capture vocabulary.
- **School voucher / privatisation arguments**: Public choice theory predicts that government monopoly schooling will serve teacher unions (concentrated interests) over students and families (dispersed interests); vouchers restore "market discipline."
- **Voter ID and election law**: Some public choice scholars have applied the framework to electoral law, arguing that institutional rules affect who bears the cost of voting.

## Effectiveness / critique

**Strengths**: Public choice theory has genuine explanatory power for regulatory capture, legislative logrolling, and bureaucratic inertia. Its insistence that government actors respond to incentives, not just stated goals, is a lasting contribution to policy analysis.

**Critiques**:
- *Empirical narrowness*: The rational self-interest model poorly predicts much actual political behaviour; legislators and bureaucrats frequently act on ideology, duty, and public concern, not pure self-interest.
- *Asymmetric application*: Critics note that public choice scholars apply self-interest assumptions rigorously to government but rarely apply the same scrutiny to private firms and markets, where corporate rent-seeking is equally documented.
- *Ideological function*: Henry Farrell, Steven Teles, and others have argued that in practice public choice became less a neutral analytical tool than a rhetorical weapon for attacking government programs the conservative-libertarian coalition opposed. The theory's policy conclusions conveniently aligned with the preferences of its major funders.
- *MacLean's contested claim*: [[Source - MacLean 2017 - Democracy in Chains]] argues that Buchanan's constitutional economics was specifically designed to entrench elite minority power against democratic redistribution. This strong claim is disputed by multiple scholars but the structural alignment between the theory's prescriptions and donor interests is acknowledged across the spectrum.
