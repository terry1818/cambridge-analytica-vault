# Section 702

**Section 702 of the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA) Amendments Act of 2008** (codified at 50 USC Â§ 1881a). Authorizes the National Security Agency (NSA) to collect foreign-intelligence information from non-US persons reasonably believed to be located outside the United States via electronic communication service providers. **Controversial for incidental collection of US persons' communications** and for FBI "backdoor searches" of the collected database for US-person queries.

## Statutory framework

### What 702 authorizes

- Targeting: non-US persons outside the US believed to have foreign-intelligence value
- Collection mechanism: compelled cooperation of US electronic communication service providers (Google, Microsoft, Facebook, etc.)
- Two principal programs: PRISM (downstream provider compelled production) and Upstream (interception at internet backbone)

### What 702 does NOT authorize (statutorily)

- Targeting US persons
- Targeting any person located in the US
- Reverse targeting (using foreign target to acquire US person communications)

## Operational controversies

### Incidental collection

US persons' communications are collected when they communicate with targeted non-US persons. The volume of this incidental collection is substantial but classified.

### FBI "backdoor searches"

The FBI is permitted to query the 702-collected database using US-person identifiers. These "backdoor searches" have been controversial because:
- They access US persons' communications without individualized FISA court probable-cause finding
- Documented misuse for non-foreign-intelligence purposes
- 2023 FISA Court opinion documented 200,000+ unauthorized FBI queries

### Documented misuse

- Queries on Black Lives Matter protesters
- Queries on January 6 defendants
- Queries on members of Congress
- Queries for purely-domestic-crime purposes

## Reauthorization history

- 2008: original FAA Section 702 enactment
- 2012: 5-year reauthorization
- 2017: 6-year reauthorization
- 2024 April: 2-year reauthorization (with limited reforms; some controversial expansions)
