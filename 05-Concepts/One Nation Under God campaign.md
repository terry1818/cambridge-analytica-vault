## Definition

The "One Nation Under God" campaign refers to the coordinated political-religious effort in the late 1940s–1950s to embed explicitly theistic language into American civic symbols and institutions: specifically, adding the phrase "under God" to the Pledge of Allegiance (enacted June 14, 1954) and establishing "In God We Trust" as the official national motto of the United States (enacted July 30, 1956, signed by Eisenhower). The campaign was not a single organized project but a convergence of multiple actors — corporate-funded clergy networks, Catholic fraternal organizations, mainline Protestant groups, and Cold War anti-communist politics — that collectively succeeded in reframing American national identity in explicitly religious terms.

Kevin M. Kruse's central argument in *One Nation Under God* (2015) is that this campaign was not a spontaneous expression of popular religiosity but was substantially shaped and funded by corporate interests that saw theistic public language as an ideological counterweight to the perceived collectivism of the New Deal and Soviet communism.

## Origin / history

- **1935**: [[James Fifield]] founds [[Spiritual Mobilization]], beginning the long project of recruiting Protestant clergy to oppose the New Deal in theological terms.
- **1951**: Spiritual Mobilization launches its "Freedom Under God" July 4th campaign, organizing religious observances across the country that explicitly link Christian freedom with anti-statist politics.
- **1952**: The Knights of Columbus, a Catholic fraternal organization, begins lobbying Congress to add "under God" to the Pledge of Allegiance.
- **February 7, 1954**: Rev. George M. Docherty, pastor of New York Avenue Presbyterian Church, preaches a sermon before President Eisenhower (seated in Lincoln's pew) proposing "under God" be added to the Pledge.
- **June 14, 1954**: Flag Day. Eisenhower signs legislation adding "under God" to the Pledge of Allegiance.
- **1956**: Congress establishes "In God We Trust" as the official national motto; the phrase begins appearing on paper currency and stamps.
- **1956**: [[Christianity Today]] is founded, providing a continuing institutional voice for the Christian-national synthesis the campaign had achieved.

## How it works

The campaign achieved its goals through a multi-channel strategy:

1. **Clergy mobilization**: Networks built by Spiritual Mobilization and similar organizations meant that thousands of ministers were primed to support public religious affirmations.
2. **Cold War framing**: The Soviet Union was characterized as "godless communism"; adding "under God" to civic symbols became a way of asserting American moral superiority over atheist collectivism. This framing allowed the campaign to attract support far beyond its original corporate-libertarian base.
3. **Presidential access**: Eisenhower, a personally devout man and a new churchgoer (he was baptized in 1953), was sympathetic; the sermon by Docherty — in Lincoln's pew — gave him a rhetorically powerful rationale.
4. **Legislative momentum**: Once the symbolic issues were framed as patriotic rather than merely religious, congressional opposition became politically costly.

## Application in this domain

The campaign is the clearest historical evidence for Kruse's core thesis: that the American equation of Christianity with conservative politics and anti-statism was not an organic popular development but was substantially engineered by corporate interests working through religious institutions.

For the vault's research frame, the campaign represents:
- The political payoff of the Fifield-Pew corporate-Christian infrastructure investment.
- An early model of how religious language and civic ceremony can be weaponized for political purposes.
- The moment when [[Christian libertarianism]] shifted from ideological project to embedded civic norm — making subsequent challenges to the equation of Christianity with conservative politics culturally costly.

The campaign's legacy is visible in ongoing fights over religious language in the public square and in the Christian nationalist claim that America is "a Christian nation" — a claim that draws directly on the symbolic achievements of the 1950s campaign.

## Examples

- The Pledge of Allegiance: "one nation under God, indivisible, with liberty and justice for all" — the phrase "under God" is a 1954 addition; the original 1892 pledge contained no religious language.
- "In God We Trust" on U.S. currency: appeared on coins sporadically since the Civil War era, but only established as a universal requirement in 1956.
- Presidential Prayer Breakfasts (became National Prayer Breakfast under Eisenhower in 1953): part of the same project of embedding religious language in civic ritual. See [[National Prayer Breakfast]].
- The Eisenhower administration's promotion of "piety on the Potomac" — the president's own church attendance, public prayer, etc.

## Effectiveness / critique

**Effectiveness**: The campaign achieved its immediate legislative goals and fundamentally altered American civil religion. The equation of American identity with generic (Protestant-inflected) theism became a durable feature of public culture.

**Critique**: Critics at the time and since have argued:
- The campaign violated the First Amendment's Establishment Clause by embedding official religious language in civic symbols.
- It represented the interests of a specific corporate-religious coalition, not universal American religiosity.
- The "under God" phrase has been challenged in court (most prominently in *Elk Grove Unified School District v. Newdow*, 2004, which the Supreme Court dismissed on standing grounds rather than resolving the constitutional question).
- Kruse's critics (e.g., at Reason magazine) contest the degree to which corporate funding *caused* the campaign versus merely *supporting* a genuinely popular religious sentiment.
