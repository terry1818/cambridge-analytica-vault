## Definition

Law and Economics is the application of microeconomic theory and methods — efficiency, cost-benefit analysis, rational-choice modeling, price theory — to the interpretation and design of legal rules. In its most influential form, it holds that legal rules should be evaluated primarily by their economic efficiency consequences, and that many intuitive legal principles (e.g., strict liability, punitive damages, regulatory mandates) are economically inefficient and therefore undesirable. As a movement, it transformed American legal scholarship and had measurable effects on judicial reasoning, particularly in antitrust, tort, contract, property, and regulatory law.

## Origin / History

The intellectual roots lie at the University of Chicago in the 1950s–1960s, where Aaron Director (financed by the Volker Fund), Ronald Coase, George Stigler, and Richard Posner developed the analytical framework. The movement's political and institutional acceleration came when Henry Manne — a former student of Coase — undertook in the early 1970s to bring these ideas directly into law schools and, crucially, to educate sitting federal judges.

Manne established law and economics centers successively at Miami, Emory, and George Mason (where he became dean of the law school in 1986). The [[John M Olin Foundation]] made its first Law and Economics grant in 1974 ($100,000 to Manne's center at the University of Miami) and became the movement's primary institutional patron. By the late 1990s, Olin had funded named Law and Economics programs at virtually every top-10 US law school.

## How It Works

The movement operated through three complementary channels:

1. **Law school programs**: Olin-funded named chairs, research programs, student fellowships, and journals at Harvard, Chicago, Yale, Stanford, Columbia, Cornell, Michigan, Virginia, and others. This created a pipeline of law students and professors trained in economic analysis of law.

2. **Judicial education**: The Economics Institute for Federal Judges — operating under Henry Manne — invited sitting federal judges to all-expenses-paid, multi-week immersion programs in Law and Economics principles, typically held at resort venues. By the mid-1990s, an estimated 40 percent of the federal judiciary had participated, including future Supreme Court justices Ruth Bader Ginsburg and Clarence Thomas. Jane Mayer's *Dark Money* details these "judicial junkets" as a primary mechanism for shifting judicial philosophy without Senate confirmation processes.

3. **Academic journals and networks**: Law and Economics produced a dense citation network that elevated economic-efficiency arguments in legal scholarship and made them credible to courts as legitimate interpretive tools.

## Application in This Domain

Law and Economics is directly relevant to the vault's constitutional-capture and judicial-capture threads:

- **Regulatory rollback**: The movement's efficiency framework provides legal theories supporting deregulation, used extensively by industry litigants and government agencies seeking to weaken environmental, labor, and consumer protection rules.
- **Antitrust**: Chicago School antitrust analysis — a Law and Economics product — narrowed antitrust enforcement and enabled the corporate consolidation that characterizes key sectors (finance, agriculture, tech) relevant to this vault's power-map analysis.
- **Judicial pipeline**: The [[Federalist Society]], which the [[John M Olin Foundation]] also funded, trained the lawyers; Law and Economics trained the judges. Together they constitute the two legs of the judicial-capture strategy documented in [[Weaponizing philanthropy]] literature.
- **Constitutional argument**: Law and Economics principles — particularly the argument that wealth-maximizing rules are optimal — provide intellectual support for the property-rights and fiscal-constraint arguments embedded in [[Constitutional entrenchment strategy]].

## Examples

- Richard Posner's *Economic Analysis of Law* (1973): the canonical textbook, making Law and Economics accessible to practitioners
- The Olin-funded judicial education programs training roughly 40% of federal judges by the mid-1990s
- Chicago School antitrust revolution: the *Bork* framework (*The Antitrust Paradox*, 1978) reshaping US antitrust enforcement for four decades
- The *Lochner*-revival scholarly project: Law and Economics arguments supporting heightened constitutional protection for property rights and economic liberty

## Effectiveness / Critique

**Effectiveness**: The movement achieved extraordinary results proportionate to its investment. Olin's $370M total giving — much of it to Law and Economics — arguably shifted billions of dollars in regulatory, antitrust, and tort outcomes over subsequent decades. Federal judicial reasoning demonstrably incorporates economic efficiency analysis in ways it did not before the 1970s.

**Critique**: Critics argue the movement systematically elevated efficiency over equity, liberty, and democratic values in legal interpretation; that the "neutral" economic framework embodied politically contestable value choices (prioritizing wealth maximization over redistribution); and that the judicial education programs constituted ethically problematic ex parte lobbying of sitting judges. Law professor Lani Guinier and others have argued the movement was not politically neutral but rather a disciplined ideological project funded by conservative donors with financial stakes in its outcomes.
