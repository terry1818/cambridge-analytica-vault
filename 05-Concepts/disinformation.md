# disinformation

## Definition

Disinformation is false or misleading information that is created and spread **deliberately, with the intent to deceive**. The defining feature is intent. This distinguishes it from **misinformation**, which is false information spread without the intent to deceive — for example, by a person who genuinely believes a false claim and shares it organically. A third term, **malinformation**, refers to genuine information shared deliberately to cause harm (e.g., leaking private but true material). In practice these categories blur: a single false claim can originate as deliberate disinformation and then spread as misinformation once unwitting people circulate it in good faith.

## Origin / history

The English word *disinformation* is a loan translation of the Russian *dezinformatsiya*. Russian use is traced to a "special disinformation office" established in 1923; the Great Soviet Encyclopedia (1952) defined the term as "false information with the intention to deceive public opinion." Within Soviet intelligence, disinformation was one of the "active measures" — alongside forgery, subversion, and front organizations — used to influence foreign populations and governments. The US Intelligence Community appropriated the term from Russian usage in the 1950s and adopted comparable techniques during the Cold War. The word re-entered mainstream Western discourse forcefully after 2016, becoming a "word of the year" amid concern over election interference; this rapid popularization also produced terminological looseness that scholars (e.g., the LSE Media blog) have criticized.

## How it works

At a basic level disinformation works by exploiting the gap between the cost of producing a false claim and the cost of verifying it: fabrication is cheap, debunking is slow. Effective disinformation typically:

1. **Anchors to a real grievance or true fact**, making the false element harder to isolate.
2. **Launders provenance** — routed through multiple outlets or sock-puppet accounts so the claim appears independently corroborated (see [[MAGA media ecosystem]]).
3. **Targets identity, not just belief** — messages designed to be shared as expressions of group loyalty spread further than messages designed to inform.
4. **Operates at scale** — in the digital era, bot networks, coordinated inauthentic accounts, and micro-targeted advertising allow a small operation to simulate a broad consensus ("computational propaganda").

## Application in this domain

Disinformation is the analytical spine of this vault. Several documented cases:

- **[[Cambridge Analytica]] / 2016:** psychographic profiling was used to target political messaging — including misleading and suppressive content — to specific micro-audiences, an industrialization of disinformation delivery.
- **Russian interference, 2016:** the Internet Research Agency ran coordinated inauthentic social-media operations; the GRU conducted hack-and-leak operations — a textbook *active measures* campaign.
- **2020 election denial:** false claims that [[Dominion Voting Systems]] and [[Smartmatic]] machines switched votes were broadcast by [[Fox News]], [[Newsmax]], and [[One America News]] and recirculated until they sustained the [[Stop the Steal]] movement; the resulting defamation litigation established the falsity of those specific claims as a legal matter.
- **2024 — Tenet Media:** a US Justice Department indictment alleged Russia covertly funded a Tennessee company that paid right-wing influencers, who were reportedly unwitting — a case where deliberate disinformation (the funders' intent) was distributed as misinformation (the influencers' belief).

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed effectiveness:** Campaigns, intelligence services, and commentators often credit disinformation with decisive political effects.
- **Empirical evidence:** Mixed. Studies of 2016 Russian social-media operations generally find measurable *reach* but contested *persuasive impact* — exposure was concentrated among users already aligned with the content. Political scientists caution that disinformation more reliably *reinforces and mobilizes* existing beliefs than it *converts* opponents.
- **Academic critique:** Scholars warn that "disinformation" has become an over-broad, politically contested label, sometimes used to dismiss inconvenient but accurate reporting. Rigorous usage requires evidence of both falsity and deliberate intent. `#claim/disputed` — the *effectiveness* of disinformation is genuinely contested even where its *presence* is documented.
