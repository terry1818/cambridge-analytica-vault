# Covenant communities

## Definition

A covenant community is an intentional Christian community in which members make a formal, usually lifelong "covenant" — a permanent commitment — to one another and to a shared rule of life. The defining feature is not residence (members typically keep their own homes and jobs) but the *covenant* and the accompanying structures of authority: each member is assigned a personal spiritual director or "head," and the community exercises real authority over members' decisions. The best-known examples grew out of the **Catholic Charismatic Renewal**, are deliberately *ecumenical* — open to baptized Christians of any tradition who affirm the Nicene Creed — and combine Pentecostal-style charismatic worship with tight communal discipline.

## Origin / history

The covenant-community model emerged with the Catholic Charismatic Renewal. Charismatic Catholics see themselves as heirs of the 1906 Azusa Street Revival; the movement reached the Catholic Church in 1967, when a retreat by professors at Duquesne University in Pittsburgh produced experiences of "baptism in the Spirit."

Two of the earliest and most influential communities formed in the upper Midwest:

- **The Word of God** — founded in Ann Arbor, Michigan, in 1967 by **Ralph Martin** and **Stephen B. Clark**, two Americans shaped by the Cursillo movement and then by the charismatic renewal. The Word of God later became the seed of an international association.
- **People of Praise** — founded in South Bend, Indiana, in 1971 by **Kevin Ranaghan** and **Paul DeCelles**.

In 1982 the Word of God spun out the **[[Sword of the Spirit]]**, an international ecumenical association of covenant communities. As of 2017 the Sword of the Spirit comprised 82 communities (45 of them Catholic); in 2023 it reported about 14,000 members. **[[People of Praise]]** is a separate network of around 1,700 members across roughly 22 branches in the U.S., Canada, and the Caribbean.

## How it works

Although members take no formal vows, after a period of discernment they make a covenant — a permanent commitment to the community. Each member has a personal "head" (sometimes historically called a "handmaid" for women) who provides spiritual and practical guidance, drawing on the Ignatian Spiritual Exercises. In practice the head's guidance can extend deep into personal life — finances, vocation, courtship, parenting. Communities place strong emphasis on covenant order, complementarian gender roles, and large families. They are parachurch bodies, not churches: members also belong to ordinary parishes or congregations.

## Application in this domain

For this vault, covenant communities are relevant as a *high-commitment institutional form* within charismatic Christianity — a model for building tightly bonded, disciplined, multi-generational communities outside ordinary church structures. They became publicly salient because of their ties to figures in public life: Supreme Court Justice **[[Amy Coney Barrett]]** and her family have been associated with [[People of Praise]], drawing intense scrutiny during her 2017 appellate and 2020 Supreme Court confirmations. The communities are not the [[New Apostolic Reformation]] and should not be conflated with it — they are an older, Catholic-rooted charismatic stream — but they belong to the same broad landscape of organized charismatic Christianity the vault tracks, and they illustrate how covenant-and-headship structures can produce durable, ideologically cohesive networks.

## Examples

- [[Sword of the Spirit]] — international ecumenical association spun out of the Word of God in 1982; ~82 communities.
- [[People of Praise]] — South Bend–founded network (1971); associated with Amy Coney Barrett's family.
- **The Word of God** (Ann Arbor) — founding community of the Sword of the Spirit; later experienced internal upheaval.
- [[Catholic Charismatic Renewal]] — the broader movement from which covenant communities emerged.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed purpose:** Communities present the covenant as a path to deeper Christian discipleship and mutual support.
- **Empirical evidence:** The model demonstrably produces durable, cohesive, multi-generational networks.
- **Critique:** In 1997 theologian Adrian Reimers, a former People of Praise member, published a sharp critique of People of Praise, Sword of the Spirit, and similar groups, alleging "abuse of authority" by leadership and arguing that headship structures "diminish individual autonomy." Critics and some former members describe the communities as controlling; the communities and many current members reject the characterization. The vault treats the existence of headship/covenant authority structures as documented and the "abuse"/"cult-like" characterizations as contested, mixed across communities and eras. `#claim/disputed`
