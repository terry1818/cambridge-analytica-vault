# The Southern Strategy

## Definition

The Southern Strategy refers to the Republican Party's deliberate electoral strategy — developed primarily between 1964 and 1972, and extended through subsequent decades — of winning white Southern voters away from the Democratic Party by appealing to racial resentment, opposition to civil rights enforcement, and cultural anxiety. The strategy did not typically employ explicit racial language; instead it used coded appeals (states' rights, law and order, anti-busing, welfare reform) that white Southern voters understood as racially referential while providing the party with plausible deniability of racial intent. The strategy is widely documented by scholars and was explicitly acknowledged by key Republican operatives.

## Origin / history

The strategic logic was first articulated publicly by Republican strategist Kevin Phillips in *The Emerging Republican Majority* (1969), which argued the GOP could build a durable majority by capturing white Southern conservative voters alienated by the Democrats' civil rights agenda. The strategy was executed in Barry Goldwater's 1964 campaign (Goldwater voted against the Civil Rights Act), refined in Nixon's 1968 and 1972 campaigns, and extended by Ronald Reagan (who opened his 1980 campaign in Philadelphia, Mississippi, invoking "states' rights") and subsequent Republican candidates. RNC Chairman Lee Atwater gave a notorious candid account of the strategy's evolution in a 1981 interview (made public in 2012), describing the shift from explicit racial language to increasingly abstract code words.

Steven Hahn's *Illiberal America: A History* (2024) situates the Southern Strategy within a longer arc of American illiberalism, arguing that the Republican capture of Southern white voters was part of a broader historical pattern in which illiberal politics — rooted in community-enforced racial, religious, and gender hierarchies — has been a persistent and central (not peripheral) force in American political culture. Hahn traces this from the 17th century through the present, with the Southern Strategy as a key 20th-century inflection point.

## How it works

The strategy operates through several mechanisms:

1. **Policy coding**: Policy positions with racially disparate impact (opposing busing, supporting "states' rights" on school integration, restricting welfare) are presented as race-neutral but understood by the target audience as racial.
2. **Culture war framing**: Appeals to cultural threat (crime, urban disorder, "traditional values") serve as proxies for racial anxiety without explicit racial language.
3. **Coalition building**: White Southern voters, previously solidly Democratic (the "Solid South"), become a reliable Republican base, enabling the party to win national elections with relatively little support from Black voters.
4. **Dog-whistle evolution**: As explicit racial language became more politically costly, the strategy evolved through successive generations of increasingly abstract code — each generation requiring a new vocabulary while maintaining the racial content.

## Application in this domain

The Southern Strategy is foundational context for the vault's analysis of how the Republican Party became the vehicle for authoritarian and illiberal politics. Specific applications: (1) understanding the racial politics undergirding Cambridge Analytica's targeting strategies — CA's psychographic models for Trump 2016 specifically targeted cultural anxiety and ethnic outbidding dynamics rooted in the Southern Strategy's legacy electorate; (2) connecting the strategy to the [[Sons of the soil]] dynamic — the white Southern voter base is the paradigmatic "sons of the soil" grievance constituency; (3) understanding Steve Bannon's explicit racialised nationalism as a continuation and radicalisation of the strategy. ## Examples

- **Goldwater 1964**: Opposition to Civil Rights Act on "states' rights" grounds won Goldwater the Deep South (but cost him the rest of the country).
- **Nixon 1968**: "Law and order" campaign; Haldeman's notes record Nixon explicitly framing strategy around Black Americans.
- **Reagan 1980**: Philadelphia, Mississippi speech invoking "states' rights" at the site of the 1964 murder of civil rights workers.
- **Lee Atwater 1981**: Explicit internal articulation of the coded-language strategy (interview revealed 2012).
- **Willie Horton ad 1988**: George H.W. Bush campaign; racial coding through crime imagery.
- **Trump 2015–2016**: Explicit immigration rhetoric (Mexican rapists, Muslim ban) — some scholars argue this represents a *return* to explicit racial language after decades of coding, while others see continuity.

## Effectiveness / critique

The historical existence and racial intent of the Southern Strategy is well-documented. Areas of debate include:

1. **Intentionality question**: Some revisionist historians (e.g., *The Hill*, 2018) argue the Southern realignment was driven more by economic and religious conservatism than race. The majority scholarly view, supported by documentary evidence (Atwater interview, Haldeman notes, Phillips's explicit strategic writing), supports the racial-intent interpretation.
2. **Periodisation**: Scholars debate when the strategy began, whether it was a coherent plan or an emergent pattern, and when (if) it ended.
3. **Voter motivation vs. elite strategy**: Even if Republican elites deployed racial appeals strategically, individual voters may have been responding to other factors. The strategy and its reception are analytically separable.
4. **Extension to the present**: Whether Trump-era rhetoric represents a continuation, radicalisation, or transformation of the Southern Strategy is actively debated among political scientists.
