# constitutional capture

## Definition

**Constitutional capture** is this vault's term for a political strategy: the deliberate effort to lock favored policy outcomes into durable structures — constitutional text, court doctrine, fiscal rules, administrative design — so that they cannot be undone by ordinary democratic majorities. Where [[Regulatory capture]] subverts an agency and election-rigging subverts a vote, constitutional capture aims higher: it removes whole questions (taxation limits, federal jurisdiction, property and contract doctrine) from the menu of things a future electorate is permitted to decide. The goal is not to win elections repeatedly but to make winning elections matter less.

## Origin / history

The concept synthesizes several documented strands rather than naming a single self-described movement:

- **Intellectual roots (1950s–1980s):** [[James M. Buchanan]] and [[Gordon Tullock]]'s [[constitutional economics]] argued that constitutional rules should bind future majorities to protect markets and minorities from redistribution. [[Ludwig von Mises]] and [[Friedrich Hayek]] supplied the prior conviction that liberty requires limits democracy cannot touch.
- **Operationalization (1980s–present):** Nancy MacLean's *Democracy in Chains* (2017) argues that the [[Koch Network]] turned Buchanan's program into a long-run political strategy — fiscal rules, privatization, judicial reshaping, and constitutional "restraints" all aimed at making elected government structurally incapable of redistributing wealth or regulating capital. MacLean frames it as saving capitalism *from* democracy.
- **Vehicles:** The [[Federalist Society]]'s reshaping of the judiciary, the [[Convention of States]] Article V campaign, and [[American Legislative Exchange Council]] model legislation are the most concrete present-day instruments.

## How it works

Constitutional capture operates by changing the *level* at which a contested question is settled. A normal policy fight is won or lost at the legislative level and can be re-fought after the next election. A captured question is moved to a level that is far harder to reverse:

1. **Constitutional entrenchment** — writing fiscal limits, jurisdiction caps, or term limits into the constitutional text (the [[Convention of States]] route). Reversal then requires a supermajority of states, effectively impossible.
2. **Judicial entrenchment** — installing judges who read the Constitution to forbid the disfavored policy (e.g., limits on federal regulatory power), so a statute alone cannot restore it.
3. **Fiscal pre-commitment** — balanced-budget rules, tax-supermajority requirements, and debt caps that mechanically prevent future spending choices.
4. **Administrative starvation** — defunding and restructuring agencies so that even unrepealed laws cannot be enforced — see [[Education-capture]].

Each mechanism converts a reversible political loss for opponents into a structural, multi-decade fact.

## Application in this domain

For the vault's long arc, constitutional capture is the patron-wing's counterpart to the theocratic wing's [[Seven Mountains Mandate]] and the data wing's surveillance infrastructure: three routes to the same end of insulating power from democratic correction. It connects the public-choice academy ([[James M. Buchanan]], [[George Mason University]], [[Mercatus Center]]), the donor network ([[Koch Network]], [[Donors Trust]]), the legislative-template machine ([[American Legislative Exchange Council]]), and the constitutional-amendment campaign ([[Convention of States]]). It is the structural endgame that psychographic targeting and election interference are, on this vault's thesis, the tactical means toward.

## Examples

- [[Convention of States]] — seeks to entrench fiscal restraints and jurisdiction limits in the constitutional text.
- [[Federalist Society]] — reshaped the federal judiciary to lock in a particular constitutional reading.
- [[American Legislative Exchange Council]] — supplies model bills entrenching tax and spending limits at the state level.
- [[Education-capture]] — administrative and structural defunding of public education.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed effectiveness:** Proponents (who would not use this label) frame entrenchment as principled constitutionalism — protecting rights and fiscal sanity from transient majorities.
- **Empirical evidence:** The judicial strand is demonstrably effective (durable doctrinal shifts); the Article V strand has not yet succeeded.
- **Academic critique:** Scholars of class entrenchment argue "neutral" restraints lock in market inequality. Democratic-theory critics call the strategy a form of democratic backsliding by legal means.
- **Contested:** MacLean's strong claim of conscious, coordinated *design* across decades is disputed by Farrell, Teles, Magness, and Munger as over-reading. This vault treats the funding relationships and institutional history as documented, and the unified-intent narrative as a contested interpretation.
