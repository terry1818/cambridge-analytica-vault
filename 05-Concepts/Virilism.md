# Virilism

## Definition

Ruth Ben-Ghiat's term from *Strongmen: Mussolini to the Present* (W.W. Norton, 2020) for the deliberate, performative cultivation of hypermasculine identity by authoritarian leaders as a governing technology. Virilism is not mere bravado — in Ben-Ghiat's analysis, it is a structured political instrument: the strongman's projections of physical potency, sexual dominance, and male camaraderie serve to place him above the ordinary constraints of law and morality (men strong enough to take what they want need not follow rules), to build a brotherhood of loyalty among male followers, and to organize political violence against feminized "weak" opponents and LGBTQ+ communities.

## Origin / History

Ben-Ghiat developed the concept in *Strongmen* (2020), drawing on her scholarly work on Italian fascism and applying it comparatively across a century of authoritarian leaders: Mussolini, Hitler, Franco, Gaddafi, Berlusconi, Putin, Bolsonaro, and Trump. The book devotes an entire chapter to virilism. The concept synthesizes gender theory (performativity, Judith Butler), fascism studies (Paxton, Mosse), and comparative authoritarianism.

## How It Works

Ben-Ghiat identifies several mechanisms by which virilism functions politically:

1. **Above the law through strength**: The display of physical and sexual power communicates that the strongman operates by a different set of rules — power itself is the legitimating force, not institutional authority. This makes corruption tolerable or even admirable to followers.

2. **Male kinship as political loyalty**: Strongmen build networks of loyalty through homosocial bonding — shared displays of masculine power (hunting, athleticism, military cosplay, sexual bragging) create ingroup solidarity that substitutes for ideological coherence.

3. **Target designation**: Virilism requires feminized enemies — the "weak," "effeminate," or sexually non-conforming out-group against whom the masculine in-group defines itself. This is why anti-LGBTQ+ politics is structurally central, not peripheral, to the strongman playbook.

4. **Foreign policy as masculinity performance**: Ben-Ghiat shows how strongmen conduct foreign policy partly as virility performance — displays of toughness that communicate to the domestic audience as much as to foreign counterparts.

5. **Corruption as masculine entitlement**: Taking from subordinates, exploiting women, accepting bribes — these are framed not as failures but as confirmations of power; the strong man takes what he can.

## Application in This Domain

This framework provides a diagnostic for analyzing leadership style in the vault's focus figures. It is particularly applicable to Trump, Bolsonaro, Orban, and comparable figures in the three-wing coalition. The analytical question is not whether a leader is genuinely hypermasculine but whether they perform virilism as a political instrument — and whether their base responds to the performance as legitimation of norm-breaking. The vault can also use this concept to analyze media ecosystems (e.g., the role of Bannon's War Room, Charlie Kirk's masculinity messaging) that propagate the virilism frame.

## Examples

- Mussolini's bare-chested agricultural harvest photos; his publicized affairs as evidence of vitality.
- Putin's shirtless horseback riding, judo displays, ice hockey staged victories.
- Bolsonaro's explicit homophobia and anti-LGBTQ+ rhetoric as virilism performance.
- Trump's sexual boasting (Access Hollywood tape) and the base's tolerance/celebration of it.
- Ben-Ghiat notes that Berlusconi pioneered the media-age version of virilism — sexual scandal as proof of masculine vitality rather than disqualifying weakness.

## Effectiveness / Critique

- **Gender essentialism risk**: Some feminist scholars argue the framework inadvertently naturalizes hypermasculinity by treating it as a coherent strategy rather than a contested, contingent performance.
- **Cross-cultural limits**: The virilism framework was developed with European and Latin American cases; its applicability to strongmen in different gender-norm contexts (e.g., Gulf region, East Asia) is less examined.
- **Cause vs. symptom**: Critics ask whether virilism *causes* authoritarian outcomes or is a symptom of structural conditions; Ben-Ghiat tends to treat it as a causal mechanism, which some find overstated.
- **Popular vs. scholarly reception**: *Strongmen* was widely read as a public intellectual work; some academic reviewers noted it is more comparative history than rigorous social science testing.
