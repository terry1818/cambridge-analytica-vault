# Islamophobia

## Definition

Islamophobia is fear, hatred of, and discrimination against Muslims and the religion of Islam. The word functions as an umbrella term spanning individual anti-Islam prejudice through to society-wide and structural discrimination against Muslims. In the U.S. context relevant to this vault, "Islamophobia" also names something more specific: an organized, well-funded political project — sometimes called the "**Islamophobia network**" or "Islamophobia industry" — that manufactures and amplifies the claim of an Islamic threat for political mobilization.

## Origin / history

The term appeared as "*islamophobie*" in early-twentieth-century French literature and was popularized in English in the late 1990s. Anti-Muslim sentiment in the United States long predates the September 11, 2001 attacks — but 9/11 is the hinge. The post-9/11 period saw the rise of an *organized*, transnational anti-Islamic movement: dozens of organizations were founded in the early twenty-first century with the explicit mission of preventing an alleged cultural or legal Islamic "takeover" of the West.

By the late 2000s this movement had a recognizable structure. Think tanks such as the **Center for Security Policy** (CSP), led by Frank Gaffney, positioned themselves as the movement's intellectual hubs, leveraging long-standing Washington contacts. Grassroots groups followed: **ACT for America**, founded in 2007 by Brigitte Gabriel and described by the Southern Poverty Law Center as the largest anti-Muslim grassroots group in the U.S.; Pamela Geller's Stop Islamization of America; and the David Horowitz Freedom Center. ACT launched its "Stop Shariah Now" project in 2009, promoting attorney David Yerushalmi's model anti-sharia legislation to state legislators.

The 2011 Center for American Progress report *Fear, Inc.* documented the network's funding: it identified the Bradley Foundation as providing some $5.37 million to network organizations between 2001 and 2009 (including the David Horowitz Freedom Center, the Center for Security Policy, and the Middle East Forum), and found that between 2008 and 2013 a set of 33 anti-Muslim groups had access to more than $204 million in revenue.

## How it works

The Islamophobia network operates as a mutually reinforcing system: think tanks produce "research" framing Islam as an inherent threat; activist groups translate it into grassroots campaigns (anti-sharia bills, mosque-construction opposition, "March Against Sharia" rallies); sympathetic media amplify it; and donors fund the whole. Two mechanisms matter for this vault. First, it manufactures a *threat narrative* — the claim of "creeping sharia" — that is empirically baseless but politically potent. Second, it offers the [[religious right]] and the nationalist movement a unifying enemy: anti-Muslim politics binds together religious conservatives, security hawks, and ethno-nationalists.

## Application in this domain

Islamophobia is a recurring mobilizing theme across the movements this vault tracks. Religious-right organizations including the American Family Association and Phyllis Schlafly's [[Eagle Forum]] were named among groups participating in anti-Muslim mobilization. Anti-sharia legislation spread to many state legislatures in the 2010s. The narrative fed directly into national politics — most consequentially the 2015 "Muslim ban" proposal and the 2017 travel-ban executive orders. [[Michael Flynn]], co-founder of the [[ReAwaken America]] tour, has been a prominent purveyor of anti-Muslim claims, calling Islam a "political ideology" rather than a religion. Anti-Muslim hate crime tracked these political waves: 2015 saw the highest level of anti-Muslim hate crime since 9/11. For the vault, Islamophobia is a case study in how a donor-funded disinformation infrastructure converts a manufactured threat into durable political energy.

## Examples

- **ACT for America** — largest U.S. anti-Muslim grassroots group; "Stop Shariah Now" (2009); "March Against Sharia" rallies (2017).
- **Center for Security Policy** — think-tank hub of the network.
- **Anti-sharia legislation** — model bills (Yerushalmi) introduced in many state legislatures in the 2010s.
- **2017 travel ban** — executive orders restricting entry from several Muslim-majority countries, the policy expression of the threat narrative.
- [[Michael Flynn]] — recurring purveyor of anti-Muslim rhetoric; links Islamophobia to the [[ReAwaken America]] milieu.

## Effectiveness / critique

- **Claimed basis:** Network organizations claim to expose a genuine threat of Islamic legal and cultural takeover.
- **Empirical evidence:** The "creeping sharia" claim is rejected by legal scholars as baseless; there is no documented effort to impose Islamic law in U.S. jurisdictions. The *measurable* effects of the network are real, however: spread of anti-sharia bills, elevated anti-Muslim hate crime, and policy outcomes such as the travel ban.
- **Academic critique:** Scholars and civil-rights organizations (CAP's *Fear, Inc.*, the SPLC, the Othering & Belonging Institute, Human Rights First) document the network as a coordinated, funded political project rather than a response to any real threat. Some critics of the *term* "Islamophobia" argue it can be used to shield Islam from legitimate criticism; the mainstream scholarly view treats the organized U.S. movement as documented bigotry and disinformation. `#claim/disputed` (on the term's scope, not on the network's existence)
