## Definition

Paleoconservatism is an American conservative tendency that emphasises national sovereignty, cultural and civilisational continuity, immigration restriction, trade protectionism, military non-interventionism, and opposition to the liberal "managerial state." The "paleo" prefix signals its claim to represent the authentic pre-neoconservative American right — the "Old Right" of the pre-World War II era that opposed US entry into the war and resisted Roosevelt's New Deal. The movement is explicitly contrasted with [[National Conservatism]], which it predates, and with neoconservatism, which it regards as a left-liberal infiltration of the Republican Party.

## Origin / history

The term "paleoconservative" was coined around 1986 as a polemical counter to "neoconservative." Paul Gottfried (historian, Elizabethtown College) and Thomas Fleming (editor of *Chronicles* magazine) are credited with the coinage. The triggering conflict was institutional: the Rockford Institute, a paleocon think-tank, broke with neoconservative intellectuals over editorial control of *This World* magazine, and three foundations linked to neoconservatives (Olin, Smith Richardson, Bradley) withdrew an estimated $700,000 per year in funding from the Institute as a result.

The movement claimed intellectual lineage from:
- The pre-war "America First" isolationism of Charles Lindbergh and Robert Taft
- Russell Kirk's traditionalist conservatism (*The Conservative Mind*, 1953)
- James Burnham's elite theory (adapted by [[Samuel Francis]])
- Albert Jay Nock's anti-statism

[[Pat Buchanan]]'s 1992 Republican primary challenge against George H.W. Bush brought paleoconservatism into mass electoral politics; his "culture war" RNC speech defined the movement for a wider audience. The movement's institutional home was *Chronicles* magazine (Rockford Institute, Illinois).

## How it works

Paleoconservatism operates as a critique both of the liberal left and of neoconservatism. Its core arguments:

1. **Against neoconservatism**: The neocons, many of them former Trotskyists or Great Society liberals, hijacked the Republican Party and committed it to Wilsonian democracy-promotion abroad and tolerance of the welfare state at home.
2. **Against liberal universalism**: Rights-based universalism erodes the particular cultural and civilisational traditions that make self-government possible.
3. **For the nation**: The nation-state, understood in ethnic and civilisational terms, is the proper unit of political loyalty.
4. **Against managerial elites**: Following Burnham (via Francis), the managerial class — including corporate executives, media figures, credentialed professionals, and government bureaucrats — forms a class interest hostile to the Middle American majority.

## Application in this domain

Paleoconservatism is significant to this vault as the 1990s ideological infrastructure that [[Samuel Francis]] theorised and [[Pat Buchanan]] politically operationalised. It represents the missing link between the Old Right (pre-war isolationist conservatism) and the Trump-era nationalist right. Its key concepts — the alienated white middle class, elite betrayal, cultural decline, America First — were absorbed wholesale into the 2016 Republican coalition, largely without acknowledgement of the paleocon source material. [[Steve Bannon]] and the Breitbart editorial line drew directly on Francis's writings.

## Examples

- *Chronicles* magazine (Rockford, IL) — institutional journal
- *The American Conservative* (founded 2002 by Buchanan, Scott McConnell, Taki Theodoracopulos) — more moderate paleocon outlet
- The Buchanan presidential campaigns (1992, 1996, 2000)
- [[Samuel Francis]]'s "Middle American Radicals" framework
- Murray Rothbard's later political alliances with paleocons (paleo-libertarianism)

## Effectiveness / critique

Paleoconservatism was politically marginal throughout the 1990s — its candidates never won national office, its institutions were underfunded relative to the neoconservative network, and its explicit ethno-nationalism was a liability in a post-civil-rights political culture. However, its intellectual legacy proved durable: the movement's critique of neoconservative imperialism was vindicated (to its own satisfaction) by the Iraq War, and its analysis of white working-class alienation proved analytically accurate when that constituency erupted in 2016. The critique of the "managerial class" became the standard rhetorical frame of right-wing populism globally.

Its liability, never fully resolved: the movement's racial undertones (explicit in Francis, more coded in Buchanan) made it a gateway ideology linking mainstream conservatism to white nationalism, as the [[David Duke]] endorsements demonstrated.
