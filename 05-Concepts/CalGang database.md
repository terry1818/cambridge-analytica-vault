## Definition

CalGang is California's shared statewide law-enforcement gang intelligence database, used by more than 6,000 law enforcement officers across all 58 California counties. The system allows officers to tag individuals as gang members or associates, accumulating records that can affect criminal sentencing, immigration status, employment, and housing. Unlike most law-enforcement systems, CalGang was not created by statute; it exists through administrative practice, governed by two non-transparent committees: the CalGang Executive Board and the CalGang Node Advisory Committee. As of 2016, the database contained approximately 150,000 entries.

## Origin / history

CalGang originated in the early 1990s as local law-enforcement agencies sought to share gang intelligence across jurisdictions. Because it was never established by legislation, there is no formal state oversight body, no statutory criteria for entry, and no formal right for individuals to know they are in the database or to contest their inclusion. The 2016 California State Audit, conducted by State Auditor Elaine M. Howle, produced the first systematic public accounting of the database's operations and errors.

## How it works

Officers add individuals to CalGang when they believe a person meets criteria for gang membership or association. The criteria are loosely defined and vary by agency. Once entered, individuals' data is shared across all participating agencies. The database has no meaningful sunset provision: individuals are not automatically removed when they age out of gang activity. Adults have no formal right to know they are listed, and no effective challenge mechanism existed prior to post-2016 reforms (which themselves have been documented as largely ineffective in practice).

## Application in this domain

In the LAPD context documented by [[Sarah Brayne]], CalGang functioned as a key node in the [[Secondary surveillance network]]. Individuals listed in CalGang could be added to the [[Operation LASER]] chronic-offender scoring system. The database thus bridged the legacy gang-intelligence infrastructure with the new data-driven predictive-policing stack. Brayne found that the LAPD's Palantir system aggregated CalGang data alongside commercial data-broker records, field interview cards, and license-plate-reader hits.

## Examples

- The 2016 audit found 42 individuals listed in CalGang who were supposedly younger than one year of age at the time of entry; 28 of these were entered for "admitting to being gang members."
- Entries for individuals arrested but not convicted were retained without distinction from convicted persons.
- The LAPD's own CalGang data was found to contain such significant inaccuracies that California Attorney General Xavier Becerra revoked LAPD's access to its own CalGang-generated records in 2018.

## Effectiveness / critique

The 2016 California State Audit found:
1. Weak leadership structure with no clear data-entry standards.
2. Systematic failure to substantiate entries.
3. No mechanism for individuals to challenge or correct erroneous entries.
4. No formal state oversight.
5. Significant racial disparity in entries (documented by ACLU).

The audit's "baby gang member" finding became a widely cited symbol of the database's structural dysfunction. Subsequent research by Voice of San Diego (2019) found that very few individuals had successfully removed themselves from the database despite official removal procedures theoretically being available.
