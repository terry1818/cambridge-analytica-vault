# Trahison des clercs

## Definition

*Trahison des clercs* (French: "treason of the intellectuals," or more loosely "treason of the clerks") names the phenomenon in which educated, often privileged intellectuals — who are supposed to be custodians of reason, universal values, and truth — instead defect to partisan, nationalist, or authoritarian causes, lending those causes intellectual legitimacy and cover. The concept was coined by French philosopher Julien Benda in his 1927 polemic *La Trahison des Clercs*, and was revived by Anne Applebaum in *Twilight of Democracy: The Seductive Lure of Authoritarianism* (2020) to analyse contemporary conservative intellectuals who have embraced illiberalism.

## Origin / history

**Julien Benda (1927)**: Writing a decade after the Bolshevik revolution and in the aftermath of the fascist march on Rome (1922) — but before Hitler's rise — Benda argued that European intellectuals had betrayed their vocation by subordinating universal reason to national, racial, and class passions. The *clerc* (intellectual, clerk) was supposed to transcend particularism; instead, Benda observed, they were embracing it. The book was a direct critique of figures like Charles Maurras (French nationalism), Maurice Barrès, and German nationalist academics.

**Anne Applebaum (2020)**: Applebaum revives the term in *Twilight of Democracy* as an analytic lens for the transformation of conservative intellectuals in the US, UK, Poland, and Hungary who were previously liberals or liberal conservatives and shifted to support for authoritarian-adjacent politics. Her case studies include figures she knew personally — drawn from the milieu of 1990s Atlantic-world conservatism — who moved toward Orbanism, Bannonism, or Polish Law-and-Justice nationalism. Applebaum argues the shift is not primarily explained by economic distress (these are prosperous people) but by a combination of psychological factors (need for order, resentment, social ambition) and ideological opportunism.

## How it works

Applebaum's analysis identifies several mechanisms driving the *trahison*:

1. **Ressentiment**: Some clercs feel insufficiently recognised; authoritarianism offers the prospect of prestige within a new order.
2. **Longing for clarity**: Applebaum draws on Karen Stenner's concept of the "authoritarian predisposition" — some people are psychologically uncomfortable with complexity and pluralism, and gravitating toward simpler, hierarchical narratives is attractive.
3. **Social conformity**: In environments where authoritarian politics is ascendant, intellectual endorsement becomes a career path and dissent becomes costly.
4. **Ideological substitution**: The treason involves not merely supporting a bad party but actively constructing an intellectual framework that legitimises authoritarianism — writing the philosophy, the legal theory, the history that the movement needs.

The consequential harm of *trahison des clercs* is not merely that intellectuals hold bad views, but that their participation provides institutional cover, launders dangerous ideas as serious scholarship, and helps movements achieve cultural hegemony.

## Application in this domain

The concept is analytically useful for the vault in several ways: (1) analysing the intellectual infrastructure of the right-wing authoritarian project — think tanks (Heritage Foundation, Claremont Institute, Hillsdale College), legal theorists ([[Adrian Vermeule]]'s "Common Good Constitutionalism," [[Curtis Yarvin]]'s Dark Enlightenment), and media intellectuals who provide the ideological architecture; (2) explaining the transition from respectable conservatism to illiberalism as a traceable sociological and psychological process, not merely a policy shift; (3) connecting [[Conservative complicity thesis]] (the complicity of mainstream conservatives) to the more active role of those who produce the intellectual legitimation.

## Examples

- **Interwar Europe**: German academics who endorsed Nazism (Carl Schmitt, the legal theorist, who joined the Nazi Party in 1933 — [[Carl Schmitt]] has a vault node); French intellectuals who collaborated with Vichy.
- **Contemporary Hungary**: Intellectuals and academics who provide scholarly legitimation for Orban's "illiberal democracy" — including figures who speak at international conservative conferences.
- **Claremont Institute and related**: US conservative legal and political theorists who moved from mainstream constitutionalism to Trump-era regime theory.
- **Public intellectuals**: Applebaum documents specific figures (without always naming them) among her former friends and colleagues in Warsaw, London, and Washington who shifted from liberal conservatism to authoritarian-adjacent politics.

## Effectiveness / critique

The concept has been influential and widely cited. Critiques include:

1. **Moralistic framing**: Benda's original formulation and Applebaum's revival are explicitly normative — they are condemning the clercs, not merely describing them. This can limit the concept's analytic precision.
2. **Definition of "universal values"**: The concept assumes there is a clear set of values intellectuals ought to defend; critics on the left note that what Applebaum calls universal values are sometimes the values of a particular Western liberal tradition that itself has blind spots.
3. **Agency vs. structure**: The concept foregrounds individual intellectual choices; it may underweight structural pressures (economic incentives, institutional capture, polarised media environment) that explain defection independently of individual psychology.
4. **Reverse application**: Some right-wing critics argue the concept applies equally to left-wing intellectuals who abandon liberal norms in favour of progressive orthodoxy — a charge Applebaum would likely contest as a false equivalence.
