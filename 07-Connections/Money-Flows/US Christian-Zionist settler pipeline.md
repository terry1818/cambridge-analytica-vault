# US Christian-Zionist donor pipeline to Israeli settler infrastructure

A connection note documenting the substantial US Christian-Zionist donor pipeline to Israeli settler-movement infrastructure. The pipeline operates principally through US 501(c)(3) intermediary organisations that receive US donor money (with US tax-deduction benefits) and channel it to Israeli settler-aligned recipient organisations.

## Principal intermediary organisations

The principal US 501(c)(3) intermediaries documented in this pipeline include:

- One Israel Fund (substantial settler-organisation funding intermediary)
- Friends of Yeshivat Itamar (yeshiva-focused settler funding)
- Christian Friends of Israeli Communities (CFOIC) Heartland
- Hayovel (Christian-Zionist West Bank settlement support)
- Hebron Fund (Hebron-settler-organisation funding intermediary)
- The Central Fund of Israel (substantial multi-recipient settler funding intermediary)
- Multiple additional smaller intermediaries

The aggregate annual flow through these intermediaries has been estimated in the high-tens-of-millions to low-hundreds-of-millions of dollars range, with substantial increases through 2023-2026.

## Operational pattern

The standard operational pattern:

1. US Christian-Zionist donor (individual or congregational) makes tax-deductible donation to US 501(c)(3) intermediary
2. Intermediary takes administrative fee and forwards balance to Israeli recipient organisation
3. Recipient organisation deploys funds for settler-aligned operations: settlement construction, security infrastructure, education, legal advocacy, and adjacent programming
4. The donor receives US-tax-deduction benefit; the underlying funding supports operations that US foreign-policy historically discouraged

## Documentation and accountability

The pipeline has been substantially documented by investigative reporting including [[Haaretz]] long-running investigative work, [[Mother Jones]] reporting, [[The Intercept]] reporting, and adjacent investigative journalism. The IRS 990 filings of the intermediary organisations are publicly available and have been substantially analysed.
