# Leo-CRC-Marble Freedom funding map

A pairwise connection note documenting the post-2020 [[Leonard Leo]] donor-coordination architecture: the structural relationship connecting the Barre Seid 2020-2021 [[Marble Freedom Trust]] transaction to the downstream conservative-legal infrastructure that Leo controls.

## The 2020-2021 transaction structure

Barre Seid (Chicago electronics-manufacturing principal, founder of Tripp Lite) donated his company Tripp Lite to the Marble Freedom Trust (a 501(c)(4) vehicle) in 2020. The subsequent sale of Tripp Lite to Eaton Corporation in 2021 produced approximately $1.6 billion in proceeds for Marble Freedom Trust. The (c)(4) recipient structure avoided substantial capital-gains tax liability and produced what is reportedly the largest single conservative-movement donor vehicle in US history.

The (c)(4) classification is operationally important: (c)(3) charitable vehicles face strict prohibitions on political activity; (c)(4) social-welfare vehicles can engage in unlimited political activity as long as it is not the organisation's primary purpose. The Marble Freedom Trust structure therefore gives Leo discretionary deployment authority over the largest single conservative-movement donor pool without the constraint of (c)(3) political-activity prohibitions.

## Downstream beneficiary structure

The principal downstream beneficiaries of Marble Freedom Trust funding:

- **[[Concord Fund]]** (formerly Judicial Crisis Network) — the principal judicial-nomination-advocacy vehicle. Concord Fund engages in confirmation-fight advocacy, judicial-related media buys, and the broader judicial-nomination political-pressure operation.
- **[[The 85 Fund]]** (formerly Judicial Education Project) — focused on broader legal-education and judicial-related programming. The (c)(3) parallel to the (c)(4) Concord Fund.
- **[[CRC Advisors]]** — the public-affairs and political-strategy firm Leo chairs; functions as the operational umbrella coordinating disbursement decisions and the broader strategic direction.
- Various smaller (c)(4) and (c)(3) vehicles that have proliferated under the Marble Freedom Trust funding capacity, including issue-specific advocacy organisations, legal-research operations, and state-level political-spending vehicles.

## Coordination with the broader conservative-legal infrastructure

Marble Freedom Trust funding flows complement (rather than replace) the broader conservative-legal infrastructure that the [[Federalist Society]] coordinates. The Federalist Society itself does not directly receive Marble Freedom Trust funding at scale; the Federalist Society's institutional model (membership-based, conference-and-publication-focused, explicitly not litigation-or-advocacy) does not require the large-dollar (c)(4) funding that Marble Freedom Trust provides.

What Marble Freedom Trust provides is the operational-advocacy layer that the Federalist Society's institutional model does not include. The two structures are operationally complementary: Federalist Society supplies the personnel pipeline; Marble Freedom Trust supplies the advocacy-and-confirmation-fight operational capacity.

## The 2024-2026 political-spending pattern

Leo-coordinated spending across the 2024 cycle was substantial. Federal-level disclosure shows major giving from Concord Fund and adjacent vehicles to Republican-aligned independent expenditure committees, judicial-related advocacy operations, and the broader conservative-legal infrastructure. State-level disclosure (harder to consolidate but partially available) shows additional substantial flows.

The 2024-2026 Trump 2.0 judicial nominations have continued to draw from the Federalist Society pipeline that Leo built. The post-confirmation advocacy and the broader judicial-related political-pressure operation continues to flow through the Marble Freedom Trust / Concord Fund / The 85 Fund architecture.

## Why this connection is load-bearing

The Leo-CRC-Marble Freedom architecture is the most operationally consequential single conservative-legal-infrastructure funding restructuring of the past two decades. The architecture gives a single individual (Leo) discretionary deployment authority over $1.6B+ in donor capital across multiple (c)(3) and (c)(4) vehicles, with downstream operational impact across the judicial-nomination, judicial-confirmation, and broader conservative-legal-advocacy ecosystem.

The structure also illustrates the operational power of (c)(4) recipient structures: capital that would face substantial tax liability if held personally, or substantial political-activity restriction if held in a (c)(3), can be deployed essentially without restriction through the (c)(4) architecture.
