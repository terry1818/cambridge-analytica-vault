# Catholic Integralism and New Apostolic Reformation theological convergence

A connection note documenting the theological convergence between [[Catholic Integralism]] and [[New Apostolic Reformation]] (NAR) despite the two movements' different theological starting points. The convergence is operationally significant because the two movements have substantially different theological histories, different denominational positioning, and different intellectual venues - yet have produced substantially convergent operational alignment on substantive policy positions and political-positioning frameworks.

## The theological starting points

**Catholic Integralism:** A post-Vatican-II Catholic intellectual movement that argues for explicit subordination of political authority to Catholic religious authority. Principal intellectual figures: [[Patrick Deneen]], [[Adrian Vermeule]], [[Sohrab Ahmari]], [[Edmund Waldstein]], adjacent contemporary Catholic-intellectual figures. Principal venues: [[First Things magazine]], [[Compact magazine]], adjacent Catholic-intellectual publications.

**New Apostolic Reformation:** A late-20th-century charismatic-evangelical-Protestant theological movement that articulates the Seven Mountains Mandate (Christian dominion over seven cultural spheres: government, business, education, media, religion, family, arts/entertainment). Principal intellectual figures: [[C. Peter Wagner]] (founder), [[Lance Wallnau]], [[Che Ahn]], adjacent contemporary NAR-aligned figures. Principal venues: International House of Prayer, Bethel Church, adjacent NAR-aligned ministry infrastructure.

## The convergence pattern

Despite the theological-historical differences, the two movements have produced substantially convergent operational positioning on:

**Family and gender policy:** Both movements explicitly oppose contemporary liberal-secular positioning on family structure, gender identity, sexual ethics, and adjacent social-policy categories. The substantive policy positions are substantially identical even when the theological argumentation differs.

**Religious liberty and church-state relations:** Both movements substantially oppose post-1947 (Everson) US church-state-separation jurisprudence. The substantive policy positions converge on substantial-religious-presence-in-public-institutions positioning even when the underlying theological framework differs.

**Anti-secular-liberalism intellectual frame:** Both movements articulate substantial critique of post-Enlightenment secular-liberal political philosophy. The Catholic Integralism critique operates through post-liberal Catholic intellectual tradition; the NAR critique operates through dispensationalist-charismatic theological tradition; the substantive analytical frames substantially overlap.

**Trump administration alignment:** Both movements have substantially aligned with both Trump first-administration and Trump 2.0 administration positioning. The specific theological articulation differs (Catholic Integralists emphasise post-liberal political theory; NAR figures emphasise prophetic-eschatological framing); the operational political-positioning substantially converges.

## Why this convergence matters

The convergence demonstrates that contemporary post-liberal religious-political positioning is operationally a cross-denominational programme rather than a denomination-specific intellectual tradition. The shared analytical critique and shared substantive policy positions produce operational coordination across substantial theological differences.

The convergence is also operationally distinctive because it represents the most substantial Catholic-evangelical Protestant cross-denominational coordination in US politics since the post-Roe (1973) alliance pattern that produced the contemporary Religious Right.
