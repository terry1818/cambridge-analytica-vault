# Source FCC Skydance Paramount Approval

## Citation

> Federal Communications Commission. (2025-07-24). *Order approving the transfer of Paramount Global broadcast licenses to Skydance / New Paramount*; statement of Chairman Brendan Carr. FCC. https://docs.fcc.gov/ — reported by Variety, "FCC Approves Paramount-Skydance Deal After Skydance's CBS Ombudsman, Anti-DEI Pledges."

## What it is

The FCC order (and accompanying commissioner statements) approving the transfer of Paramount Global's broadcast licenses to the merged [[Paramount Skydance]] entity. The Commission voted **2-1** on July 24, 2025. Chairman [[Brendan Carr]] and Commissioner Olivia Trusty voted to approve; Democratic Commissioner Anna Gomez dissented.

## Key claims / findings

- Approval was **conditioned on written commitments** from Skydance to ensure the new company's programming "embodies a diversity of viewpoints from across the political and ideological spectrum."
- Skydance pledged to **employ an ombudsman for two years** at CBS to evaluate complaints of bias.
- Skydance committed to **eliminate "invidious forms of DEI discrimination"** in hiring — language echoing Carr's broader anti-DEI campaign.
- The approval came **22 days after** Paramount settled President Trump's lawsuit over a *60 Minutes* interview, prompting accusations that the FCC review was leverage.
- Commissioner Gomez's dissent warned the conditions amounted to government control over newsroom editorial decisions.

## Notable quotes

## Reliability assessment

- Primary government document — Tier-1 for the order's contents and vote.
- Independent corroboration: Variety, Reuters, Senator Adam Schiff's August 2025 oversight letter all describe the conditions identically.
- Known biases / agenda: The order is a government text; the Carr statement is interpretive. Critics (Gomez, Schiff, press-freedom groups) contest the conditions as viewpoint coercion. `#claim/disputed` — whether the conditions constitute unconstitutional editorial control is contested.
- Disputed by: FCC majority frames conditions as benign; dissent and First Amendment scholars frame them as coercive.
