# Baylor Religion Survey

## Citation

> Baylor University Institute for Studies of Religion. (2005–2021). *Baylor Religion Survey, Waves I–VI*. Baylor University. https://baylorreligionsurvey.research.baylor.edu/

## What it is

The Baylor Religion Survey (BRS) is a recurring, nationally representative survey of religious belief, behavior, and identity in the United States, conducted by the Baylor University Institute for Studies of Religion (ISR) in partnership with the Gallup Organization. It has been fielded in six waves: 2005 (Wave I), 2007 (Wave II), 2010 (Wave III), 2014 (Wave IV), 2017 (Wave V), and 2021 (Wave VI). Each wave runs a long instrument covering religious affiliation and practice, images of God, supernatural belief, moral attitudes, politics, and — increasingly in later waves — conspiratorial thinking and Christian nationalism. Because it repeats core items across nearly two decades, the BRS supports trend analysis as well as cross-sectional study, making it one of the most valuable quantitative instruments for studying American religion over time.

The BRS belongs in this vault as a *source-instrument*: it is the empirical evidence base for many quantitative claims about how widespread Christian nationalist belief is, who holds it, and how it correlates with political behavior and conspiracy belief. It is one of the principal data sources behind the academic study of [[Christian nationalism]].

## Key claims / findings

- **Christian nationalism prevalence (Wave VI, 2021):** Approximately 21% of Americans can be classified as "strong Christian Nationalists"; roughly 36% are "strong Rejectors" of Christian nationalism — establishing it as a minority but substantial orientation.
- **Christian nationalism as a predictor:** Wave VI found that beliefs about whether the U.S. government should promote Christianity strongly predict attitudes toward social movements, conspiracy theories, the COVID-19 pandemic, and perceived "threats to American unity."
- **Out-group threat perception:** Strong Christian nationalists are substantially more likely to view Muslims, atheists, and Buddhists as threats to national unity — quantifying the exclusionary dimension of the orientation.
- **Conspiratorial thinking:** Wave VI documented a measured rise in conspiratorial belief and found religiosity and conspiratorial belief to be linked along specific dimensions.
- **Earlier waves** established baseline measures of God-images, supernatural belief, and the religious-political correlation that later researchers built on.

## Reliability assessment

- **Primary or secondary?** Primary — it is an original data-collection instrument, not a commentary on other sources.
- **Independent corroboration:** BRS findings on Christian nationalism are broadly consistent with other instruments, notably the Whitehead–Perry work using separate survey data ("Taking America Back for God") and PRRI's Christian nationalism surveys; convergence across independent instruments strengthens reliability.
- **Known biases / agenda:** Baylor is a Baptist university, and its ISR has been criticized at the margins for question framing; however, the survey is methodologically rigorous, Gallup-fielded, and widely used by scholars across institutions. The "strong Christian Nationalist" classification depends on where analysts set thresholds — a methodological judgment, not a contested fact.
- **Disputed by:** No serious dispute of the data itself; debates concern interpretation and the operational definition of "Christian nationalism."
