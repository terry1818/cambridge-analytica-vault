## What it is

A community-maintained encyclopedia article on the [[Institutional Revolutionary Party]] (Partido Revolucionario Institucional, PRI), the Mexican party that governed the country continuously from 1929 to 2000. The article aggregates party history, electoral practices, and decline into one narrative. It serves as a navigational secondary source for the comparative one-party-dominance thread — a model of authoritarian rule maintained through nominal elections.

## Key claims / findings

- The PRI was founded in 1929 as the National Revolutionary Party (PNR), became the Party of the Mexican Revolution (PRM) in 1938, and adopted the PRI name in 1946.
- It was founded by Plutarco Elías Calles, the post-revolutionary "Jefe Máximo."
- The party held uninterrupted power for 71 years (1929–2000), the longest continuous single-party rule of any party in the 20th century.
- Under President Lázaro Cárdenas it built a mass corporatist base of peasants and workers, sustaining one-party dominance into the 1980s.
- It is described as having relied on electoral fraud, exclusion of opposition poll watchers, nepotism, jailing of critics, press suppression, and political violence.
- Its 71-year rule ended in 2000 when PAN's Vicente Fox defeated PRI candidate Francisco Labastida.
