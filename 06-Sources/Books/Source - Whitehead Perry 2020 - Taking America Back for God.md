## Thesis & scope
Whitehead and Perry define Christian nationalism as "a cultural framework — a collection of myths, traditions, symbols, narratives, and value systems — that idealizes and advocates a fusion of Christianity with American civic life." Their central argument is that this framework — not merely partisan affiliation or even theological conservatism — is the most powerful predictor of a wide range of political attitudes, including views on immigration, Muslims, gun control, police shootings, atheists, and gender roles. The book is the foundational empirical study of the phenomenon: it uses multiple waves of national survey data (drawn over decades) and in-depth qualitative interviews to operationalize, measure, and analyze Christian nationalism as a distinct cultural-political ideology.

## Key content relevant to the vault
**Four-typology framework (core methodological contribution):**
Whitehead and Perry construct a 24-point "Christian Nationalism Scale" based on six survey statements (e.g., "The federal government should declare the United States a Christian nation," "The federal government should advocate Christian values," "The federal government should allow prayer in public schools"). They classify Americans into four orientations:
- **Ambassadors** (19.8%) — strongly embrace Christian nationalism; believe the US was founded as a Christian nation and government should actively advocate Christian values
- **Accommodators** (32.1%) — generally supportive; comfortable with some fusion of Christian identity and national identity
- **Resisters** (26.6%) — skeptical; value religious pluralism
- **Rejecters** (21.5%) — strongly oppose; advocate strict separation of church and state

Total supportive of CN (Ambassadors + Accommodators): ~51.9% of Americans at time of survey

**Key empirical findings:**
- Christian nationalism is a better predictor of Trump support in 2016 than other evangelical or conservative religious measures
- CN predicts: opposition to immigration, negative views of Muslims, support for authoritarian leadership, tolerance of police violence against Black Americans, opposition to same-sex marriage, support for traditional gender roles
- CN adherents are disproportionately white, but the framework is not identical to racial resentment — the book carefully disaggregates the two
- CN is turning people away from Christianity: the politicization of faith is driving religious disaffiliation, particularly among young people

**Definitional precision:**
- Christian nationalism is distinguished from: (a) political conservatism, (b) evangelical identity, (c) theological orthodoxy — this conceptual precision is essential for the vault's analytical work
- CN is a "cultural framework," not a theology — it functions as an identity-and-boundary-maintenance system independent of doctrinal commitment

**Implications documented:**
- CN provides ideological cover for racial hierarchy by framing white Christian culture as the normative American culture
- CN legitimizes authoritarian leadership styles and undermines democratic norms of pluralism and equal treatment
- CN is the ideological infrastructure that connects the theocratic wing's religious agenda with the patron wing's political agenda
