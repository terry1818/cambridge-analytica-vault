# Source — When the Clock Broke: Con Men, Conspiracists, and How America Cracked Up in the Early 1990s (John Ganz, 2024)

## Bibliographic
John Ganz. *When the Clock Broke: Con Men, Conspiracists, and How America Cracked Up in the Early 1990s*. Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2024. 400 pp. ISBN 978-0374605445.

## Thesis & scope
The early 1990s — not 2015 — is when American political culture cracked. Ganz argues that Pat Buchanan's 1992 primary challenge, Ross Perot's independent campaign, David Duke's electoral career, and the rage energized by Crown Heights, the LA riots, and recession together normalized paleoconservative nationalism, conspiracist thinking, and racial grievance politics that would eventually produce MAGA. Trump did not invent American authoritarian populism; he inherited it from a decade that had already crossed a threshold.

The book is narrative and biographical: Ganz follows specific actors through specific events rather than offering a general theory. Historiographically it sits alongside Levitsky/Ziblatt and Stanley on backsliding but is focused on the American far-right's *pre-history* rather than on institutional erosion.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**People covered in depth:**
- **Pat Buchanan** (paleoconservative politician, NOT the economist James M. Buchanan): 1992 Republican primary challenger; delivered "culture war" speech at Houston GOP convention; argued for immigration restrictionism, anti-globalism, and civilizational nationalism. His "America First" slogan and rhetoric are the direct antecedent of Trump's.
- **David Duke**: Louisiana politician; former KKK Grand Wizard who ran in 1988 (Democrat) and 1990–91 (Louisiana Senate and gubernatorial races) and 1992 Republican presidential primary; demonstrated mainstream viability of white nationalist electoral politics.
- **Ross Perot**: Texas billionaire who ran 1992 independent presidential campaign on economic nationalism, NAFTA opposition, and conspiratorial suspicion of establishment; received ~19% of popular vote. His campaign showed the depth of alienated "Middle American Radical" sentiment.
- **Rush Limbaugh**: radio broadcaster who transformed AM talk radio into a vehicle for right-wing mobilization and conspiratorial framing during the early 1990s.
- **Samuel Francis**: paleoconservative intellectual who theorized "Middle American Radicals" (MARs) — the economically anxious, culturally conservative working-class whites who felt dispossessed by both parties. His framework directly anticipates Trumpism.

**Events and phenomena:**
- Crown Heights (1991): anti-Black/anti-Jewish riots in Brooklyn; exploited by right-wing media.
- 1992 LA riots: triggered by Rodney King verdict; used by paleocons to argue for civilizational decline.
- Pat Buchanan's Houston "culture war" speech (1992 GOP convention): explicit framing of American politics as a "religious war" and "cultural war."
- Heartland survivalist movements, militia culture, and anti-government sentiment in early 1990s as precursors to the 1990s militia boom and Oklahoma City context.
- Neoconservative vs. paleoconservative split: internal right-wing intellectual war that the paleocons lost institutionally but whose ideas percolated into the base.

**Key concepts Ganz uses:**
- "Middle American Radicals" (MARs): Samuel Francis's term for the social base of proto-Trumpism.
- Paleoconservatism as distinct intellectual tradition from neoconservatism.
- Conspiracism as a mode of political explanation normalized by mainstream media figures.
