# Source — White Too Long: The Legacy of White Supremacy in American Christianity (Robert P. Jones, 2020)

## Bibliographic
- Author: Robert P. Jones (founder and president, Public Religion Research Institute / PRRI; former Southern Baptist; author of *The End of White Christian America*, 2016)
- Publisher: Simon & Schuster, 2020
- ISBN: 978-1982122867
- 320 pp.; winner of the 2021 American Book Award

## Thesis & scope
Jones argues that white American Christians — across all denominations, not just evangelicals in the South — have not merely been passive bystanders to white supremacy but have been its active constructors and sustainers. The book's scope is explicitly pan-denominational: evangelical Protestants, mainline Protestants, and Catholics are all implicated. Jones uses PRRI survey data, historical evidence, and personal memoir (as a white Southern Baptist) to demonstrate that white Christian denominational identity correlates with racial resentment and opposition to racial-justice policies even when controlling for other variables. The book's goal is not reconciliation but reckoning — Jones argues that genuine change requires naming the complicity and reforming institutions, not merely apologizing.

## Key content relevant to the vault
**Empirical foundation (PRRI data):**
- Survey evidence showing that white Christians — across Catholic, mainline Protestant, and evangelical categories — score higher on racial resentment scales than white Americans who are religiously unaffiliated
- Christians in former slaveholding states tend toward higher racial resentment, political conservatism, and opposition to affirmative action — a structural rather than merely attitudinal correlation
- The "warmth gap": white Christians report feeling warm toward Black Americans but oppose equitable policies; Jones documents this as a structural feature of white Christian racial formation

**Historical evidence:**
- Basil Manly Sr. — slavery-apologist theologian and Southern Baptist; Jones uses him as a case study in the theological construction of racial hierarchy
- First Baptist Church, Jackson, Mississippi — profiled as a white supremacist institutional hub; hosted segregationist governors and was a center of pro-segregation activism and propaganda in the civil rights era
- White denominational churches as institutional supports for Jim Crow: Jones documents specific churches, ministers, and denominational bodies that provided theological cover for segregation
- The Southern Baptist Convention's 1845 founding as a defense of slaveholder rights — the denominational origin as white supremacist founding moment

**Conceptual contributions:**
- "White too long" — the argument that white Christian institutions have benefited from white supremacy for so long that it is structurally embedded in their culture, property, and theology
- The expanded definition of white supremacy: not just overt racism but the institutional and intellectual channels through which whites justify disproportionate benefits — relevant to the vault's analysis of the patron class
- Pan-denominational scope: the argument that this is not uniquely a Southern evangelical problem but a feature of white American Christianity broadly

**Personal dimension:**
- Jones's own formation as a white Southern Baptist; his account of growing up in a white church during the civil rights era provides evidential texture
