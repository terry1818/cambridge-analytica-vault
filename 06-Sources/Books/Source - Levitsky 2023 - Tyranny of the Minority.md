# Source — Tyranny of the Minority: Why American Democracy Reached the Breaking Point (Steven Levitsky & Daniel Ziblatt, 2023)

## Bibliographic
Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt; *Tyranny of the Minority: Why American Democracy Reached the Breaking Point*; Crown (Random House); September 2023. 336 pp. ISBN 9780593443071.

Sequel/companion to *How Democracies Die* (2018). Both authors are professors of Government at Harvard University.

## Thesis & scope
Where *How Democracies Die* focused on behavioral norms, *Tyranny of the Minority* focuses on institutional structure. The US has an exceptional concentration of counter-majoritarian veto points — the Electoral College, Senate malapportionment plus the filibuster, lifetime Supreme Court appointments with judicial review — that allow a persistent electoral minority to block majority preferences and even hold power without winning majorities. The Republican Party, having become an ethnic-minority party as the US diversifies, has responded not by broadening its coalition but by exploiting these veto points to entrench minority rule. The book compares the US unfavorably to peer democracies, proposes ten reforms, and argues five constitutional amendments are necessary.

## Key content relevant to the vault

- **Counter-majoritarian veto-point inventory**: The book systematically catalogues: (1) the Electoral College allowing presidential candidates to win with a minority of votes; (2) Senate apportionment giving Wyoming voters ~70x the representation of California voters; (3) the filibuster requiring 60 Senate votes to pass legislation; (4) lifetime Supreme Court appointments insulated from electoral change; (5) the Article V amendment process requiring 3/4 of states, allowing 13 states representing ~4% of the population to block change.

- **The GOP as "ethnic outbidding" party**: Drawing on comparative cases of parties in ethnically diversifying democracies, Levitsky and Ziblatt argue the Republican Party has followed a pattern common in other countries — abandoning democracy rather than broadening its coalition when demographic change threatens its majority status. This is documented through white-share decline data and electoral math.

- **The Republican Party's "semi-loyal democrat" posture**: Building on *How Democracies Die*, the book argues that most Republican leaders are "semi-loyal democrats" — they verbally endorse democratic norms but enable and embolden authoritarian behavior. The January 6 response is the primary US case study.

- **Comparative case: Chile under Allende (1970–73)**: The military coup against Salvador Allende is analyzed as the paradigm case of a constitutional minority (the right, with institutional veto power) destroying democracy rather than accepting majoritarian outcomes. This is the book's central cautionary analogy.

- **Comparative case: Hungary under [[Viktor Orban|Viktor Orbán]]**: Orbán's constitutional capture after winning a supermajority in 2010 is the paradigm case of a majority using institutional entrenchment to lock in permanent power. Levitsky and Ziblatt present this as the mirror image of the US problem but equally instructive.

- **Comparative case: Brazil and [[Jair Bolsonaro]]**: Bolsonaro is analyzed as a case where independent courts proved decisive in containing populist authoritarianism — the "activist courts" comparison to the US.

- **Comparative case: Republican Spain, Weimar Germany, Third Republic France**: Historical cases of democracies that failed to constrain minority-veto exploitation.

- **Proposed five constitutional amendments**: (1) abolish the Electoral College; (2) reform Senate representation; (3) abolish the filibuster; (4) reform Supreme Court appointments; (5) lower the Article V amendment threshold. These are framed as necessary to bring the US into line with modern peer democracies.

- **Ten policy reforms short of amendment**: Including term limits for Supreme Court justices (18-year terms), statehood for DC and Puerto Rico, automatic voter registration, and others.

- **The US as global outlier**: Extensive comparative data showing that among wealthy democracies, the US has the highest concentration of counter-majoritarian veto points and the highest degree of minority-rule capacity.

- **Article V's role as a veto on reform**: The book treats the Article V ratification threshold as the meta-veto — the mechanism that makes all other structural reforms nearly impossible. This directly intersects with the vault's COS thread (the counter-COS argument is that the convention route is dangerous precisely because the Article V amendment threshold has made the congressional route unavailable).

- **The "racial realignment and norm breakdown" connection**: The book deepens the *How Democracies Die* argument that racial desegregation disrupted the artificial consensus that had sustained democratic norms, linking demographic anxiety to Republican radicalization.
