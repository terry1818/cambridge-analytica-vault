# Source — Strongmen: Mussolini to the Present (Ruth Ben-Ghiat, 2020)

## Bibliographic
Ruth Ben-Ghiat; *Strongmen: Mussolini to the Present*; W. W. Norton & Company; 2020.

## Thesis & scope
Ben-Ghiat, a historian of fascism at NYU, argues that authoritarian leaders across the 20th and 21st centuries draw from a common operational playbook regardless of their ideological coloring, national context, or era. The book is organized around four core "tools of rule" — propaganda, virility, corruption, and violence — and traces their use across approximately 17 rulers from Mussolini (1922) to the present. The central thesis is that these tools are functionally interlinked, not independent: strongman corruption is enabled by virilism (he is above the law because he is the supreme masculine figure); propaganda normalizes both corruption and violence; violence enforces the hierarchy that propaganda constructs. Ben-Ghiat also provides a structural analysis of how strongmen come to power and how they lose it — a three-part arc (ascent, tools of rule, endings) that is explicitly prescriptive for resistance movements.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**The Four Tools of Rule**:

- **Propaganda** — not merely communication management but the construction of a reality in which the ruler is the nation's only legitimate interpreter. Ben-Ghiat examines the use of film, rally aesthetics, and media capture. Mussolini pioneered the model; modern strongmen adapt it to social media and owned media ecosystems.

- **Virility / masculine performance** — hyper-masculine self-presentation as political capital. The strongman performs physical dominance, sexual prowess, and martial identity to signal that he is above normal rules. Institutionalized misogyny is structural, not incidental: it signals the hierarchy of domination that extends from gender to all social relations. This connects natalist policies, anti-LGBTQ legislation, and cult-of-personality performance.

- **Corruption** — not a bug but a feature. Corruption binds elites to the ruler because they become complicit; it also signals to followers that the ruler is genuinely powerful (he can break laws others must follow). Ben-Ghiat specifically examines how rulers often pursued public office *while under investigation* as a form of legal self-defense — immunity through power.

- **Violence** — paramilitaries, targeted killings, and state violence used strategically, not randomly. Ben-Ghiat examines how violence is calibrated: enough to terrorize critics, structured to preserve deniability, and used to test the limits of institutional resistance.

**The Three-Part Structural Arc**:
- *Getting to Power*: fascist takeovers, military coups, and electoral-path new authoritarians — the vault's Trump model fits the third category.
- *Tools of Rule*: the four-tool playbook in operation.
- *Losing Power*: assassination, exile, military revolt, democratic resistance, or natural death — and what resistance strategies have worked historically.

This connects the NAR's gender theology, MAGA's performative masculinity, and the authoritarian playbook.

## Evidence tier
Tier-2. Ben-Ghiat is a recognized historian of Italian fascism and authoritarianism (NYU); the book is grounded in substantial primary-source historical research on Mussolini and Hitler and solid secondary research on contemporary cases. Contemporary case analyses (Trump, Bolsonaro) involve real-time judgment calls that are inherently less settled than historical cases. Nonetheless, the four-tool framework is well-supported across cases and widely adopted in authoritarian-studies scholarship.
