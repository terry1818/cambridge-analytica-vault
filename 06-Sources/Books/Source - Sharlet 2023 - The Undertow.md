# Source — The Undertow: Scenes from a Slow Civil War (Jeff Sharlet, 2023)

## Bibliographic
Jeff Sharlet; *The Undertow: Scenes from a Slow Civil War*; W. W. Norton; 2023. National Book Critics Circle Finalist for Nonfiction; NYT Bestseller; NYT 100 Notable Books of 2023.

## Thesis & scope
Sharlet argues that American conservatism has undergone a qualitative shift: reaction has morphed into delusion, social division into paranoia, and paranoia into fantasized and sometimes actual violence. The book is a literary travel narrative spanning roughly 2020–2022, reporting from Trump rallies, Ashli Babbitt memorial gatherings, NAR megachurches, militia circles, and self-declared prophet networks. Its central claim is that a significant fraction of the American right now treats US democracy itself as an existential threat to God's order — and that this is not fringe but infrastructurally backed.

## Key content relevant to the vault
- Documents the **martyrdom cult around Ashli Babbitt** — the January 6 insurrectionist killed at the Capitol — as a live organizing phenomenon, drawing on memorial services and online networks Sharlet attended.
- Reports from inside a **televangelist megachurch in Miami** (described as preaching material prosperity while glorifying civil war as spiritual cleansing) and a **self-declared prophet network in Omaha** — demonstrates that NAR-adjacent prophetic culture has spread well beyond the flagship apostolic networks.
- Profiles Christian nationalist men — armed, organized, and framing civil war as divine absolution — as a mobilized constituency, not a random fringe; documents explicit civil-war rhetoric at prayer meetings, gun shows, and Trump rallies.
- Shows [[Sean Feucht]]'s "Let Us Worship" tour functioning as recruiting infrastructure for a new charismatic nationalist movement, blending [[New Apostolic Reformation]] worship aesthetics with MAGA political identity.
- Documents [[Michael Flynn]]'s prophet-warrior circuit appearances, where Flynn frames himself as a divinely appointed military leader fighting a spiritual war.
- Tracks the **Appeal to Heaven flag** (the Pine Tree flag repurposed by [[Dutch Sheets]] and the NAR) as an ambient visual marker at rallies, linking insurrectionist networks to NAR spiritual-warfare theology.
- Extends Sharlet's earlier Fellowship/Family reporting: shows how the elite C-Street model of "Jesus plus power" has popularized downward — the same theology now animates working-class armed militia culture, not just Senate corridors.
- Examines how the "slow civil war" frame circulates within these communities as prophecy-validated inevitability, not as metaphor.
