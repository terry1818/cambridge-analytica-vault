# Source — The Attention Merchants: The Epic Scramble to Get Inside Our Heads (Tim Wu, 2016)

## Bibliographic
Wu, Tim. *The Attention Merchants: The Epic Scramble to Get Inside Our Heads*. Alfred A. Knopf, 2016. 417 pp.

Tim Wu is a Columbia Law School professor, former chair of the FTC's Technology Committee, and coiner of the term "net neutrality." He served on the National Economic Council 2021–2023.

## Thesis & scope
Wu's argument is that the attention economy — the commercial logic of harvesting human attention and reselling it to advertisers — is not a new phenomenon born of the internet, but is the structural principle that has organized American commercial media since the nineteenth century. Every major media platform innovation — penny newspapers, radio, broadcast television, cable, and finally the internet and social media — followed the same model: offer free (or cheap) content to generate audience, then sell that audience to advertisers. The result is a culture of chronic attentional colonization in which human consciousness is treated as a productive resource to be extracted. Wu argues that Facebook and Google represent the most advanced and total version of this logic yet achieved, but they are continuous with, not a break from, Benjamin Day's New York Sun.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**Benjamin Day and the penny press (1830s).** Wu begins with Day's New York Sun as the founding case of the attention-merchant model: by selling papers for a penny (below production cost), Day generated a mass audience that advertisers would pay to reach. The subsidy model — content paid for by advertisers, not readers — established the template.

**Claude Hopkins and patent medicine advertising.** Hopkins (former evangelical preacher turned advertising copywriter) developed the first systematic techniques for emotional persuasion in advertising in the early twentieth century, applying a preacher's rhetorical arsenal to selling patent medicines. Wu frames this as the first weaponization of psychological understanding against audience attention.

**World War I propaganda.** Britain's Lord Kitchener and the US Committee on Public Information under George Creel are analyzed as the first large-scale demonstrations of attention capture at the population level. George Creel ran a network of 75,000 "Four Minute Men" (volunteer speakers) and distributed 75 million pamphlets — the first modern government propaganda campaign. [[Edward Bernays]], Freud's nephew and a Creel Committee veteran, then applied these techniques to commercial advertising.

**Edward Bernays as founding figure.** Wu gives Bernays an extended treatment as the figure who consciously synthesized Freudian psychology and wartime propaganda into commercial persuasion — the intellectual ancestor of Cambridge Analytica's psychographic model. Bernays's 1928 book *Propaganda* is cited as the foundational text. This connects directly to the vault's [[Edward Bernays]] entry.

**Nazi Germany as extreme case.** Wu devotes a chapter to Joseph Goebbels's Reich Ministry of Public Enlightenment and Propaganda as the limit case of total attention control — combining entertainment, mandatory listening, and political content, preventing competition in the attention market by force.

**Radio and NBC/CBS duopoly.** The creation of the NBC and CBS radio networks is analyzed as the first instance of commercial attention capture at national scale in real time, establishing the advertising-supported broadcast model.

**Television and soap operas.** The book traces how Procter & Gamble (named) literally invented the soap opera format as an advertising vehicle — programming designed to hold women's attention between commercial messages. This is an early case of content produced not for its audience's benefit but for its advertiser's.

**The internet turn.** Wu analyzes Google's AdWords and Facebook's News Feed advertising as the logical endpoint of the century-long attention-merchant project — now operating at individual-level personalization and 24/7 engagement extraction. The combination of infinite content supply, behavioral tracking, and algorithmic optimization makes social platforms the most efficient attention merchants in history.

**Backlash cycles.** Wu identifies recurring "moments of revulsion" in which audiences rebelled against attention colonization — the Radio Acts of the 1920s, the 1960s television debates, and ad-blocking adoption in the 2010s — as evidence that the attention merchants always overreach and generate political resistance.
