# Source — No Democracy Lasts Forever: How the Constitution Threatens the United States (Erwin Chemerinsky, 2024)

## Bibliographic
Erwin Chemerinsky, *No Democracy Lasts Forever: How the Constitution Threatens the United States* (W. W. Norton, 2024). Chemerinsky is founding dean of UC Berkeley School of Law, one of the most-cited constitutional law scholars in the United States.

## Thesis & scope
The Constitution of 1787 cannot be adequately reformed through the Article V amendment process because the same counter-majoritarian features that distort democracy also control the amendment procedure. Chemerinsky catalogs specific structural defects — the Electoral College, equal-state Senate, lifetime Supreme Court tenure, filibuster, undersized House, and partisan gerrymandering — and argues that only a new constitutional convention producing a replacement document can resolve them. He is unusual among liberal constitutional scholars in endorsing a convention as the solution, which puts him in uncomfortable proximity to the Article V / Convention of States movement the vault documents — though his convention would be a replacement convention, not a limited amendments convention.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**Electoral College.** Two presidential elections since 2000 (2000, 2016) produced winners who lost the popular vote. The structure gives small, predominantly white rural states disproportionate weight. Chemerinsky traces this to slave-state compromises at the founding — an origin the vault's counter-majoritarian thread should register.

**Senate malapportionment.** Wyoming (≈580,000 people) has the same two senators as California (≈39 million). In the first two years of the Biden presidency, 50 Democratic senators represented 42 million more Americans than 50 Republican senators. Chemerinsky presents this as the single most distorting structural feature of American governance — enabling patron-class minority legislative control.

**Supreme Court lifetime tenure.** Average tenure has grown from roughly 15 years in the founding era to 26+ years now. Chemerinsky calls for 18-year staggered terms with one appointment per presidential term, which would reduce the stakes of each confirmation battle and partially de-politicize the Court. He connects the current crisis to the [[Federalist Society]] pipeline that the vault documents.

**Filibuster.** The 60-vote Senate threshold is not in the Constitution; it is a Senate rule. Chemerinsky argues it is a primary mechanism for minority veto over majority legislation and should be abolished. No major legislation on voting rights, climate, or gun safety can pass while it exists.

**Undersized House.** The House has been capped at 435 members since 1929. Chemerinsky argues that doubling or tripling House size would reduce the per-district population, making gerrymandering less effective and each representative more accountable to a smaller, more coherent constituency.

**Gerrymandering and the *Rucho* problem.** Following *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019), federal courts cannot police partisan gerrymandering, leaving it entirely to state courts and state constitutions. Chemerinsky sees this as a deliberate choice by the Roberts Court to ratify minority-rule entrenchment.

**The amendment paradox.** Article V requires two-thirds of both chambers and three-fourths of states to ratify any amendment. This means 13 states representing roughly 4% of the US population can block any constitutional change. The same small-state structural advantage that distorts elections protects the distortion from correction. This is directly relevant to the vault's Article V / COS thread: the COS movement claims Article V as democratic, but Chemerinsky shows its supermajority thresholds are themselves anti-majoritarian.

**The replacement convention proposal.** Chemerinsky calls for a new convention to draft an entirely new constitution — not a limited amendments convention. He distinguishes this from the COS/balanced-budget Article V convention, which he criticizes as dangerous because a convention cannot be reliably limited to its stated purpose (the "runaway convention" problem the vault already documents). This is a significant analytical node: the same Article V mechanism that the patron class seeks to use for fiscal and social conservative entrenchment is the mechanism Chemerinsky says would need to be invoked to fix democracy — but in a form that could itself be captured.
