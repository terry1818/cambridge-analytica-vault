# Source — One Nation Under God: How Corporate America Invented Christian America (Kevin M. Kruse, 2015)

## Bibliographic
Kruse, Kevin M. *One Nation Under God: How Corporate America Invented Christian America*. Basic Books, 2015. 352 pp. ISBN 978-0-465-04949-3.

## Thesis & scope
The idea of the United States as a formally Christian nation is not ancient. It was manufactured between roughly 1935 and 1955 by a deliberate alliance between corporate executives opposed to the New Deal and a cadre of entrepreneurial clergymen willing to frame free-market capitalism in Christian language. The institutional products of that alliance — the National Prayer Breakfast, "In God We Trust" as official motto, "under God" in the Pledge — are recent inventions serving a political agenda, not recovered traditions. Kruse's evidence base is primarily archival: NAM records, Spiritual Mobilization papers, Eisenhower White House files, Billy Graham's papers.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**Core figures**
- **James Fifield Jr.** (Congregationalist minister, First Congregational Church Los Angeles): the central organizational figure. Fifield founded **Spiritual Mobilization** (1935), recruited corporate sponsors from NAM and the oil industry, and built a network of thousands of pro-business clergy who preached what Kruse calls "Christian libertarianism" — the doctrine that state welfare programs were un-Christian violations of individual freedom. Fifield received substantial funding from oil executives including J. Howard Pew.
- **[[Abraham Vereide]]**: Methodist minister who separately built elite prayer breakfast networks for business executives and Congressmen from the 1930s onward. Vereide connected Eisenhower's circle to what became the **National Prayer Breakfast** (1953) and is the founder of what the vault knows as [[The Fellowship]]. Kruse documents his role as the bridge between corporate patrons and Washington political ritual.
- **Billy Graham**: the mass popularizer of Christian libertarianism. Kruse documents Graham's close relationship with corporate executives (including Hearst), his early anti-New Deal messaging, and his later shift as he managed the transition from Eisenhower-era civic religion to the partisan Moral Majority era.
- **Dwight Eisenhower**: the political instrument who institutionalized the manufactured Christian America — adding "under God" to the Pledge (1954), establishing "In God We Trust" as the official motto (1956), making the National Prayer Breakfast a permanent government ritual. Kruse argues Eisenhower was personally not deeply religious but understood the political utility.

**Organizations**
- **Spiritual Mobilization** (1935–1961): NAM-funded clergy network; precursor structure to every later conservative religious mobilization.
- **Freedom Under God / "Breakfast Groups"**: Vereide's elite Congressional prayer circles, direct ancestor of [[The Fellowship]] / C Street.
- **National Association of Manufacturers**: the corporate funder behind Fifield; also key in Phillips-Fein's account, demonstrating NAM's dual role in both ideological and religious manufacturing.

**Mechanisms**
The core mechanism is **corporate subsidy of religious messaging** as an alternative to direct political advocacy — using tax-exempt clergy to say what executives could not say politically. This is the 1940s version of the 501(c)(3) dark-money model documented in the vault for [[DonorsTrust]] and the [[Council for National Policy]]. Kruse also documents the backlash mechanism: after Eisenhower, the merger of Christianity and conservatism made mainline Protestants and liberal clergy uncomfortable, accelerating the politicization of religion in both directions.

## How it maps to the vault
The vault's [[Religious Right MOC]], [[Abraham Vereide]], [[The Fellowship]], [[Moral Majority]], and [[Jerry Falwell Sr.]] nodes cover the 1970s-onward Religious Right but are thin on the corporate-origin story of the 1930s–1950s. Kruse provides the institutional genealogy explaining *why* the Religious Right took the specific form it did — Christian libertarianism rather than social gospel — and *who funded it*. The Fifield/Spiritual Mobilization thread is entirely absent from vault. The Kruse-Vereide account also deepens the existing [[Abraham Vereide]] node significantly.
