# Source — The Contrarian: Peter Thiel and Silicon Valley's Pursuit of Power (Max Chafkin, 2021)

## Bibliographic
Max Chafkin; *The Contrarian: Peter Thiel and Silicon Valley's Pursuit of Power*; Penguin Press; 2021. ISBN 978-1984878533. New York Times Notable Book of the Year 2021. Chafkin is a features editor at Bloomberg Businessweek; the book draws on 150+ interviews, many on background given subjects' fear of Thiel's retribution.

## Thesis & scope
Chafkin argues that Peter Thiel is the single person most responsible for creating Silicon Valley's defining ideology — the belief that technological progress should be pursued relentlessly with little regard for societal costs — and for connecting that ideology to an explicitly anti-democratic political project. The book is simultaneously a biography, an intellectual history of Silicon Valley's political coming-of-age, and a warning about a billionaire who uses litigation, dark money, and political patronage to insulate himself from accountability. Central claim: Thiel's power flows from a coherent worldview (monopoly-seeking, anti-democratic, millenarian) that he has systematically built into institutions.

## Key content relevant to the vault

- **Ideological formation at Stanford**: Thiel co-founded the *Stanford Review* (1987) as a vehicle for conservative and anti-multicultural politics. His 1995 co-authored book *The Diversity Myth* (with David Sacks) attacked Stanford's multiculturalism. Chafkin documents Thiel's intellectual radicalization — he viewed Stanford's liberalism as "uniquely despicable, maybe even dangerous."
- **PayPal (1998–2002)**: co-founded with Max Levchin, Luke Nosek, Ken Howery, and others. Thiel's vision: PayPal would "end government control over money" — explicitly framed as a libertarian political project. The "PayPal Mafia" alumni network (Reid Hoffman, Elon Musk, Keith Rabois, David Sacks) becomes Thiel's primary social capital.
- **[[Palantir Technologies]] (2003–present)**: Thiel co-founded Palantir with [[Alex Karp]] (CEO) after 9/11, explicitly to commercialize government surveillance and data-fusion. Early client: CIA. Michael Flynn — later Trump's National Security Adviser — made "an urgent request to the Department of Defense to buy enough Palantir licenses for the entire force in Afghanistan" (2010). H.R. McMaster also an enthusiastic early champion. Palantir's business with US government grew dramatically under Trump. Chafkin documents Palantir's 2016 lawsuit against the US Army (for failing to allow it to bid on a contract), which ultimately won Palantir an $800 million Army contract — a model for using litigation as market capture.
- **Facebook investment (2004)**: Thiel invested $500,000 for ~10% of Facebook, mentored Mark Zuckerberg in the "Roman Empire" growth-at-any-cost strategy, and helped engineer the corporate structure giving Zuckerberg permanent control. Thiel remained on Facebook's board through the book's publication despite fundamental ideological conflict with the platform's direction. In 2017, Thiel invested in **Clearview AI** — a facial recognition company scraping Facebook photos and real names to build a police database.
- **[[Founders Fund]]**: Thiel's venture capital vehicle; co-investors include Keith Rabois, Ken Howery, Luke Nosek. Portfolio includes [[Palantir]], SpaceX, and numerous other tech-right enterprises.
- **[[Mithril Capital]]**: Thiel-associated VC where [[JD Vance]] worked before his Senate run; Thiel gave $10 million in PAC support to Vance's 2022 Ohio Senate campaign.
- **Gawker litigation**: Gawker outed Thiel as gay in 2007. Thiel spent approximately $10 million over years secretly funding Hulk Hogan's invasion-of-privacy lawsuit, ultimately winning a $140 million judgment that bankrupted Gawker Media in 2016. Chafkin documents this as a decade-long, methodically planned campaign of legal destruction — not a hot-tempered response.
- **Alt-right network building**: Thiel's sustained association with anti-democratic provocateurs including [[Curtis Yarvin]] (Mencius Moldbug, the neoreactionary blogger), Charles Johnson, and Milo Yiannopoulos. Chafkin documents these as deliberate alliances, not casual associations.
- **Trump 2016**: Thiel was the only major Silicon Valley figure to publicly back Trump; spoke at the 2016 Republican National Convention. Chafkin documents Thiel's role as "shadow president" during Trump's 2017 transition — agitating for "maximal disruption within the White House." His affinity with the Trump administration was a business asset: Palantir's federal contracting soared.
- **[[Blake Masters]]**: co-author (with Thiel) of *Zero to One* (2014); ran as Thiel-backed Republican Senate candidate in Arizona 2022. Thiel donated to Masters' campaign.
- **Josh Hawley**: Thiel made a series of campaign donations (2015–2017); Hawley then launched legal investigations into Google — Thiel's "greatest corporate bête noire."
- **Thiel Fellowship**: Thiel's $100,000 grants to young people to drop out of college and build companies — explicitly anti-university, part of Thiel's campaign against higher education.
- **Roth IRA exploitation**: Thiel used a Roth IRA to shelter investment gains — a tax vehicle designed for middle-income retirement savings, used to shelter billions in venture capital gains through a loophole allowing pre-IPO shares to be placed in it at near-zero value.
- **Anti-democracy thesis**: Thiel's 2009 Cato Unbound essay: "I no longer believe that freedom and democracy are compatible." The women's suffrage and welfare state, he wrote, had made it harder for libertarians to win. Chafkin treats this as Thiel's operating philosophy, not a throwaway provocation.
- **Seasteading Institute**: Thiel provided seed funding for [[Patri Friedman]]'s Seasteading Institute — the project to build sovereign societies on the high seas outside democratic oversight. Direct connection to Slobodian's zones thesis.
- **Surveillance state contractor paradox**: Chafkin notes the core contradiction — Thiel is a self-described libertarian whose main company (Palantir) is the pre-eminent government surveillance contractor. Chafkin's reading: for Thiel, government power is acceptable when it is exclusionary and coercive (immigration enforcement, counterterrorism) and unacceptable when it is redistributive.
