# Source — Means of Control: How the Hidden Alliance of Tech and Government Is Creating a New American Surveillance State (Byron Tau, 2024)

## Bibliographic
Byron Tau; *Means of Control: How the Hidden Alliance of Tech and Government Is Creating a New American Surveillance State*; Crown; 2024.

## Thesis & scope
The US government, unable to legally compel collection of mass commercial data, instead purchases it from a sprawling private sector of data brokers — circumventing the Privacy Act of 1974 and the Fourth Amendment's third-party doctrine. The alliance formed after 9/11 when intelligence and law enforcement agencies discovered that consumer data companies like [[Acxiom]] had already assembled richer dossiers on Americans than any government agency. The book traces this partnership from 2001 through the early 2020s, documenting specific agencies, vendors, and legal architectures that enabled what Tau calls a "new American surveillance state built by corporate America and blessed by government lawyers."

## Key content relevant to the vault
- **Acxiom** (Conway, Arkansas): profiled as the paradigmatic early vendor; had eleven of the nineteen 9/11 hijackers in its database before the attacks. Invited to White House demonstrations in 2001 alongside Seisint.
- **Seisint** (later acquired by LexisNexis/RELX): built the "MATRIX" (Multistate Anti-Terrorism Information Exchange) law enforcement data fusion system post-9/11.
- **Neustar, Venntel, X-Mode, UberMedia, Premise Data**: location-data brokers whose products were purchased by US government agencies including [[DHS]], [[FBI]], [[CBP]], [[NSA]], and [[NGA]] (National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency).
- **Accurint** (LexisNexis subsidiary): used by [[TSA]] for ID verification behind the scenes.
- **"Bad Guys Database"**: an early FBI-era product from commercial data brokers; Tau describes it as amounting to racial profiling of Arab Muslim men.
- **Smith v. Maryland** (1979) and **Carpenter v. United States** (2018): the legal framework the government exploits; third-party doctrine allows warrantless purchase of data that would require a warrant if directly collected.
- **Privacy Act of 1974**: drafted for a world where government held its own records; inapplicable to government subscriptions to commercial platforms.
- **Fourth Amendment Is Not For Sale Act**: bipartisan congressional effort to close the loophole; had not passed as of writing.
- **Google advertising IDs / Apple IDFAs**: unique device identifiers assigned by platforms, purchased by agencies to track movement without warrants.
- **NSA's break-in to Google's internal network** (Muscular program, pre-Snowden): noted as context for the government's parallel track of direct intrusion alongside commercial purchase.
- **9/11 Commission finding** that US intelligence was "built in a different era to confront different dangers" — used as political justification for the commercial data turn.
- **National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (NGA)**: used Twitter geotags for humanitarian operations (tsunami aid) — early dual-use example.
- **IRS, Department of Veterans Affairs**: among civilian agencies subscribing to data broker platforms for fraud detection.
- [[Palantir]] is contextually referenced as the downstream aggregator/analyzer of this purchased data.
