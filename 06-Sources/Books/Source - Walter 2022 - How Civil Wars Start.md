# Source — How Civil Wars Start: And How to Stop Them (Barbara F. Walter, 2022)

## Bibliographic
Barbara F. Walter. *How Civil Wars Start: And How to Stop Them*. Crown, 2022. 272 pp. ISBN 978-0593137796. Walter is Professor of International Relations at UC San Diego and served on the CIA's Political Instability Task Force.

## Thesis & scope
Modern civil wars do not start as conventional military conflicts between armies — they begin as insurgencies, guerrilla conflicts, and terrorism, typically in countries that are neither fully democratic nor fully autocratic (a condition Walter calls "anocracy"). The second necessary condition is ethnic or factional political mobilization: when one of the major political parties becomes an identity-based faction rather than an ideology-based coalition, the door to civil violence opens.

Walter applies this framework to the United States and argues that the country's Polity score declined from +10 (full democracy) to approximately +5 (anocracy) during the Trump years, and that the Republican Party's transformation into a faction organized around white Christian identity fulfills the second condition. She does not predict an imminent conventional civil war but warns of sustained political violence, insurgency, and terrorism — the form modern civil wars actually take.

Walter draws on the data and methods of her CIA advisory work and comparative civil war research (she has studied dozens of post-1945 civil conflicts).

## Key content relevant to the vault

**The anocracy thesis:**
The Polity IV scale measures democratic quality from -10 (full autocracy) to +10 (full democracy). Countries in the +6 to -6 range are "anocracies" — partial democracies in which executive constraints are eroding. Civil wars are most likely in this middle zone, not in full autocracies or full democracies. The US moved from +10 to approximately +5 by Walter's assessment at time of writing (2021–22).

**Ethnic/factional factionalism:**
The second risk factor is when citizens begin to identify primarily with an ethnic, racial, or religious group rather than with civic/ideological categories, and when that group-identity maps onto partisan politics. Walter argues the Republican Party has become a faction organized around white Christian identity.

**Downgrading:** The Polity IV score decline was largely driven by the January 6 events and the erosion of institutional constraints on the executive. Walter notes the Freedom House and Polity assessments as objective indicators.

**The "sons of the soil" model:**
In many civil wars, the conflict arises when a previously dominant group perceives itself as losing status and territory to an out-group. Walter applies this to the demographic anxiety of white rural Americans who perceive themselves being displaced by immigration, urban growth, and demographic change. The "son of the soil" insurgent is the paradigm.

**Accelerants:**
- Social media as a radicalization accelerant: platforms amplify grievance and organize isolated individuals into networks; Walter cites the role of social media in facilitating January 6 planning.
- The "Deciders" — Walter's term for elites who choose whether to allow a violent movement to proceed or to contain it; Republican legislators who declined to hold Trump accountable after January 6 are her primary example of "Deciders" who chose passivity.
- Militia movements: the vault has [[Oath Keepers]], [[Proud Boys]], [[Three Percenters]], and [[Patriot Front]] — Walter's framework contextualizes these as the organizational infrastructure of a potential insurgency.

**International cases used:**
Northern Ireland, former Yugoslavia (Slobodan Milošević as the canonical "ethnic entrepreneur"), Rwanda (Hutu Power radio as radicalization infrastructure), Iraq post-2003, Syria 2011.

**Slobodan Milošević** as the paradigm "ethnic entrepreneur": a politician who deliberately inflamed ethnic identity to build a political base, then found he could not control what he had unleashed. Walter draws explicit analogies to Trump.

## Evidence tier
Tier-2: Walter is a credentialed political scientist with direct experience advising the CIA's Political Instability Task Force. The comparative civil war research she draws on is peer-reviewed. The application to the US involves predictive claims about a situation still unfolding — treat those as risk assessments, not predictions. The Polity score data is third-party and verifiable. Some critics argue she overstates the civil-war risk by applying frameworks designed for weaker states to the US; that critique should be noted when using this source.
