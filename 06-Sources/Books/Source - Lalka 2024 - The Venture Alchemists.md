# Source — The Venture Alchemists: How Big Tech Turned Profits into Power (Rob Lalka, 2024)

## Bibliographic
- **Author:** Rob Lalka (Albert R. Lepage Professor in Business, Tulane University; Executive Director, Albert Lepage Center for Entrepreneurship and Innovation)
- **Publisher:** Columbia University Press, 2024
- **ISBN:** 9780231210263
- **Research method:** Uses leaked documents and previously unpublished archival material alongside public record

## Thesis & scope
Lalka argues that the toxic structural features of Big Tech — surveillance business models, power concentration, contempt for regulation, displacement of democratic accountability — were not accidental emergent properties but were seeded in the founding moments of the major firms. By recovering the original, often disavowed, documents and decisions from each company's earliest days, Lalka shows that founders' youthful fears, ambitions, and moral compromises created path dependencies that persist. The book covers Meta/Facebook, Alphabet/Google, Palantir, and other major firms as case studies.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**Facebook / Meta origin.** Lalka documents that Mark Zuckerberg launched Facemash in October 2003 as a Halloween prank — coding it with images he stole from Harvard's servers, primarily of underage women freshmen without their consent. This is framed not as youthful mischief but as structurally predictive: non-consent, surveillance of women, unauthorised data extraction, and contempt for institutional limits are all features of Facebook's later product decisions, not departures from them.

**Google / Alphabet origin.** Sergey Brin and Larry Page's foundational 1998 Stanford thesis explicitly warned against a corporate-advertising model for search engines, arguing it would corrupt results and mislead users. Google then built exactly that model. Lalka uses this as evidence that the founders understood the harm they were creating and chose growth anyway — making the later harms a product of deliberate compromise rather than ignorance.

**Palantir origin.** Lalka traces [[Palantir Technologies]]' origin to a campus controversy at Stanford over hateful speech and [[Peter Thiel]]'s response to it — framing Palantir's adversarial stance toward civil liberties protections as foundational rather than instrumental. The chapter ("A New Crusade") links Thiel's early ideological commitments to Palantir's later role in government surveillance infrastructure.

**General mechanism: "alchemist" model.** The book's central metaphor is that venture capital performed an alchemical transformation: converting raw profit into political power, institutional capture, and regulatory immunity. This is distinct from normal market consolidation — it is specifically about tech firms using capital to purchase the conditions of their own deregulation.

**Leaked/archival material.** Lalka claims to draw on documents not previously in the public record for some of these origin stories; these are Tier-2 claims that require cross-referencing where possible.
