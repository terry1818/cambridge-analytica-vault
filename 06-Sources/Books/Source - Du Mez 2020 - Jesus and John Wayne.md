# Source — Jesus and John Wayne: How White Evangelicals Corrupted a Faith and Fractured a Nation (Kristin Kobes Du Mez, 2020)

## Bibliographic
Kristin Kobes Du Mez; *Jesus and John Wayne: How White Evangelicals Corrupted a Faith and Fractured a Nation*; Liveright Publishing Corporation (W. W. Norton); 2020.

## Thesis & scope
Du Mez, a historian at Calvin University, argues that white evangelical support for Donald Trump was not a betrayal of evangelical values but their fulfillment: decades of deliberate cultural production — books, music, conferences, films, merchandise — had systematically replaced the "gentle Jesus" of the Gospels with a militant, patriarchal warrior-Christ modeled on the archetype of John Wayne. The book is a 75-year sweep (roughly 1949–2020) tracing how "militant masculinity" became the cultural center of white evangelicalism, making evangelical culture structurally receptive to authoritarian political leadership. This is the foundational scholarly account of the *cultural* mechanism by which Christian nationalism was reproduced across mass audiences.

## Key content relevant to the vault
- **Billy Graham** (from 1949 onward) — documents Graham's Cold War alignment with US militarism and his cultivation of the "muscular Christianity" frame as evangelicalism's entry point into political culture.
- **[[James Dobson]]** and **[[Focus on the Family]]** — Du Mez's deepest single-subject analysis. Documents how Dobson built a multi-decade cross-denominational political base through parenting advice, disciplinary theology, and explicit patriarchal family structure as a political model. Focus on the Family becomes the template for how evangelical cultural infrastructure doubles as political mobilization infrastructure.
- **Bill McCartney** and **Promise Keepers** — documents Promise Keepers (founded 1990) as a mass-mobilization men's movement that combined stadium events, militant masculinity theology, and racial reconciliation rhetoric to build evangelical organizational capacity. By the late 1990s Promise Keepers was drawing hundreds of thousands to Washington DC. No vault entry for either McCartney or Promise Keepers.
- **Tim LaHaye** — vault has a person note; Du Mez documents LaHaye's *Left Behind* series and his co-founding of the [[Council for National Policy]] (1981) as a dual cultural/political impact: *Left Behind* mainstreamed apocalyptic militarism in evangelical mass culture while CNP built the infrastructure.
- **Ed Cole and the Christian Men's Network** — documents Cole as the originator of the specific masculine-warrior discipleship model that Dobson, McCartney, and eventually Driscoll all drew on. No vault entry.
- **John Eldredge** and *Wild at Heart* — evangelical bestseller (2001) that Du Mez documents as a pivotal mass-market text constructing the wild-warrior Christ who resonates with Trump's alpha-male persona. No vault entry for Eldredge.
- **Mark Driscoll** and **Mars Hill Church** (Seattle, 1997–2014) — Du Mez's longest case study. Documents how Driscoll constructed the "cage-stage Calvinist" brand of hyper-masculinist Christianity that became a national template for the young evangelical hard-right, with Mars Hill planting churches nationally. Driscoll's organizational collapse (2014) did not kill the model; it spread. No vault entry for Driscoll or Mars Hill.
- **Militarism and torture endorsement** — Du Mez documents evangelical organizations' explicit endorsement of Abu Ghraib-era military torture, framed as righteous masculine force, as a data point in the militarist cultural formation.
- **Oliver North** as evangelical hero — documents North's rehabilitation and lionization in evangelical culture as a warrior-for-God archetype after Iran-Contra, demonstrating how evangelical cultural infrastructure can absorb and sanctify criminal political actors.
- **The commodification of militant masculinity** — Du Mez shows how the evangelical industrial complex (publishing, conferences, music, merchandise) commercially incentivized the production of militant masculinity content, creating a self-reinforcing market feedback loop independent of theology.
- Documents that Trump's 2016 appeal to white evangelicals is *structurally explained* by this 75-year cultural formation — evangelical voters were not hypocrites; they were behaving in complete alignment with their media-formed values.
