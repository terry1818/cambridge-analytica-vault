# Source — Let Them Eat Tweets: How the Right Rules in an Age of Extreme Inequality (Jacob S. Hacker & Paul Pierson, 2020)

## Bibliographic
Hacker, Jacob S., and Paul Pierson. *Let Them Eat Tweets: How the Right Rules in an Age of Extreme Inequality*. Liveright / W. W. Norton, 2020. 304 pp. ISBN 978-1-63149-684-4.

## Thesis & scope
Since the early 1980s, the Republican Party has governed almost exclusively for economic elites — radical tax cuts, union destruction, deregulation, and financial-sector capture — while winning and holding working-class white votes through escalating racial, cultural, and disinformation appeals. The authors call this combination "plutocratic populism." The book's central analytical concept is the **Conservative Dilemma**: in a democracy with universal suffrage, a party serving concentrated wealth must either expand its program or suppress the ability of the majority to constrain it. The GOP has chosen the latter path with increasing urgency as inequality deepens.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**The Conservative Dilemma (structural argument)**
The core claim is not new (it echoes Schumpeter and Lindblom) but Hacker and Pierson quantify it: as the top 0.1% share of income rises, the preference gap between economic elites and median voters widens, making genuine economic populism impossible for a plutocratic party. The solution is to substitute identity and cultural mobilization for economic appeal, and to use counter-majoritarian institutional levers — gerrymandering, voter suppression, Senate malapportionment, Electoral College, judicial veto — to insulate plutocratic policy from electoral arithmetic.

**Plutocratic wing: the donor class**
- **[[Koch Network]]** and [[Americans for Prosperity]]: central to the supply of plutocratic policy; the book documents AFP's role in killing Obama-era climate and healthcare legislation despite majority public support.
- **[[DonorsTrust]]** and the dark-money infrastructure: the vehicle for funding without accountability.
- **Business Roundtable** and the Chamber of Commerce: the organized-business side of the coalition that has consistently blocked labor and redistributive policy since the 1970s.
- **Donor summits** (Koch seminars): the mechanism for coordinating policy demands across the donor class.

**Populist wing: racial and cultural backlash**
- Systematic documentation of the post-1965 Southern Strategy's evolution from Nixon-era "dog whistles" to Trump-era explicit white identity mobilization.
- **Fox News** as the media infrastructure that makes the populist wing functional: the authors treat Fox not as a news outlet but as a radicalization and coordination mechanism, essential to maintaining the illusion of shared purpose between donors and base.
- **NRA** as a case study in how a single-issue organization becomes a vehicle for cultural identity politics, serving donor interests (blocking gun liability legislation) while activating a base.

**Counter-majoritarian institutional capture**
The book is particularly strong on the mechanisms by which the GOP has made policy reversals structurally difficult: Federalist Society judicial capture, Senate supermajority requirements, Electoral College bias toward low-population (whiter, more rural) states, and the use of reconciliation to pass tax cuts but require 60 votes for social spending. This directly illuminates the vault's coverage of [[Federalist Society]], [[Leonard Leo]], and [[Heritage Foundation]] operations.

**"Organizational combat"**
Hacker and Pierson argue that the asymmetry between left and right is not primarily ideological but organizational: the right built a coordinated, funded, patient infrastructure (what the vault tracks as the patron-class apparatus) while the left remained fragmented by unions and single-issue advocacy groups without cross-cutting coordination.

## How it maps to the vault
This book provides the best single political-science framework for *why* the three-wing coalition (patron/populist/theocratic) is structurally stable despite internal tension. The vault documents the *what* (Koch, Mercer, DonorsTrust, Heritage, Project 2025) while Hacker and Pierson explain the *why it works*: plutocratic policy is hidden behind populist appeals that exploit the counter-majoritarian features of US institutions.
