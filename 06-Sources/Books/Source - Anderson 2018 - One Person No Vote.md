# Source — One Person, No Vote: How Voter Suppression Is Destroying Our Democracy (Carol Anderson, 2018)

## Bibliographic
Carol Anderson, *One Person, No Vote: How Voter Suppression Is Destroying Our Democracy* (Bloomsbury, 2018). Anderson is Charles Howard Candler Professor and Chair of African American Studies at Emory University; author also of *White Rage* (2016). Longlisted for the National Book Award; finalist for PEN/John Kenneth Galbraith Award.

## Thesis & scope
The 2013 *Shelby County v. Holder* decision removed the preclearance requirement that had restrained the most discriminatory state voting laws since 1965. In the five years following *Shelby*, Republican-controlled states implemented a comprehensive architecture of racially targeted voter suppression — photo ID requirements, mass voter-roll purges (including Interstate Crosscheck), polling-place closures, felon disenfranchisement, and restrictions on registration assistance. Anderson documents each mechanism state by state, arguing this is not incidental but a deliberate response to demographic change — specifically the rise of a Black, Latino, and young-voter majority coalition demonstrated in 2008 and 2012.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**The Shelby pivot.** Within hours of the June 2013 ruling, Texas announced a previously-blocked strict photo ID law. Within weeks, North Carolina enacted an omnibus voting restriction law (HB 589) covering photo ID, early voting cuts, same-day registration elimination, and out-of-precinct voting restrictions. The 4th Circuit later struck down HB 589, finding it targeted Black voters "with almost surgical precision" — a phrase Anderson quotes centrally.

**Photo ID laws.** Anderson documents that the IDs most likely to be absent from Black, Latino, elderly, and low-income voters — student IDs from public universities (not always accepted), non-driver state IDs, government-issued housing assistance IDs — are precisely the categories of ID excluded by state photo ID laws while hunting licenses and gun permits are included. This pattern of inclusion/exclusion is documented as racially intentional.

**Interstate Crosscheck.** Kris Kobach, Kansas Secretary of State (and Trump's Presidential Advisory Commission on Election Integrity co-chair), promoted the Interstate Crosscheck program, which matched voter rolls across states to detect duplicate registrations. The program used first and last name only — no middle name, no date of birth matching — producing error rates up to 99% among legitimately registered voters of color (common Latino and Black surnames produced mass false matches). Anderson documents that 7.2 million voters were flagged for removal in one cycle. In a Kansas court case, only 43 non-citizens out of 1.8 million registered voters were found — confirming fraud claims were fabricated.

**Brian Kemp in Georgia.** Kemp, while serving as Secretary of State and simultaneously running for governor in 2018, oversaw the "exact match" law requiring voter registration data to match driver's license or Social Security records character-for-character (hyphens, accent marks, spacing). This resulted in 340,000 voter registrations put on hold, 70% of them Black voters, in the weeks before the 2018 election. He refused to recuse from overseeing the election he was contesting.

**John Husted in Ohio.** Ohio's "use it or lose it" purge process removed voters who had not voted in two consecutive federal elections. The Supreme Court upheld it in *Husted v. A. Philip Randolph Institute* (2018). Anderson documents the racially disparate impact across urban Ohio counties.

**Felon disenfranchisement.** Florida's permanent disenfranchisement of 1.5 million citizens with felony convictions — disproportionately Black men — is documented as a legacy of post-Reconstruction suppression maintained through the 20th century. Florida voters passed Amendment 4 restoring voting rights in 2018; the legislature then required payment of all fines and fees before restoration (effectively a poll tax).

**Polling-place closures.** Anderson documents systematic closure of polling locations in Black-majority precincts in Georgia, Texas, and Arizona following *Shelby*, using long lines and travel burdens as de facto barriers. In Maricopa County, Arizona, the number of polling places dropped from 200 in 2012 to 60 in 2016.

**Resistance.** The book closes with organizing efforts: Stacey Abrams's voter registration work through [[Fair Fight]] / New Georgia Project, NAACP litigation, and the 2017 and 2018 special election results as evidence that suppression does not guarantee Republican victory when turnout organizing is mobilized.
