# Source — Illiberal America: A History (Steven Hahn, 2024)

## Bibliographic
Steven Hahn. *Illiberal America: A History*. W. W. Norton, 2024. 448 pp. ISBN 978-0393635928.

## Thesis & scope
The standard narrative of American history tells a story of progressive expansion of liberal values — from slavery to emancipation, from exclusion to inclusion. Hahn argues this narrative is structurally misleading. Running alongside and frequently dominating the liberal tradition is a persistent *illiberal* current animated by fixed hierarchies of race, religion, gender, and ethnicity; by communal will overriding individual rights; and by violent exclusion of perceived threats to the community. January 6, MAGA, and Trumpism are not aberrations — they are the latest expression of this recurrent tradition. The book is a counter-history: not a history of "when liberalism failed" but a history of illiberalism as a durable political tradition with its own internal logic and social base.

Scope: roughly 1790s to 2021, organized thematically by era and movement rather than chronologically. Hahn is a Pulitzer Prize-winning historian (for *A Nation Under Our Feet*, 2003, on Black politics in the South).

## Key content relevant to the vault

**Eras and phenomena Hahn traces:**

*Early republic / Jacksonian era:*
- Expulsions of Black Americans, Native Americans, Catholics, Mormons, and abolitionists in the 1830s; the "democracy" of Jacksonian America was illiberal in its enforcement of white Protestant communal norms.
- Know-Nothing Party (1840s–50s): nativist, anti-Catholic, anti-immigrant mass movement that briefly became a major electoral force; direct ancestor of later nativist movements.

*Post-Civil War / Reconstruction:*
- Southern "Redeemer" governments' violent dismantling of Reconstruction; the systematic denial of constitutional guarantees by terrorist organizations including the KKK.
- The KKK as a paramilitary political organization, not merely a hate group; Hahn analyzes the Women of the Ku Klux Klan as accounting for nearly half of Klan membership in some states — "KKK feminism."

*Progressive Era:*
- Eugenics as an "almost across-the-board" commitment among Progressive reformers; the convergence of scientific racism and liberal reform.
- Immigration restriction movement culminating in the 1924 Immigration Act (Johnson-Reed Act), which established national-origin quotas explicitly designed to reduce Southern and Eastern European (and ban Asian) immigration.

*Interwar / 1930s:*
- American fascist movements (overlapping with Maddow/Prequel): Hahn situates these within the longer illiberal tradition rather than treating them as German imports.

*Postwar / civil rights era:*
- George Wallace: honed a cross-regional language of anti-federal grievance and "states' rights" that built a national coalition outside the South; Wallace's 1968 third-party run got 13.5% of the popular vote and 46 electoral votes.
- Richard Nixon's Southern Strategy: the Republican Party's explicit decision to capture Wallace's coalition by translating overt racism into coded policy language.
- "Law and order" as coded illiberal discourse.

*Contemporary:*
- The Tea Party movement as illiberalism's most recent pre-Trump mass mobilization.
- January 6 as continuous with, not discontinuous from, the historical tradition of violent resistance to democratic outcomes perceived as threatening to the illiberal community.

**Key analytical concepts:**
- **Illiberal tradition**: not a single movement but a recurring pattern defined by (a) fixed hierarchies, (b) communal will over individual rights, (c) violent exclusion of perceived threats.
- **"KKK feminism"**: women's organized participation in KKK as an illiberal feminist project — protecting white Protestant womanhood.
- **Coding**: the translation of explicitly racial/religious illiberal claims into facially neutral policy language (Nixon; Reagan; Tea Party).

## Evidence tier
Tier-2: Hahn is a peer-reviewed academic historian with a Pulitzer. The book covers an enormous historical span, which means individual chapters are necessarily synthetic rather than primary-research-based. The core thesis (illiberalism as a persistent tradition) is a historiographical argument, not a contested empirical claim per se — but historians disagree about how to weight it against the liberal tradition. Use specific historical episodes (Know-Nothings, KKK, 1924 Act, Wallace) as Tier-2; the overarching "illiberal tradition" framing as an analytical lens to test evidence against.
