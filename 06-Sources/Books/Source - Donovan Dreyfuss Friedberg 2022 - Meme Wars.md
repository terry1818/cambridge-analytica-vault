# Source — Meme Wars: The Untold Story of the Online Battles Upending Democracy in America (Joan Donovan, Emily Dreyfuss & Brian Friedberg, 2022)

## Bibliographic
Donovan, Joan, Emily Dreyfuss, and Brian Friedberg. *Meme Wars: The Untold Story of the Online Battles Upending Democracy in America*. Bloomsbury Publishing, September 2022. 336 pp.

Authors: Joan Donovan (PhD; then Research Director, Harvard Kennedy School Shorenstein Center); Emily Dreyfuss (tech journalist, Shorenstein Technology and Social Change team); Brian Friedberg (cultural ethnographer, Shorenstein Technology and Social Change team).

## Thesis & scope
Meme Wars argues that the strategic deployment of internet memes — images, phrases, rituals, and in-group symbols — became the primary organizational and propaganda infrastructure of the contemporary far-right. This is not a story about foreign bots or platform algorithms: it is about communities of human actors who learned to use memetic spread as a weapon against liberal institutions, legacy media, and democratic norms. The book traces the playbook from Gamergate (2014) through the Trump 2016 campaign to Pizzagate, QAnon, and the Stop the Steal movement, concluding with the January 6, 2021 Capitol attack. It is the first book-length account of how Stop the Steal moved from online spaces to real-world insurrection.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**Named actors and operations.** The book gives sustained attention to: Alex Jones (Infowars as meme-distribution infrastructure); Milo Yiannopoulos (alt-right bridge between fringe internet and mainstream conservatism); [[Nick Fuentes]] (white nationalist meme warfare); [[Roger Stone]] (tactical connector between online far-right and formal political campaigns); and the broader ecosystem of 4chan, Reddit, and Telegram channels that generated and spread content.

**Gamergate as template.** The authors treat Gamergate (2014) as the laboratory for techniques later scaled to political disinformation: coordinated harassment campaigns, strategic use of "free speech" framing, manufacturing outrage to force mainstream media coverage, and building cross-platform networks that mutually amplified content. This is a named origin story for methods the vault tracks in later operations.

**Stop the Steal.** The book provides the most detailed public account of how the "Stop the Steal" narrative was seeded online before Election Day 2020, amplified through meme communities, and converted into the January 6 mobilization. [[Roger Stone]] is identified as a connector between online communities and the formal political apparatus. Ali Alexander is named as the primary organizer of the physical Stop the Steal network.

**QAnon mechanics.** The book analyzes QAnon not as a static conspiracy but as a participatory meme game — followers were recruited through gamified revelation of "drops," creating an in-group with strong identity investment in the narrative. This is distinct from passive consumption of disinformation.

**Shorenstein Center research context.** The book draws on ethnographic access to online communities — Friedberg embedded in forums and channels — as well as media manipulation case study research from the Harvard Shorenstein Center's Technology and Social Change (TaSC) team.

**Platform complicity.** The authors document how Facebook, YouTube, and Twitter's recommendation algorithms systematically amplified far-right meme content not because of operator instruction but because it generated higher engagement metrics. This connects to the vault's Facebook and platform-power threads.
