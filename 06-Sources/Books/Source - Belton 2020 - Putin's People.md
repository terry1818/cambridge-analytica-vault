# Source — Putin's People: How the KGB Took Back Russia and Then Took On the West (Catherine Belton, 2020)

## Bibliographic
Catherine Belton; *Putin's People: How the KGB Took Back Russia and Then Took On the West*; Farrar, Straus and Giroux; 2020.

## Thesis & scope
Belton's central argument is that Putin and a tight cohort of former KGB/FSB officers — the *siloviki* — never ceded real power during the Yeltsin era, used the chaos of the 1990s to embed themselves in criminal networks and resource industries, then systematically recaptured the Russian state after 1999. The book traces how this group replaced the oligarchs of the Yeltsin era with loyalist ones, seized Russia's hydrocarbon wealth, and subsequently projected money, influence operations, and political leverage deep into Europe and the United States. It is based on 15+ years of Financial Times Moscow reporting and on-the-record interviews with insiders, oligarchs, and Western intelligence figures.

**Libel context:** Between 2021 and 2022, Rosneft and four oligarchs (Roman Abramovich, Mikhail Fridman, Petr Aven, and Mikhail Gutseriev) filed or threatened libel proceedings in UK courts. HarperCollins settled with Fridman and Aven (minor wording amendments to four paragraphs and a footnote; no damages, no admission of defamation). All named litigants have since been sanctioned by the UK/EU following the 2022 invasion. The core thesis and evidentiary claims were not retracted.

## Key content relevant to the vault

- **[[Vladislav Surkov]] and the Ozero Cooperative**: The *Ozero* dacha cooperative (founded 1996) is the organisational nucleus — Putin, Yury Kovalchuk, Vladimir Yakunin, Nikolai Shamalov, Gennady Timchenko, and others pooled assets; the cooperative's bank account could be accessed by all members, creating a plausibly deniable conduit for passing money to Putin.
- **[[Vladimir Putin]] and the Tambov gang**: Security for the Ozero cooperative was provided by a company controlled by the Tambov criminal organisation (aka Tambovskaya Bratva), led by Vladimir Barsukov (Kumarin); Putin's period as deputy mayor of St. Petersburg overlapped with his documented dealings with the gang, including facilitating their monopoly over the city's fuel-distribution network.
- **Bank Rossiya** as the Kremlin's financial hub: Yury Kovalchuk used Bank Rossiya as the principal vehicle through which the Ozero circle accumulated and moved assets; Sergei Roldugin, Putin's cellist friend, served as a nominee shareholder — in 2010 a Roldugin-linked company bought Bank Rossiya shares for $6.50/share, sold them days later for 32x that price, then Gazprom paid 169x the original price for the same shares; confirmed through Panama Papers cross-reference.
- **Igor Sechin and Yukos**: Sechin, then Putin's deputy chief of staff and a KGB alumnus, drove the prosecution of [[Mikhail Khodorkovsky]] (2003–2004) and the forced dismemberment of Yukos; Rosneft, chaired by Sechin, acquired Yukos assets for below-market prices through a rigged bankruptcy proceeding.
- **Gennady Timchenko**: Studied German alongside Putin in KGB training; built a $14 billion fortune through Gunvor oil trading, which Belton argues functioned as a revenue-extraction vehicle for the Putin circle.
- **Roman Abramovich** and Chelsea FC: Belton argues the 2003 Chelsea purchase was directed by the Kremlin as a soft-power/influence operation in the UK, not simply a personal business decision — Abramovich contested this claim (the principal libel action against Belton personally, not settled; Abramovich was sanctioned in 2022 before the case was resolved).
- **Kremlin penetration of the West**: Belton documents the use of London as a money-laundering and political-influence hub ("Londongrad"), including the role of UK PR firms, law firms, and accountants in laundering reputational as well as financial assets.
- **Active measures updated**: Belton frames the Western penetration as a continuation of Soviet KGB active-measures doctrine, now executed through oligarchic capital flows, sports, media ownership, and funding of European far-right parties — including connections to Marine Le Pen's party financing from a Russian-linked bank.
