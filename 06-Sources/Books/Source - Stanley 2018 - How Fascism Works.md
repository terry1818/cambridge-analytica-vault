# Source — How Fascism Works: The Politics of Us and Them (Jason Stanley, 2018)

## Bibliographic
Jason Stanley; *How Fascism Works: The Politics of Us and Them*; Random House; 2018.

## Thesis & scope
Stanley, a Yale philosopher of language and propaganda, argues that fascism is not primarily an ideology but a *political method* — a strategy for acquiring and holding power by replacing truth with myth, manufacturing in-group unity through out-group demonization, and dismantling the institutional conditions for liberal democracy. He identifies ten recurring structural features ("pillars") of fascist politics drawn from historical cases (Nazi Germany, Fascist Italy, Mussolini's Italy) and contemporary movements, arguing that these features appear in early-stage fascism before any formal fascist takeover and are therefore diagnostically useful for current liberal democracies. The book is explicitly applied to Trump's America, Bolsonaro's Brazil, Modi's India, and Orbán's Hungary.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**The Ten Pillars of Fascist Politics** (each a chapter):
1. **The mythic past** — nostalgia-politics; appeal to a glorious national past that has been stolen; "Make America Great Again" as prototype.
2. **Propaganda** — not persuasion but the creation of a separate reality; targets liberal institutions' epistemological authority.
3. **Anti-intellectualism** — attacks on universities, expertise, and "elites"; education framed as indoctrination by enemies.
4. **Unreality** — deliberate manufacture of confusion; the goal is not to convince but to destroy the factual commons; useful for Stanley's "normalization" mechanism.
5. **Hierarchy** — natural social hierarchy restored; anti-egalitarianism as a feature rather than bug; connects to racial and gender order.
6. **Victimhood** — the dominant group positioned as the true victim of minorities and elites; enables otherwise-prohibited aggression.
7. **Law and order** — the dominant group's lawbreaking excused or celebrated; law weaponized against out-groups; connects to [[CSPOA]] and "constitutional sheriff" doctrine.
8. **Sexual anxiety** — traditional gender order as political resource; anti-LGBTQ mobilization; hyper-masculine strongman performance.
9. **The heartland** — rural authentic nation vs. corrupt urban/cosmopolitan elites; "real Americans" vs. coastal elites.
10. **Dismantling public goods and unions** — destroying institutions that empower working-class solidarity; connects to anti-union and privatization agendas.

**Normalization**: Stanley's concept of how extraordinary behaviors become ordinary through repetition; each small normalization makes the next step easier. Key mechanism for explaining how democratic backsliding occurs without a single identifiable coup moment.

**Propaganda vs. lying**: Stanley distinguishes between propaganda (creating an alternative epistemic reality) and lying (asserting something false). Fascist propaganda doesn't require believability — it works by undermining trust in all truth-claims.

**Named cases used**: Adolf Hitler, Benito Mussolini; contemporary: [[Donald Trump]], [[Jair Bolsonaro]], [[Viktor Orban]], [[Narendra Modi]], [[Rodrigo Duterte]].

**Liberal democracy's vulnerability**: Stanley's argument that liberal democracy contains structural weaknesses fascist politics exploits — in particular, that free speech and democratic norms can be weaponized to destroy free speech and democratic norms (the "paradox of tolerance" in political form).

## Evidence tier
Tier-2. Philosophical-analytical work grounded in historical cases but explicitly prescriptive/diagnostic. Stanley is a credentialed scholar (Yale, philosophy of language) applying a framework derived from historical fascism to contemporary cases. The framework is widely cited in the scholarly debate; critics note it risks over-broadening the fascism concept. Tier-2 because it is rigorous argument, not primary evidence.
