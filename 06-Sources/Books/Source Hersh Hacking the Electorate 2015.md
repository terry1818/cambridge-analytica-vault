# Source: Hersh Hacking the Electorate 2015

## Citation

Hersh, Eitan D. (2015). *Hacking the Electorate: How Campaigns Perceive Voters.* Cambridge University Press. ISBN 978-1107434837.

## What it is

[[Eitan Hersh]]'s **academic monograph** — the **single most important empirical baseline** for understanding how US political campaigns actually use voter data, **published in 2015, three years before the CA scandal broke**. Hersh based the book on extensive field research with campaign operatives, voter-file vendors, and party operations across the 2008-2014 US election cycles. The book argues that **real-world campaign data operations are bounded by what's available in public voter files and commercial data brokers**, that **campaigns perceive voters through this filter (not directly)**, and that **most operational effects are at the margin — turnout and persuasion among already-likely voters of one's own party, not at-scale persuadable-flipping**.

This baseline matters enormously for the vault: it is the **principal empirical counterweight** to maximalist post-2018 narratives about CA's electoral influence. Hersh's pre-scandal framework, **not affected by post-scandal selection bias**, provides the field-research benchmark against which to evaluate CA's marketing claims and actual operational impact.

## Key claims / findings

- **The "perceived electorate" thesis:** Campaigns target what they can perceive — public voter files plus a handful of commercial data overlays — which means campaign targeting is **shaped more by data-availability than by predictive sophistication**
- **Voter-file primacy:** US **state voter files** (party registration, voting history, demographics) are the foundational data layer; commercial overlays (consumer purchases, modeled income) are secondary
- **Effects are at the margin:** Documented effects of data-driven campaigning are **small but real** — turnout lift of a few percentage points in targeted segments, persuasion effects smaller still
- **Already-likely-voters bias:** Sophisticated targeting is **disproportionately deployed against voters already likely to vote one's way** (efficient turnout) rather than against true persuadables (low base rates, expensive to reach, hard to move)
- **Psychographics doesn't add much:** When tested against standard demographic + voting-history models, **personality / psychographic overlays add marginal lift** at best
- **Industry vs. reality:** Hersh documents a **systematic gap between vendor marketing claims and operational reality** in the campaign-data space — a finding that prefigures the CA marketing-vs-reality discussion

## Significance for the vault

- **The empirical baseline against which CA's claims must be evaluated.** When [[Alexander Nix]] claimed in the [[Channel 4 Sting 2018|Channel 4 sting]] that CA had "**four or five thousand data points on every individual**" and modeled "**personality of every adult across the United States**," Hersh's prior research establishes what is operationally plausible vs. what is marketing inflation.
- **Pre-scandal independence:** Hersh's data and analysis are **unaffected by post-2018 selection bias** that distorts retrospective accounts
- **The disputed-claims anchor:** Hersh's argument that **psychographic overlays add marginal lift** is central to [[Lens - Disputed Claims]] and to the [[Methodology - Verifying a CA claim]] verification methodology
- **The empirical complement to [[Source Kosinski Stillwell Graepel 2013 PNAS|Kosinski et al. 2013]] and [[Source Matz Kosinski Nave Stillwell 2017 PNAS|Matz et al. 2017]]:** the academic field demonstrates predictive accuracy (Kosinski) and bounded behavioral effects (Matz); Hersh demonstrates **operational deployment effects in actual campaigns** — the third leg of the empirical picture

## Reliability assessment

- **Type:** academic monograph from Cambridge University Press — peer-reviewed academic publishing standards
- **Author's track record:** [[Eitan Hersh]] is a tenured political scientist (Tufts, previously Yale); subsequent work has continued to develop the framework
- **Methodology:** mixed methods — field research with campaign operatives, voter-file analysis, secondary-source synthesis
- **Strengths:**
  - **Pre-scandal independence** (data collected 2008-2014, before CA was publicly known)
  - **Multi-cycle field access** across many campaigns
  - **Operational-realism focus** rather than vendor-marketing extraction
- **Caveats:**
  - **US-only scope** — the book does not directly address UK / non-US contexts where CA also operated
  - **Pre-2016 data** — does not include the 2016 election cycle where CA was active
  - Subsequent revisions (in journal articles) have refined some specific empirical claims
- **Disputed by:** some industry practitioners; not significantly contested in the academic literature
