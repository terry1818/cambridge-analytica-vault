# Source — Crack-Up Capitalism: Market Radicals and the Dream of a World Without Democracy (Quinn Slobodian, 2023)

## Bibliographic
Quinn Slobodian; *Crack-Up Capitalism: Market Radicals and the Dream of a World Without Democracy*; Metropolitan Books / Henry Holt; 2023. ISBN 978-1250753892. Fortune Best Nonfiction Book of 2023. Slobodian is a history professor at Wellesley College and author of *Globalists* (2018), the earlier study of neoliberal international institutions.

## Thesis & scope
Slobodian argues that since the 1990s, the most radical strand of market libertarianism has abandoned the project of capturing entire governments and instead pursues a strategy of "crack-up capitalism": carving special zones — free ports, tax havens, charter cities, seasteads, crypto-sovereignties — out of existing states, where market rules apply and democratic ones do not. Rather than seeking a borderless world, these actors now seek an *intricately bordered* one, with thousands of competing micro-jurisdictions that enable "sovereignty shopping" by capital. Slobodian identifies approximately 5,400 such zones globally, and traces the intellectual genealogy from Milton Friedman's Hong Kong admiration through Hayek, Buchanan, Paul Romer, and Patri Friedman to contemporary figures like Peter Thiel, Curtis Yarvin, and the Praxis Society.

## Key content relevant to the vault

- **The "zone" as political technology**: Slobodian's core analytical object — special economic zones, free ports, charter cities, tax havens — as instruments for separating capital from democratic accountability. Key examples: Hong Kong (the model), Dubai, Singapore, Cayman Islands, Irish tax zones.
- **Milton Friedman and Hong Kong**: Friedman's use of Hong Kong as "a laboratory experiment in what happens when government is limited to its proper function" — explicitly celebrating a colony with no electorate as proving his economic theories. Slobodian traces this as the intellectual origin of the zones movement.
- **Paul Romer and charter cities**: Nobel-winning economist; proposed "charter cities" where a developed-country government would administer a zone in a developing country under its own legal system — "colonialism for the 21st century." Romer approached Madagascar and Honduras. Honduras enacted charter city legislation (ZEDE law, 2013).
- **Honduras ZEDEs (Zones for Employment and Economic Development)**: The Honduran ZEDE law allowed corporations to set up zones with their own laws, courts, police, and tax systems. Key ZEDE: **Próspera** on Roatán island — funded by [[Peter Thiel]] and [[Marc Andreessen]] via **Pronomos Capital** (a VC fund for autonomous city projects led by [[Patri Friedman]]). Próspera adopted Bitcoin as currency, offered $25,000 gene therapy, and operated as a privatized city-state. The Honduran government repealed the ZEDE law in 2022; Honduras Próspera Inc. responded with a $10.7 billion investment-treaty arbitration claim — potentially bankrupting the nation.
- **[[Patri Friedman]]** (Milton Friedman's grandson): founder of the **Seasteading Institute** (with Peter Thiel seed funding); founder of Pronomos Capital. The Seasteading Institute's project: building new sovereign societies on the high seas outside democratic jurisdiction.
- **[[Peter Thiel]]**: provided cash to launch the Seasteading Institute; invested in Próspera/Pronomos. His 2009 statement: "I no longer believe that freedom and democracy are compatible. The great task for libertarians is to find an escape from politics in all its forms." His advice: "If we want to increase freedom, we want to increase the number of countries."
- **[[Curtis Yarvin]]** (Mencius Moldbug): neoreactionary blogger; views Dubai as a model "patchwork" of privatized sovereignties. Slobodian traces how Yarvin's "patchwork" concept — replacing states with city-state corporations — derives directly from the zones logic.
- **Hans-Hermann Hoppe**: libertarian philosopher and paleoconservative; his concept of "radical legal pluralism" and covenant communities as the anarcho-capitalist alternative to democracy. Slobodian identifies Hoppe as a theoretical ancestor of the zones movement.
- **Praxis Society**: tech-billionaire funded startup aiming to create a new libertarian city-state to "restore Western civilization." Backed by Thiel-adjacent capital. Slobodian covers its ambitions.
- **Crypto-sovereignty**: the zones movement's digital extension — crypto networks as stateless jurisdictions. Slobodian traces the intellectual connection from seasteading to Balaji Srinivasan's "Network States" concept.
- **[[Balaji Srinivasan]]**: Slobodian covers Srinivasan's "Network States" concept — digital-first sovereignty experiments that bypass geographic jurisdiction. Connected to a16z and the crypto-right.
- **Free ports and financial secrecy zones**: Geneva Free Port (fine art), Singapore, Delaware, Cayman Islands as the mature infrastructure of capital-without-democracy.
- **Antony Fisher / Atlas Network**: Slobodian traces the neoliberal think-tank replication strategy (Antony Fisher → Heritage Foundation → Atlas Network of 500+ libertarian think tanks globally) as a parallel to the zones strategy — both aim at decapitating democratic state capacity.
- **"Plutocratic secession"** (historian Nils Gilman's term, cited by Slobodian): the structural logic whereby wealthy individuals and corporations route their activities through zone jurisdictions, escaping taxation and regulation while continuing to depend on democratic states' infrastructure and legal enforcement.
- **Seasteading as extreme zonal logic**: rather than carve friendly jurisdictions inside existing states, build new ones outside them — on the ocean, in orbit, or in digital space.
- **Geographic scope**: Hong Kong, Dubai, Singapore, Cayman Islands, Delaware, Honduras (Próspera), Madagascar (failed charter city), California, Canary Wharf, Isle of Man. The zones are global but the ideology is concentrated in Anglo-American and Silicon Valley capital.
