# Source — The Kingdom, the Power, and the Glory: American Evangelicals in an Age of Extremism (Tim Alberta, 2023)

## Bibliographic
- Author: Tim Alberta (staff writer, The Atlantic; son of evangelical pastor)
- Publisher: HarperCollins, 2023
- ISBN: 978-0063226883
- ~500 pages; four years of reporting

## Thesis & scope
Alberta's central argument is that a critical mass of American evangelicals has made political and cultural power an idol, fusing partisan identity with religious identity in ways that are destroying the church from within. The mechanism is fear — fear of secular encroachment — which is weaponized by entrepreneurial pastors and political operatives alike. The book is both a reportorial investigation and a personal memoir (Alberta's father was an evangelical pastor who resisted the MAGA turn). Alberta identifies Christian nationalism as the ideological expression of this capture and shows it operating at the congregational, denominational, and institutional level.

**Pastors and congregations profiled:**
- Greg Locke (Mount Juliet, TN) — viral anti-pandemic-regulation content turned him into a right-wing evangelical star; Alberta traces the incentive structure that rewards performance over theology
- Bill Bolin (Michigan) — profiled as emblematic of the fusion of Christian civil religion with populist conservatism at the local-church level
- Brian Zahnd (Missouri) — counterexample: a conservative pastor who moved to disentangle partisan politics from the pulpit and lost much of his congregation
- Robert Jeffress (First Baptist Dallas) — treated as the archetype of the politically useful pastor; close Trump ally

**Conceptual contributions:**
- The fear-based logic of politicized faith: besieged culture warriors hemorrhage members, which reinforces the siege mentality
- Christian nationalism as self-destructive — Alberta documents how it drives people away from Christianity
