# Source — Anointed with Oil: How Christianity and Crude Made Modern America (Darren Dochuk, 2019)

## Bibliographic
Dochuk, Darren. *Anointed with Oil: How Christianity and Crude Made Modern America*. Basic Books, 2019. 688 pp. ISBN 978-0-465-06086-3.

## Thesis & scope
From the first oil strike in Pennsylvania (1859) through the early twenty-first century, American Christianity and the petroleum industry developed in symbiosis, each shaping the other's theology, politics, and geography. Dochuk's organizing distinction is between two oil cultures: (1) the **"civil religion of crude"** — the major integrated companies (Standard Oil, Rockefeller) that embraced centralized management, social gospel philanthropy, and ecumenical Christianity; and (2) **"wildcat Christianity"** — the independent oilmen forced west and south by Rockefeller's monopoly, who fused charismatic evangelical faith, anti-regulatory libertarianism, and fierce individual property rights into the cultural formation that produced the modern Republican right. The book traces how the independent oilmen's Sun Belt Christianity became the dominant strain of American conservatism.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**Civil religion of crude (Rockefeller / major companies)**
- **John D. Rockefeller Sr.**: devout Northern Baptist whose Standard Oil profits funded mission philanthropy; model of corporate social gospel. His philanthropy pioneered modern foundations.
- **Standard Oil** and its successors: represented by ecumenical, socially moderate Christianity — the YMCA, mainline denominations, Ivy League funding. Rockefeller's social gospel is the foil against which wildcat Christianity defines itself.

**Wildcat Christianity (independents)**
- **Lyman Stewart** (co-founder, Union Oil of California): the archetype wildcat oilman-as-patron-of-fundamentalism. Stewart financed *The Fundamentals* (1910–1915), the series of essays that gave "fundamentalism" its name and its theological agenda. A direct, documented causal link between California oil money and the founding of Protestant fundamentalism.
- **J. Howard Pew** (Sun Oil / Sun Company, Philadelphia): the 20th century's central figure connecting oil money to conservative evangelical and Presbyterian institutions. Pew funded **Spiritual Mobilization** (Fifield), the **National Association of Evangelicals**, Billy Graham's early crusades, and dozens of other evangelical organizations. Pew's personal theology — that the New Deal was socialism incompatible with Christianity — shaped the doctrine of the organizations he funded. He is the single most important patron-class figure in American evangelical history and is absent from the vault.
- **Fred Koch** (Koch Industries precursor): appears as a Sun Belt wildcatter who fused anti-communist, anti-New Deal politics with oilfield independence. Bridge to the later Koch network.
- **H. L. Hunt** (Hunt Oil, Dallas): extreme right-wing Texas oilman; funder of early right-wing radio and print media (Life Line radio program, Facts Forum). One of the wealthiest Americans of the mid-20th century, with documented influence on Dallas's political culture prior to JFK assassination.
- **Clint Murchison Sr.** and **Sid Richardson**: Texas oil money flowing into right-wing political causes in the 1940s–1960s, including early efforts to recruit Eisenhower.

**Organizational products of wildcat Christianity**
- **National Association of Evangelicals (NAE)** (founded 1942): J. Howard Pew was a key early funder. The NAE is the institutional precursor to the modern evangelical-right organizational ecology.
- **Christianity Today** (magazine, 1956): founded with J. Howard Pew's money at Billy Graham's initiative; designed to counter the liberal National Council of Churches. The founding of the evangelical media infrastructure.
- **Full Gospel Business Men's Fellowship International** (FGBMFI, founded 1952): charismatic/Pentecostal businessmen's network that spread Pentecostalism through the business class and Sun Belt; prototype for later evangelical men's organizations.

**Sun Belt geography**
Dochuk documents how the westward migration of independent oilmen from Pennsylvania and the Mid-Continent to California, Texas, and Oklahoma created a distinct political-religious geography — what he calls the "oil patch" right — that became the base of the modern Republican Party. This explains the spatial concentration of conservative evangelical power in states with significant petroleum history.

**Connection to post-1970 Religious Right**
The book's final chapters trace the lineage from wildcat Christianity through the New Right of the 1970s, explicitly connecting J. Howard Pew's funding networks to the organizations that Falwell, Robertson, and Weyrich built. The oil-money-to-Religious-Right pipeline is not metaphorical; it is documented through grant records and personal correspondence.
