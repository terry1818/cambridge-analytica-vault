# Source — The Constitution in Jeopardy: An Unprecedented Effort to Rewrite Our Fundamental Law and What We Can Do About It (Russ Feingold & Peter Prindiville, 2022)

## Bibliographic
Russ Feingold and Peter Prindiville; *The Constitution in Jeopardy: An Unprecedented Effort to Rewrite Our Fundamental Law and What We Can Do About It*; PublicAffairs (Hachette); August 30, 2022. 320 pp. ISBN 9781541701526.

Feingold: three-term US Senator (Wisconsin), president of the American Constitution Society. Prindiville: fellow at the Stanford Constitutional Law Center.

## Thesis & scope
Article V of the Constitution contains a two-route amendment mechanism: Congress can propose amendments, or two-thirds of state legislatures can call a constitutional convention. The second route has never been used. Over the past two decades a well-funded far-right campaign has methodically worked state legislatures toward the 34-state threshold required to trigger it. Feingold and Prindiville argue that the convention route is uniquely dangerous because Article V provides no procedural guardrails: delegates could rewrite the Constitution wholesale, unconstrained by the limited mandate that summoned them. The book combines a full legal and historical account of Article V with a critique of the current convention campaign and a set of proposed reforms.

## Key content relevant to the vault

- **The two-route structure of Article V**: The 130-word amending clause allows (1) Congress to propose amendments ratified by 3/4 of states, or (2) 2/3 of states to call a convention whose output must still be ratified by 3/4 of states. The second route is the target of the contemporary campaign.

- **The runaway-convention risk**: No Article V provision specifies who selects delegates, how they are credentialed, or whether a convention is limited to the subject matter named in state applications. Feingold and Prindiville document the legal vacuum and trace every failed attempt to fill it legislatively. The analogy to the 1787 Philadelphia Convention — which exceeded its mandate to "amend" the Articles of Confederation and produced an entirely new document — is developed at length.

- **State-application accounting**: The book tracks state applications across the current campaign, noting that different campaign organizations (balanced-budget, term limits, general limitation on federal power) count their applications differently and that the legal question of whether subject-matter-different applications aggregate toward the 34-state threshold is itself contested and unresolved.

- **[[Convention of States Action]]** and its parent [[Citizens for Self-Governance]], led by [[Mark Meckler]], are named and documented as the primary organizational vehicle for the general-powers-limitation application drive.

- **[[ALEC]]'s role**: The book documents ALEC's model Article V resolution, which has been the template for many state legislative applications, and ALEC's coordination with COS Action.

- **Legal precedent surveyed**: The book engages *Dillon v. Gloss* (1921 — Congress may set a reasonable ratification deadline), *Hawke v. Smith* (1920 — ratification by state legislature cannot be submitted to a popular referendum), and *Coleman v. Miller* (1939 — the Court found certain Article V questions nonjusticiable political questions, leaving them with Congress). These precedents define the zone of judicial non-intervention that makes a runaway convention especially difficult to stop legally once launched.

- **The "political question" doctrine**: The *Coleman v. Miller* holding that courts will not adjudicate most Article V disputes is treated as the key structural vulnerability: there is no judicial backstop.

- **Balanced-budget application track**: The book documents the separate balanced-budget convention campaign (associated with [[ALEC]] and groups like the Balanced Budget Amendment Task Force), which has obtained a substantial number of state applications and runs parallel to the COS Action general-powers track.

- **Term-limits application track**: A third concurrent application track seeks a term-limits convention; the book treats the aggregation problem (can these differently-scoped applications be counted together?) as unresolved and dangerous.

- **[[Jim DeMint]]** (Heritage Foundation / Senate Conservatives Fund) is named as a key figure bridging conventional GOP politics and the Article V campaign.

- **[[Eric O'Keefe]]** (Wisconsin, dark-money network, Citizens for Self-Governance co-founder) is treated as a key organizational architect.

- **[[Michael Farris]]** (HSLDA founder, ADF legal work) is documented as an early intellectual contributor to the convention application campaign.

- **The dysfunctional amending clause more broadly**: The book argues that a hyper-restrictive amendment process — requiring 2/3 of Congress plus 3/4 of states — concentrates enormous veto power in small-state minorities and has been a factor enabling the workaround strategies both from the right (convention campaign) and from the left (judicial reinterpretation).

- **Proposed reforms**: Feingold and Prindiville call for (a) lowering the ratification threshold, (b) a national referendum option for amendments, and (c) statutory rules governing convention delegate selection before any convention is called. They endorse a bipartisan congressional commission.

- **Feingold's Senate career context**: The book draws on Feingold's experience with the McCain-Feingold campaign finance reform (BCRA 2002), later gutted by *Citizens United*, as an example of constitutional workaround by judicial reinterpretation.
