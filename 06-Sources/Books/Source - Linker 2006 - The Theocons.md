# Source — The Theocons: Secular America Under Siege (Damon Linker, 2006)

## Bibliographic
- Author: Damon Linker (journalist and political theorist; was associate editor then editor of *First Things* magazine under Richard John Neuhaus, May 2001–February 2005; insider-turned-critic)
- Publisher: Doubleday, 2006 (paperback: Anchor Books, 2007)
- ISBN: 978-0385516471 (hc); 978-1400096855 (pb)
- ~306 pp.

## Thesis & scope
Linker — writing from direct insider knowledge as a former *First Things* editor — argues that a small, tightly networked group of Catholic intellectuals (and their evangelical allies) constructed the intellectual architecture for a theocratic turn in American politics during the Reagan-Bush era. The "theocons" are not fringe figures but respected academics and public intellectuals who occupy positions at elite institutions and whose goal — stated with increasing candor — is to end secular politics and replace it with governance rooted in orthodox Christianity, specifically orthodox Catholicism. Linker's core claim: this is not mere religious engagement in public life but a deliberate project to subordinate democratic self-governance to religious authority, and it reached its practical apex in the George W. Bush presidency.

**Conceptual contributions:**
- **"The naked public square"** — Neuhaus's concept (from his 1984 book) that secular liberalism has illegitimately stripped religious reasoning from public discourse; the theocons' foundational grievance and call to action
- **Evangelical-Catholic fusionism** — the strategic alliance forged in the Reagan era between evangelical Protestants and orthodox Catholics; the intellectual foundation for the "values voter" coalition that elected George W. Bush and anticipated the current theocratic-wing coalition
- **"Civil religion rooted in orthodox Christianity"** — the theocons' stated goal; Linker documents how this was articulated with increasing explicitness in *First Things* and at conferences
- **The "End of Democracy?" symposium (1996)** — *First Things* published a symposium asking whether judicial decisions on abortion and euthanasia had rendered American democratic legitimacy forfeit; several contributors argued yes; a documented radicalization moment in the theocon project; Linker covers this in detail

**Historical scope:**
- The theocons' origins in the 1960s New Left: Neuhaus began as a civil rights and anti-Vietnam activist; Novak was a liberal Catholic; their journey from progressive Christianity to theoconservatism tracks the broader neoconservative turn
- The Reagan administration as the first practical vehicle for theocon influence
- The George W. Bush presidency as the apex: faith-based initiatives, stem cell policy, the Terri Schiavo case, abortion jurisprudence
