# Source — The Road to Unfreedom: Russia, Europe, America (Timothy Snyder, 2018)

## Bibliographic
Timothy Snyder; *The Road to Unfreedom: Russia, Europe, America*; Tim Duggan Books / Crown Publishing; 2018.

## Thesis & scope
Snyder argues that the post-Cold War assumption that history was moving inevitably toward liberal democracy ("politics of inevitability") collapsed into its authoritarian mirror image ("politics of eternity"), which he traces primarily through Vladimir Putin's Russia and its westward export. The book locates the ideological engine of Putinist authoritarianism in the rediscovered writings of the Russian fascist philosopher Ivan Ilyin, and documents how Russia weaponized this worldview through the invasion of Ukraine, interference in European elections, and intervention in the 2016 US election. The central claim is that fascism, not just authoritarianism, is the correct framework for Putin's Russia — and that Western democracies imported this crisis partly by abandoning their own historical self-understanding.

## Key content relevant to the vault

- **Politics of inevitability vs. politics of eternity**: Snyder's master framework. "Inevitability" = the Fukuyamite assumption that markets and democracy are the natural telos of history. "Eternity" = cyclical time in which the nation is perpetually victimized by enemies; politicians manage anxiety rather than solve problems; truth is irrelevant. The transition from one to the other is Snyder's model for democratic backsliding.

- **[[Ivan Ilyin]]** (1883–1954): Russian émigré philosopher who developed a Christian-fascist ideology of Russian redemption. Putin's government repatriated his remains in 2005 and distributes his writings to officials. Key Ilyin tenets: the state as a spiritual organism led by a redeemer figure; politics as the identification and destruction of enemies; Western liberalism as satanic fragmentation; Russia as the victim-nation destined to redeem civilization. Snyder reads Ilyin as Putinism's operating manual.

- **[[Carl Schmitt]]** connection: Ilyin borrowed directly from Nazi legal theorist Schmitt's "friend/enemy" distinction. Snyder traces this lineage explicitly, linking Putinist politics-as-enmity back to 1930s fascist thought.

- **Schizofascism**: Snyder's coinage for a regime that is functionally fascist while projecting the fascist label onto its opponents (e.g., Russia calling Ukrainian resisters "fascists" in 2014). Analytically important: it shows how fascist regimes inoculate against the fascism label by weaponizing it.

- **Ukraine as fulcrum**: The 2013–14 Maidan revolution and Russian invasion of eastern Ukraine are treated as the first battlefield of the politics of eternity vs. politics of novelty. Russia's disinformation campaign framing Maidan as a US-engineered fascist coup is analyzed as a template later applied to Western elections.

- **2016 US election intervention**: Snyder documents Russian cyber and information operations in the 2016 cycle as a direct export of the politics of eternity — seeding unreality, amplifying division, undermining the factual basis of democratic deliberation. Places [[Internet Research Agency (IRA)]] and [[Russian interference]] in an explicitly fascist-ideological frame rather than a mere strategic/intelligence frame.

- **European right-wing connections**: Snyder documents Russian material and ideological support for the European far right — [[Marine Le Pen]], [[Geert Wilders]], [[Viktor Orban]] — as vehicles for importing the politics of eternity into democracies.

- **"Implausible deniability"**: Russia's technique of supporting both sides of divisive Western debates while denying involvement — a concept the vault could apply to IRA operations and to later influence operations documented in the Doppelganger thread.
