# Source — Prequel: An American Fight Against Fascism (Rachel Maddow, 2023)

## Bibliographic
Rachel Maddow. *Prequel: An American Fight Against Fascism*. Crown, 2023. 320 pp. ISBN 978-0593444511. Companion to the podcast *Ultra* (MSNBC, 2022).

## Thesis & scope
American fascism is not an import — it has been a recurring domestic phenomenon. Maddow argues that in the 1930s and early 1940s a sophisticated, well-funded network of American fascist organizations worked to steer the US toward the Axis, promote antisemitism, spread Nazi-produced disinformation, undermine the war effort, and ultimately aim at overthrowing the government and installing authoritarian rule. A small group of committed prosecutors and public servants fought them off. The book's implicit argument: the institutions and individuals who resisted then are the template for resistance now.

The scope is primarily 1933–1944, ending at the Great Sedition Trial. Maddow draws extensively on declassified FBI files, congressional records, and contemporaneous journalism.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**Organizations covered:**
- **Silver Legion of America** (Silver Shirts): founded by William Dudley Pelley, 1933; a paramilitary organization modeled on the SA; openly pro-Hitler, antisemitic, sought an "American Reich."
- **Christian Front**: antisemitic paramilitary organization with ties to Father Charles Coughlin; plotted to assassinate prominent Jews and overthrow the government; FBI sting operation led to arrests in 1940.
- **American White Guard** (California): plotted to steal military weapons, assassinate Hollywood Jews, and trigger a pogrom.
- **German-American Bund**: US-based organization run by Fritz Kuhn that operated as a de facto branch of the Nazi Party; held mass rallies including a 1939 Madison Square Garden event.

**Key individuals:**
- **William Dudley Pelley**: screenwriter and spiritualist; founded the Silver Shirts; the book's primary villain.
- **George Sylvester Viereck**: German-American propagandist who covertly produced and distributed Nazi propaganda through US congressional franking privileges; convicted of failing to register as a foreign agent.
- **Lawrence Dennis**: American fascist intellectual, sometimes called "America's most brilliant fascist"; wrote *The Coming American Fascism* (1936).
- **Father Charles Coughlin**: Catholic radio broadcaster with 30+ million listeners; trafficked in antisemitism and distributed Nazi propaganda in his magazine *Social Justice*.
- **Fritz Kuhn**: leader of the German-American Bund.
- **Senator Burton Wheeler** and other isolationist senators: facilitated Viereck's disinformation distribution (knowingly or unknowingly).
- **Leon Lewis**: Jewish attorney who ran a private spy network in Los Angeles to infiltrate fascist organizations in the 1930s; a hero of the book.
- **John O'Brien** (DoJ): one of the federal prosecutors who pursued sedition cases.

**Events:**
- 1939 German-American Bund rally at Madison Square Garden (~22,000 attendees).
- 1940 Christian Front arrests by FBI.
- The Great Sedition Trial (1944): federal government prosecuted 30 defendants including Viereck, Dennis, and Pelley under the 1940 Smith Act; mistrial after the presiding judge died; never retried.
- Congressional franking privilege abuse: Viereck used sympathetic isolationist congressmen to mail Nazi propaganda at government expense.

**Key concepts:**
- Domestic fascism as a recurring, not exceptional, American phenomenon.
- Use of religious broadcasting as a vector for proto-fascist disinformation (Coughlin).
- The "sedition trial" model: can the government effectively prosecute political extremism without creating martyrs?
- The Smith Act (1940) as an instrument of political prosecution, with dual-use risks.

## Evidence tier
Tier-2: Maddow draws on primary sources (FBI files, congressional records, court documents) and the work of established historians. The book has faced some criticism (notably from libertarian/conservative reviewers at *Reason*) for presentist framing, but the underlying historical facts are well-documented and cross-checkable. Treat the explicit "this is just like today" analogies as Maddow's argument, not established fact.
