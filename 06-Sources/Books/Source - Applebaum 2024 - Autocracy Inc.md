# Source — Autocracy, Inc.: The Dictators Who Want to Run the World (Anne Applebaum, 2024)

## Bibliographic
Anne Applebaum; *Autocracy, Inc.: The Dictators Who Want to Run the World*; Doubleday; 2024.

## Thesis & scope
Applebaum argues that the world's autocratic regimes no longer require shared ideology to cooperate — what binds them is the shared interest in preserving personal wealth and power. She names this loosely coordinated system "Autocracy, Inc." — a transnational network of mutual support among regimes as varied as Russia, China, Iran, North Korea, Venezuela, Belarus, Nicaragua, Myanmar, Cuba, and Zimbabwe. The network provides a "regime survival package" combining financial aid, surveillance technology, propaganda expertise, and diplomatic protection. The book's practical implication is that Western democracies have enabled this network through their own financial infrastructure (tax havens, money laundering, permissive legal and banking regimes) and that closing that infrastructure is the most actionable counter-measure.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**"Regime survival package"** — Applebaum's core analytical concept: autocrats extend to one another a package of tools that any one regime may need at any moment. Components include:
- Russian export of disinformation expertise and mercenaries (e.g., Wagner Group)
- Chinese export of surveillance technology and Belt and Road economic dependency
- Iranian military hardware and strategic alliances
- Diplomatic mutual protection in UN votes (Zimbabwe, Belarus, Cuba voted to support Russian annexation of Crimea, 2014)
- Smaller autocracies serving as test cases (Belarus 2020 election manipulation as laboratory)

**No unifying ideology** — The analytical distinction Applebaum makes most sharply: unlike the Cold War communist bloc or the Axis powers, Autocracy, Inc. has no common vision of the good society. What unites [[Vladimir Putin]], [[Xi Jinping]], [[Nicolas Maduro]], [[Alexander Lukashenko]], and others is purely instrumental — preserving power and personal enrichment. This makes the network harder to counter ideologically and explains its internal flexibility.

**Kleptocracy and Western financial complicity** — Applebaum documents how the "soft underbelly" of Western financial systems — offshore tax havens, shell companies, law firms, banks willing to handle oligarch money — enables autocratic wealth preservation and thus regime survival. Without the ability to hide and deploy their wealth in Western financial systems, many autocrats' domestic hold would be fragile. This is Applebaum's primary policy prescription: close the financial infrastructure.

**Named regimes and cases**:
- [[Russia]] and [[Vladimir Putin]] — primary case; Doppelganger/IRA disinformation export
- [[China]] — surveillance tech export, Belt and Road as dependency tool
- Venezuela 2016 election manipulation — detailed case study
- Belarus 2020 — election fraud + suppression as template
- Japan "Kansai Airport" incident — Chinese-affiliated disinformation reaching Taiwanese media, illustrating disinformation cascade
- [[Saudi Arabia]], [[UAE]], [[Mohammed bin Salman]], [[Mohammed bin Zayed]] — implicit (Gulf autocracies fit the model though less central in the text)

**Disinformation as a regime-survival export** — Russia is the leading exporter of disinformation technique; Applebaum documents this not as ideologically motivated but as a service offered to regime partners to destabilize their domestic opponents and Western critics.

**The role of Western enablers** — Lawyers, PR firms, lobbyists, and bankers who work for autocratic clients are part of the system. Applebaum frames Western professional services as a structural component of Autocracy, Inc., not merely peripheral opportunists.

**"Is this fascism?" open question**: Applebaum's framework is explicitly *not* a fascism framework — she argues the new autocracies are post-ideological, united by interest not ideology. This is a productive tension with Snyder (who sees fascist ideology) and Paxton (who requires fascist mobilization): Applebaum's model captures the oligarchic and kleptocratic dimension that pure fascism analysis may miss.

## Evidence tier
Tier-2. Applebaum is a Pulitzer Prize-winning journalist and historian with deep primary sourcing on Eastern Europe and Russia; this book is more analytical and prescriptive than her historical works. Arguments about post-ideological autocratic cooperation are well-supported by documented cases but involve interpretive claims about motivation ("interest not ideology") that are difficult to prove. Named as "Book of the Year" by The Economist, 2024.
