# Source — The Tech Coup: How to Save Democracy from Silicon Valley (Marietje Schaake, 2024)

## Bibliographic
- **Author:** Marietje Schaake (former MEP, 2009–2019; International Policy Director, Stanford HAI)
- **Publisher:** Princeton University Press, 2024
- **ISBN:** 9780691241173

## Thesis & scope
Schaake's central argument is that tech companies have not merely grown large — they have occupied functions that historically belonged to elected governments: communications infrastructure, military logistics, intelligence collection, election systems, and monetary policy. This is not incidental but structural: democratic institutions failed to regulate, and Silicon Valley moved into the vacuum. The book is prescriptive as well as diagnostic: it proposes a "public stack" (public digital infrastructure as counterweight to corporate control) and insists democratic governments must reassert sovereign authority through procurement policy, regulation, and international coordination. Schaake writes from a European vantage point but focuses heavily on US dynamics.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**Starlink / Ukraine precedent.** Schaake treats Elon Musk's control of Starlink access in the Ukraine war as the paradigmatic instance of private-sovereign power. Musk's decision to restrict Starlink coverage in contested Ukrainian territory — reportedly to prevent drone strikes — was a private individual making battlefield decisions. Ronan Farrow's characterisation ("private citizen who became the arbiter of the outcome of this war") is cited. The subsequent episode in which [[X (Twitter)]] rerouted traffic and [[Elon Musk]] encouraged Brazilian users to use VPNs after the Brazilian Supreme Court ban illustrates the same dynamic: tech companies openly defying sovereign regulatory authority.

**Outsourced government.** Schaake enumerates the functional government dependencies: the US space program effectively outsourced to [[SpaceX]]; military logistics and drone targeting increasingly reliant on [[Anduril Industries]], [[Google]], [[Microsoft]], and [[Palantir Technologies]]; law enforcement data infrastructure reliant on Palantir and surveillance vendors; election systems reliant on private platforms.

**Infrastructure chapter.** Chapter 2 covers the expansion of tech into physical communications infrastructure — microchip fabrication, submarine cables, data centers — arguing these constitute de facto strategic chokepoints owned by private actors without public accountability.

**Regulatory capture argument.** Schaake documents how corporate lobbying shaped the regulatory environment that failed to constrain tech growth. She cites the EU's General Data Protection Regulation ([[GDPR]]) and the EU's Digital Services Act ([[Digital Services Act]]) as incomplete but meaningful countermodels, while arguing the US has retreated from even those frameworks under tech-aligned administrations.

**AI governance gap.** Schaake argues that AI is the latest domain where governments have ceded authority before establishing guardrails, with military AI applications (autonomous weapons, targeting systems) contracted to the same firms that handle commercial surveillance — creating a civilian-military blurring with no democratic oversight.

**"Public stack" proposal.** Her policy prescription is a public digital infrastructure layer — analogous to public utilities — to provide accountability, interoperability, and resilience that private platforms cannot. She frames this as reinvigorating rather than displacing markets.
