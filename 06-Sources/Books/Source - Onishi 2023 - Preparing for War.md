# Source — Preparing for War: The Extremist History of White Christian Nationalism—and What Comes Next (Bradley Onishi, 2023)

## Bibliographic
- Author: Bradley Onishi (scholar of religion, Fordham; co-host of Straight White American Jesus podcast; former evangelical Christian nationalist and onetime Evangelical minister who left the movement while completing a theology DPhil at Oxford)
- Publisher: Broadleaf Books, 2023 (released January 6, 2023)
- ISBN: 978-1506482163

## Thesis & scope
Onishi provides a hybrid memoir-history that traces white Christian nationalism's ideological genealogy from the John Birch Society and post-WWII Orange County conservative evangelicalism through the Religious Right's construction (1964–2015) to the Trump presidency and January 6. His central claim: January 6 was not an aberration but the logical endpoint of a movement Onishi himself was part of. The foundational traits he identifies in the January 6 rioters — the myth of the Christian nation, nostalgia for past glory, and an apocalyptic view of the nation's future — were cultivated across decades of institutional infrastructure. The book is distinctive for combining personal testimony (as a former insider) with academic religious studies methodology.

## Key content relevant to the vault
**Historical genealogy documented:**
- **John Birch Society** — Onishi traces the origins of conspiratorial thinking in Christian nationalism to the JBS, particularly its influence in the Sunbelt migration communities of Orange County, CA in the 1950s–60s; white evangelical and ultra-conservative suburban communities as the soil for JBS conspiracy theories
- **Sunbelt Migration thesis** — the demographic and geographic transformation of the 1950s–60s (white evangelical families relocating to Orange County and similar Sunbelt suburbs) as the material basis for the movement's growth; a structural argument about movement sociology
- **Christian school movement** — the founding of private Christian academies as a response to desegregation (Bob Jones University case; IRS tax-exemption controversy) and as institutional infrastructure for WCN formation
- **Purity culture** — the gender and sexuality disciplinary apparatus of evangelical subculture as a mechanism for producing movement identity and loyalty; Onishi draws on his own formation within it
- **Right-wing media ecosystem** — the role of Salem Radio, Christian television, and eventually social media in creating a closed information environment that made radicalization possible
- **Segregationist theologies** — white supremacy and theological justifications for racial hierarchy in the Christian school movement origins

**Analytical contributions:**
- Three foundational WCN traits: (1) myth of the Christian nation, (2) nostalgia for past glory, (3) apocalyptic national future — a typology useful for the vault's concept nodes
- The Religious Right (1964–2015) as the incubation period that made Trump's WCN presidency possible; continuity rather than rupture
- January 6 as "preparing for war" — the insurrection prefigured by decades of martial, apocalyptic, and dominionist rhetoric

**Insider testimony:**
- Onishi's personal account of formation inside a California evangelical-nationalist subculture; his participation in and eventual departure from the movement gives the book unique evidential texture
- Documents the culture of submission, authority, and isolation that makes deradicalization difficult
