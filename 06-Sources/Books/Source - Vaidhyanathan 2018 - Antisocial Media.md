# Source — Antisocial Media: How Facebook Disconnects Us and Undermines Democracy (Siva Vaidhyanathan, 2018)

## Bibliographic
Vaidhyanathan, Siva. *Antisocial Media: How Facebook Disconnects Us and Undermines Democracy*. Oxford University Press, 2018. 288 pp.

Vaidhyanathan is professor of media studies at the University of Virginia, author of several prior works on Google and intellectual property.

## Thesis & scope
Vaidhyanathan's argument is that Facebook is not a social network that has been misused — it is a surveillance system optimized for behavioral modification that is constitutively "antisocial" because its architecture segregates rather than connects, amplifies low-quality emotional content over reasoned information, and provides authoritarian actors with an inexpensive precision propaganda tool. The book spans the United States, Myanmar, India, Cambodia, Turkey, and Brazil to demonstrate that Facebook's harms are global and structurally produced by the business model, not by edge-case misuse. Vaidhyanathan explicitly disputes the "Facebook as neutral tool" framing and argues that treating it as a public utility rather than a private surveillance corporation is the required regulatory move.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**Myanmar / Rohingya genocide.** The book gives early, pre-scandal scholarly treatment to Facebook's role in the Rohingya crisis. Ethnic nationalist groups used Facebook (the de facto internet in Myanmar) to circulate hate speech, coordinate violence, and organize anti-Muslim campaigns. Facebook's algorithms amplified inflammatory content because it generated engagement. Vaidhyanathan treats this as an early, documented instance of the platform enabling ethnic cleansing — connecting it to the vault's existing [[Facebook and the Myanmar Rohingya genocide]] entry.

**India / Narendra Modi 2014.** The book analyzes how [[Narendra Modi]]'s 2014 campaign systematically exploited Facebook for political propaganda — building audience lists, running targeted messaging, and using the platform's cheap reach to political audiences who had never been targetable before. This, Vaidhyanathan argues, was a template that [[Rodrigo Duterte]] in the Philippines and Jair Bolsonaro in Brazil subsequently applied.

**Surveillance architecture.** Vaidhyanathan maps how Facebook's data collection — behavioral tracking on-platform and via the Like button embedded across the web — creates a surveillance system that users do not perceive as such. The "newsworthiness exemption" (Facebook's stated policy allowing newsworthy content to escape normal moderation standards) is analyzed as a mechanism that systematically advantages political actors willing to generate controversy.

**Epistemic segregation.** The book distinguishes between echo chambers (which the empirical literature partially challenges) and what Vaidhyanathan calls "epistemic fragmentation" — not that users only see confirming content, but that the shared informational commons required for democratic deliberation has been dissolved. This is a more precise claim than filter-bubble theory.

**Business model as the root cause.** Unlike accounts that focus on regulatory failures or Russian interference, Vaidhyanathan locates the problem in the advertising business model itself: because engagement drives revenue, and outrage drives engagement, the platform has a structural interest in maximizing polarization. He argues that no amount of content moderation or algorithmic adjustment will fix this structural incentive.

**Facebook's "missionary spirit."** The book analyzes Zuckerberg's "connecting the world" ideology as a genuinely believed but catastrophically hubristic mission that caused Facebook to expand aggressively into markets where it lacked the linguistic and cultural competence to moderate content — Myanmar being the most lethal example.

**Global comparison.** Beyond India and Myanmar, Vaidhyanathan examines Cambodia (Hun Sen's use of Facebook for political control), Turkey (Erdogan-era use for mobilization and surveillance), and Brazil (Bolsonaro infrastructure) as a set of cases demonstrating that the platform's design produces authoritarian-adjacent outcomes across political systems.
