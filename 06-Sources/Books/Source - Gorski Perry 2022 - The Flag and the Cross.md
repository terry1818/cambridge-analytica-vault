# Source — The Flag and the Cross: White Christian Nationalism and the Threat to American Democracy (Philip S. Gorski & Samuel L. Perry, 2022)

## Bibliographic
- Authors: Philip S. Gorski (Professor of Sociology, Yale; comparative historical sociologist of religion and politics); Samuel L. Perry (Associate Professor of Sociology, University of Oklahoma; co-author of [[Source - Whitehead Perry 2020 - Taking America Back for God]])
- Publisher: Oxford University Press, 2022
- ISBN: 978-0197618684
- Foreword by Jemar Tisby
- ~176 pp.; accessible academic press

## Thesis & scope
Gorski and Perry argue that white Christian nationalism is not a recent aberration but a durable ideological tradition that has animated American politics from the colonial era forward. Their "deep story" framework: America was founded as a Christian nation by (white) Christian men, its founding documents express Christian principles, and it occupies a divinely chosen role in God's plan — a story that has historically licensed the oppression and exclusion of minorities while securing privilege for white Protestants. The book traces the tradition's emergence, transformation, and contemporary resurgence; identifies how apparently incoherent strands of libertarianism, racism, and authoritarianism fuse into a coherent ideological compound; and argues that this compound now represents a direct threat to democratic governance, as evidenced by January 6.

## Key content relevant to the vault
**Conceptual framework:**
- The "deep story" of white Christian nationalism: a narrative structure (not a theology) that makes grievance legible and authorizes violence
- The distinction between white Christian nationalism (a political ideology) and Christianity (a religion) — crucial for the vault's analytical precision
- How WCN generates anti-democratic, authoritarian, and violent political behavior: not incidentally but structurally
- The toxic fusion of libertarianism, racism, and authoritarianism as the ideology's three incoherent-seeming but interlocking legs

**Historical periodization:**
- Colonial origins: the "errand into the wilderness" Puritan framework as proto-WCN
- Antebellum era: white churches as institutional supporters of slavery; the theological elaboration of racial hierarchy
- Post-Reconstruction: Lost Cause Christianity as a re-articulation of WCN after military defeat
- 20th century: WCN's adaptation through anti-communism, resistance to civil rights, and Cold War Americanism
- Contemporary: the January 6 insurrection as the culmination of WCN's authoritarian logic — rioters carrying Christian symbols (Appeal to Heaven flags, crosses, "Jesus Saves" banners) treated as diagnostic

**Analytical move:**
- WCN is not primarily a religious phenomenon but a political one that uses religious language; Gorski and Perry are careful to distinguish it from Christian faith
- The role of racial resentment as the connective tissue between libertarian economics, cultural conservatism, and authoritarian politics
