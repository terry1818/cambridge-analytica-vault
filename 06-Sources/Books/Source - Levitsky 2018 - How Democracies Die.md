# Source — How Democracies Die (Steven Levitsky & Daniel Ziblatt, 2018)

## Bibliographic
Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt; *How Democracies Die*; Crown (Random House); January 2018. 320 pp. ISBN 9781524762933.

Both authors are professors of Government at Harvard University. Levitsky specializes in Latin American politics and comparative authoritarianism; Ziblatt specializes in European democratic development.

## Thesis & scope
Democracies today rarely die by coup. They die gradually, through legal mechanisms, as elected leaders dismantle the institutional guardrails that constrain them. Two informal norms — **mutual toleration** (treating political opponents as legitimate rivals rather than existential enemies) and **institutional forbearance** (restraint in using legal powers to the maximum extent) — are more important than formal constitutional rules in sustaining democracy. When these norms erode, formal institutions become weapons. The book draws on comparative cases from Latin America (Venezuela, Peru, Chile, Brazil), Europe (interwar Germany and Italy, contemporary Hungary), and the US to build and apply this framework. Trump's rise is analyzed as the product of Republican gatekeeping failure and norm erosion.

## Key content relevant to the vault

- **The four-indicator authoritarian litmus test**: (1) rejection of or weak commitment to democratic rules of the game; (2) denial of legitimacy to political opponents; (3) toleration or encouragement of violence; (4) readiness to curtail civil liberties of opponents including media. The authors apply this to Trump and find all four present.

- **Gatekeeping failure**: Political parties historically served as filters preventing demagogues from reaching the ballot. The shift from smoke-filled-room nomination to primary elections eroded this gatekeeping. The GOP's failure to block Trump's 2016 nomination is analyzed as a structural failure of the gatekeeping function.

- **Mutual toleration and forbearance** as foundational norms: The book argues these norms, not constitutional text, are what made US democracy stable for most of its history — and that both are now critically degraded.

- **Historical comparative cases**: Hugo Chávez (Venezuela) and Alberto Fujimori (Peru) as paradigm cases of elected leaders who legally dismantled democratic institutions; Recep Tayyip Erdoğan (Turkey) and [[Viktor Orban|Viktor Orbán]] (Hungary) as contemporary parallels; Getulio Vargas (Brazil), Benito Mussolini, and Adolf Hitler as 20th-century cases of democratically elected leaders who became dictators.

- **The "capturing the referees" pattern**: Autocrats first move to control courts, prosecutors, electoral bodies, and intelligence services — the "impartial umpires" — before moving on bolder anti-democratic actions. Chávez and Erdoğan are the primary illustrations.

- **The "United States as exception" argument**: The book's historical arc shows that American democracy was protected for most of the 20th century less by its constitutional design than by an informal norm of racial exclusion that produced artificial elite consensus — a deeply uncomfortable finding. The expansion of the electorate (Civil Rights era) broke that artificial consensus and began the norm-erosion process now visible in extreme polarization.

- **The Republican Party's radicalization**: The book documents how the GOP moved toward asymmetric radicalization over the 1990s–2010s (Gingrich era, Tea Party, Freedom Caucus) in a pattern that parallels European parties that enabled fascism.

- **Newt Gingrich**: Named as a key figure in Republican norm erosion, specifically the weaponization of procedural mechanisms (government shutdowns, debt ceiling hostage-taking, scorched-earth impeachment) that violated forbearance norms.

- **The role of partisan media**: Fox News and right-wing media are discussed as drivers of radicalization that break down the shared factual reality that mutual toleration requires.

- **What to do**: The book ends with a call for cross-partisan coalitions including business, military, and religious conservatives who recognize the democratic threat — a frame directly relevant to counter-COS and counter-P2025 strategy.
