# Source — Democracy in Chains: The Deep History of the Radical Right's Stealth Plan for America (Nancy MacLean, 2017)

## Bibliographic
Nancy MacLean; *Democracy in Chains: The Deep History of the Radical Right's Stealth Plan for America*; Viking Press; 2017. 368 pp. ISBN 978-1101980989. Finalist, National Book Award for Nonfiction 2017; winner, Lillian Smith Book Award 2018.

## Thesis & scope
MacLean argues that Nobel-winning economist James M. Buchanan — founder of public-choice theory at the Virginia school — provided the intellectual architecture for a decades-long, Koch-funded project to constitutionally entrench minority rule and render majoritarian democracy unable to redistribute wealth or regulate capital. The book traces the lineage from Buchanan's work in the segregation-era South through the Mont Pelerin Society milieu, his alliance with Charles Koch in the 1990s, and forward to the contemporary COS/Article V movement and judicial capture strategy. MacLean frames this not as ordinary conservatism but as a stealth program to "reverse-engineer all of America back to the political economy and oligarchic governance of midcentury Virginia."

## Key content relevant to the vault

- **James M. Buchanan** (1919–2013): founder of public-choice economics at the Thomas Jefferson Center, University of Virginia, then at George Mason University. His core claim: democratic majorities systematically over-spend and over-regulate because politicians buy votes with other people's money; constitutional rules must be hardwired to prevent this. Nobel Memorial Prize in Economics, 1986.
- **Virginia school origins in segregation**: MacLean traces Buchanan's intellectual formation to Prince Edward County, Virginia, where white politicians closed all public schools (1959–1964) rather than integrate — using school-voucher schemes to fund private segregation academies. Buchanan and Milton Friedman's school-voucher advocacy runs through this moment.
- **Mont Pelerin Society**: the transnational neoliberal network (founded 1947, Hayek, Friedman) within which Buchanan's Virginia school operated. MacLean argues this is the ideological supply chain feeding [[Koch Network]].
- **Charles Koch** and the Virginia school: Koch's discovery of Buchanan's Center for Study of Public Choice at George Mason University in the late 1990s. Koch funded and operationalized Buchanan's constitutional-constraint program. Documents from Buchanan's papers (opened after his 2013 death) show Koch's investment in the Center and correspondence with Buchanan on strategy.
- **Center for Study of Public Choice, George Mason University**: the institutional node Koch invested in; became a pipeline for libertarian economists into think tanks, judiciary, and policy networks.
- **Constitutional entrenchment strategy**: Buchanan's program called for "constitutional chains" — supermajority requirements, balanced-budget amendments, property-rights protections, spending caps — that would be practically irreversible by democratic majorities. This is the philosophical predecessor to [[Article V Convention]] / [[Convention of States Action]].
- **[[Americans for Prosperity]]**: the Koch political mobilization vehicle; MacLean links its agenda directly to Buchanan's constitutional program.
- **[[ALEC]] (American Legislative Exchange Council)**: the state-level legislative pipeline — Buchanan's influence visible in model bills constraining government spending and taxation.
- **[[State Policy Network]]**: the 64-state-think-tank apparatus that disseminates the constitutional-constraint agenda at state level, directly connected to [[Koch Network]].
- **[[Cato Institute]]**: Koch-founded libertarian think tank drawing on Buchanan's intellectual program.
- **[[Mercatus Center]], George Mason University**: Koch-funded economics center, direct institutional successor to the Virginia school model.
- **[[Federalist Society]]**: MacLean connects the judicial-selection project to the same constitutional-entrenchment logic.
- **[[DonorsTrust]] / dark money pass-through**: the anonymous-giving infrastructure enabling Koch-network funding of the constitutional-capture apparatus.
- **Chile / Pinochet connection**: Buchanan visited Chile in 1981 during the Pinochet dictatorship; MacLean argues he helped advise on constitutional constraints that locked in market reforms against future democratic reversal. (This specific claim is contested by libertarian critics who argue Buchanan was critical of the regime.)
- **School privatization / vouchers**: traced from Friedman's 1955 essay through Buchanan's Virginia work to the contemporary DeVos-era school-choice apparatus.
- **Public-choice theory as political weapon**: MacLean's core analytical move — Buchanan's ostensibly value-neutral theory functions in practice as a rationale for dismantling the administrative state, unions, Social Security, and any institution through which democratic majorities exercise collective power.

## Evidence tier
Tier-2. MacLean is a credentialed historian (Duke University) with access to primary archival sources (Buchanan papers). The core Koch–Buchanan connection is documented from primary documents. Some specific interpretive claims (Buchanan's relationship to Southern Agrarians, the Chile visit framing) have been substantively disputed by libertarian scholars; these should be treated as contested rather than established fact. The structural argument — that Koch funded Buchanan's program to achieve constitutional entrenchment of minority rule — rests on documented funding relationships and is credible at Tier-2.
