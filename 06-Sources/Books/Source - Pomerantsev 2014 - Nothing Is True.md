# Source — Nothing Is True and Everything Is Possible: The Surreal Heart of the New Russia (Peter Pomerantsev, 2014)

## Bibliographic
Peter Pomerantsev; *Nothing Is True and Everything Is Possible: The Surreal Heart of the New Russia*; PublicAffairs; 2014.

## Thesis & scope
Pomerantsev spent roughly a decade (early 2000s–2011) working as a TV producer in Moscow, principally at the entertainment network TNT. The book argues that Putin's Russia has constructed a wholly new form of authoritarianism: not the oppressive uniformity of Soviet-era propaganda, but a deliberate saturation of the information environment with contradictory, sensationalist, and cynical narratives that renders stable truth impossible. The controlling intelligence behind this system is [[Vladislav Surkov]], whom Pomerantsev calls the Kremlin's "hidden author" and describes as a political technologist who simultaneously funds civil-society NGOs and nationalist movements attacking those same NGOs, sponsors avant-garde art festivals and Orthodox fundamentalists who vandalize modern art exhibitions — the goal being to own all political discourse and prevent any coherent opposition from crystallising.

## Key content relevant to the vault

- **[[Vladislav Surkov]] as architect of managed unreality**: Surkov is described as directing Russian politics "like a great reality show," deliberately keeping all factions off-balance and dependent on Kremlin patronage; Pomerantsev frames this as a conscious doctrine, not ad-hoc opportunism. The concept is distinct from Soviet agitprop — it is *post-truth* management, not a single "correct" line.
- **The television apparatus as control mechanism**: Pomerantsev documents TNT's operation as a "news-free zone" producing escapist entertainment that depoliticises the population while Kremlin-loyal channels (NTV, Channel One, Rossiya) control political framing; the two-tier media ecosystem (entertainment + political manipulation) functions as a single integrated system.
- **"Managed democracy" (surkovshchina)**: [[Managed democracy (Surkovism)]] is the political system Pomerantsev describes from the inside — opposition parties are allowed to exist but are funded or infiltrated by the Kremlin; protest movements are co-opted or discredited; the appearance of political pluralism masks a total information monopoly.
- **Nationalist movements as Kremlin instruments**: Nashi (the pro-Kremlin youth movement), Orthodox nationalist groups, and far-right militias are shown as simultaneously genuine and Kremlin-managed — illustrating how the state can mobilise contradictory social forces without committing to any of them.
- **Export of the model**: Pomerantsev identifies that the same techniques of narrative chaos, the dismissal of factual grounding, and the weaponisation of "everything is relative" are being exported westward via RT (Russia Today) and social media operations — predating the vault's coverage of the IRA and Doppelganger campaigns by showing the doctrinal origin.
- **The "siloviki dream" in cultural form**: The book shows how former KGB officers and organised crime figures were being aestheticised and mainstreamed via television drama and celebrity culture, normalising their status as legitimate power-brokers.
- **RT (Russia Today)** as a propaganda vehicle: Pomerantsev offers one of the earliest detailed analytical treatments of RT's function — not simply to convince foreign audiences of Russian positions, but to spread general cynicism about the existence of trustworthy information anywhere.

Tier-2. First-person reportage by a practitioner inside the Russian TV system with named sources and direct observation; published by an established academic press; Pomerantsev's analytical framework has been extensively cited in subsequent academic and policy literature on Russian information warfare.
