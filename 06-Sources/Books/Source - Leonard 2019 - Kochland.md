# Source — Kochland: The Secret History of Koch Industries and Corporate Power in America (Christopher Leonard, 2019)

## Bibliographic
Christopher Leonard; *Kochland: The Secret History of Koch Industries and Corporate Power in America*; Simon & Schuster; 2019. 704 pp. ISBN 9781476775388. J. Anthony Lukas Prize (2017 work-in-progress award); finalist, Financial Times/McKinsey Business Book of the Year 2019. Jane Mayer in *The New Yorker* called it "deeply and authoritatively reported."

## Thesis & scope
Leonard's central argument is that Koch Industries is not merely a very large private company but a political-influence machine of "rare scope," and that the two cannot be separated: the same philosophy that drove Charles Koch's business decisions — extreme hostility to government regulation, environmental law, labor unions, and democratic oversight — also drove his decades-long project to reshape American politics. The book traces Koch Industries from a regional pipeline company (Fred Koch's death, 1967) through its transformation into a sprawling conglomerate, documenting specific instances of regulatory violations, market manipulation, and union-busting alongside the build-out of the political network. It is the most operationally detailed account of how Koch wealth was generated and how it was used.

## Key content relevant to the vault

- **Charles Koch** and the Market-Based Management philosophy: Koch's internal corporate ideology — treating the company as a pure market system — as both a management doctrine and a political philosophy that regards all government intervention as distortion.
- **Fred Koch / founding**: Fred Koch made his initial fortune building oil refineries for Stalin's Soviet Union in the 1930s and later for Nazi Germany; Charles inherited the company and vastly expanded it from 1967.
- **Koch Industries corporate structure**: privately held, no public shareholders, no quarterly reporting obligations — enabling long-term political investments that public companies could not sustain. Revenues estimated at $115 billion by 2019.
- **1990s diversification and financial markets**: Koch's entry into commodity trading and derivatives; the book gives the first detailed account of how Koch generated astronomical profits trading in commodities markets, often by exploiting information asymmetries Koch's infrastructure position provided.
- **2000 California electricity crisis**: Koch traders manipulated the deregulated California electricity market, taking hundreds of thousands of dollars in profits through unlawful trading strategies. Leonard documents this as a direct product of the deregulation agenda Koch's political network had lobbied for.
- **Georgia-Pacific acquisition (2005)**: Koch's $21 billion purchase of the paper/forest products company — the largest private acquisition in history at the time — brought 30,000 union workers into Koch's orbit, leading to sustained union-busting.
- **Environmental violations**: Koch pled guilty in 1999 to knowingly discharging contaminated wastewater into Minnesota wetlands (more than 300 oil spills in six states); Leonard documents a pattern of treating regulatory fines as a cost of doing business cheaper than compliance.
- **Purina Mills failure (1996)**: Koch's disastrous acquisition of the animal feed company — bought for $670 million, sold after turbulent years at a massive loss — used by Leonard to show Koch is not infallible.
- **[[Koch Network]] political infrastructure**: the final section is the most vault-relevant — documenting how Koch's regulatory entanglements directly funded his motivation to build the political machine. Specific organizations Leonard details:
  - **[[Americans for Prosperity]]**: Koch's mass-mobilization vehicle; Leonard traces its role in killing the 2009–2010 cap-and-trade legislation (the Waxman-Markey bill) — the last serious Congressional climate bill.
  - **[[Cato Institute]]**: Koch-founded libertarian think tank.
  - **[[Heritage Foundation]]**: recipient of Koch money.
  - **[[ALEC]] (American Legislative Exchange Council)**: state-level bill pipeline.
  - **[[State Policy Network]]**: 64-state think tank network.
  - **[[Heartland Institute]]**: climate-denial operations.
  - **Citizens for a Sound Economy**: Koch-founded predecessor to Americans for Prosperity and FreedomWorks.
  - **[[DonorsTrust]]**: the dark-money pass-through vehicle.
- **Climate denial as deliberate strategy**: Leonard documents Koch's investment in climate-denial infrastructure beginning in the mid-1990s, explicitly timed to neutralize the emerging scientific and regulatory consensus. This was not passive funding — Koch's political operation helped kill the Kyoto Protocol domestically and the Waxman-Markey bill.
- **No Climate Tax Pledge**: Koch network's mechanism for binding Republican legislators to oppose any climate pricing.
- **Koch donor summits**: the semi-annual meetings at which Koch convened his donor network — the "Kochtopus" — coordinating strategy across hundreds of organizations. Leonard provides granular detail on how these worked operationally.
- **Union-busting at Pine Bend Refinery, Minnesota**: detailed case study of Koch's sustained campaign to break the Oil, Chemical and Atomic Workers union local at its Minnesota refinery, including the 1972 lockout — a template for the network's later labor policy agenda.
- **Charles Koch vs. David Koch / family battle**: the internecine dispute between Charles and his brothers (Frederick and William) over control of the company, resulting in a 1980 buyout and a bitter litigation battle through the 1990s. Relevant as a character study of Charles Koch's methods.
- **The "Kochtopus"**: Leonard adopts this term (originally coined by libertarian critics of Koch) for the interlocking network of organizations Koch funds and coordinates.
