# Source — Minority Rule: The Right-Wing Attack on the Will of the People—and the Fight to Resist It (Ari Berman, 2024)

## Bibliographic
Ari Berman, *Minority Rule: The Right-Wing Attack on the Will of the People—and the Fight to Resist It* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux / Macmillan, 2024). Berman is senior reporter at Mother Jones and author of the earlier *Give Us the Ballot* (2015) on the Voting Rights Act.

## Thesis & scope
A shrinking white conservative minority has deliberately rigged electoral processes — through structural constitutional features, voter suppression law, gerrymandering, dark money, and judicial capture — to maintain political power against majority will. The book surveys the full stack: founding-era counter-majoritarian architecture, the post-*Shelby County* suppression wave, [[Heritage Action]] model legislation, and state-level grassroots resistance. It ends by arguing state constitutions can serve as majoritarian counterweights.

## Key content relevant to the vault

**Counter-majoritarian architecture.** The Electoral College and the equal-state Senate are presented not as neutral features but as products of compromises with slaveholding states, now systematically exploited. Two senators per state regardless of population gives Wyoming the same Senate weight as California — a structural precondition for the patron-class minority-rule project the vault documents.

**[[Heritage Action]] model legislation pipeline.** Berman provides granular reporting on Heritage Action's "Roadmap to Election Integrity" and its targeting of eight battleground states: Wisconsin, Georgia, Florida, Nevada, Texas, Arizona, Iowa, and Michigan. Heritage Action drafted or co-drafted voting restriction bills and exported them to friendly state legislatures — effectively a Koch-network-adjacent ALEC-style distribution mechanism for voter suppression law. Iowa was described as "the first state we got to work in." Georgia's 2021 SB 202 incorporated Heritage-drafted provisions.

**Voter suppression mechanics.** Photo ID requirements that exclude government-issued housing IDs; aggressive voter-roll purges; restrictions on absentee voting; bans on private election administration funding (directed at Zuckerberg-funded grants in 2020); closure of polling places in majority-Black precincts; limits on early voting.

**Gerrymandering.** Wisconsin example: Republicans won 63% of state assembly seats in 2022 with 48% of the vote. The book connects this to REDMAP (covered more fully in Daley 2016) and to the Supreme Court's 2019 *Rucho v. Common Cause* ruling that federal courts cannot police partisan gerrymandering.

**Judicial capture.** [[Federalist Society]] role in populating the federal judiciary with judges who then blessed suppression laws. The book covers *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013, gutting Section 5 preclearance), *Crawford v. Marion County* (2008, upholding photo ID), *Brnovich v. DNC* (2021, weakening Section 2 VRA), and *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019, permitting extreme partisan gerrymanders).

**Heritage Action's confessed strategy.** Republican state senators in Wisconsin were recorded stating that voter ID laws would "boost the party's prospects" by depressing votes around Milwaukee and college campuses. Berman presents this as direct evidence of discriminatory intent rather than fraud prevention rationale.

**Grassroots resistance.** Fair Fight Action ([[Fair Fight]]), Arizona voter registration drives, Michigan's 2018 redistricting commission ballot initiative (Proposal 2), and state constitutions as majoritarian counterweights.
